# Source, Collison interviews Tyler Cowen at Stripe (2017)

## Framing

Unusual format: Patrick agreed to appear only on condition he could interview Tyler. The episode inverts the show's typical structure. Patrick describes it as "the conversation Patrick wanted to have with Tyler, not the one you wanted to have."

The signal: Patrick's questions reveal how Patrick thinks more than Tyler's answers do.

## Patrick's Substantive Questions (revealing his intellectual frames)

### On Progress and Macroeconomics

"To what degree do you have confidence that economics will ever get to durable answers to these questions, and what do you imagine the version of the Paul Romer paper in 30 years to look like?"

Frame revealed: skepticism toward expert certainty; interest in whether fields actually converge to consensus.

### On Culture and Globalization

"If you think culture is so important and so underinvested in and so understudied, is it not too hasty to advocate for a force that's producing a net reduction in the quantity of it in the world?"

Frame revealed: concern with *stocks* of cultural diversity and long-term depletion. Patrick thinks about civilizational portfolio risk.

### On Diversity (the nuanced version)

"Can you say a bit more about the concept of diversity with regard to culture or ideas or whatever?"

Patrick probes "diverse monocultures" without taking the surface diversity rhetoric. Suggests he distinguishes structural from cosmetic.

### On Immigration

"If you could put forward any immigration policy tomorrow, what would your policy be, for the US in particular?"

Frame revealed: policy-design thinking over ideology. Plus attention to how policies signal national identity.

### On Books and Information Infrastructure

"Do you have any heuristics for getting better at more quickly finding, identifying books that are likely to change our minds?"

"Is there something between blogs and books that you think ought to exist that does not?"

Frame revealed: interest in *information infrastructure*, how knowledge systems function, where the gaps are.

### On Religion

"Is religion one of the few remaining sources of durable cultural diversity in an increasingly connected world?"

Frame revealed: thinking about what institutions preserve distinctiveness across centuries.

### On Clusters and Heterodoxy

"Was it obviously a good decision to hire them at the point where you did?"

"If we go home and we all conclude that we should ourselves become part of some movement to help create more of these heterodox clusters, is the characteristic you're just describing the thing you should be trying to do even outside of economics?"

Frame revealed: interest in *replicating institutional conditions* for unconventional thinking. Operator-level question about cluster design.

## Tyler's Notable Answers (engineering relevance)

### On Macro's Limits

"In macro, we're very good at retrodiction. But in terms of control, we believed in the great moderation; we were wrong."

On policy design: "If you have a good rule, you have to wonder if all systems can be gamed. So I'm not sure there's an eternal solution, but I think there are policy semi-solutions that at least are good for 20 or 30 years."

### On Cluster Design (most directly relevant)

"You need the prod. You need the insulation. You need the common language. You need the barriers. You need to be weird and have a theory of your own weirdness that's different from what your own weirdness actually is."

On George Mason: emphasized "determination, and stick with our strategy" across hiring.

On reading the system: "There's a kind of embedded algorithm in blogs, Twitter, other social media. If you're really good at reading the system, that's when you learn things."

### On Context-Dependence

"Be careful about overgeneralizing from your experience here. You have the cluster. So there's a lot of things you can do and get away with that don't work in a more general way."

A warning to founders against transplanting Silicon Valley patterns to contexts that lack the supporting structure.
