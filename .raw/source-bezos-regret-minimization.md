# Source, Bezos Regret Minimization Framework

## Context

Bezos was 30 years old, employed at hedge fund D.E. Shaw & Co. in New York, when he started thinking about launching an online bookstore. His boss told him to think for 48 hours before deciding.

## The Framework (verbatim Bezos)

> "The framework I found, which made the decision incredibly easy, was what I called, which only a nerd would call, a 'regret minimization framework.'"

> "I want to project myself forward to age 80 and say, 'Okay, now I'm looking back on my life. I want to have minimized the number of regrets I have.'"

## How He Applied It

> "I knew that when I was 80 I was not going to regret having tried this. I knew that if I failed I wouldn't regret that, but I knew the one thing I might regret is not ever having tried. I knew that that would haunt me every day."

## The Decision Criterion

The framework distinguishes between:
1. The regret of *attempting* something that fails
2. The regret of *not attempting* something at all

These are not symmetric. Failure-after-attempting can be processed and moved past. Not-attempting becomes a permanent counterfactual that compounds with time.

The framework is useful precisely when the analytical case is balanced. When pros and cons cancel out, the regret asymmetry becomes the deciding factor.

## Provenance Note

This is a secondary-source summary containing Bezos's primary quotes that have been verified across multiple interview sources (Achievement.org 2001 interview, various retrospectives). The verbatim quotes shown above are consistent across sources; treat as canonical.

Bezos has applied this framework in publicly-documented decisions beyond the 1994 Amazon decision, including (per various interviews) decisions about Blue Origin and his Washington Post acquisition.
