## What This Is

The most comprehensive recorded long-form interview with Carmack, recorded August 2022 shortly after he resigned from Meta to found Keen Technologies. 5h 23m, mostly unstructured discussion. Notable because Carmack is in his most reflective mode: 30+ years of engineering retrospect compressed into a single sitting with a willing interlocutor.

## Carmack's Workflow Discipline (3:53:30)

Public references to Carmack's daily work pattern, consistent across multiple interviews:

- Long unbroken focused sessions, often 8-12 hours
- Reads on paper, not screen, when ingesting research
- Keeps a private engineering journal (separate from .plan files)
- Refuses meetings during productive blocks
- Treats interruption as the primary cost to manage

The discipline is itself the engineering planning artifact: the system that produces consistent technical output over decades is not technique but the protection of focused time.

## On Programming Languages (9:49)

Carmack's documented language progression and current views (cross-referenced from multiple sources including the keynote series):

- Started: assembly, Pascal, C
- Production: C, then C++
- Side projects: Haskell, Racket (Scheme), Lisp variants
- Recent (Keen Technologies): exploring Python for ML, returning to C for AI inference paths

Recurring advice: learn one language deeply rather than many superficially. Pick a low-level systems language (C, Rust) and a high-level functional or dynamic language (Haskell, Racket). The two together teach orthogonal disciplines that compound when combined.

## On AGI (4:10:39)

Carmack's framing of his Keen Technologies bet, expanded throughout 2023-2024 talks but introduced here:

- AGI is not far off in terms of fundamental compute; it is far off in terms of conceptual scaffolding.
- The breakthrough will look like a small team (handful of people) writing a few thousand lines of code, not a megacorp throwing models at compute.
- Current scaling-up approaches address symptoms; the missing piece is a learning algorithm closer to how mammalian brains work.
- He explicitly compares this to id Software in 1991: a small team with a fresh frame outperforming much larger competitors.

The planning frame: Carmack thinks AGI is a 6-year bet for him personally. He moved jobs and capital structures to optimize for the right thing for that window.

## On Advice for Young People (5:09:49)

Recurring Carmack themes from this interview and others:

- Programming is a craft built over years of compounding hours. There is no shortcut.
- The thing to optimize is your environment to enable deep focus, not the tools or techniques.
- Read code, especially code you didn't write. The skill of reading code is rarer than writing code.
- Build things. The portfolio is the evidence of capability.
- Be willing to throw away your own code when you learn something better.
- Track your own mistakes; the patterns will reveal what to work on next.

## On Modern Programming (40:53)

Carmack's critique of modern engineering culture:

- Too many layers of abstraction, often justifying their existence by the difficulty of removing them.
- Languages have improved (type systems, immutability, package management); culture has regressed (over-engineering, framework churn, complexity tolerance).
- The compiler is your friend; reject toolchains that don't catch errors at compile time when they could.
- Static analysis discipline (from QuakeCon 2013) reinforced here as essential.

## On Genius (1:08:01)

A theme Carmack returns to often: genius is rarer than people think. Most "genius" engineering is actually the compounded result of years of deliberate practice and protected focus. The same person, working without the discipline, would produce mediocre work. The same person with the discipline produces extraordinary work.

This frames why his advice is consistently about discipline (protect focus, track your mistakes, build a portfolio) rather than technique.

## What This Source Teaches

- The engineer-planner archetype is built on discipline, not talent.
- Long unbroken focused sessions are the substrate; technique without them produces nothing durable.
- Throw away your own code willingly; the willingness to revise is the second-most-important discipline after focus.
- AGI is a planning bet, not just a technical one; it is the kind of thing that rewards small teams with fresh frames.
- Read code more than you write it; the skill is rare.
