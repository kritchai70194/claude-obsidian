# Source, High Output Management (Grove)

## Author Context

Andy Grove co-founded Intel and served as CEO from 1979 to 1997, growing market cap from $4B to nearly $200B with 64,000 employees. The book originated as the textbook for an internal Intel management course; the revised editions added contemporary examples. Endorsed by [[Patrick Collison]] and the Stripe Press canon; the book is the most-cited management text in the technology industry.

## Core Principle: Managerial Leverage

The book's central formula:

> A manager's output = the output of his organization + the output of the neighboring organizations under his influence

Managers are multipliers, not individual contributors. Great managers positively amplify the performance of teams around them; poor managers dampen it. This reframes the management job: not "what did I produce" but "what was produced because I was here."

## High-Leverage Activities

> "The art of management is selecting and concentrating on one, two, or three high-leverage activities and ignoring the rest."

Examples of activities Grove explicitly identifies as high-leverage:

- **Training.** "The highest leverage activity a manager can do to increase the output of an organization." Twelve hours preparing training for ten team members yields a 1% lift in their output indefinitely.
- **Motivating and teaching.** Long-term outcomes; effort compounds.
- **One-on-one meetings.** Maximum leverage through direct reports.

## One-on-One Meetings (mechanism, not vibe)

Grove's specific recipe:

- The meeting is the subordinate's meeting, not the manager's.
- Agenda set by the subordinate; provided in advance.
- Manager's role: ask questions, surface issues, mentor.
- Frequency calibrated to subordinate's task-relevant maturity (TRM).

The principle: information transfer is bidirectional, but the structure is designed so the subordinate brings the actionable items, not the manager.

## Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM)

> The salesman got promoted because of his high TRM in sales. But in his new position as a manager, he had low TRM.

TRM is task-specific competence. Promoting a high-TRM individual to a new role resets their TRM to zero in that role. Implication: don't manage everyone the same way. High-TRM reports need direction-setting and obstacle-removal; low-TRM reports need close supervision and structured learning.

## Manufacturing Principles Applied to Management

The book's pedagogical move: start with a "breakfast factory" analogy. Establish manufacturing principles (output rate, bottleneck, work-in-progress, throughput). Then transfer those principles to management work: meetings, decisions, performance reviews.

The implication: management is operations, not strategy. The output of a management group is measurable; the bottleneck is identifiable; the workflow can be optimized.

## OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

Grove's framework that John Doerr later carried to Google and that the industry now treats as canonical. Concept:

- **Objectives:** what you're trying to achieve, qualitative
- **Key Results:** how you'll measure achievement, quantitative

Originated at Intel as a way to align distributed teams on direction without micromanagement.

## Hybrid Organizations (Grove's Law)

> "All large organizations with a common business purpose end up in a hybrid organizational form."

A balance between functional teams (deep expertise) and mission teams (cross-functional execution). Individuals belong to both. The "matrix" structure most large companies operate under is downstream of this insight.
