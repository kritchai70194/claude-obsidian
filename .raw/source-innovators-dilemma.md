# Source, The Innovator's Dilemma (Christensen)

## Author Context

Clayton Christensen was a Harvard Business School professor. The book's central concept (disruptive innovation) is now industry standard vocabulary, though often used loosely. Christensen's strict definition narrower than the casual usage.

## Core Distinction: Sustaining vs Disruptive

The book's central framework:

### Sustaining innovation

What incumbents are best at. Improves a product's performance based on the needs and feedback of mainstream customers. Listen to customers, find improvement opportunities, ship them. Most innovation effort in mature companies is sustaining.

### Disruptive innovation

Lower performance on the metrics mainstream customers care about. Higher performance on different metrics that don't initially matter to the mainstream. Initially serves new or emerging markets that incumbents can ignore.

The technical structure: "Disruptive technology is usually built around proven technologies put together into a novel product architecture that offers the customer a new set of attributes never before available."

## The Core Dilemma

Why do well-managed incumbents lose to disruptors? Because the practices that made them successful (listening to customers, maximizing profit on existing lines, focusing on the biggest markets) become counterproductive when disruptive technology appears.

> "Good management strategies allocate resources for sustaining innovations that enhance current products' performance, but this strategy often blocks the big firms' vision and mobility for uncovering and developing niche-but-unproven applications and markets for disruptive products."

The dilemma is structural, not a failure of competence. Doing everything "right" by the playbook of the existing business actively prevents you from responding to disruption.

## Key Principles Incumbents Must Address

Christensen names four reasons incumbents struggle:

- **Resource dependence:** current customers determine where resources flow, including innovation budget. Disruptive ideas can't compete for resources against sustaining ones.
- **Small markets don't move the needle:** a niche market that could become huge doesn't look material when it's still small, so the incumbent assigns it low priority.
- **Disruptive futures are unknowable:** by definition, the market and use case for a disruptive technology are not yet legible. Traditional market analysis can't predict the trajectory.
- **Capabilities are constraints:** an organization's processes and values are tuned for the current business. Asking it to pursue a different business with different economics asks the impossible.

## Implications for the Operating Frame

- **Customer obsession has limits.** Listening to existing customers can blind you to disruption. The corrective is to also pay attention to non-customers and emerging segments. This is a nuance on [[Bezos 2015 Letter|Bezos's customer obsession]] frame: customer-first does not mean current-customer-first to the exclusion of future customers.
- **Resource allocation is the failure mode.** The disruptive idea fails because it can't get resources, not because it's wrong. Mechanism: create a separate organization with its own resources and metrics to pursue disruption.
- **Listen to non-customers.** What do the people NOT using your product use instead? That's where disruption usually lives.
