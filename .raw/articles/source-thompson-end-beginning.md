# Source, Thompson End of the Beginning (2020)

## What This Is

Thompson's 2020 essay arguing that cloud + mobile represent the culmination, not the launchpad, of major tech disruption. The strategic implication: the big platform players (Amazon, Microsoft, Apple, Google) are now stable like General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler in 1920, and the next wave of value creation comes from companies that take cloud+mobile as a given and transform society on top of them.

Selected as the applied modern essay because it is the most-cited "where are we now" Thompson piece since the original Aggregation Theory, and it sets the strategic frame for builders deciding what to build in the 2020s.

## The Core Thesis

> "The cloud meant applications and data could be accessed from anywhere; mobile made the I/O layer available anywhere. The combination of the two make computing continuous."

Cloud + mobile together made computing continuous. Before, computing was discrete (sessions, locations, devices). After, it is always-on, always-accessible. This is the endpoint of a multi-decade evolution from batch processing through PCs through web through mobile.

The corollary: there is no obvious next major substrate change. Voice, AR, IoT are incremental layers on cloud+mobile, not replacements. The substrate is stable.

## The Auto Industry Analogy

> "By 1920, manufacturing consolidated to three players (GM, Ford, Chrysler), yet the car's societal impact accelerated afterward, not during the competitive formation phase."

The car industry's competitive formation happened in 1900-1920. The car's societal transformation (suburbs, highways, drive-in everything) happened in 1950-1980, decades later, with stable manufacturers.

Tech is at the same point. The platform layer consolidated in 2005-2015 (Amazon, Microsoft, Apple, Google, Facebook). The societal transformation enabled by cloud+mobile is happening now (Uber 2009, Airbnb 2008, Stripe 2010, Notion 2016, OpenAI 2022) and will continue for decades.

## The Dominant Players

The four (sometimes five) stable platform incumbents:
- **Amazon** (AWS + retail, the cloud-aggregator hybrid)
- **Microsoft** (Azure + Office 365, the enterprise platform)
- **Apple** (devices + App Store + services, the consumer aggregator)
- **Google** (search + Android + cloud, the information aggregator)
- **(Facebook)** (the social aggregator, sometimes counted)

These are now structural features of the technology landscape, not disruptable in any conventional sense. New startups do not displace them; they build on top of them.

## Implications for Next-Generation Startups

> "New ventures no longer challenge cloud/mobile infrastructure directly. Instead, they take their presence as an assumption, and seek to transform society in ways that were previously impossible."

The strategic frame for building today:
- Do not try to be a platform; the platforms are taken
- Do not try to disrupt the incumbents at their core; the moats are deep
- Build on top of cloud + mobile, take that substrate as given
- Find societal problems that cloud+mobile makes newly addressable
- Aim to be an aggregator in a vertical the incumbents have not entered yet

Examples this frame predicts:
- Healthcare delivery (cloud+mobile makes telehealth viable; aggregators emerge)
- Logistics (cloud+mobile makes real-time coordination viable; Uber/DoorDash/etc.)
- Financial services (cloud+mobile makes embedded finance viable; Stripe/Square/Plaid)
- AI applications (cloud makes inference cheap; new aggregators around use cases)

## Why This Source Matters

For someone trying to build a unicorn in 2025-2030, this essay is the strategic context-setter. It answers:
- "Should I try to build a new cloud or AI infrastructure provider?" (Probably not.)
- "Should I take cloud+mobile as a given and build on top?" (Yes.)
- "What kinds of opportunities does this substrate make newly addressable?" (Look for societal transformations that cloud+mobile enables.)
- "How do I think about the dominant players?" (As stable substrate, not as targets.)

The essay is not a prescription. It is a map: here is the landscape; here is what is stable; here is where the value remains to be captured.

## How This Connects to Aggregation Theory

The original Aggregation Theory essay (2015) explained the dynamics of the previous wave. This essay (2020) names the post-aggregation landscape: the aggregators have won their categories, and the next wave of value creation happens above them, not against them.

For a builder, the practical question becomes: in which vertical (healthcare, finance, AI, etc.) can I be the aggregator before one of the incumbents enters?

## What This Source Teaches

- Cloud + mobile is the endpoint of the multi-decade substrate evolution, not a stop along the way.
- The platform layer has consolidated (Amazon, Microsoft, Apple, Google, Facebook) and is now structural.
- The next wave of value creation comes from companies that take cloud+mobile as given and transform vertical industries.
- Do not build platforms; build on platforms. Do not attack the incumbents; route around them into industries they have not entered.
- Look for societal transformations that cloud+mobile enables (telehealth, logistics, fintech, AI applications) and aim to aggregate them before incumbents do.
