# Each Necessary, But Only Jointly Sufficient (2012, kitchensoap.com)

## Core Thesis

John Allspaw argues that the widespread assumption of a single "root cause" for complex system failures is fundamentally flawed. Failures emerge from multiple contributing factors that are each necessary but only jointly sufficient to trigger failure.

## Against Linear Causality

> "for complex socio-technical systems (web engineering and operations) there is a myth that deserves to be busted...there is no root cause."

Engineers naturally prefer reductionist thinking, seeking single causes they can fix. However, this approach misses how complex systems actually fail.

## Limitations of Five Whys

The Five Whys method assumes "each presenting symptom has only one sufficient cause." This fails to capture "jointly sufficient causes that explain a symptom." The method validates hindsight bias and ignores system interconnectedness.

## Organizational Pressures

Management demands quick answers, engineers avoid uncomfortable discussions about failure, and hindsight bias makes causes seem simpler than they are — all pushing toward oversimplification.

## The Better Framework

Allspaw endorses Richard Cook's perspective: failures "require multiple contributing causes, each necessary but only jointly sufficient."

Quoting Hollnagel et al.:

> "Accidents emerge from a confluence of conditions...each necessary but only jointly sufficient...able to trigger failure."

## Implications for Learning

Effective incident investigation requires:
- Recognizing organizational factors alongside technical ones
- Expecting emergent behaviors
- Accepting nonlinear system dynamics
- Understanding why people's actions made sense given their context (local rationality)
