# The Infinite Hows (or, the Dangers of the Five Whys) (2014, kitchensoap.com)

## Core Argument

"Why?" is the wrong question. Organizations should ask **"How?"** to understand the conditions enabling incidents rather than assigning blame.

## Problems with the Five Whys

**Linguistic Bias Toward Blame.** "Why" questions naturally lead to identifying "who" caused the problem. "How" questions generate rich operational narratives describing actual workplace conditions.

**Oversimplification.** The method assumes linear causality — that a chain of "why" answers reveals a single root cause. This ignores the complexity of sociotechnical systems where multiple conditions jointly produce outcomes.

**Hindsight Bias.** Investigators possess "the benefit of hindsight" when evaluating past decisions. What appears "wrong" only seems so because we know the outcome occurred.

## The Alternative: "How" Questions

Instead of Five Whys, gather narratives through prompts addressing:

- **Cues observed** at critical moments
- **Knowledge and experience** applied
- **Expectations** about how events would develop
- **Influences** shaping interpretation and action
- **Communication patterns** used
- **Goals** and time pressures involved

This approach reveals **local rationality** — "how it made sense for someone to do what they did" at the time.

## Philosophical Foundation

First stories vs second stories:
- **First stories** identify human error as cause.
- **Second stories** reveal systemic vulnerabilities.

Allspaw draws on Sidney Dekker, Nancy Leveson, and Todd Conklin to argue that complexity theory demands moving beyond "Newtonian-Cartesian" reductionism.

**Human error becomes "a starting point, not a conclusion"** when investigating through systemic rather than individual lenses.
