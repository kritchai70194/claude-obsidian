# Source, Lenny PMF Definitive Guide

## What This Is

Lenny's most-cited PMF essay. Notable not for an original framework but for being the most thorough synthesis of how the best operators define and detect PMF. He compiled definitions and signals from Marc Andreessen, Steve Blank, Sean Ellis, Michael Seibel, Elad Gil, and others, then organized them into a workable diagnostic.

## Lenny's Working Definition

> "[PMF is when] you've made a product people want, can find and keep people sustainably, and makes a profit delivering to people at scale."

Three dimensions: desirability, sustainability, profitability. Most "PMF" claims fail one of the three.

## Three Patterns of PMF

Based on his synthesis of how PMF actually shows up:

1. **Sudden and significant pull.** Hockey-stick growth, viral spread, can't keep up with demand (Airbnb 2010, Dropbox 2008).
2. **Gradual but compounding pull.** Steady week-over-week growth, retention curve flattening, the "boring" PMF (Stripe, Notion).
3. **Hitting a milestone that proves the model works.** First $100K MRR, first cohort retaining at year 1, first B2B deal that closes without discount (B2B SaaS often looks like this).

The implication: don't expect PMF to look like Airbnb 2010. Most unicorns hit (2) or (3), not (1).

## Specific Signals of "Pull"

Market pull appears in many forms:

- An inflection in organic growth and word of mouth
- Customers complain loudly when your site goes down (they care)
- People keep using the product even when it is broken (they need it)
- Customers ask to pay before you ask them to (the canonical signal)
- Sales cycles shorten dramatically
- Inbound demand exceeds your ability to handle it
- Press writes about you without your pitching

The first three are the strongest. The "pay before asked" signal is the cleanest binary indicator.

## The Sean Ellis Survey

The canonical PMF survey (originated by Sean Ellis, popularized through Lenny):

> "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"
>
> A. Very disappointed
> B. Somewhat disappointed
> C. Not disappointed (it isn't really that useful)
> D. N/A

**Rule of thumb: 40%+ "very disappointed" = PMF.**

Caveats: works best for products with clear use cases; less reliable for early experiments; survey the right users (active, not lapsed).

## Cohort Retention as PMF Diagnostic

> Cohort retention is the most reliable quantitative measure of PMF. A retention curve that flattens (instead of declining to zero) proves that some group of users keeps coming back.

The curve shape is the signal. Not the number at day 1 or day 7. Watch what happens by day 30, day 60, day 90: does it flatten, or does it asymptote to zero?

Specifically: strong day-30 retention is >= 25% for consumer products, >= 35% for B2B (per Lenny's benchmark research; see [[Lenny Retention Benchmarks]]).

## Operator Quotes Compiled in the Essay

Lenny cites multiple definitions, including:

> Marc Andreessen: "Product/market fit means being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market."

> Steve Blank: PMF happens when you have "validated that you've solved a problem worth solving for a substantial market."

> Sean Ellis: "Product/market fit is when 40% of your users say they would be 'very disappointed' if they couldn't use your product anymore."

Lenny's synthesis: each definition is correct from a different vantage. The operator (Andreessen) thinks market fit; the customer-dev person (Blank) thinks problem-solution fit; the growth person (Ellis) thinks survey signal. Real PMF satisfies all three.

## Pre-Product vs Post-Product Signals

Pre-product (before launch):
- Founder has been the customer for years; problem is "scratching own itch"
- Existing manual solutions exist (people are paying for ugly workarounds)
- Customer interviews surface the same pain in the same words

Post-product:
- Retention curve shape
- Organic growth inflection
- Sean Ellis survey result
- Inbound revenue (customers seek you out)

The pre-product signals are weaker but cheaper. The post-product signals are stronger but require shipping.

## What This Source Teaches

- PMF is measurable, not a feeling.
- Three patterns: sudden, gradual, milestone. Most unicorns are the second or third.
- The strongest signals: customers complain when down, keep using when broken, ask to pay before asked.
- Sean Ellis survey: 40%+ very-disappointed = PMF.
- Cohort retention curve shape is the most reliable single metric.
- Multiple definitions are all correct from their angles; real PMF satisfies all three.
