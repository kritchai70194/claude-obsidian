# Source, Thompson Bill Gates Line (2018)

## What This Is

Thompson's 2018 essay drawing the canonical distinction between platforms and aggregators. Named after a Bill Gates quote (via Chamath Palihapitiya) that gives a clean test for whether a business is truly a platform.

The essay is load-bearing for any strategic decision about whether to BUILD a platform, build ON a platform, build an aggregator, or build ON an aggregator. The four answers have very different economic outcomes.

## The Bill Gates Quote

> "A platform is when the economic value of everybody that uses it, exceeds the value of the company that creates it."

The test: add up the value created by everyone building on top, plus the company itself. If the third-party value exceeds the platform value, it is a real platform. If not, it is just a product.

## Platform vs Aggregator

**Platforms** facilitate relationships between third-party suppliers and end users. They enable value creation across an ecosystem. The platform captures a minority of total value; third parties capture the majority.

Examples: Windows (Microsoft captured much less value than the Windows software ecosystem), AWS (the value of companies built on AWS dwarfs Amazon's AWS revenue), Azure.

**Aggregators** intermediate and control the relationship between suppliers and users. They own the user relationship; suppliers function as commodities.

Examples: Google Search (websites are the commoditized supply; Google owns the user), Facebook (publishers are commoditized; Facebook owns the user).

The distinction is about who captures the value: platforms distribute it; aggregators concentrate it.

## Below the Line: App Stores

Thompson places Apple's App Store **below the Bill Gates Line**. The App Store technically enables third parties to build, but Apple captures the majority of ecosystem value (30% rake, plus control of distribution). It is a platform in structure, an aggregator in economics.

This is a useful warning: just because third parties can build on top does not make something a real platform. The economic test matters.

## Strategic Implications

For builders deciding what to be:

- **Build a real platform**: Strong network effects, but you capture less of the value pool. Need scale and longevity.
- **Build an aggregator**: You capture more value, but you must own the user relationship and never let third parties intermediate.
- **Build ON a platform** (e.g. AWS): Strong leverage, captures value above the platform's slice. Recommended for most startups.
- **Build ON an aggregator** (e.g. Yelp on Google): Structurally disadvantaged. The aggregator can disintermediate you at any time.

The Yelp-on-Google example: even if Google featured Yelp content prominently, users would eventually bypass Yelp entirely. Building on an aggregator means giving the aggregator the option to take your business.

## How This Connects to Aggregation Theory

The 2015 Aggregation Theory essay explained how aggregators win. The 2017 Defining Aggregators essay provided the taxonomy. This 2018 essay completes the trilogy by distinguishing aggregators from platforms, which are structurally different despite both being "tech companies with users".

The strategic move sequence:
1. Determine if your market is being aggregated (Aggregation Theory)
2. If yes, determine which level (Defining Aggregators)
3. Determine whether you should be a platform, aggregator, or build on top of one (Bill Gates Line)

## What This Source Teaches

- The Bill Gates Line: a real platform is one where third-party value exceeds the platform's own.
- Platforms distribute value across ecosystems; aggregators concentrate it.
- App stores are below the line: structurally platforms, economically aggregators.
- Build on platforms (AWS), not on aggregators (Yelp on Google).
- The strategic question is not "platform or product" but "platform or aggregator", and the answers have very different economics.
