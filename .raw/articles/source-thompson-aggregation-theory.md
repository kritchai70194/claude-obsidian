# Source, Thompson Aggregation Theory (2015)

## What This Is

The foundational Stratechery essay that named and defined Aggregation Theory. The most-cited modern strategy framework. Explains why Google, Facebook, Amazon, Uber, Airbnb, and Netflix won the internet era while traditional incumbents could not respond.

Notable for being free (most Stratechery is paywalled) because Thompson considers it definitional foundation.

## The Value Chain Frame

> "The value chain for any given consumer market is divided into three parts: suppliers, distributors, and consumers/users."

Pre-internet: distributors integrated backward with suppliers, controlling access to consumers through scarcity of distribution channels. Whoever owned distribution owned the value chain.

## The Internet's Two Inversions

Two changes that flipped the dynamics:

1. > "The Internet has made distribution (of digital goods) free, neutralizing the advantage that pre-Internet distributors leveraged to integrate with suppliers."

2. > "The Internet has made transaction costs zero, making it viable for a distributor to integrate forward with end users/consumers at scale."

Distribution scarcity, gone. Transaction cost, gone. The two pillars of incumbent advantage, gone.

## The New Pattern: Aggregation

In the post-internet model:

> "Aggregators integrate forward with consumers instead, commoditizing suppliers in the process."

The reversal: where distributors used to integrate backward to suppliers, aggregators integrate forward to consumers. Suppliers become the commodity layer; consumers become the moat.

## The Virtuous Cycle

> "The most important factor determining success is the user experience: the best distributors/aggregators/market-makers win by providing the best experience, which earns them the most consumers/users, which attracts the most suppliers, which enhances the user experience in a virtuous cycle."

The flywheel: better UX → more users → more suppliers attracted (commoditized) → better UX → more users. The cycle is self-reinforcing and produces winner-take-all dynamics within a category.

## Company Examples (Thompson's Own)

> **Google** "modularized individual pages and articles, making them directly accessible via search" while integrating "search results with search and profile data about users."

> **Facebook** modularized advertisements, allowing "advertisers to target customers directly, not via proxy."

> **Amazon** "modularized distribution first via e-commerce and then via e-books."

> **Netflix** "modularized broadcast availability by making its entire library available at any time."

> **Uber** "modularized fleet management by working with independent drivers."

> **Airbnb** "modularized vacant properties by building a reputation system for trust."

The pattern across all six: modularize the supply side (commoditize it), integrate the demand side (own the user relationship).

## How This Differs from Christensen

Thompson connects to Christensen's *Conservation of Attractive Profits*:

> "Profits shift when another company successfully modularizes the incumbent and integrates another part of the value chain."

But the mechanism differs from traditional disruption (attacking the low-end market). Aggregators:
- Do not attack low-end first
- Do not require any quality compromise
- Attack by commoditizing suppliers entirely while owning the consumer relationship
- Create winner-take-all dynamics across entire markets simultaneously

This is why incumbent responses to Aggregation Theory rarely work: the incumbent is structurally on the supplier side. There is no low-end to defend; the supplier is being modularized whether they fight or not.

## The Strategic Implication

The strategic question for any digital business:

> Am I on the supplier side or the aggregator side?

If supplier: the moat must be deeper than commodification (irreplicable IP, exclusive content, regulatory protection). Most "moats" do not survive aggregation.

If aggregator: the moat is the user relationship. Protect it; never let a third party intermediate it.

## What This Source Teaches

- The internet inverted two pillars of incumbent advantage: distribution scarcity and transaction costs.
- The new winners integrate forward to consumers and commoditize suppliers.
- The virtuous cycle (UX → users → suppliers → better UX) is self-reinforcing.
- The moat is the user relationship, not the product features.
- Incumbents lose because they are structurally on the supplier side; the response playbook from pre-internet eras does not apply.
- Strategy for any digital business: figure out which side you are on, and act accordingly.
