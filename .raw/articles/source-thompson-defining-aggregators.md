# Source, Thompson Defining Aggregators (2017)

## What This Is

Thompson's 2017 essay refining Aggregation Theory. Where the 2015 essay defined the pattern, this one provides the taxonomy: not all aggregators are equal. The hierarchy (Level 1 → Super-Aggregator) predicts which businesses will face the steepest competition and which have built unkillable structural advantages.

## Three Defining Characteristics of an Aggregator

All three must be present:

1. **Direct user relationship**

> "Aggregators have a direct relationship with users. This may be a payment-based relationship, an account-based one, or simply one based on regular usage."

The user belongs to the aggregator, not to a third party. No intermediation.

2. **Zero marginal costs**

> "The goods 'sold' by an aggregator are digital and thus have zero marginal costs."

Plus zero distribution costs (internet) and near-zero transaction costs (automation). The cost curve of serving the next user is flat.

3. **Demand-driven multi-sided networks**

> "Customer acquisition costs decrease over time; marginal customers are attracted to the platform by virtue of the increasing number of suppliers."

The virtuous cycle from Aggregation Theory 2015. The longer the aggregator runs, the cheaper acquisition gets.

## The Taxonomy

### Level 1: Supply Acquisition Cost Aggregators

Thompson example: **Netflix**

The aggregator must pay to acquire supply (content licensing, exclusive deals). Market power comes from superior buying power, but supply is finite and contested. Competitive moat is operational scale; competitors can outbid.

### Level 2: Supply Transaction Cost Aggregators

Thompson example: **Uber**

Supply is in principle infinite (any driver), but onboarding has friction (background checks, vehicle verification, regulatory compliance). The aggregator's moat is operational machinery for onboarding supply at scale; competitors must rebuild that machinery.

### Level 3: Zero Supply Cost Aggregators

Thompson example: **Google, social networks**

> "Suppliers...actively make themselves more easily searchable and discoverable."

Supply comes to the aggregator for free, because being discoverable is itself valuable. The aggregator pays nothing for supply; the supplier pays (in attention, in SEO labor, in content production) for the chance to be aggregated.

This is the strongest structural position. The aggregator's only cost is operating the platform; supply is free and self-organizing.

### Super-Aggregators

> "Multi-sided markets with at least three sides, users, suppliers, and advertisers, and have zero marginal costs on all of them."

Only **Facebook** and **Google** qualify. Users come for free (the product), suppliers come for free (to be discovered), advertisers come for free (to reach users). Three sides, all zero marginal cost.

This is structurally unkillable. There is no cost lever a competitor can pull to attack the position.

## Winner-Take-All Dynamic

> "Since the value of an aggregator to end users is continually increasing it is exceedingly difficult for competitors to take away users or win new ones."

The flywheel from Aggregation Theory 2015, made explicit:
- More users → more value per user (from network effects, from more supply)
- More value per user → cheaper acquisition (because users seek the aggregator out)
- Cheaper acquisition → more users
- Repeat

Competitors face the inverse flywheel: fewer users → less value → harder to acquire → fewer users.

The dynamic is winner-take-all within a category. Two viable competitors at the Super-Aggregator level is unstable; one wins.

## The Implication for Strategy

For an existing business:
- Identify which level you are. Level 1 (Netflix) faces ongoing supply cost pressure. Level 3 (Google) does not.
- Climb the levels if possible. Netflix originals are a Level 1 → Level 2 move (commission supply at scale, not just license it).
- A Level 3 aggregator in your category is the existential competitor; everything else is noise.

For a new business:
- Build for Level 3 from the start if the market allows it. Many do not, but some that look like Level 1 are actually Level 3 in disguise.
- Identify the multi-sided network early; design for it explicitly.

## What This Source Teaches

- An aggregator has three defining attributes: direct user relationship, zero marginal costs, demand-driven multi-sided network.
- Three levels by supply cost: Level 1 (Netflix, paid), Level 2 (Uber, onboarding friction), Level 3 (Google, free).
- Super-Aggregators (Facebook, Google) operate three-sided markets at zero marginal cost on all sides; structurally unkillable.
- Winner-take-all within a category; the flywheel is self-reinforcing.
- Strategic move: climb the levels when possible; identify which level you and your competitors are at; treat Level 3 competitors as existential.
