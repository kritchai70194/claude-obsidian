# Seed Corpus, Operating at Scale Pole

Phase 5 of the unicorn-expansion roadmap. Adds the **Operating at Scale pole** (8th pole) covering the disciplines of going from 10 → 100 → 1000 people. Specifically: OKR + management cadence (Grove), raising standards and tempo (Slootman), culture and hiring philosophy (McCord), high-bar hiring rubrics (Rabois).

Andy Grove was previously referenced (the existing `wiki/sources/High Output Management.md` is a stub); this phase brings him in as a full mentor with proper source coverage.

---

## Mentors (4)

### Andy Grove, c-000147

CEO of Intel 1987-1998. Author of *High Output Management* (1983), *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996), *Swimming Across* (memoir, 2001). The originator of OKRs (later popularized by Doerr and adopted at Google). The canonical operator on engineering management at scale: managerial leverage, training as the highest-leverage activity, the one-on-one as the central management tool, strategic inflection points.

### Frank Slootman, c-000148

CEO of Snowflake 2019-2024 (IPO Sept 2020 at $33B, peak market cap > $100B). Previously CEO of ServiceNow (2011-2017, IPO 2012) and Data Domain (2003-2009, acquired by EMC for $2.4B). Author of *Amp It Up* (2022) and *Tape Sucks* (2011). The canonical voice on raising standards and increasing tempo across an organization; the "amp it up" operating posture.

### Patty McCord, c-000149

Chief Talent Officer at Netflix 1998-2012. Co-author with Reed Hastings of the original Netflix Culture Deck (2009). Author of *Powerful: Building a Culture of Freedom and Responsibility* (2018). The canonical voice on the Netflix culture model: hire fully-formed adults, pay top-of-market, run a high-performance org via context (not control), be willing to part with people whose skills are mismatched to the current stage.

### Keith Rabois, c-000150

General Partner at Khosla Ventures (2018-2025). Previously OIR at Founders Fund, COO at Square, VP at Slide and LinkedIn, partner at multiple companies. PayPal Mafia. Stanford BA + Harvard JD. The canonical voice on hiring rubrics: the "barrel" framework (operator who can run an entire function), interview discipline, the cost of "good but not great" hires.
