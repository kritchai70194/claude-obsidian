# Secrets and Inflections

Synthesis page bridging [[Peter Thiel]]'s "secrets" framing with [[Mike Maples]]'s "inflections" framing. Both name the same underlying discipline from different angles: **contrarian thesis formation with reasoning, not vibes**.

Most founders pitch ideas. The best founders identify secrets (Thiel) or inflections (Maples) first, then build the idea on top. The page distinguishes the two, shows where they overlap, and gives the operational toolkit for finding them.

---

## Thiel's "Secrets"

> What important truth do very few people agree with you on? ([[Thiel Zero to One]])

A secret is a true belief that mainstream wisdom rejects. Three categories:

- **Natural secrets:** truths about how the physical world works that haven't been formally proven or widely accepted (curing aging, room-temperature superconductors, fusion economics).
- **Human secrets:** truths about how people actually behave vs how they're supposed to behave (in marketing, in dating, in voting, in product preferences).
- **Business secrets:** truths about market dynamics that incumbents and most VCs reject (everyone "knows" the milkshake business is fine the way it is, until Christensen reframes it as a morning-commute job).

The secrets test:

1. Can you articulate the belief in one sentence?
2. Do most experts in the field disagree with you?
3. Is the belief specific enough to be falsifiable?
4. Have you tested it with real users, real data, real circumstances?

Most "contrarian" beliefs fail #3 (too vague to be falsified) or #4 (untested theory). Real secrets pass all four.

Examples of real secrets at founding:
- **Stripe (2010):** developers want a 7-line API to accept payments; banks make this impossible; we can fix that. Mainstream wisdom: payments require complex integrations, the existing player (Authorize.net + Verisign + bank gateway stack) is sufficient.
- **Airbnb (2008):** people will sleep in strangers' homes if there's enough trust signal. Mainstream wisdom: people only stay in hotels.
- **PayPal (1999):** peer-to-peer money will compete with banks. Mainstream wisdom: banks own this.
- **Tesla (2003):** electric cars can be premium, not compliance products. Mainstream wisdom: EV is for compliance regulations.

Each of these was rejected by most VCs at the time. The secret was specific, falsifiable, and tested.

## Maples's "Inflections"

> Inflections precede ideas. Most ideas are responses to inflections that have already happened. ([[Maples Inflections vs Ideas]])

An inflection is an empirical shift in the world that enables previously-impossible solutions. Categories:

- **Technology inflections:** mobile (2007 iPhone), cloud (2006 AWS), AI (2020-2025 LLMs).
- **Regulatory inflections:** ride-sharing law changes, cannabis legalization, telemedicine rules during COVID.
- **Behavioral inflections:** remote work normalization (2020), creator economy emergence (2018-2020), social-feed exhaustion (2018+).
- **Cost inflections:** solar panel costs (down 90% 2010-2020), genome sequencing (down 1000x), GPU compute economics.
- **Demographic inflections:** millennials entering the home-buying market, GenZ entering the workforce.

The inflection test:

1. Is the shift empirical? (Show the data: cost curve, regulatory change date, behavioral metric.)
2. Has the shift happened recently enough that incumbents haven't fully adapted?
3. Is the magnitude meaningful? (10% changes are usually noise; 10x changes are inflections.)
4. Does the shift enable solutions that weren't possible before?

Maples's frame: most startups fail because they ride an idea (a proposed solution) without identifying the inflection that makes it possible. The result is "ahead of the market" failures.

Examples of inflection-driven startups at founding:
- **Twitter (2007):** SMS character limit + mobile + always-on connectivity inflection produced status-update behavior.
- **Twitch (2011):** broadband + multi-stream video + gaming-as-entertainment inflection.
- **Lyft (2012):** smartphone GPS + payments + regulatory ambiguity inflection.
- **AngelList (2010):** SEC rules on accredited investor solicitation + post-2008 founder culture inflection.

## Where They Overlap

The Thiel secret and the Maples inflection are often the same thing seen from different angles:

- **A secret** is a belief about how the world works.
- **An inflection** is empirical evidence the world is changing in that direction.

If you have a secret without an inflection, you have a thesis that the market is not ready for; you may be 5-10 years early.

If you have an inflection without a secret, you have a market change but no proprietary insight into how to act on it; you are competing on speed of execution against everyone else who sees the same data.

The double match is the highest-leverage starting position:

> The best founders combine a contrarian thesis (secret) with empirical validation (inflection) that the world is moving in that direction. ([[Mike Maples]] + [[Peter Thiel]] synthesis)

## How to Find a Secret / Inflection

### Live in the future (Maples)

> The founders who already use the technology of the future as part of their daily life see opportunities the rest of the market hasn't noticed. ([[Maples Live in the Future]])

You can't reason about secrets you don't experience. The best way to find a secret is to:
1. Use the new technology heavily yourself (early-stage AI tools, niche developer tools, new social platforms).
2. Use the new behaviors (remote work patterns, creator-economy participation).
3. Talk to other early users about what they actually do, not what they tell you they want.

### Read across disciplines (Munger latticework + Thiel)

Secrets often live at boundaries between disciplines (Stripe = developer tools + financial regulation; Airbnb = trust signals from social networks + hospitality industry; Tesla = consumer brand + battery chemistry + manufacturing).

### Ask the "what would have to be true" question (Maples)

> What would have to be true for this to be the right answer?

If the founder can list 3-5 specific empirical claims that must be true, and 2-3 are already validated, they have an inflection + partial secret. If they can only list vibes, they don't yet.

### Look at the boring underbelly (Thiel + PG schlep blindness)

Most secrets are hidden in "boring" domains because no one is looking. The dating app secret (Tinder = trust through photos + radius + speed) was hidden in plain sight while the industry was building elaborate compatibility algorithms. The Stripe secret was hidden in the payments boring underbelly while the industry was building consumer wallets.

Cross-reference [[Paul Graham]] schlep blindness: founders avoid the schlep work that contains the real opportunity.

## What's NOT a Secret or Inflection

- **"AI is going to be huge."** Not a secret (everyone agrees), not specific.
- **"Remote work is the future."** Not an inflection now (happened in 2020-2021); maybe an inflection for specific verticals.
- **"This market is broken."** Vague. What specifically is broken? For whom? What is the metric?
- **"Incumbents are slow."** True everywhere; not actionable.
- **"Trust is the key."** Generic. Trust in what, between whom, signaled by what mechanism?

Failed startups are full of these. Real secrets and inflections are specific enough to be falsified.

---

## Operational Use

When evaluating a thesis (yours or someone else's):

1. **State the secret in one sentence.** Can you?
2. **Identify the inflection that validates it.** Specific empirical shift, with dates and magnitudes.
3. **List 3 falsifiable predictions.** What would have to be true in 18 months for this thesis to be right?
4. **List 3 experts who disagree.** If no one disagrees, it's not a secret. If everyone disagrees, you might be wrong (or you might be the Tesla case).
5. **Identify the "boring underbelly" of the market.** What schlep work would have to be done that nobody is doing?

If steps 1-5 produce specific, falsifiable, contrarian-but-evidence-backed answers, you have a real thesis. If they produce vibes, you don't.
