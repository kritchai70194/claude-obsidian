# Strategic Narrative

Synthesis page on [[Andy Raskin]]'s 5-part narrative arc. The framework turns a product pitch into a movement by establishing the world the customer operates in before introducing the product. Most pitches lead with the product and lose; the strategic narrative leads with the world change and wins.

The 5 parts have to be in order. They cannot be rearranged without breaking the rhetorical logic. Reorder them and you have a normal pitch deck; preserve the order and you have a narrative arc that buyers cannot easily forget.

---

## The 5-Part Arc

### 1. Name a Big, Undeniable Change in the World

Start by naming a specific, observable shift that everyone in the room has noticed but not yet articulated. The change must be:

- **Big enough** to make the new behavior necessary (not nice-to-have).
- **Undeniable enough** that nobody argues with it.
- **Specific enough** to anchor the rest of the narrative.

Examples:
- Zuora (2016): "The world is moving from one-time transactions to subscription relationships."
- Drift (2017): "Buyers no longer want to be marketed to; they want conversations."
- Salesforce (early): "Software is moving from client-server to the cloud."

The named change is the hardest part of the narrative to get right. Too vague ("AI is changing everything") and nobody believes the rest. Too narrow ("our specific market segment is changing") and the narrative becomes a product description.

### 2. Show the Stakes

Once the change is named, show winners and losers in the new world. Make the audience feel the stakes for themselves.

- Winners: companies that adapt to the change capture the next wave of value.
- Losers: companies that don't adapt become irrelevant or go bankrupt.

This is the part where the narrative becomes urgent. Without stakes, the change is just an interesting observation; with stakes, the audience feels they MUST act.

Specific is better than abstract: "Sears, Borders, and Blockbuster all lost their categories to companies that built for the digital shift" beats "many companies fail to adapt to change".

### 3. Paint the Promised Land

Describe what the future looks like for the winners. Not your product. The world.

- What does customer experience feel like in the new world?
- What do operations look like for the company that adapted?
- What outcomes (revenue, retention, efficiency) does the winner achieve?

The Promised Land is aspirational but specific. It is the customer's "after" state in the new world, not a feature list of your product.

### 4. Identify Obstacles

Name what stops most companies from reaching the Promised Land. This is where most narratives fail (by skipping obstacles entirely or naming weak ones).

Strong obstacles:
- Specific (named systems, processes, beliefs, structures that need to change).
- Not too easy (if removing the obstacle is trivial, the customer doesn't need help).
- Not the product (the obstacle is in the customer's world, not "they don't have your product yet").

The obstacle creates the gap the product fills. Without obstacles, the product appears unnecessary. With well-named obstacles, the product is the obvious answer.

### 5. Show Evidence: Your Company as the Guide

Now and only now introduce the product. The product is not the hero of the narrative; the customer is. Your company is the guide.

The evidence section:
- Specific capabilities you have that solve the named obstacles.
- Proof (customer stories, metrics, case studies) that you actually deliver.
- The "magic" or "special sauce" that makes you uniquely positioned to be the guide.

The product feels inevitable when introduced at this stage. The audience has already accepted the world change, the stakes, the Promised Land, and the obstacles. Your product is the natural fit.

## Why the Order Matters

Reversing the order produces a normal pitch and fails for a specific reason: the audience cannot care about the product until they understand the world the product is for.

Standard pitch order:
1. "We're [company] and we make [product]."
2. "Our product does X, Y, Z."
3. "Our customers include [logos]."
4. "Our metrics are [numbers]."

This fails because step 1 demands the audience already cares why they should listen, but they don't yet. Each subsequent step is downstream of an unestablished "why".

Strategic Narrative order:
1. "The world is changing in this specific way."
2. "There are winners and losers."
3. "Here is what winning looks like."
4. "Here are the obstacles."
5. "Here is how we help you overcome them."

By step 5, the audience is bought in on every preceding step. The product introduction lands on prepared ground.

## The Zuora Case Study (Raskin 2016)

The breakthrough example. Zuora's pre-2016 pitch was a standard product pitch ("we sell subscription billing software"). Sales cycles were slow; deal sizes were stuck.

The 2016 reframe using Strategic Narrative:

1. **Change:** "The world is shifting from product transactions to subscription relationships. Every industry will eventually run on subscriptions."
2. **Stakes:** "Companies that don't rebuild for subscription will lose to those that do. Examples: Adobe (transition succeeded), Blockbuster (transition failed)."
3. **Promised Land:** "Recurring revenue, predictable cash flow, deep customer relationships, financial systems that match the business model."
4. **Obstacles:** "Legacy ERP and billing systems were built for transactions. They cannot represent subscription concepts (renewals, upgrades, prorations, churn) natively. You can't rebuild your business on broken financial infrastructure."
5. **Evidence:** "Zuora is the subscription economy platform. We power [logos] with subscription-native billing, finance, and analytics."

The reframe transformed enterprise sales. Deal sizes increased; sales cycles shortened; Zuora went public in 2018 at a multi-billion valuation.

## Where Strategic Narrative Applies

The narrative is the same across multiple use cases:

- **Fundraising pitch:** investors want to fund the world change, not the product.
- **Sales pitch:** buyers want to be on the right side of the world change.
- **Recruiting pitch:** top talent wants to work on the leading edge of change.
- **Press / PR:** journalists want to write about change, not about products.
- **Internal alignment:** teams need to know the world change that justifies their work.

A company with a strong strategic narrative uses one core story across all five. A company without a narrative tells five different stories and confuses everyone.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Naming the change too narrowly.** "Our segment is growing 20%" is not a world change.
- **Skipping the obstacles section.** "Here's the future and here's our product" feels presumptuous and unsupported.
- **Making the product the hero.** "We solve [problem]" puts the company in the center; the strategic narrative puts the customer in the center.
- **Weak evidence section.** Saying "we're the leader" without metrics or proof breaks the trust the narrative built.
- **No emotional charge.** The named change should feel urgent. If it doesn't, the audience doesn't feel they have to act.

## How to Build One

The Raskin discovery process:

1. **Customer interviews.** What change have they noticed in their world that they don't yet have language for? The change is found, not invented.
2. **Pattern recognition.** Across 10-20 customer interviews, the same world change usually emerges.
3. **Articulate it as a single sentence.** If you can't, you don't have it yet.
4. **Test it.** Show the named change to customers (not your team). Do they nod? Do they push back? Do they articulate it back to you in their own words?
5. **Build the rest of the arc.** Stakes → Promised Land → Obstacles → Evidence flow naturally once the change is solid.

The mistake most founders make: drafting the narrative in a conference room with the team. Founders are too close to the product to find the change with fresh eyes; customers are not, and the narrative discovery has to start with them.
