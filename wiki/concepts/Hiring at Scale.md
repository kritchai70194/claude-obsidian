# Hiring at Scale

Synthesis page on hiring discipline as the company grows. Three frameworks converge: [[Keith Rabois]]'s "barrels and ammunition", [[Patty McCord]]'s "fully formed adults" + "keeper test", [[Jeff Bezos]]'s bar-raiser. They share a premise: hiring is the highest-leverage activity at any stage, and the cost of mis-hires compounds.

The cross-pole reference: [[Ben Horowitz]]'s "hire for strength, not lack of weakness" sits inside the Founder Resilience pole, but applies fully here. The 1:10 ratio rule: one excellent hire produces 10x the value of an average one, because excellent hires recruit other excellent hires; average hires lower the bar.

---

## The Rabois "Barrels and Ammunition" Frame

> Every company has Barrels (can take a problem from idea to shipped, can recruit, can manage budget, can own outcome) and Ammunition (execute tasks well but cannot own outcomes). ([[Rabois Barrel and Ammunition]])

Both types are necessary. The ratio varies by stage and function:

- **Pre-PMF startup:** 80% Barrels (everyone needs to be an owner; ammunition costs more than it produces).
- **Post-PMF growth stage:** 30-40% Barrels (the company needs a critical mass of executors).
- **Scaled company:** 10-20% Barrels (the operating system supports ammunition).

The diagnostic for Barrel vs Ammunition:

- Would this person take initiative without being asked? (Barrel: yes. Ammunition: needs assignment.)
- Do they ask for context or wait for instruction? (Barrel: context. Ammunition: instruction.)
- Can they recruit others to their function? (Barrel: yes. Ammunition: not yet.)
- If you handed them a $1M budget and a 6-month deadline, would they ship? (Barrel: yes. Ammunition: needs structure.)

Most companies over-hire Ammunition because Ammunition is easier to find and faster to evaluate. The discipline is to actively look for Barrels even when it takes 3-5x longer to fill the role.

## The Bezos Bar Raiser

Amazon's institutional discipline. Every hire requires a "Bar Raiser" interviewer who has no stake in the team's headcount needs and whose job is to ensure the new hire raises the bar.

The Bar Raiser veto: any Bar Raiser can stop a hire even if the hiring manager wants the person. This is the institutional defense against headcount pressure ("we need someone now") producing mid-bar hires.

The compounding effect:
- Each new hire should be better than the median of the team they join.
- Standards drift down by default; Bar Raiser prevents drift.
- Over time, average team quality rises mechanically.

The Amazon variant works in a context with high process and clear documentation. Startups can adapt: assign a "high-bar interviewer" who is not the hiring manager, ideally from a different team, with veto power.

## The McCord Keeper Test

> Would I fight to keep this person if they told me they were leaving for a competitor? If no, they're a candidate for a transition. ([[McCord Powerful]])

The reframe: instead of asking "should I fire this person", ask "would I fight to keep them". The framing surfaces the answer because most managers can't honestly say "fight to keep" about marginal performers.

The Netflix application:
- Run the Keeper Test annually for every direct report.
- For "no" answers, have an honest conversation: the role is changing, your skills don't match the next stage, here's a generous severance and 60-day transition.
- The off-ramp is more humane than keeping people in roles they're failing.

Cultural prerequisite: high-trust environment. Without trust, the Keeper Test becomes "the manager is plotting to fire me", which destroys engagement. Netflix's culture deck and continuous feedback loops created the trust.

## Hire for Strength, Not Lack of Weakness

> When hiring executives, your selection methodology must focus on the strength required for the role. Weaknesses are only relevant if they will torpedo the executive's ability to deliver the strength. ([[Horowitz Hard Things Book]])

The Horowitz frame addresses an interview bias: balanced rubrics select for "no major weaknesses" rather than "outstanding strengths". Outstanding people are usually outstanding in 1-2 dimensions and adequate-or-worse in others.

Examples:
- The best VP Sales is often poor at process documentation.
- The best VP Engineering is often poor at executive presence.
- The best Head of Product is often poor at managing rejected ideas without alienating engineers.

The discipline: define the 1-2 strengths the role absolutely requires; hire on those; design around the predictable weaknesses.

## The Interview Discipline

Combining Rabois + Bezos + Roberge (from GTM pole) on the interview process:

1. **Define ideal traits with a rubric.** (Roberge: coachable, curious, prior success, work ethic, intelligence; Rabois: ability to own outcome, recruit, manage budget.)
2. **Each interviewer evaluates 1-2 dimensions deeply.** (Bezos: distributed evaluation prevents one person from dominating.)
3. **Ask the same questions across candidates.** (Roberge: comparability requires consistency.)
4. **Score on rubric, not feelings.** (All three.)
5. **Decision within 24-48 hours.** (Rabois: slow decisions signal weak leadership and lose top candidates.)
6. **Bar Raiser / high-bar interviewer with veto.** (Bezos.)
7. **Reference checks for senior roles.** (Talk to people who managed them, not just peers.)
8. **The 90-day test.** (Rabois: written objectives + context + success criteria; if new hire can't crush 90 days, hiring decision was wrong.)

Companies that follow this discipline have hiring turnover under 10% in first year; companies that skip have 30-50% turnover and 6-month time-to-productivity gaps.

## When to Hire vs When to Delay

The right answer to "should we hire for this role?" is usually "delay if possible":

- **Hire when:** the role is permanent, the candidate is unambiguously Barrel-quality, the 90-day plan is clear.
- **Delay when:** unclear what the role does in 12 months, no Barrel candidates available, current team can absorb the work for 2-3 more months.

The cost of hiring too fast (mid-bar hire, fix later) is far higher than the cost of hiring too slow (interim discomfort). Most startups under-appreciate the asymmetry.

The CEO/founder's special role: recruiting is the CEO's job, not the recruiter's. Outsourcing early hires to recruiters is a sign the CEO doesn't yet understand the job. Recruiters become valuable after the CEO has hired the first 10-20 directly and can articulate exactly the profile.

## Compensation Discipline

> Pay top-of-market for top performers. ([[Patty McCord]] + [[Keith Rabois]])

Three principles:

1. **Pay above market for great hires; below market for mediocre.** Compress the band; reward excellence.
2. **Equity for Barrels, salary for Ammunition.** Owners get ownership; executors get cash certainty.
3. **Refresh grants annually.** Initial grants vest in 4 years; refreshes prevent senior people from being underpaid relative to new hires.

The unspoken corollary: don't try to retain underperformers with raises. The Keeper Test answers that case.

## The 1:10 Rule

> 1 great hire is worth 10 average hires. ([[Keith Rabois]])

The compounding math:
- Great hire produces 5-10x average hire output directly.
- Great hire recruits other great hires (referral network).
- Great hire raises the bar for who is allowed to follow them.
- Average hire produces ~1x output, lowers the bar, and creates noise that great hires want to escape.

The implication: when in doubt, hire one great person at 2x the cost rather than two average people. The total compensation might be similar; the total output is 5-10x.
