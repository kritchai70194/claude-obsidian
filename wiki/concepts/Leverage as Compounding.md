# Leverage as Compounding

> "All the returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest." ([[Naval Almanack]])

The Naval thesis reduced to one operating posture: durable outcomes (wealth, knowledge, reputation, relationships) are all interest curves on rare inputs that the founder is responsible for selecting and protecting across a whole career, not inside a single venture. This page consolidates the leverage frame as it applies to founder resilience. The adjacent concept [[Founder Resilience]] holds the survival half (Horowitz mode awareness, Feld mental health); this page holds the compounding half.

## Principles That Anchor the Frame

### 1. Specific knowledge cannot be trained, only attracted

The only durable edge in an efficient market is something the market itself cannot teach. By definition, it is rare, unteachable, and individual.

> "Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity and passion rather than whatever is hot right now." ([[Naval How to Get Rich]])

> "Building specific knowledge will feel like play to you but will look like work to others." ([[Naval Specific Knowledge]])

The diagnostic is internal. What do you compulsively read about, talk about for free, work on after hours without anyone asking. That is the seed. Operators who suppress specific knowledge in their teens and twenties because it does not look prestigious wonder in their thirties why their work feels generic.

### 2. Specific knowledge multiplied by leverage is the formula

The input alone stays small. The multiplier alone is generic and eventually outflanked. The combination is the founder edge.

> "Fortunes require leverage. Business leverage comes from capital, people, and products with no marginal cost of replication (code and media). Code and media are permissionless leverage." ([[Naval How to Get Rich]])

Four kinds of leverage: labor, capital, code, media. Code and media are the new permissionless layer (no one's approval required, infinite scale, zero marginal cost). Labor and capital are bought with the first two.

### 3. Play long-term games with long-term people

Compound interest applies to relationships, knowledge, and reputation just as much as to capital. The same people in repeated games produce disproportionate returns.

> "Pick an industry where you can play long-term games with long-term people." ([[Naval How to Get Rich]])

> "Play iterated games. All the returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest." ([[Naval How to Get Rich]])

A founder who treats one failed company as a definitive verdict has confused a single cycle for the long-run distribution.

### 4. Take risks under your own name

The downside is named blame; the upside is unbounded. Most operators choose the salary because they cannot bear the named downside, which is also why named accountability is rare and well-rewarded.

> "Embrace accountability, and take business risks under your own name. Society will reward you with responsibility, equity, and leverage." ([[Naval How to Get Rich]])

### 5. Seek wealth, not money or status

Three different games, regularly confused. Wealth is the means of producing while you sleep (positive-sum). Status is rank in the social hierarchy (zero-sum and addictive). Money is the in-between.

> "Seek wealth, not money or status. Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep." ([[Naval How to Get Rich]])

> "Status games are zero-sum. Wealth-creation is positive-sum." ([[Naval Almanack]])

Founders optimizing for status (visibility, conference talks, awards) make different decisions than founders optimizing for wealth (compounding ownership in productive assets). The frame determines the path.

## How This Brain Uses It

When evaluating any career, calendar, or capital decision:

1. **What am I compounding here, and on what timescale?** Hours that do not feed a compound curve (specific knowledge, code/media artifacts, named reputation, long-term relationships) are linear at best. Audit the calendar against the curves.
2. **What is my specific knowledge, by name?** Most operators have not named it. The exercise: write the three things at work where you suspect you are unusually good and where the work feels like play. That list is the seed.
3. **Is the company's edge rooted in something specifically mine (taste, pattern, technical idiom) or in capital and distribution alone?** The first compounds. The second is eventually outflanked by a rival with more of either.
4. **Am I taking the risk under my own name?** If the work is anonymous, the upside is also capped. Named accountability is the precondition for unbounded equity.

## Tensions With Other Frames

- **vs [[Capital Strategy]]:** The Naval frame is multi-decade and individual; the capital-strategy pole is multi-stage and corporate. They can pull opposite directions in the short run (blitzscale logic asks the founder to spend capital quickly even if the personal-compounding logic would conserve optionality). The synthesis: leverage is what the founder compounds across companies; capital strategy is how a single company deploys the resulting position.
- **vs [[Secrets and Inflections]]:** Specific knowledge is the operator's analog of a secret. A secret is a belief about the market; specific knowledge is a skill the operator alone has. Both pass the same falsifiability bar (rare, defensible, not commoditized), but secrets are extractable in a pitch and specific knowledge is not.
- **vs [[Customer Obsession]]:** Customer obsession is outward and immediate (read the customer, ship the outcome). Leverage compounding is inward and long-horizon (read yourself, compound the input). A founder running only customer obsession with no compounding input will execute well on a thesis that ages. A founder running only compounding with no customer obsession will compound on the wrong thing.
- **vs [[Long-Term Thinking]]:** The Bezos long-horizon frame treats long-term as a corporate posture (invest now for outcomes years out). Naval treats long-term as a personal arithmetic on iterated games. The two reinforce: Bezos's regret-minimization presupposes the multi-decade horizon Naval names explicitly.
