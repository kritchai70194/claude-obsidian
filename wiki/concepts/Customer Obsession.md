# Customer Obsession

> "We will continue to focus relentlessly on our customers." ([[Bezos 1997 Letter]] criterion #1)

The canonical Amazon operating posture. Customer-back, not competitor-back, not internal-back. It is the first item on the 1997 list of nine decision criteria and the lead defense named in the 2016 Day 1 letter, restated every year through Jassy's 2021 transition. This page consolidates the discipline; see [[Working Backwards]] for the artifact-level practice (PR/FAQ) that operationalizes it.

## Principles That Anchor the Pole

### 1. Start from the customer, not from the competitor

The default in most large companies is to read the competitor's roadmap and fast-follow. Bezos's posture inverts that.

> "Most big technology companies are competitor focused. They see what others are doing, and then work to fast follow. In contrast, 90 to 95% of what we build in AWS is driven by what customers tell us they want." ([[Bezos 2015 Letter]])

Customer-first is falsifiable: 90 to 95% is a number, not a slogan.

### 2. Customers are never satisfied (use this)

The danger of a "happy customer" reading is complacency. Bezos's reframe: dissatisfaction is the renewable resource.

> "Customers are always beautifully, wonderfully dissatisfied, even when they report being happy and business is great." ([[Bezos 2016 Day 1 Letter]])

> "No customer ever asked Amazon to create the Prime membership program, but it sure turns out they wanted it." ([[Bezos 2016 Day 1 Letter]])

The Prime example is load-bearing: customer obsession is not customer-survey-following; it is reading what customers cannot yet articulate.

### 3. Listening is necessary but insufficient

The sharpest rebuttal in the corpus to pure voice-of-customer methodologies.

> "It's critical to ask customers what they want, listen carefully to their answers, and figure out a plan to provide it thoughtfully and quickly. No business could thrive without that kind of customer obsession. But it's also not enough. The biggest needle movers will be things that customers don't know to ask for. We must invent on their behalf." ([[Bezos 2018 Letter]])

> "No one asked for AWS. No one. Turns out the world was in fact ready and hungry for an offering like AWS but didn't know it." ([[Bezos 2018 Letter]])

### 4. Fear, not loyalty

The operational definition of customer obsession as institutional discipline.

> "I constantly remind our employees to be afraid, to wake up every morning terrified. Not of our competition, but of our customers. We consider them to be loyal to us, right up until the second that someone else offers them a better service." ([[Jeff Bezos]], from Bezos 1998 Letter)

Customer loyalty has a half-life of one click. The institutional posture is daily paranoia about losing the customer, not annual review of NPS.

### 5. Customer obsession survives leadership transition

[[Andy Jassy]]'s 2021 letter restates the same compact under new leadership.

> "Iterative innovation creates magic for customers. Constantly inventing and improving products for customers has a compounding effect on the customer experience, and in turn on a business's prospects." ([[Bezos 2021 Letter]])

The principle is architectural, not personal. It outlives the founder who articulated it.

## How This Brain Uses It

When evaluating any product or strategy proposal:

1. **Who is the customer, and what changes in their world?** Not "what feature ships" but "what outcome moves." If you cannot name the changed customer state, the proposal is not customer-back.
2. **Are we reading the customer or the competitor?** Competitor-back products fast-follow into commoditized markets. Customer-back products discover Prime.
3. **What are customers not yet asking for that they would love?** Listening alone is insufficient. Inventing on their behalf is the harder half.
4. **What happens when we get this wrong?** If a feature reduces revenue but is right for the customer, ship it ([[Bezos 2003 Letter]] Instant Order Update example). Real principles cost something when followed.

## Tensions With Other Frames

- **vs [[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]]:** Current-customer obsession can blind you to non-customers and emerging segments. The customers being listened to do not want what the disruptors are building. The synthesis: customer obsession means customer-outcome, not customer-request, and explicitly includes listening to non-customers.
- **vs [[Working Backwards]]:** Customer Obsession is the posture; Working Backwards is the artifact-level practice (PR/FAQ, four documents) that operationalizes it. The posture without the practice is a slogan; the practice without the posture is theater.
- **vs [[Long-Term Thinking]]:** Some customer-right moves hurt this-quarter numbers (price cuts, free shipping, Trusted Advisor telling customers to spend less). The long-horizon frame makes those moves rational; without it, customer obsession collapses under quarterly pressure.
