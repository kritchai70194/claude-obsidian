# Disruption vs Aggregation

Synthesis page comparing [[Clayton Christensen]]'s Disruption Theory (1997) against [[Ben Thompson]]'s [[Aggregation Theory]] (2015). Both predict why incumbents lose to upstarts. Both are widely-cited. They predict different competitive dynamics and apply to different domains. Confusing them produces strategic errors.

The short version: **Disruption explains how upstarts beat incumbents by entering markets the incumbents reasonably ignore. Aggregation explains how upstarts beat incumbents by controlling the user relationship and commoditizing the suppliers the incumbents depend on.**

Both can apply to the same industry at different times. Both can apply simultaneously. But the strategic response to each is different, so the brain has to know which dynamic dominates a given situation.

---

## Disruption Theory in One Page

Source: [[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]] + [[Christensen Innovators Solution]] + [[Christensen JTBD]].

**Core claim:** Incumbents fail not from bad management but from good management. They listen to their best customers, who want sustaining innovations (better performance along existing trajectories). They reasonably ignore low-margin emerging segments. The upstart enters from below or via a new market, builds learning and capability there, and eventually moves up to overrun the incumbent.

**Two flavors:**

- **Low-end disruption.** The upstart sells a "good enough" product at lower price to under-served segments. Examples: Honda (small motorcycles vs Detroit), steel mini-mills (rebar vs integrated steel mills), Korean car makers (1980s economy cars vs US/Japan).
- **New-market disruption.** The upstart enables a previously non-consuming population to consume. Examples: Sony transistor radios (portable, lower-fidelity, enable teenagers to buy radios for their bedrooms vs vacuum-tube family radios), PCs vs mainframes (home/small office vs corporate IT), DVDs at home vs cinema.

**Why incumbents can't respond:**

The incumbent's resources, processes, and values (RPV framework) are calibrated to its existing markets. Disruptive innovations require different RPV. The standard incumbent response (build an internal autonomous unit to pursue the disruption) usually fails because the parent's metrics, hiring, and resource allocation continue to favor the established business.

**Where Disruption applies best:**

Manufacturing, traditional consumer goods, industries with established performance trajectories, tangible products with clear "good enough" thresholds.

## Aggregation Theory in One Page

Source: [[Aggregation Theory]] (synthesis page) + [[Thompson Aggregation Theory]] + [[Thompson Defining Aggregators]] + [[Thompson Bill Gates Line]].

**Core claim:** The internet inverted the structural economics of distribution. Pre-internet, the value chain bottleneck was distribution and transaction cost; whoever controlled those (newspapers, broadcasters, retailers) owned the customer relationship and could dictate to suppliers. Post-internet, distribution is free and transaction costs are near-zero; the bottleneck moves to user attention. Aggregators (Google, Facebook, Amazon, Netflix, Uber, Airbnb) win by owning the user relationship and commoditizing the supplier side.

**Three defining attributes of aggregators:**

1. Direct relationship with users
2. Zero marginal cost for serving users
3. Demand-driven multi-sided networks (lower customer acquisition cost as the network grows)

**Four levels by supplier cost:**

- **Level 1: Netflix** (acquire supply at high cost, own the catalog)
- **Level 2: Uber** (low-friction supply, no inventory)
- **Level 3: Google, Facebook** (zero-cost supply; aggregators of free content)
- **Super-Aggregators (FB+):** users + advertisers + content suppliers all multi-sided

**Why incumbents can't respond:**

Incumbents have integration with the old distribution chain. Their business model depends on suppliers and customers being separated. The aggregator removes that separation. The incumbent cannot disintermediate itself without destroying its business.

**Where Aggregation applies best:**

Software, media, digital services, two-sided markets, anywhere the internet has changed the cost structure of distribution.

## When Each Applies

| Domain | Dominant Dynamic | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Traditional manufacturing (steel, autos) | Disruption | Performance trajectory + cost asymmetries |
| Newspapers, media | Aggregation | Distribution went to zero, attention became scarce |
| Cloud infrastructure (AWS vs IBM) | Disruption (low-end) | AWS started with developers AWS-style cost performance, moved up to enterprise |
| Cloud infrastructure (AWS vs Heroku at PaaS layer) | Aggregation in part | AWS owns developer relationships; PaaS layer commoditized |
| Mobile (iPhone vs Nokia) | Disruption + Platform Power | New job (mobile internet), iPhone's app store made it a platform |
| Search engines | Aggregation | Distribution cost zero, attention scarce, Google owned users + advertisers |
| Streaming (Netflix vs cable) | Both | Disruption (lower cost, on-demand vs scheduled) + Aggregation (direct user relationship) |
| AI foundation models | Open question | Depends on whether models commoditize (Aggregation toward LLM apps) or remain scarce (Disruption from below by efficient models) |

The frame matters because the strategic response differs:

- **Against a Disruption threat:** create an autonomous business unit, possibly outside the parent's headquarters, with its own metrics. Be willing to cannibalize.
- **Against an Aggregation threat:** the incumbent often cannot win head-on. The choice is to become a supplier to the aggregator (with reduced margin) or to find a domain the aggregator's model cannot serve (regulated industries, physical-presence businesses, content with unique IP).

## Where They Differ

| | Disruption | Aggregation |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Upstart enters under-served segment, moves up | Upstart owns user relationship, commoditizes suppliers |
| **Cost structure** | Cost asymmetry favors upstart at low end | Distribution cost goes to zero, attention becomes the scarce resource |
| **Why incumbent loses** | Listens too well to best customers; ignores low end | Cannot disintermediate own value chain without destroying business |
| **Where it applies best** | Tangible products with performance trajectories | Software, media, two-sided markets |
| **Speed** | Slow (years to decades) | Fast (months to years once tipping point passed) |
| **Defense** | Autonomous unit, willing to cannibalize | Become a supplier; or find a domain the aggregator cannot serve |
| **Resulting market structure** | Multiple winners across segments | Winner-take-all or winner-take-most |

---

## Where They Converge

Both theories agree that:

- **Incumbents fail through rational decisions, not through stupidity.**
- **The dominant competitive threat is rarely from the direction incumbents watch.**
- **Market segmentation by demographic is misleading; segmentation by job (Christensen) or by user-relationship structure (Thompson) is causal.**

The Mungerian inversion: ask not "how do we beat the upstart" but "what would the upstart have to do to guarantee they cannot beat us". If the answer is "do exactly what they're doing", you are about to lose.

## Implications for the Brain's Strategic Reasoning

When the existing [[Aggregation Theory]] page is the lead frame and Disruption is not considered, the brain can miss:

- Slow-moving Disruptions from below (manufacturing, regulated industries, hardware).
- New-market Disruptions that enable previously-non-consuming populations.

When Disruption is the lead frame and Aggregation is not considered, the brain can miss:

- Internet-era distribution inversions.
- Two-sided market dynamics where supplier commoditization is the key.

The decision frame **"Is this defensible?"** (frame 9 in [[Persona]]) needs both lenses:

- Aggregation Theory side: are we on the supplier-being-commoditized side or the aggregator-owning-users side?
- Disruption Theory side: are we vulnerable to a low-end or new-market upstart? Are we so focused on our best customers that we are ignoring the slope?

Updated frame 9 reads: "Are we aggregator or supplier? Are we disruptor or sustaining? Are we Powers-stacked (7 Powers)? Each lens names a different competitive geometry."
