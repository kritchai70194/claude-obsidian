# Repeatable GTM

Synthesis page on building a repeatable sales motion from PMF to scale. Combines [[Mark Roberge]]'s data-driven sales discipline (hire predictably, train uniformly, manage by metric, generate predictable demand) with [[Aaron Ross]]'s outbound prospecting framework (SDR/AE specialization, Cold Calling 2.0, three sales engines).

The other GTM concept pages address adjacent questions: [[Strategic Narrative]] covers what the pitch IS (Raskin); [[Positioning as Strategy]] covers which market you're in (Dunford); this page covers the operating motion that turns a positioned, narrated product into recurring revenue.

The single most important precondition: PMF first. Repeatable GTM scales an already-working motion; it does not create one. Trying to build sales process before PMF burns capital faster than any other founder mistake.

---

## The 4-Step Sales Acceleration Formula (Roberge)

> Hire the same kind of salesperson repeatedly. Train them the same way. Give them a consistent process. Generate predictable demand. ([[Roberge Sales Acceleration Formula]])

Each step is a discrete discipline:

**1. Hiring.** Define ideal salesperson traits with rubric (coachable, curious, prior success, work ethic, intelligence). Score interviews quantitatively. Reject "gut feel" hiring. Roberge's HubSpot data: top performers shared 5 specific traits; hiring rubric raised first-year retention from 50% to 80%.

**2. Training.** Same onboarding, same playbook, same objection handling, same product knowledge for every hire. The training itself is the script for what consistency looks like.

**3. Managing.** Track lead-to-revenue conversion rates at every stage. Coach to the bottleneck. Weekly 1:1s focused on metrics, not anecdotes.

**4. Demand Generation.** Inbound + outbound + customer expansion. Each requires different content, different cadence, different cost structure.

Get one step wrong, the whole formula breaks. Most companies skip hiring rigor (it's slow and uncomfortable) and pay for it later in turnover.

## The 3 Sales Engines (Ross)

> Most companies need all three: Seeds, Nets, and Spears. ([[Ross Predictable Revenue]])

- **Seeds:** customer referrals and word-of-mouth. Highest conversion, longest cycle to scale, requires happy customers first.
- **Nets:** inbound marketing (content, SEO, paid ads to a free trial or demo). Marketing-led. Scales with content and reach.
- **Spears:** outbound SDR prospecting. Sales-led. Researched targeting + Cold Calling 2.0 cadence.

The right mix depends on stage and ACV (Annual Contract Value):

- **< $1K ACV:** product-led growth (Nets dominant), little or no Spears
- **$1K-10K ACV:** Nets + light Spears, AEs handle inbound and modest outbound
- **$10K-100K ACV:** balanced Nets/Spears, SDR/AE split
- **$100K+ ACV:** Spears dominant, longer sales cycles, account-based selling
- **$1M+ ACV (enterprise):** named-account selling, multi-thread, board-level involvement

Mismatching engine to ACV is a common failure: paying $50K/year for an SDR to prospect $200 ACV deals never works; hoping inbound marketing alone will close $500K ACV enterprise deals also never works.

## The SDR / AE Split (Roberge + Ross)

> Specialization beats generalist sales reps at scale. ([[Roberge Sales Acceleration Formula]] + [[Ross Predictable Revenue]])

The split:

- **SDR (Sales Development Rep):** prospecting and qualification. Owns top-of-funnel. Compensated on qualified meetings booked.
- **AE (Account Executive):** closing. Owns the deal from qualified meeting to closed-won. Compensated on revenue closed.

Why the split:
- Different skill sets (researched outbound vs deal navigation).
- Different cost structures (SDR ~$60-100K total comp, AE $150-400K total comp).
- Different cadences (SDR works at high volume, AE at high concentration).

The pod ratio: 1 SDR per 4-5 AEs early; can shift to 1:2 or 1:3 in mature orgs with longer cycles.

The most common SDR/AE failure mode: tying SDR compensation to revenue (instead of qualified meetings). This corrupts the SDR's incentive to qualify rigorously; they accept low-quality leads to pump short-term metrics.

## The Unit Economics Discipline (Roberge)

> If unit economics don't work at scale, fix product or pricing, not sales motion. ([[Roberge Math of B2B Sales]])

The 4 metrics every B2B SaaS company tracks:

- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):** fully-loaded cost to acquire a paying customer. Includes sales salaries, marketing spend, sales tools, prorated overhead.
- **LTV (Lifetime Value):** expected revenue per customer × gross margin × expected retention life.
- **LTV/CAC ratio:** at scale, > 3:1 is healthy. < 2:1 means unprofitable customer economics.
- **CAC Payback Period:** months for a new customer to repay their CAC. < 12 months is healthy for SaaS; > 24 months suggests overcompensated sales or weak product.

Where founders fudge the numbers:
- **CAC excluding founder/exec time** (especially in early-stage when founders are doing sales).
- **LTV with no churn assumption** (assuming 100% NRR for 10 years is fantasy).
- **Marginal CAC instead of fully-loaded CAC** (counting only direct sales spend, ignoring infrastructure).

The honest unit economics conversation usually happens too late. By the time the board demands real numbers, the company has hired ahead of where the economics support.

## The Niche Discipline (Ross)

> Nail a niche before broadening. ([[Ross Predictable Revenue]])

Counterintuitive but consistently proven: narrowing the target customer profile increases revenue, doesn't decrease it. Three reasons:

1. **Referenceability compounds.** Customers in the same niche talk to each other; reference calls become acquisition channel.
2. **Sales-collateral leverage.** One case study works for 20 prospects when they're all similar.
3. **Product focus.** A focused product wins niches; a feature-bloated product wins none.

The expansion playbook:
- Dominate Niche 1 (50%+ market share in a specific vertical or segment).
- Use Niche 1 as case-study capital to enter adjacent Niche 2.
- Repeat until you have 3-4 niches; then it makes sense to broaden the GTM story.

Companies that try to broaden before dominating Niche 1 burn capital and produce no case studies anyone wants to read.

## When to Hire the First AE

The most common founder mistake: hiring the first salesperson too early.

The signals it's time:
1. **Founder-led sales is working but consuming 50%+ of CEO time.**
2. **Funnel is reasonably consistent week-over-week** (lead → demo → close cycles repeatable).
3. **You can articulate why customers buy** in one sentence (positioning is locked).
4. **The first sales hire can be onboarded with a written playbook**, not "shadow me for 6 months".

If any of these is no, don't hire yet. The first AE in a non-repeatable motion fails; you waste 6 months and a salary; the company concludes "we can't hire sales" when actually the motion wasn't ready.
