# Scaling Operations

Synthesis page on the operating disciplines required to go from 10 → 100 → 1000 people. Combines [[Andy Grove]] (managerial leverage + OKRs + 1:1 cadence) with [[Frank Slootman]] (amp it up, tempo, raise standards) and [[Patty McCord]] (freedom and responsibility, context not control).

Each stage transition has distinct failure modes. The discipline is recognizing which stage you're in and matching the operating posture.

---

## Grove's Foundation: The Manager's Output Equation

> The Manager's Output = Output of his organization + Output of neighboring organizations he influences. ([[Grove High Output Management]])

Reframes management away from individual contribution. The manager's output is leverage on others' work.

High-leverage activities (do these):
- Training subordinates (multiplies output of N people for years).
- Removing bottlenecks (unblocks parallel work).
- Building decision frameworks (others can decide without escalating).
- One-on-ones (subordinate-led, weekly, 90 minutes).

Low-leverage activities (minimize):
- Status reporting that doesn't change a decision.
- Meetings about meetings.
- Approval chains that delegate would solve.
- Politics.

## OKRs as Grove Designed Them

> Objectives are ambitious. Key Results are measurable. Score 0.0 to 1.0; 0.7 average is healthy. ([[Grove OKR Origin]])

The Grove rules (often violated by companies "doing OKRs"):

- **Quarterly cadence.** Annual OKRs are too slow; monthly is too granular.
- **3-5 Objectives, 3-5 Key Results each.** Too many = nothing prioritized. Too few = under-using the framework.
- **70% scoring as healthy stretch.** 100% means you sandbagged. 30% means you missed badly. The target is ambitious-but-achievable.
- **Public OKRs.** Transparency is the alignment mechanism. Private OKRs become bureaucratic exercises.
- **Bottom-up draft, top-down alignment.** Teams draft their OKRs; leadership aligns. Pure top-down OKRs are not OKRs, they are mandates.

## Slootman's Tempo Layer

Grove's discipline is necessary but not sufficient. Slootman's amp-it-up adds the velocity dimension.

> Tempo is a choice. Most companies operate at the tempo of the slowest function. The CEO's job is to amp the tempo to match the urgency. ([[Slootman Amp It Up]])

Four principles:

1. **Raise standards.** Every new hire better than the median of the function they join. Standards drift down by default; actively raise them.
2. **Align as a team.** Cross-functional alignment via shared metrics and shared timeline, not via process.
3. **Sharpen focus.** Refuse to expand into adjacent products until the core dominates. Most CEOs hedge; concentrate instead.
4. **Pick up the pace.** "We're in a hurry around here." CEO tone sets tempo. Slack tone produces slack work.

The Slootman discipline is uncomfortable. Most executives default to professional management cadence (slower, more process); Slootman insists on the urgency dimension.

## The Cadence Hierarchy

Three levels, each with its own meeting structure:

**Daily standup** (engineering teams, 15 min):
- What did I do yesterday?
- What am I doing today?
- What's blocking me?

**Weekly 1:1** (manager + report, 90 min):
- Subordinate brings the agenda.
- Manager listens > talks (Grove rule: manager prepares to listen).
- Topics: career, current work, organizational health, anything else.

**Quarterly business review** (exec + functional leads, 4 hours):
- OKR scoring for last quarter.
- OKR setting for next quarter.
- Strategic discussion of anything that requires executive alignment.

**Annual strategic offsite** (exec, 2-3 days):
- The "where are we going" conversation.
- Decisions that need stretch deliberation.
- Team-building element.

Companies that have all four levels working in rhythm scale; companies that skip levels (especially the 1:1) hit organizational dysfunction by 100-person scale.

## McCord's "Freedom and Responsibility"

Grove's discipline scales operations. Slootman's tempo scales urgency. McCord's culture frame scales decision-making.

> High context, low control. The exec layer communicates strategy and outcomes; the team chooses how to deliver. ([[McCord Powerful]])

Specific practices:

- **No vacation policy.** "Use good judgment in the interest of the company."
- **No expense policy.** Same.
- **Pay top-of-market.** One excellent engineer is worth 5-10 average ones; pay the excellent 3x.
- **The Keeper Test.** "Would I fight to keep this person if they tried to leave?" If no, transition with generous severance.
- **Continuous feedback.** Annual reviews are too slow; honest, frequent conversations replace them.
- **Hire fully formed adults.** Don't write policies for the worst case.

The model works because the precondition is rigorous hiring. Companies that adopt "freedom and responsibility" without first raising the hiring bar create chaos.

## Stage Transitions

Each stage has distinct dynamics:

| Stage | Size | Operating mode |
|---|---|---|
| Family | 1-9 | Founder-led, all-in-one-room |
| Tribe | 10-99 | First execs, weekly all-hands, OKRs introduced |
| Village | 100-999 | Functional org, layers of management, formal performance reviews |
| City | 1000-9999 | Multiple business units, executive layer, formal strategy process |
| Nation | 10000+ | Holding company, distributed decision-making |

(Sizes from Hoffman's *Blitzscaling* stages, used here for stage naming.)

Transition failure modes:

- **Family → Tribe:** founder doesn't let go of decisions; org bottlenecks at CEO.
- **Tribe → Village:** founder hires execs but micromanages them; or hires weak execs because they're easier to control.
- **Village → City:** functional silos form; lateral coordination fails; multi-product decisions get politicized.
- **City → Nation:** business units fail to invest in shared infrastructure; or shared infrastructure suffocates units.

The discipline at each transition: identify the operating bottleneck and address it specifically. Generic "we need more process" is the wrong answer; "we need to stop CEO-bottlenecking on hiring decisions" is the right kind of answer.
