# Early-Stage Discipline

The compressed discipline of the first year of a startup. Synthesizes [[Paul Graham]]'s four canonical essays into the operational frame for what to do (and not do) before PMF.

The PG essays were written between 2005-2013 but distill patterns from observing thousands of YC startups. The discipline is durable because the underlying mechanics (compounding growth, finite money, attention to early users) do not change.

---

## The Compressed Frame

Four claims, each from one PG essay, that together define the discipline:

1. **A startup is a company designed to grow fast.** ([[PG Startup = Growth]]) Not stage, not industry. If you are not growing at 5-7% weekly minimum, you are not really a startup yet.
2. **Ideas are noticed, not invented.** ([[PG How to Get Startup Ideas]]) The best ideas come from problems the founder personally lives. Brainstormed ideas are mostly tar pits.
3. **Build something people want, by removing the suck.** ([[PG How to Start a Startup]]) Not "find a breakthrough"; find something people already try to do badly, and fix it. People + product + frugality.
4. **Do things that don't scale.** ([[PG Do Things That Dont Scale]]) The early-stage work IS the unscalable work. Recruit users manually; serve them disproportionately; start narrow.

These four claims chain: notice a problem you live → build a product that fixes it → recruit users manually → measure growth ruthlessly.

## Detailed Discipline

### Idea selection (before commitment)

The verb is **notice**, not **think up**.

> "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself." ([[PG How to Get Startup Ideas]])

Where to look:
- Problems you have personally (the strongest signal)
- Problems you observe people you trust having
- The leading edge of a rapidly changing field ("live in the future")
- Schlep-blind opportunities: obvious ideas no one is doing well because the work is unappealing

What to avoid:
- Tar pit ideas: plausible-sounding concepts where you must argue for the problem
- Made-up ideas where friends say "interesting" but do not preorder
- "Brainstorm sessions" that produce ideas without an underlying lived problem

### Team composition

> "Ideally you want between two and four founders... Could you describe the person as an animal?" ([[PG How to Start a Startup]])

2-4 founders. For technical cofounders: the "animal" test. Obsessively excellent at their craft.

The cofounders matter more than the idea. Bad cofounders are the most common cause of startup death.

### First six months: do the unscalable work

> "If you're worried that the things you're doing are unscalable, then you're probably doing the right thing." ([[PG Do Things That Dont Scale]])

The pattern, with examples:
- **Manual user recruitment**: Airbnb founders door-to-door in NYC; Stripe Collison installation; Wufoo handwritten thank-yous
- **Start narrow**: Facebook at Harvard; the contained fire creates critical mass
- **Disproportionate care on early users**: they ARE the company at this stage
- **B2B consulting mode**: pick one customer, perfect for them, generalize

The math justifies the unscalable work: 100 users + 10% weekly growth = 14K in a year, 2M in two years. Manual recruitment of 10 users per week from a base of 100 hits the math.

### Measure growth, not vanity

> "A good growth rate during YC is 5-7% a week. If you can hit 10% a week you're doing exceptionally well. If you can only manage 1%, it's a sign you haven't yet figured out what you're doing." ([[PG Startup = Growth]])

The numerical compass. Growth as integrated signal of whether you are on track.

> "You can use that target growth rate to make all your decisions for you; anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right." ([[PG Startup = Growth]])

Growth as decision-making compass. Collapses the multifarious problem of running a startup into a single problem.

### Frugality

> "The slower you burn through your funding, the more time you have to learn." ([[PG How to Start a Startup]])

Money buys learning time, the most precious early resource. Frugality is not asceticism; it is preserving the ability to iterate before running out.

The founders should learn every job in the company before delegating. The cross-functional knowledge is durable advantage at scale.

## What the Discipline Rejects

- "Build for scale from day one" → ignore. The early-stage work is the unscalable work.
- "Find a brilliant breakthrough idea" → ignore. Notice a real problem.
- "Big launch with PR campaign" → ignore. Manual recruitment beats publicity.
- "Hire fast to look professional" → ignore. Founders should do every job first.
- "Move fast and break things" → only partially. Fast yes; but on the right problem with the right users.
- "Pivot at the first sign of trouble" → no. Iterate on the same problem before pivoting; if the retention curve flatlines, then consider pivot.

## What This Concept Teaches

- A startup is defined by growth rate, not stage or industry.
- Ideas are noticed (from lived problems), not invented (from brainstorming).
- Build something people want by removing the suck from things they already try to do.
- The early-stage work IS the unscalable work; do it manually, disproportionately.
- Frugality buys learning time; founders should learn every job.
- Growth as the compass: 5-7% weekly minimum; 10%+ exceptional.
- Reject the standard "build for scale" advice; the standard advice is for scaled companies, not pre-PMF startups.
