# Positioning as Strategy

Synthesis page on [[April Dunford]]'s framework for positioning as strategic discipline distinct from messaging. Most marketing failures are not bad copy or weak design; they are wrong positioning. The product is mismatched to the market context, and no amount of clever taglines fixes it.

Positioning is the deliberate context you set for the customer. Without intentional positioning, the customer chooses the context, usually wrong. The wrong context puts your product into a category where it loses on features or price; the right context highlights what your product uniquely does.

---

## The 5 Components

Dunford's framework. Every positioning statement explicitly answers all five.

### 1. Competitive Alternatives

**What does the customer do if your product doesn't exist?**

Not "who are your competitors". The customer doesn't think in industry categories; they think in "what do I do instead". The competitive alternatives might be:

- A competitor in your category.
- A different category entirely (a manual process, a spreadsheet, a different tool).
- "Do nothing" (the most common alternative; if customer doesn't see the problem, they pick this).

Naming the actual alternatives correctly is harder than it sounds. Most founders name "perceived" competitors (other startups in the same category) and miss the real alternative (the spreadsheet the customer is using, the manual process, the legacy tool the customer is keeping because it works).

### 2. Unique Attributes

**What features or capabilities does your product have that the alternatives don't?**

These must be:
- Specific and verifiable (not "ease of use" or "best-in-class").
- Differentiated against the actual alternatives (not generic product strengths).
- Real (the customer can verify within 5 minutes of using the product).

If your "unique" attributes are also available in the alternatives, they aren't unique. Re-examine.

### 3. Value Derived From Attributes

**Why does the customer care about the attributes?**

The translation from attribute to value. Customers don't buy attributes; they buy what the attributes enable for them.

- Attribute: "real-time sync across devices."
- Value: "marketing teams can collaborate without version-control errors that lose campaigns."

The value must be benefit the customer actually cares about. If the value is generic ("save time", "improve productivity") it isn't sharp enough.

### 4. Target Customers

**Who is the specific customer profile that values these attributes most?**

The customer profile that:
- Has the problem most acutely.
- Has the budget and authority to buy.
- Will derive disproportionate value from your specific attributes.

Most founders try to be everything for everyone. Strong positioning narrows to a specific buyer profile. The reflex test: can you describe the customer in one sentence? ("Mid-size DTC e-commerce brands ($10-100M revenue) with 5-25 person marketing teams running 10+ campaigns/month")? If not, you don't have target customers yet, you have a target market.

### 5. Market Category

**What context do you want the customer to compare you in?**

The category determines the customer's evaluation criteria. Choose the wrong category and you're competing on the wrong dimensions:

- If Salesforce positioned as "database company", customers would evaluate it on database performance and pick Oracle.
- Salesforce positioned as "cloud CRM" and customers evaluated on CRM workflows where Salesforce won.

Three styles of category positioning:
- **Existing category (head-to-head):** "We are the best [category] for [use case]." Win by features in known terms.
- **Subcategory:** "We are [category] for [specific use case]." Narrows the field of competitors.
- **New category creation:** "[Category name] is a new way to [outcome]." High-risk, high-reward; if the category catches on, you own it.

## Why "Obvious" Positioning Is Strong

Dunford's signature claim: great positioning is obvious, not creative. A reader should understand the value in seconds without parsing clever language.

The test: show your positioning statement to someone outside your industry. If they understand within 30 seconds what you do, who it's for, and why they'd buy it, the positioning is working. If they're confused, it isn't.

Most failed positioning is caused by:
- **Overuse of jargon** (the team uses internal terms; customers don't know them).
- **Vague differentiation** ("best-in-class", "industry-leading" tells the customer nothing).
- **Trying to be multiple things** (positioning A or B is stronger than positioning A and B and C).
- **Hiding the target customer** (founders fear "narrowing"; broad positioning is weak).

## Positioning ≠ Messaging

The most important Dunford distinction.

- **Positioning** is strategic. It's the deliberate choice of category, target, alternatives, attributes, value. Done once, revised when the business shifts.
- **Messaging** is tactical. It's how you express the positioning in specific contexts (homepage hero, sales deck slide 3, email campaign).

Most teams skip positioning and start with messaging. The result: messaging that contradicts itself across surfaces, because there's no underlying positioning to unify it.

Symptom of bad positioning: every department writes their own copy, and the homepage, sales deck, customer success email, and recruiting page each describe a different company.

---

## Negative Positioning ("Who Is This NOT For")

Dunford's most-cited tactical insight: negative positioning is more powerful than positive positioning.

Saying "we are for X" is weaker than saying "we are NOT for Y". The reason: positive positioning competes with everyone else's positive positioning (the customer sees five companies all "for marketing teams"); negative positioning differentiates immediately ("we are for in-house marketing teams, not for agencies").

The discipline:
- "We are not for solo founders" prevents wasting sales time.
- "We are not for enterprises over 5,000 employees" prevents overpromising on enterprise features.
- "We are not for healthcare regulated industries" prevents compliance scope creep.

Negative positioning feels uncomfortable. The fear is losing potential customers. The reality is the opposite: clear "not for" statements increase win rates with the actual target by sharpening the message.

## The 4 Positioning Styles (Dunford)

When repositioning, choose deliberately:

1. **Head-to-Head:** "We are the best [established category]." Wins by features and execution. Example: a new payroll software competing against Gusto + Rippling + ADP. Requires real feature advantages.
2. **Big Fish, Small Pond:** "We are the best [established category] for [specific use case]." Wins by niching down within an established category. Example: HR software specifically for restaurants.
3. **Reframe the Game:** "The category is shifting in this direction; we are best positioned for the new game." Wins by reframing the evaluation criteria. Example: Slack reframing from "team chat" to "platform for work".
4. **Pioneer a Category:** "[New category] is a new way to [outcome]; we are the original." Wins big or fails hard. Example: HubSpot creating "Inbound Marketing" as a category.

Match the style to your actual market position. Pioneering a category requires real innovation; reframing the game requires significant marketing investment; head-to-head requires real feature advantages.

---

## When to Reposition

Signs your positioning is broken:

- **Sales cycles are getting longer.** Customers don't quickly understand what you do.
- **Win rates are dropping.** Customers compare you on wrong dimensions.
- **Onboarding shows mismatch.** Customers use the product differently than the sales pitch suggests.
- **Marketing copy contradicts across surfaces.** No shared positioning to unify.
- **Internal disagreement on "what we do".** Team members describe the company differently.

If 2+ of these are true, repositioning is overdue. Dunford's process: 1-2 week workshop with founders + customers + sales + product + marketing, output is a positioning canvas with all 5 components filled in.
