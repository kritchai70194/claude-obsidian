# Long-Term Thinking

> "We believe that a fundamental measure of our success will be the shareholder value we create over the long term." ([[Bezos 1997 Letter]])

The founding compact of the Amazon operating philosophy and the master frame of the founder-operator pole in this brain. Long-term thinking is not patience; it is a willingness to wear short-term costs that the analytical case justifies on a multi-year window. The 1997 letter named nine decision criteria and signed them; every subsequent Bezos letter through 2020 appended that original as a reminder.

## Principles That Anchor the Pole

### 1. Long-horizon over local maxima

The canonical formulation. When the 7-year answer contradicts the comfortable quarterly answer, take the 7-year answer.

> "We will continue to make investment decisions in light of long-term market leadership considerations rather than short-term profitability considerations." ([[Bezos 1997 Letter]])

> "When forced to choose between optimizing the appearance of our GAAP accounting and maximizing the present value of future cash flows, we'll take the cash flows." ([[Bezos 1997 Letter]])

The honest tradeoff: Bezos does not pretend long-term and short-term align. A real principle costs you something when followed.

### 2. The voting machine vs the weighing machine

Written in the "Ouch" letter after the 2000 dot-com crash dropped Amazon's stock 80%. The clearest reframe of market-cap noise vs. business reality in the corpus.

> "As the famed investor Benjamin Graham said, 'In the short term, the stock market is a voting machine; in the long term, it's a weighing machine.' Clearly there was a lot of voting going on in the boom year of '99, and much less weighing. We're a company that wants to be weighed, and over time, we will be." ([[Bezos 2000 Letter]])

Operating metrics first; market cap last. Build a heavier company.

### 3. Wandering as the counterbalance to efficiency

Pure-efficiency operating models (OKR-driven, lean) cut the slack that produces non-obvious bets. Long-term thinking requires defending the wandering against the gravitational pull of this-quarter optimization.

> "Wandering in business is not efficient ... but it's also not random. It's guided, by hunch, gut, intuition, curiosity, and powered by a deep conviction that the prize for customers is big enough that it's worth being a little messy and tangential to find our way there. Wandering is an essential counter-balance to efficiency. You need to employ both." ([[Bezos 2018 Letter]])

### 4. Failure scale is the invention scale

If the size of your failures is shrinking, you are not inventing at a size that moves the needle. Long-term thinking funds the failures that make the wins possible.

> "If the size of your failures isn't growing, you're not going to be inventing at a size that can actually move the needle. Amazon will be experimenting at the right scale for a company of our size if we occasionally have multibillion-dollar failures." ([[Bezos 2018 Letter]])

The Fire phone is the named loss. Echo is the named win. Same engineering org, same year of inception.

### 5. Durable profitability funds R&D reinvestment

The Collison reformulation of the same compact. Profit is the mechanism, not the goal.

> "Stripe was profitable in 2024, and we expect to be so in 2025 and beyond. Durable profitability allows us to plow back much of our operating earnings into research and development. In each of the last six years, Stripe has reinvested a much higher proportion of our earnings in R&D than any comparable company." ([[Stripe Annual Letter 2024]])

Same shape as the 1997 cash-flows compact: profit exists to make the long-horizon investment possible without dilution or external pressure.

### 6. Compounding takes centuries

The historical-comparison move. Patrick Collison's list of projects completed in surprisingly short timeframes (Pentagon 491 days, Empire State Building 410 days, Amazon Prime 6 weeks) is the inverse compass: long timelines can be a symptom of slow decision-making, not a requirement.

> The Stripe 2024 letter inventories customers spanning nine centuries: Oxford (1096), Church of England (1534), Hershey (1894), NVIDIA (1993), Perplexity (2022). ([[Stripe Annual Letter 2024]])

> "Of the 500 companies in the S&P 500 at its inception in 1957, only 53 remain in the index today. ... Back in 1957, companies could expect to remain in the index for 61 years. ... Today, it's just under 20 years. Enduring businesses are increasingly rare." ([[Stripe Annual Letter 2024]])

The argument by enumeration ([[Collison Fast]]) is the rhetorical signature: don't argue, show.

## How This Brain Uses It

When facing any decision where short and long horizons diverge:

1. **What is the 5-to-7-year version of this decision?** If it contradicts the quarterly version, the long version wins.
2. **Are we voting or weighing?** Voting is noise; weighing is the underlying business. Optimize for the operating metric that adds weight.
3. **What wandering are we protecting?** If efficiency has eaten all the slack, no non-obvious bet can survive. Name what is being protected and why.
4. **Is failure scaling proportionally?** If every bet is small enough to safely lose, no bet is large enough to move the needle.

## Tensions With Other Frames

- **vs [[Customer Obsession]]:** Long-term thinking funds customer moves that hurt short-term numbers (price cuts, free shipping, customer-friendly defaults). Without the long-horizon frame, customer obsession collapses under quarterly pressure. The two principles are mutually reinforcing.
- **vs growth-rate discipline (PG, Lenny):** Startup-stage thinking emphasizes weekly compounding (5-7% per week per [[Bezos 1997 Letter|PG's Startup = Growth]]). Long-term thinking emphasizes decade-scale durability. The synthesis: weekly growth is the input; decade durability is the output. Stage of the company decides emphasis.
- **vs [[Two-Pizza Team|operational discipline]]:** Long-term thinking can rationalize indefinite delay ("we are playing the long game"). The operational counterweight is shipping at 70% information ([[Bezos 2016 Day 1 Letter]]) and protecting wandering inside a culture of velocity, not as an alternative to it.
