# Dunford Positioning Not Messaging

The thesis essay distinguishing positioning from messaging, distilled from April Dunford's Ambient Strategy blog and the standard opening section of her in-person workshops. The argument predates [[Dunford Obviously Awesome]] but is the cleanest standalone statement of the distinction that runs through both books.

## Core Argument

> Positioning is a strategic decision. Messaging is a tactical expression of that decision. Most marketing teams optimize messaging without revisiting positioning, which is why their messaging keeps not working.

Positioning sets the frame: what category the product competes in, what alternatives it is best against, what unique attributes drive the advantage, what value those attributes deliver, which customers care most. Messaging is the copy that expresses that frame, in headlines, decks, landing pages, ad creative, and sales scripts.

The error: treating positioning and messaging as the same activity, both owned by marketing, both refined by iteration on copy. The result: teams rewrite messaging endlessly while the underlying frame stays muddled, and the messaging keeps failing for reasons the team cannot diagnose.

## The Distinction in Practice

| | Positioning | Messaging |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | Strategic frame | Tactical expression |
| Output | Written internal document | External copy |
| Owned by | Founder + product marketing + sales leadership | Marketing |
| Cadence | Reviewed every 18-24 months | Iterated weekly |
| Test | "Who is this NOT for?" | A/B test |
| Wrong when | The frame is muddled | The copy is unclear |

The diagnostic move: when messaging is not working, the first question is not "is the copy clear?" but "is the positioning clear?" If the positioning is muddled, no rewrite of the copy will fix it.

## Why Marketing Teams Skip Positioning

Dunford's recurring observation across consulting engagements. The skip happens for structural reasons, not because marketing teams are lazy:

1. **Positioning requires founder time.** It is a strategic decision the founder must own and ratify. Founders are busy. Marketing teams cannot draft positioning in isolation.
2. **Positioning requires saying no.** Narrowing the target customer, naming the category, choosing the competitive alternatives, all force trade-offs that feel like leaving money on the table.
3. **Positioning has no clear deliverable for marketing.** A new website is a deliverable. A new internal positioning document is not visible to anyone outside the team that wrote it.
4. **Messaging is measurable, positioning is not.** A/B testing copy produces numbers. Positioning produces a document. Marketing teams optimize for what is measurable.
5. **Positioning work looks like rework.** When the team has been rewriting copy for two years, going back to the frame feels like admitting the previous two years were wasted.

The structural result: positioning is the most-skipped activity in B2B marketing, and the absence of it is the most common root cause of "our marketing is not working".

## The Diagnostic Question

> If you cannot answer "who is this NOT for?" in a single sentence, you do not have positioning yet.

The diagnostic is the negative-positioning test. A clear positioning excludes most of the market. If the team cannot name the excluded segments, the positioning has not yet been done.

The follow-up diagnostic: if every salesperson on the team describes the product differently in their first sentence, the team is improvising positioning per call. The positioning has not been written down. Each rep is doing their own.

## On the Order of Operations

The recurring sequence Dunford insists on:

1. Positioning first. Written. Internal. Ratified by founder, product marketing, and sales leadership.
2. Sales narrative second. Derived from positioning. The 7-part pitch from [[Dunford Sales Pitch Book]] is built on top of the positioning.
3. Messaging third. Headlines, landing pages, ad copy, all derived from the positioning and the narrative.
4. Brand and design fourth. Visual expression of all of the above.

Companies that reverse the order, starting with brand or messaging, produce surface polish on top of muddled strategy. The polish does not survive the first hard sales call.

## On the "Tagline" Trap

The most common founder request to a positioning consultant: "help me write a tagline." Dunford's standard response: a tagline is the last thing to write, not the first. A tagline produced before positioning is decoration. A tagline produced after positioning is a compression of strategic work.

> If you can hand me your positioning document, I can write you a tagline in an afternoon. If you cannot hand me your positioning document, no tagline I write will help you.

## Connection to Other Brain Frames

- The positioning-vs-messaging distinction is the strategy-vs-tactics distinction applied to GTM. The strategic decision sets the constraints inside which tactical optimization happens. Optimizing tactics with broken strategy is the most common operator failure mode across poles ([[Helmer 7 Powers Book]] makes the parallel argument for strategy vs operational excellence).
- The "who is this NOT for?" diagnostic maps to [[Clayton Christensen]] JTBD: the unmet job is the inverse of the segments your product does not serve. Both frames force the team to make exclusions explicit.
- The order of operations (positioning → narrative → messaging → brand) is the GTM-side counterpart to [[Andy Raskin]]'s narrative arc, which provides the story scaffolding once the positioning is set.
