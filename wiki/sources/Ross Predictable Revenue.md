# Ross Predictable Revenue

The canonical text on outbound sales as a repeatable, scalable engine. Written by Aaron Ross with Marylou Tyler, published by PebbleStorm Media in 2011. Subtitled *Turn Your Business Into a Sales Machine with the $100 Million Best Practices of Salesforce.com*. The book that named Cold Calling 2.0, codified the SDR/AE split, and gave the gtm-sales pole its operating vocabulary.

## The Salesforce Origin Story

In 2002 Ross joined Salesforce as Director of Corporate Sales. The company was inbound-driven, hitting a ceiling, and could not figure out how to manufacture pipeline on demand. Ross was given a small prospecting team and told to build something new.

The conventional wisdom at the time was that outbound meant cold calling lists of names in alphabetical order. Reps dialed for dollars; managers tracked dials per day. Ross threw it out.

The new model: separate the prospecting role from the closing role. Have the prospectors target by research, not by list. Have them email decision-maker referrals, not pitches. Measure opportunities passed to AEs, not dials made. Within three years the team drove an additional $100M ARR. The model is now standard across SaaS.

## The SDR / AE Split

> The biggest mistake in sales hiring is asking one person to prospect and close. They are different jobs requiring different humans.

Ross's central structural argument. Prospecting is a high-volume, rejection-heavy, researched-outbound discipline. Closing is a relationship-driven, deal-shaping, multi-stakeholder discipline. The skills do not transfer cleanly and the activities cannibalize each other in the same calendar.

The fix:
- **SDR (Sales Development Rep)**: prospects only. Owns the top of funnel. Compensated on qualified opportunities passed to AEs, not closed revenue. Reports to sales leadership.
- **AE (Account Executive)**: closes only. Owns deals from qualified opportunity to signature. Compensated on closed revenue. Carries quota.

Early-stage ratio: 1 SDR per 4-5 AEs. The SDR feeds the AEs; the AEs close what the SDR passes. The split makes pipeline a tracked input rather than an emergent property of AE motivation.

## Cold Calling 2.0

The named methodology. (See [[Ross Cold Calling 2.0]] for the full mechanics.) The summary:

1. Build a researched list of target accounts that fit the ICP.
2. Send a short cold email to a senior contact at each account, asking for a referral to the right person (not pitching).
3. The senior contact replies with a name, ignores, or refers internally.
4. SDR follows up with the referred contact via a structured email cadence.
5. Qualified opportunities pass to an AE.

Reply rate is the leading indicator. Qualified meetings per reply is the conversion test. The numbers are tracked weekly per SDR.

## Seeds, Nets, Spears

The three sales engines:

**Seeds.** Existing-customer growth, referrals, expansion. Highest conversion rate of any source. Slowest to build because it requires actual customer success. Becomes the dominant revenue engine in mature SaaS.

**Nets.** Inbound marketing. Content, SEO, ads, webinars, organic. Casts a wide net; some of the catch is qualified. Medium conversion, requires a content engine, scales with marketing spend and time.

**Spears.** Outbound SDR. Targeted, researched, controllable. Lowest conversion per touch but the most predictable to scale; you decide how many SDRs to hire and roughly how much pipeline you get.

> Most companies need all three. The mix depends on stage and ACV. Below $25K ACV inbound dominates. Above $50K ACV outbound becomes structural.

## Sales is a Math Problem

Four numbers run the engine:

- **Pipeline coverage**: 3-4x quota at start of quarter, or the quarter is already lost.
- **Conversion rate by stage**: prospecting → qualified opp → proposal → close. Track each step.
- **Cycle time**: average days from first touch to close, by ACV band.
- **Average contract value**: rising or falling. ACV trends determine hiring and territory design.

If you do not track these four, you are not running a sales engine; you are hoping.

## The Niche

> The fastest way to grow revenue is to dominate a small niche before expanding. Most founders do the opposite and stay broad to keep optionality.

Niching down increases revenue because:
1. Referenceability compounds inside a tight ICP. Customer N+1 already knows customers 1 through N.
2. Marketing copy gets sharper because it is written for one persona, not three.
3. Sales conversations get shorter because the rep already understands the prospect's stack and language.
4. Product roadmap gets clearer because the same feature requests recur.

The counterargument ("but the market is too small") is almost always wrong at early stage. The cost of broad targeting is not lost upside; it is missed PMF.
