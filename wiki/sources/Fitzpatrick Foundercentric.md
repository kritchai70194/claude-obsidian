# Fitzpatrick Foundercentric

Applied examples from Fitzpatrick's consulting practice at Foundercentric. The Mom Test book gives the philosophy and the question patterns; Foundercentric is where those patterns get pressure-tested against real enterprise problems, at real scale, across hundreds of product teams. The blog and consulting writeups document what holds up and what bends.

## What Foundercentric Does

Foundercentric trains product teams to run customer-discovery interviews at the level of discipline the Mom Test book implies. The typical engagement: a product team that has "done customer interviews" but cannot point to a single behavioral commitment from those interviews. Foundercentric retrains the team on question pattern, observation discipline, and synthesis cadence, then shadows them through a learning loop.

The implicit thesis of the consulting practice: the book is widely read but rarely applied. The gap between knowing the Mom Test and running interviews that pass it is larger than founders expect. Practice closes the gap; reading does not.

## Converting Interview Data Into Product Decisions

The Foundercentric writeups document the synthesis layer that the book leaves implicit. After 5 to 10 interviews, the team gathers and asks:

1. What patterns recur across multiple interviews, not just one?
2. What surprised us? (Surprise is signal that our prior model was wrong.)
3. What did we hear about behavior that contradicts what we hear about opinion?
4. What commitments did we secure? (Time, reputation, money.) If zero, the cohort produced no signal.
5. What is the next hypothesis to test, and what question pattern tests it?

Decisions emerge from patterns, not from individual quotes. A single customer saying "I'd pay for this" is noise. Five customers describing the same workaround in the same language is signal.

## Customer Interviews at Scale

Several Foundercentric writeups document engagements where enterprise teams ran 1000+ interviews across a multi-quarter discovery program. The lessons from scaling:

- The question pattern degrades fast under volume pressure. Untrained interviewers default back to hypothetical questions within a few weeks if not monitored.
- Synthesis lag is the bottleneck. Teams can run interviews faster than they can synthesize; insight rate is gated by the synthesis cadence, not the interview rate.
- Cross-interviewer calibration is required. Two interviewers asking the same question pattern get different answers from the same customer because of subtle cue differences.
- Recording and re-listening beats note-taking for synthesis. The customer's actual words matter; the interviewer's paraphrase loses signal.

> At 1000 interviews, the difference between trained and untrained interviewers is not 10% better data. It's the difference between data and noise.

## Training Product Teams to Do It Themselves

The Foundercentric model is teach-to-fish: train the team, then leave. Several writeups document the curriculum:

- Week 1: question pattern audit. Every question the team currently asks gets categorized as bad / good / borderline and rewritten.
- Week 2: shadowed interviews. Foundercentric observes; the team interviews; debrief immediately after each one on what the interviewer missed.
- Week 3: solo interviews with recording review. Team runs interviews alone; Foundercentric reviews recordings for drift.
- Week 4: synthesis cadence. Team runs the 5-to-10-interview synthesis cycle independently.

The output is not a research report. The output is a team that can do customer discovery as a permanent capability, without external consultants.

## Recurring Failure Modes Across Engagements

The Foundercentric writeups catalog the failure modes that show up repeatedly across companies, suggesting they are structural, not idiosyncratic.

- **Interviews disguised as sales calls.** The founder cannot resist explaining the product. The conversation becomes a pitch; the data dies.
- **Confirming, not learning.** The team only books interviews with users who already love the product. The interviews confirm the prior; nothing is learned.
- **Skipping synthesis.** Teams run interviews continuously without pausing to update hypotheses. The 50th interview is no more informative than the 5th because no synthesis has happened in between.
- **No commitments asked.** The interview ends with "thanks for your time" and no behavioral ask. No commitment, no signal.
- **Treating opinion as data.** Quotes from interviews used to justify product decisions, when the quotes were opinions about a hypothetical future, not descriptions of past behavior.
