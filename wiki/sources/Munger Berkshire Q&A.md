# Munger Berkshire Q&A

Compiled Q&A excerpts from Munger's appearances at the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting (Omaha, 1996-2023), the Wesco Financial annual meeting (Pasadena, 1986-2011), and the Daily Journal annual meeting (Los Angeles, 2012-2023). Transcripts exist across several sources (Tilson Funds notes, Whitney Tilson's compilations, journalist write-ups in *Outstanding Investor Digest*, video recordings of the later Daily Journal sessions on YouTube).

This is the **applied layer** of everything in the books. The published works state the principles. The Q&A sessions show how Munger reasoned through specific questions, often delivered with the bluntness that became his signature.

The fidelity is structural-summary: themes and representative quotes are reproducible, but no single canonical transcript exists.

## Recurring Themes

### Incentives Drive Behavior, Always

The single most-repeated theme across the meetings. Asked about almost any organizational failure (Wells Fargo cross-selling, mortgage-broker fraud in the 2007 crisis, public-company quarterly-earnings games), Munger reduces it to the incentive structure.

> Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome.

His specific application: if you want to predict behavior in a system, look at what is being rewarded and what is being punished, not at what people say they value. The mortgage-broker example: a broker paid per loan closed, with no consequence for default, will produce defaults. The system, not the people, is the cause.

### Circle of Competence

The disciplinary anchor of the Berkshire investment process.

> Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant.

The size of the circle matters less than knowing the perimeter. Most investment disasters happen one inch outside the circle. The fix is not to expand the circle aggressively; it is to stay disciplined about the edge and pass on the things outside it.

Asked repeatedly why Berkshire passed on technology investments for decades:

> If we couldn't figure out which technology companies were going to win, we didn't have to. There are easier games to play.

### The Value of Saying "I Don't Know"

Munger consistently identified this as a competitive advantage. Most active investors fail because they pretend to know. Most CEOs fail because they answer the question they should have refused.

> If you don't know, and you have power, you're in a very dangerous position.

The operator application: optimize for the willingness to say "I don't know" in the rooms where pretending to know is socially expected. Most strategic mistakes are made because someone could not bear the social cost of the honest answer.

### Why Most Active Investors Lose

The asymmetry of avoiding stupidity vs seeking brilliance, applied to the investment management industry.

> It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent.

The active-management industry is structurally configured to reward the brilliant move (which is rare and rewarded with fees) over the avoided stupid move (which is invisible). Berkshire ran on the inverse posture: catastrophic losses end the compounding; the central discipline is avoiding them, not making the brilliant call.

### The Right Cost of Capital

Asked many times what Berkshire's cost of capital is, Munger's answer changed the question:

> The cost of capital is the opportunity cost, what else could you do with the money?

The opportunity-cost frame as the correct version of the cost-of-capital question. The textbook CAPM answer is the wrong question. The right question is: of the things I could put the next dollar into, what is the best risk-adjusted return I can actually access?

### How Investors Fool Themselves

Munger consistently named the self-deceptions:

- **Overoptimism**: every forecast is too rosy.
- **Anchoring**: the price you paid is irrelevant; the only question is what it is worth now.
- **Inconsistency-avoidance**: defending a bad investment because you bought it, rather than re-evaluating on current information.
- **Social proof**: the diligence shortcut "everyone else is investing" masquerading as analysis.

Each is one of the 25 causes from [[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]], applied live.

### The Fishing Hat Principle

> The first rule of fishing is fish where the fish are. The second rule of fishing is don't forget rule number one.

The applied-to-investing version: most of the work of investing is selecting the right opportunity set. Once you are in the right pond, picking the specific fish is much easier. The error is fishing in the wrong pond and trying to compensate with brilliant casting.

## Munger on Munger

Asked many times for his own operating philosophy, Munger reduced it to a few moves:

> I think a couple of times a year, I sit down and think hard about what is changing.

> I try to invert every problem I face. I try to see what the failure mode looks like and work backwards.

> Reading is the cheapest possible tuition. I read for hours every day and I have for sixty years.

The combination of inversion plus reading plus the latticework is the entire operating system in three moves.
