# Slootman Tape Sucks

Slootman's first book, published in 2011, on the Data Domain story. Out of print for periods and less widely read than *Amp It Up*, but the operating posture in *Amp It Up* is already fully present in *Tape Sucks*, eleven years earlier. The book is the proof that the playbook is not a Snowflake-era invention. It is the same posture Slootman ran from 2003 onward.

## The Data Domain Story

Data Domain (founded 2001, Slootman joined as CEO in 2003) sold deduplication storage appliances. The product compressed backup data by an order of magnitude, displacing tape backup as the dominant enterprise backup medium. The book's title is a thesis statement: tape is a worse technology, customers know it, and the only reason the displacement was not faster is incumbent inertia.

The company went from roughly $1M in revenue in 2003 to over $500M by the time of the EMC acquisition in 2009 for $2.4B. The arc included an IPO in 2007 and a competitive bidding war between NetApp and EMC that materially raised the final price.

## Operating Lessons (Source for the Brain)

### Dissatisfaction with the Status Quo as Engine

Slootman is explicit that the engine of Data Domain was customer dissatisfaction with tape, not Data Domain's technical cleverness. The thesis: a good company is built on a problem the customer is already angry about. Marketing a slightly-better solution to a customer who is content with the existing solution is the harder route. Find the angry customer; serve them well; the rest follows.

This is the early version of what *Amp It Up* later frames as "sharpen focus." Both books say: pick the bet where the demand is already there, do not try to manufacture demand for a marginal improvement.

### The Kitchen Cabinet

Slootman describes the inner circle of trusted executives, the "kitchen cabinet," whose job was to be honest with him about what was actually working and what was theater. The thesis: every CEO ends up surrounded by people who want to tell them what they want to hear. The defense is a small group of executives whose loyalty is to the truth, not to the CEO's comfort.

The kitchen cabinet at Data Domain shaped the major product, pricing, and sales decisions through frank conversation that the formal exec staff meetings could not produce. This concept reappears in *Amp It Up* as the executive team's job to disagree productively.

### Customer Feedback Loops as Operating Discipline

Data Domain's product roadmap was driven by direct CEO contact with customers, especially backup administrators and IT directors who lived with tape day to day. Slootman's discipline: take the customer call yourself. Do not delegate the customer relationship to a CSM until the product is mature.

The book describes Slootman personally on customer calls deep into the company's growth phase, including with Fortune 100 buyers. This is the early demonstration of the *Amp It Up* tempo principle: the CEO's calendar tells the org what matters. If the CEO is on customer calls, customers matter. If the CEO is in process meetings, process matters.

### The EMC Acquisition as Outcome of Execution

The book treats the EMC outcome ($2.4B, 2009) not as a destination but as a side effect of consistent execution. The company was not built to be sold. The acquisition came because the company was visibly winning, both NetApp and EMC needed the deduplication capability, and the bidding war drove the price.

The lesson Slootman extracts: build the company to win the market, and exit options follow. Build the company for the exit, and you optimize for the wrong things.

### The Tempo Lesson, Already

Even in 2011, the tempo language is present. Data Domain ran weekly forecasting reviews, weekly sales reviews, weekly engineering reviews, all in a tight cadence. The book describes Slootman's frustration with "natural" enterprise software tempo, the quarterly review, the annual planning cycle, the three-week deal close. He pushed everything tighter.

## Why This Source Matters

Two reasons.

First, it shows the operating posture is **portable across companies**. Same CEO, different decade, different market (storage hardware vs SaaS), different team, same posture, same outcome shape. That repeatability is what distinguishes Slootman as a mentor from CEOs whose one win could be circumstantial.

Second, it shows the posture is **older than the book that named it**. *Amp It Up* (2022) made the posture famous. *Tape Sucks* (2011) shows the posture was already mature a decade earlier, when Slootman was running Data Domain. This is operator wisdom, not author-circuit content.
