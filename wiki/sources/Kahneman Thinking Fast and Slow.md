# Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman's 2011 popular synthesis of four decades of cognitive psychology, much of it conducted with Amos Tversky. The canonical text on dual-process cognition, the heuristics-and-biases research program, prospect theory, and the experiencing-vs-remembering self distinction. Roughly 500 pages, structured in five parts.

## Structure

1. **Two Systems.** The dual-process model: System 1 (fast, automatic, intuitive) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, logical). The cognitive ease and effort distinction. How System 1 hands off to System 2 and when it fails to.
2. **Heuristics and Biases.** Anchoring, availability, representativeness, the base-rate fallacy, the conjunction fallacy (Linda problem), regression to the mean.
3. **Overconfidence.** WYSIATI, the illusion of validity, the planning fallacy, the illusion of skill in stock picking and pundit prediction.
4. **Choices.** Prospect theory in popular form. The reference point, loss aversion, the endowment effect, framing effects, the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes.
5. **Two Selves.** The experiencing self vs the remembering self. The peak-end rule. The cold-water and colonoscopy experiments. Implications for life evaluation.

## Core Definition

System 1 is the default. It runs continuously, generates intuitions, judgments, and impressions without conscious effort. System 2 is the lazy controller. It can override System 1 but rarely does, because override is effortful and System 1's answers usually look good enough.

> System 1 operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control. System 2 allocates attention to the effortful mental activities that demand it, including complex computations. The operations of System 2 are often associated with the subjective experience of agency, choice, and concentration.

## Key Passages

### On the relationship between the systems

> When all goes smoothly, which is most of the time, System 2 adopts the suggestions of System 1 with little or no modification.

The implication: most "deliberation" is post-hoc rationalization of an intuitive answer. The judgment was made fast; the explanation was constructed slow.

### On the Linda problem

The classic conjunction fallacy demonstration. Subjects are told Linda is single, outspoken, bright, philosophy major, concerned with social justice. They are then asked which is more probable:

> (a) Linda is a bank teller.
> (b) Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement.

Roughly 85 percent of subjects (including statistically trained ones) pick (b), violating the basic rule that a conjunction cannot be more probable than either of its conjuncts. Representativeness (the description fits the feminist-bank-teller stereotype) overrides logic.

### On priming and anchoring

The wheel-of-fortune-and-UN-membership experiment. Subjects spin a wheel that lands on either 10 or 65 (rigged), then estimate the percentage of African countries in the UN. Those who saw 10 estimated 25 percent. Those who saw 65 estimated 45 percent. The obviously irrelevant number anchored the estimate.

> Any number that you are asked to consider as a possible solution to an estimation problem will induce an anchoring effect.

### On loss aversion

> Losses loom larger than gains.

The asymmetry coefficient lands around 2.0 to 2.5 in most experiments. Lose $100 to feel as bad as gaining $200 feels good. This is the structural reason that status quo bias, endowment effects, and certain framing effects work.

### On WYSIATI

> The measure of success for System 1 is the coherence of the story it manages to create. The amount and quality of the data on which the story is based are largely irrelevant.

Coherence is cheap to fake. Confidence tracks story quality, not evidence quality. This is the engine behind overconfidence in experts, in pundits, in founders, in interviewers.

### On the two selves

The colonoscopy study: patients rated procedures by their peak pain and end pain, not their total duration. A longer procedure with a gentler ending was remembered as less unpleasant than a shorter procedure with a sharp ending.

> Odd as it may seem, I am my remembering self, and the experiencing self, who does my living, is like a stranger to me.

Implication: life evaluations track memory, not lived moments. Vacations, jobs, relationships are graded by their peaks and endings.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **System 1 runs almost all everyday cognition; System 2 is the expensive override.** The job is to design the moments where override has to fire.
- **Losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good.** Loss aversion shapes pricing, negotiation, hiring, exit decisions.
- **Anchoring biases every subsequent estimate, including against obviously irrelevant numbers.** The first number on the table is load-bearing.
- **WYSIATI: confidence tracks story coherence, not evidence quality.** Absence of information is invisible to System 1.
- **The remembering self overweights peaks and endings.** The lived experience is not how the experience is graded later.
