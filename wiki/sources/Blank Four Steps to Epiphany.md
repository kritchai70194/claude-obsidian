# Blank Four Steps to Epiphany

The foundational text of the lean-startup movement, self-published by Blank in 2005 through his own K&S Ranch imprint. Physical copies are rare; a PDF circulates widely and is hosted on Stanford's E145 course materials site. This book predates Eric Ries's *The Lean Startup* by six years and contains the underlying methodology that Ries later simplified for a mass audience.

## What the Book Argues

A startup is not a small company. A small company executes a known business model. A startup is searching for a business model that does not yet exist in repeatable form. Applying the management tooling of execution (formal plans, waterfall product development, traditional sales hiring, detailed financial projections) to a search-phase organization is the dominant cause of startup failure.

The corrective is **Customer Development**, a parallel process to Product Development conducted explicitly to test customer assumptions. The book is structured around the four stages of this process.

## The Four Stages

### 1. Customer Discovery

Find a problem worth solving and a customer who has it. The output of this stage is not a sale. The output is a validated hypothesis grid: who the customer is, what problem they have, why current alternatives fail, and what would have to be true for the product to be worth buying.

> The goal of Customer Discovery is to turn the founders' initial hypotheses about their market and customers into facts.

Exit criterion: at least some customers will commit to buying the product as currently envisioned. If no one will commit, you have not exited Discovery.

### 2. Customer Validation

Find a repeatable, scalable sales process. The same pitch, to the same customer profile, produces the same outcome. The output is a sales roadmap that a new salesperson could execute.

Exit criterion: a small set of customers has bought without founder heroics, on a repeatable basis. If every sale requires the founder, you have not exited Validation. This is where most startups discover they have a sales model that does not scale.

### 3. Customer Creation

Drive end-user demand into the sales channel built in Validation. This is the stage that looks like traditional marketing and sales scaling, but it is done against a now-known model, not a hypothetical one.

### 4. Company Building

Transition from a search-phase organization (small, flat, hypothesis-driven, founder-led) to an execute-phase company (departmentalized, process-driven, professionally managed). This is where the management model itself has to change.

## The Parallel Process

Product Development on the engineering track and Customer Development on the customer track run in parallel from day zero. The two tracks meet at gates. The traditional model (build the product, then sell it) is rejected because it tests the product hypothesis without testing the customer hypothesis, and the customer hypothesis is the one most likely to be wrong.

## Hypotheses, Not Features

Every startup begins with a set of unverified beliefs. Blank's contribution is to name them as hypotheses and to insist that they be tested rather than assumed:

- **Customer hypothesis:** who exactly will buy this
- **Problem hypothesis:** what problem they have that this solves
- **Solution hypothesis:** that this specific product solves that problem
- **Channel hypothesis:** how the product reaches the customer
- **Market hypothesis:** the size and shape of the addressable market

Customer first because the wrong customer makes every downstream hypothesis irrelevant. Solution late because solution is cheapest to change.

## Search vs Execute

The book's most enduring conceptual contribution. Search-phase organizations and execute-phase organizations have different management models, different metrics, different hiring profiles, different planning horizons. Mode confusion (running a startup like a small company, or running a scaling company like a startup) kills both.

> No business plan survives first contact with customers.

The implication: the business plan is a list of hypotheses to test, not commitments to execute.

## Death by Feature

A recurring failure pattern Blank names directly. Engineering-led startups, in the absence of customer signal, default to adding features. Each feature adds complexity. None resolves the underlying problem, which is that the customer hypothesis was never tested. The product becomes a Christmas tree of capabilities that no one specifically wants.

The corrective is to get out of the building before adding the feature.
