# Christensen Innovators Dilemma

Clayton Christensen's 1997 book introducing Disruption Theory. The canonical text. Refined a doctoral thesis on the disk drive industry into a general theory of why successful incumbents lose to upstarts. The 2011 revised edition adds a foreword reflecting on what the framework got right and what was misread by readers who used "disruption" loosely. Central to the strategy canon Patrick Collison endorses (see [[Collison Cowen Interview]]).

## The Foundational Case Study: Disk Drives

Christensen tracked every disk drive company from 1976 to 1992 across five generational transitions (14-inch to 8-inch to 5.25-inch to 3.5-inch to 2.5-inch). The pattern held in every transition: established leaders lost. Not because they failed to develop the new technology (most of them developed prototypes early). They lost because their best customers did not want the new product, and the resource allocation process inside the firm could not justify funding a product their best customers rejected for a market that did not yet exist.

The disk drive industry mattered for the theory because the rapid generational cycle let Christensen observe the same pattern five times in fifteen years. Slower industries hide the mechanism behind decades of confounding variables.

## The Central Argument

> The very decision-making and resource-allocation processes that are key to the success of established companies are the very processes that reject disruptive technologies.

Good management does not protect against disruption. Good management causes the failure. The practices that make a company successful (listen to customers, invest where margins are highest, pursue larger markets over smaller ones) are exactly the practices that prevent it from pursuing disruptive innovations, which start in small markets, at low margins, against customers who do not yet exist.

## Sustaining vs Disruptive (the binary)

**Sustaining innovation:** improves performance along trajectories the mainstream customer already values. Incumbents excel at sustaining innovation. Most innovation in mature companies is sustaining.

**Disruptive innovation:** worse performance on the dominant metric, better performance on a different metric the mainstream does not yet value. Initially serves new markets or low-end segments the incumbent rationally ignores. Over time the disruption improves on the dominant metric and takes the mainstream.

Christensen on the structural definition:

> Disruptive technology is usually built around proven technologies put together into a novel product architecture that offers the customer a new set of attributes never before available.

The binary matters because the strategic response is different. Sustaining innovations should be matched head-on; incumbents usually win those fights. Disruptive innovations require a separate organization with different metrics; incumbents almost never win those fights from inside the core organization.

## The Principles of Disruption

Christensen identifies four structural reasons incumbents lose:

1. **Resource dependence.** Customers and investors determine where resources flow. A product the best customers reject cannot win the internal allocation contest against products they want.
2. **Small markets do not solve the growth needs of large companies.** A $50M market that could become $5B does not move the needle for a $5B incumbent today. By the time it does, the upstart owns it.
3. **Markets that do not exist cannot be analyzed.** Disruptive opportunities are by definition not yet legible to market research. Traditional planning processes demand evidence that does not exist.
4. **Technology supply trajectories often outrun demand trajectories.** Sustaining innovation eventually overshoots what mainstream customers can use. The overshot opens room below for disruption to enter.

## Value Networks

Christensen's key analytic move: a company is embedded in a value network of suppliers, customers, channels, and competitors that share cost structures and performance metrics. A firm's capabilities are tuned to its value network. Moving to a different value network (the one a disruption is creating) requires capabilities the firm does not have and cannot easily build, because building them would harm the existing network.

The value network frame explains why incumbents can see disruption clearly and still not respond. The capability to respond does not exist inside the current network.

## Strategic Response

The recommended mechanism: create a separate organization with its own resources, metrics, decision rights, and customer base to pursue the disruptive technology. The standard organization will starve the disruption; an autonomous organization can grow it.

This connects to [[Working Backwards Book]] single-threaded leadership at Amazon: one team, one objective, separated from competing claims on resources. It also previews the more developed prescription in [[Christensen Innovators Solution]].

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Customer obsession has limits.** Current-customer obsession can blind you to non-customer needs. Nuance on [[Bezos 2015 Letter|Bezos's customer obsession]] frame.
- **Listen to non-consumers as much as customers.** What do the people NOT using your product use instead? Disruption usually lives there.
- **Resource allocation is the actual strategy.** Most disruptive ideas die because they cannot get internal resources, not because they are wrong.
- **Separate the disruption from the core.** Structurally, not just culturally.
- **Sustaining vs disruptive is a binary, not a spectrum.** A 10% improvement is sustaining. A 10x improvement on a different metric is disruptive.
- **Small markets that do not matter today are where tomorrow's disruption sits.** Discount the "small market" objection.
