# Bezos 2003 Letter ("Long-term thinking is true ownership")

[[Jeff Bezos]]'s 2003 shareholder letter. Five-page letter, opens with the Christmas-tree-nailed-to-the-floor analogy: long-term thinking is what separates owners from tenants. The letter then walks through concrete non-abstract customer-experience decisions where short-term metrics and long-term metrics actively diverged, and Amazon took the long-term side anyway.

## Headline Takeaways

1. **Long-term thinking is what ownership means.** The tenant/owner analogy is the operational definition. Most investors are tenants because they turn portfolios fast.
2. **Three concrete examples** where short-term and long-term diverged, and Amazon took the hit:
   - Customer reviews (including negative ones) that cost sales in the short term
   - Instant Order Update (reduced repeat sales of duplicates, "measured with statistical significance")
   - Free shipping + ongoing price reductions
3. **Pricing strategy is not margin maximization.** "Our pricing strategy does not attempt to maximize margin percentages, but instead seeks to drive maximum value for customers and thereby create a much larger bottom line — in the long term."

## Key Quotes (verbatim)

### The Tenant/Owner Frame

> "Long-term thinking is both a requirement and an outcome of true ownership. Owners are different from tenants. I know of a couple who rented out their house, and the family who moved in nailed their Christmas tree to the hardwood floors instead of using a tree stand. Expedient, I suppose, and admittedly these were particularly bad tenants, but no owner would be so short-sighted. Similarly, many investors are effectively short-term tenants, turning their portfolios so quickly they are really just renting the stocks that they temporarily 'own.'"

### Customer Reviews That Cost Sales

> "For instance, shortly after launching Amazon.com in 1995, we empowered customers to review products. While now a routine Amazon.com practice, at the time we received complaints from a few vendors, basically wondering if we understood our business: 'You make money when you sell things — why would you allow negative reviews on your website?' Speaking as a focus group of one, I know I've sometimes changed my mind before making purchases on Amazon.com as a result of negative or lukewarm customer reviews. Though negative reviews cost us some sales in the short term, helping customers make better purchase decisions ultimately pays off for the company."

### Instant Order Update (the measured-bad-for-revenue case)

> "Another example is our Instant Order Update feature, which reminds you that you've already bought a particular item. Customers lead busy lives and cannot always remember if they've already purchased a particular item, say a DVD or CD they bought a year earlier. When we launched Instant Order Update, we were able to measure with statistical significance that the feature slightly reduced sales. Good for customers? Definitely. Good for shareowners? Yes, in the long run."

### The Price-Cost Structure Loop

> "Eliminating defects, improving productivity, and passing the resulting cost savings back to customers in the form of lower prices is a long-term decision. Increased volumes take time to materialize, and price reductions almost always hurt current results. In the long term, however, relentlessly driving the 'price-cost structure loop' will leave us with a stronger, more valuable business."

### Pricing as Strategy

> "Our pricing strategy does not attempt to maximize margin percentages, but instead seeks to drive maximum value for customers and thereby create a much larger bottom line — in the long term."

### Customer Experience as Top-Level Term

> "We use the term customer experience broadly. It includes every customer-facing aspect of our business — from our product prices to our selection, from our website's user interface to how we package and ship items. The customer experience we create is by far the most important driver of our business."

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **The tenant/owner test.** If you're optimizing decisions that an owner would never make (turnover, short-term margin, headline metric vibe), you're acting as a tenant on your own asset. This generalizes way beyond capital allocation.
- **The honest tradeoff.** The Instant Order Update example is the gold-standard primary source for "we measured this and it hurt revenue, and we shipped it anyway." Most "customer obsession" claims are unfalsifiable; this one is falsifiable and the company chose customer.
- **Customer reviews as the canonical case.** Bezos explicitly identifies negative reviews as costing sales. The decision to allow them anyway is the foundational customer-obsession move. (Note: this also seeded the modern review economy.)
- **The price-cost structure loop.** Lower price → higher volume → lower per-unit cost → lower price. Same flywheel logic as 1999, applied to economics. Drives the brand.
- **Customer experience as the broadest term.** Not UX. Not service. Everything customer-facing including price, selection, packaging.
