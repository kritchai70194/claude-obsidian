# Altman AGI Economics

Two essays that together form Altman's canonical AGI-economics thesis. "Moore's Law for Everything" (March 2021) makes the cost-collapse claim and frames the redistribution requirement. "The Intelligence Age" (September 2024) makes the timeline claim and stakes a position on the productivity dividend.

URLs:
- https://moores.samaltman.com/
- https://ia.samaltman.com/

These are the most-cited Altman texts in the policy literature on AI. They are also the texts that founders point at when they explain why they are building an AI-native company instead of a SaaS company.

## Why This Source

Most AI commentary in 2021-2024 was either capability-focused (what the models can do) or risk-focused (what could go wrong). Altman is one of the few writers staking a precise claim on the economics. Specifically: the marginal cost of cognitive work collapses, the productivity surplus is enormous, the surplus is concentrated unless policy redistributes it, and the timeline is shorter than most institutions are planning for.

If the thesis is right, almost every founder strategy needs updating. If the thesis is wrong, AI is just another software wave. The bet on which is true is the structural call every 2026+ founder is making, knowingly or otherwise.

## Core Theses

### Moore's Law for everything

The 2021 framing. The cost of producing intelligence, defined as the marginal cost of cognitive labor performed by a sufficiently capable AI system, follows a Moore's Law-like decline. It will halve repeatedly over the next decade. By the early 2030s, the cost of high-quality cognitive work approaches zero. The supply of intelligence becomes effectively unlimited.

### The productivity dividend is enormous and concentrated

If intelligence becomes near-free, every industry that runs on knowledge work has its cost structure rewritten. The total productivity surplus is large. The default distribution is unequal: capital owners (compute owners, model owners, equity holders in AI companies) capture most of the surplus; workers whose tasks are automated capture little or none.

### Redistribution is required or the social contract breaks

Altman's policy claim. Without redistribution mechanisms (his 2021 proposal: a tax on capital, particularly land and equity in large companies, redistributed as a citizen dividend), the productivity surplus pools and the social contract becomes unsustainable. He does not argue this from leftist priors. He argues it from system stability priors.

### The Intelligence Age timeline is short

The 2024 framing. Superintelligence is plausibly within a decade. The general-purpose technology comparison (electricity, internet) holds but the timeline is compressed. Major civilizational changes that took 50-100 years in prior platform transitions will happen in 10-20 years in this one. Institutions, including companies, are not planning for this rate.

### Deep learning worked, and that is the thing that matters

The 2024 retrospective. The single most important technical claim of the past two decades is that deep learning, scaled up, keeps producing more capable systems. There is no clear cap visible. The bet on continued scaling is the structural bet underneath the entire AI economy.

## Direct Passages

From "Moore's Law for Everything" (2021):

> In the next five years, computer programs that can think will read legal documents and give medical advice. In the next decade, they will do assembly-line work and maybe even become companions. And in the decades after that, they will do almost everything, including making new scientific discoveries that will expand our concept of "everything."

> This technological revolution is unstoppable. And a recursive loop of innovation, as these smart machines themselves help us make smarter machines, will accelerate the revolution's pace.

> If we don't plan for it, AI will probably make wealth inequality even worse. We need policies that allow a lot more people to have a lot more wealth, and reward the workers who contribute to this transformation.

From "The Intelligence Age" (September 2024):

> It is possible that we will have superintelligence in a few thousand days; it may take longer, but I'm confident we'll get there.

> Many of the jobs we do today would have looked like trifling wastes of time to people a few hundred years ago, but nobody is looking back at the past, wishing they were a lamplighter. If a lamplighter could see the world today, he would think the prosperity all around him was unimaginable.

> In the next couple of decades, we will be able to do things that would have seemed like magic to our grandparents.

> Deep learning worked. That is the most important fact of our time.

> Prosperity alone doesn't necessarily make people happy, but it does meaningfully improve the lives of people who otherwise have a lot of difficulty.

## Implications for Founders

Three structural calls fall out of the thesis.

First, **price intelligence as a near-zero-marginal-cost input** in the 2030s plan, even if it is expensive today. The strategic position is to be ready when the cost curve flips.

Second, **the productivity dividend in your industry is the prize**. If your industry runs on knowledge work, AI rewrites its cost structure, and the founder who maps the workflow correctly captures the surplus. The wrapper-vs-platform-vs-workflow question, treated in [[Altman Interviews Synthesis]], is the operational form of this.

Third, **plan for compressed timelines**. If superintelligence is plausibly within a decade, the strategic plans that mature in 5-7 years are operating on the wrong horizon. Either the plan accelerates or it gets rendered irrelevant by the curve.

## Critiques Worth Naming

The cost-collapse thesis depends on the scaling laws continuing to hold. If they plateau, the timeline stretches and the redistribution argument loses urgency. Several researchers, including [[Dario Amodei]] in his own writing, agree on the trajectory; others argue the curve flattens. The bet is not settled.

The redistribution mechanism is under-specified. Altman names the problem but does not solve it. The 2021 essay proposes a land-and-equity tax; the 2024 essay is quieter on policy mechanics. The political economy of capturing-and-redistributing AI productivity gains is harder than the technical problem.

The "deep learning worked" claim is true and is also the strongest survivorship-bias claim in AI. The methods that worked are the ones we know about. The bet that they continue to work is the bet the entire industry is making.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Intelligence cost collapses; price the future, not the present.**
- **The productivity dividend in knowledge-work industries is the prize.**
- **Plan for compressed timelines; superintelligence is plausibly within a decade.**
- **Without redistribution, the surplus pools and the social contract fractures.**
- **Deep learning, scaled up, keeps working; that is the structural bet of the era.**
