# Thompson Aggregation Theory

The foundational Stratechery essay that named and defined Aggregation Theory. The most-cited modern strategy framework. Explains why Google, Facebook, Amazon, Uber, Airbnb, and Netflix won the internet era while traditional incumbents could not respond.

Free to read (most Stratechery is paywalled) because Thompson considers it definitional foundation.

## The Value Chain Frame

> "The value chain for any given consumer market is divided into three parts: suppliers, distributors, and consumers/users."

Pre-internet: distributors integrated backward with suppliers, controlling access to consumers through scarcity of distribution channels.

## The Internet's Two Inversions

Two changes flipped the dynamics:

> "The Internet has made distribution (of digital goods) free, neutralizing the advantage that pre-Internet distributors leveraged to integrate with suppliers."

> "The Internet has made transaction costs zero, making it viable for a distributor to integrate forward with end users/consumers at scale."

Distribution scarcity, gone. Transaction cost, gone. The two pillars of incumbent advantage, gone.

## The New Pattern: Aggregation

> "Aggregators integrate forward with consumers instead, commoditizing suppliers in the process."

Where distributors used to integrate backward to suppliers, aggregators integrate forward to consumers. Suppliers become the commodity layer; consumers become the moat.

## The Virtuous Cycle

> "The most important factor determining success is the user experience: the best distributors/aggregators/market-makers win by providing the best experience, which earns them the most consumers/users, which attracts the most suppliers, which enhances the user experience in a virtuous cycle."

Self-reinforcing flywheel. Winner-take-all dynamics within a category.

## Company Examples

| Company | What it modularized |
|---|---|
| Google | "Individual pages and articles, making them directly accessible via search" |
| Facebook | "Advertisements, allowing advertisers to target customers directly, not via proxy" |
| Amazon | "Distribution first via e-commerce and then via e-books" |
| Netflix | "Broadcast availability by making its entire library available at any time" |
| Uber | "Fleet management by working with independent drivers" |
| Airbnb | "Vacant properties by building a reputation system for trust" |

Pattern: modularize the supply side, integrate the demand side.

## How This Differs from Christensen

Connects to Christensen's *Conservation of Attractive Profits*:

> "Profits shift when another company successfully modularizes the incumbent and integrates another part of the value chain."

But aggregators do not attack the low-end first; they commoditize suppliers entirely while owning the consumer relationship. Incumbents lose because they are structurally on the supplier side; the pre-internet response playbook does not apply.

## Strategic Question

Am I on the supplier side or the aggregator side?

- Supplier: moat must be deeper than commodification (irreplicable IP, exclusive content, regulatory protection).
- Aggregator: moat is the user relationship; never let a third party intermediate it.
