# Carmack Farewell Meta 2022

The capstone document of Carmack's decade in VR. Internal Meta resignation memo dated mid-December 2022, leaked in fragments to Business Insider, then posted in full by Carmack to his personal feed to control the framing. It is the cleanest distillation in his corpus of his organizational-failure diagnosis, written by someone with the deepest plausible insight into a company that had spent tens of billions of dollars on the problem he had been hired to solve.

## Context

Carmack joined Oculus as CTO in August 2013, two months after the Facebook acquisition. He transitioned to Consulting CTO in 2019 specifically to free time for AI research. He left fully in December 2022, the same window as Meta's Reality Labs writedowns and broader layoffs. The memo is therefore both a personal exit and a structural diagnosis.

His one-day-a-week period had become a distraction sink: "I have been officially 'one day a week' at Meta for a couple years now, but I still wound up checking in on groups and email every day, which was often a distraction when I should be concentrating on AI."

## Core verbatim claims

> "This is the end of my decade in VR."

> "We have a ridiculous amount of people and resources, but we constantly self-sabotage and squander effort."

> "There is no way to sugar coat this; I think our organization is operating at half the effectiveness that would make me happy."

> "I have never been able to kill stupid things before they cause damage, or set a direction and have a team actually stick to it."

> "I was wearied of the fight."

> "VR can bring value to most of the people in the world, and no company is better positioned to do it than Meta."

> "Everything necessary for spectacular success is right there, but it doesn't get put together effectively."

## Named patterns

**Operating at half effectiveness.** The diagnostic frame: not "the wrong people" or "the wrong technology" but "the right people and resources, organized in a way that wastes half their output." A specifically engineering-mind take on organizational dysfunction. The waste is not theft, it is friction.

**Killing stupid things before they cause damage.** Carmack's stated, unmet authority. He had the title (CTO, then Consulting CTO) but not the power. The implicit principle: technical leadership without kill-authority over bad initiatives is decorative.

**Setting a direction and having a team actually stick to it.** The companion failure. Strategy without follow-through. Diagnostic claim: large orgs default to drift; the work of leadership is to prevent drift, not to invent direction.

**Wearied of the fight.** Rare emotional disclosure from Carmack. The implicit cost model: organizational fights are a finite resource for senior individual contributors, and "spent" is a recoverable state but only if you exit.

**"Everything necessary for spectacular success is right there."** The hardest sentence in the memo. The failure was not about access to resources, talent, or technology. It was assembly. This becomes the implicit thesis for why Carmack now believes AGI is achievable by a small team or individual: large org overhead, not small team capability, is what holds work back.

## Frames cited

- [[Operating at Half Effectiveness]] (new). Organizational dysfunction is friction loss, not capacity loss.
- [[Engineering Velocity]]. Velocity at scale requires kill-authority and direction-holding, not just headcount and resources.
- [[Pragmatism over Theory]]. Pragmatic engineering judgment cannot survive in an environment that overrides it consistently.
- [[Individual Judgment over Process]] (extends [[Carmack Inlined Code]] frame to organizational level).
- [[Wearied of the Fight]] (new). Senior IC capital is exhaustible; staying past depletion costs everyone.

## Position in the Carmack corpus

This is the negative-space companion to [[Carmack QuakeCon 2013]]. The 2013 keynote is the engineering-thinking-out-loud at the peak of a craft career inside a small high-velocity company (id Software). The 2022 memo is the same mind, having survived a decade inside the largest VR org ever assembled, naming exactly what went wrong. The two together define Carmack's view of when engineering excellence translates into output (small org, kill authority, direction-holding) and when it does not (large org, no kill authority, drift).

The line that connects forward to [[Carmack on AGI]] is "everything necessary for spectacular success is right there." If you believe that about organizations of 10,000+, you can plausibly believe AGI is a small-team problem and not a Manhattan-project problem.
