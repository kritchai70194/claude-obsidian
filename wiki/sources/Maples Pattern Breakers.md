# Maples Pattern Breakers

The 2024 Maples and Ziebelski book that consolidates fifteen years of Floodgate seed-investing pattern recognition into a single thesis: the breakthrough startups that produce the largest returns are pattern breakers, not pattern matchers, and the seed investor's job is to identify the conditions under which pattern breaking is even possible. The book sits structurally as the capital-strategy pole's seed-stage anchor, paired with [[Peter Thiel]]'s *Zero to One* on monopoly thesis and read against [[Bill Gurley]] on capital discipline.

## Central Argument

> Most startup ideas are tar pits. They look plausible, they attract effort, and they cannot escape because the world does not yet support them.

The book opens by separating breakthrough startups from the rest. The argument is empirical: across Floodgate's portfolio (Twitter, Twitch, Lyft, Okta) and across the broader venture record, the outsized returns came from companies that the standard pattern-matching filter would have rejected. Twitter looked like SMS spam. Airbnb looked like couch surfing for the desperate. Lyft looked like an illegal-taxi novelty. The conventional VC filter rejected all three at seed.

The thesis: pattern matching is the wrong instrument for the largest outcomes. What is needed is a different filter, one that asks whether the founder is riding an inflection, whether the founder lives in the future, and whether the founder can articulate a specific, falsifiable belief about how the world will change.

## Inflections

> An inflection is a change in the world that makes previously impossible solutions newly possible.

Mobile broadband. The smartphone. Cloud compute. The transformer. GLP-1s. Regulatory shifts. Generational behavior changes. Inflections are empirical, not aspirational. Either the underlying enabling condition exists or it does not.

The book's central claim about startup formation: most successful breakout startups ride one to three inflections. Ideas without inflections fail not because they are bad ideas, but because the world's physics do not yet support them. Webvan and Pets.com were not bad ideas; they were ahead of their inflection (the broadband-ubiquitous, smartphone-ubiquitous, last-mile-logistics-ubiquitous world that Instacart and Chewy later occupied).

The diagnostic: the founder should be able to answer "why now?" with a specific, recent, empirically verifiable shift. If the answer is generic ("everything is moving online"), the inflection is not real. If the answer is specific ("transformer-based language models crossed a capability threshold in 2022 that makes X newly possible"), the inflection is real.

## Insights vs Assumptions

> Most pitches are assumptions framed as insights.

A real insight is a specific, falsifiable claim about how the world works that the pitcher can defend with evidence the rest of the market has not yet processed. An assumption is a generic belief dressed in confident language.

The example: "video is the future" is an assumption. "Mobile broadband plus front-facing cameras plus generational comfort with publicness produces a new category of short-form vertical video, which the existing landscape-format platforms cannot serve because their UI and creator tools assume horizontal" is an insight. The first cannot be falsified; the second can.

The seed investor's job is to separate the two. Maples's claim: most founders cannot articulate a real insight, and the few who can are disproportionately the ones who produce outsized returns.

## The Secret Manifesto

> A backable founder can articulate, specifically, what they believe about the future that the world does not yet accept.

Borrowed from and convergent with [[Peter Thiel]]'s secrets framework. The secret manifesto is the founder's articulated belief about the future, written down, defensible under questioning, and at variance with the current consensus.

The diagnostic: ask the founder to state their secret in one paragraph. Vague founders produce generic claims about industry trends. Backable founders produce specific, falsifiable, contrarian beliefs.

## Pattern Breakers vs Pattern Matchers

> The seed filter that rejects Twitter is the seed filter that misses the largest returns.

Pattern matching is structurally biased against the largest outcomes. By definition, the largest outcomes are anomalies, and pattern matching rejects anomalies. The book argues that the dominant VC filter (does this team look like that successful team, does this product look like that successful product) is the source of the venture industry's middling median returns.

Pattern breaking requires a different filter: does this founder live in the future, does this team ride a real inflection, does this product respond to a real insight, does this movement have pull energy. The filter is harder to apply, which is why most investors default back to pattern matching.

## Movements, Not Products

> Breakthrough startups build movements that pull customers in. Products push messaging out.

A movement is a felt belief about the future that customers want to be part of. A product is its tangible expression. Lyft was not "Uber with a pink mustache"; it was a movement about reclaiming the commute as a social experience, with a product attached. Airbnb was not "couches for rent"; it was a movement about belonging anywhere, with a product attached.

The seed test: does this team have a movement, or only a product? Movements pull; products push. Push economics require permanent paid acquisition; pull economics compound.

## Connection to Other Brain Frames

- [[Peter Thiel]] and *Zero to One*: secrets and inflections are convergent operations. Thiel asks what secret you know; Maples asks what inflection you are riding. The synthesis lives in [[Secrets and Inflections]].
- [[Paul Graham]] "Live in the Future": PG's framing predates Maples and is repeatedly cited as foundational. Both treat the founder's lived experience as the leading indicator of the future.
- [[Andrew Chen]] growth loops: the loop is what gets deployed against the inflection. Without the inflection, the loop has no fuel; without the loop, the inflection has no compounding mechanism.
- [[Bill Gurley]] marketplace economics: Gurley names how to evaluate a marketplace at scale; Maples names how to evaluate one at seed, before the metrics exist.
