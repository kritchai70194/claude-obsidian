# Slootman Amp It Up

Slootman's canonical operating manual. Written after the Snowflake IPO, drawing on three CEO runs (Data Domain, ServiceNow, Snowflake) executing the same posture. Published by Wiley in March 2022. The thesis fits on a postcard: most companies operate well below their potential not because they lack capability but because no one is willing to raise the bar. The CEO's job is to do that.

## The Four Core Principles

The book is structured around four moves that the CEO must make simultaneously, not sequentially.

### 1. Raise Standards

Standards drift down by default. Every hire below the median pulls the median down. Every tolerated B-performance teaches the org that B is acceptable. Every shipped feature that is "good enough" sets the next bar at "good enough." The CEO is the only person in the org with the authority and the vantage point to refuse this drift.

> Mediocrity has a way of sneaking up on you. You don't decide to be mediocre; you simply stop deciding to be excellent.

Concretely: every new hire must be better than the existing median of the function they join. Every deal must clear a pricing bar. Every product release must clear a quality bar. Every executive review must produce a decision, not a status update.

### 2. Align as a Team

Not "team-building." Mission alignment. Slootman is hostile to the modern corporate impulse to engineer happiness, run engagement surveys, or treat the workforce as a constituency to be managed. The thesis: make the mission compelling, make the standards clear, and the right people will align and the wrong people will self-select out.

> If you want to make people happy, sell ice cream. If you want to build a great company, align them around a mission worth doing.

The corollary: do not try to retain people who do not want to be there. The org is healthier without them and the message it sends to the rest of the team about standards is louder than any retention package.

### 3. Sharpen Focus

The book is most aggressive on focus. Most CEOs hedge: a few bets in the core, a few bets in adjacencies, a few exploratory bets in case the core saturates. Slootman calls this the natural failure pattern of scale-up companies. Every bet steals attention from the bet that is actually winning.

Snowflake under Slootman refused to build data engineering or BI products until the core data warehouse position was dominant. The discipline was visible in every all-hands and every earnings call: this is what we do, this is what we will not do, no exceptions this quarter.

> You can do anything but you cannot do everything. Pick the one thing that wins and starve everything else.

### 4. Pick Up the Pace

Tempo. The most distinctively Slootman of the four. Most companies run at the tempo of their slowest function (typically legal, finance, or comp). The CEO's job is to amp the tempo to match the urgency of the market, not to match the comfort of the slowest function.

> We're in a hurry around here.

The line is a tone-setter. Slack tone produces slack work. If the CEO answers emails in 24 hours, the org runs on a 24-hour clock. If the CEO answers in an hour, the org runs on an hour. The CEO's clock becomes the org's clock whether the CEO intends it or not.

## Specific Operating Practices

**The Monday cadence.** Executive team meets weekly, every Monday, no exceptions, no rotating in. The meeting is for decisions, not updates. Updates go in writing in advance.

**The "all in" demand on executives.** Slootman expects executives to be "all in" on the company: not necessarily working 80-hour weeks, but treating the company as the central professional bet of their career. Executives who treat the role as one of several priorities do not last.

**The willingness to fire.** Several chapters cover the mechanics of removing executives. The thesis: the cost of waiting on a known-wrong executive is paid by the team that has to work around them. Slow firing is not kindness. It is cowardice routed through the org.

**Hiring at the bar.** Every senior hire goes through Slootman. He has personally interviewed thousands of senior candidates across the three companies. The bar is not "can this person do the job." The bar is "is this person better than the median of the function they will join."

## Case Studies

The book is structured around vignettes from the three CEO runs.

- **Data Domain.** Displacing tape backup with deduplication storage. The kitchen cabinet of trusted execs. The role of dissatisfaction with the status quo as the engine of the company.
- **ServiceNow.** Scaling from a struggling $100M business to $1.4B ARR. The pricing repositioning. The shift from "IT service management tool" to "enterprise platform."
- **Snowflake.** The IPO posture. The decision to refuse adjacencies until the core was dominant. The discipline of net revenue retention as the primary metric.

## Why This Source Matters to the Brain

It is the operating posture, written by the operator who executed it three times. Not advice from a coach. Not a theory from an academic. The book is read by Slootman's own executives as a manual for how they are expected to behave. That makes it ground truth on the operating-at-scale pole.
