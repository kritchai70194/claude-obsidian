# Chesky 11-Star Experience

The most-circulated single framing tool in Chesky's body of work. Originally told in detail on Reid Hoffman's *Masters of Scale* podcast episode "Handcrafted" (2017), and re-told across Lenny's podcast, First Round Review interviews, and various conference talks since. The idea is so portable that it has been adopted as a product-strategy tool inside companies that have no other Airbnb DNA.

In the corpus as **C4**, the framing-tool complement to [[Chesky Snow White Design]]. Where Snow White is the artifact-driven method (draw the experience), 11 Stars is the imagination-driven method (extrapolate the ceiling).

## The Core Method

Take the customer experience you currently offer. Imagine what 11 stars (on a 5-star scale) would look like. Then work backwards: what does 10 stars look like? 9? 8? 7? Most companies stop their thinking at 5 because "good enough" is the local maximum. The 11-star ladder is a strategy tool for finding where the actual ceiling lives.

The Airbnb examples Chesky uses on Masters of Scale, paraphrased:

**5 stars (current):** the guest arrives at the listing, the host is present or has left clear instructions, the place is clean, the stay is uneventful, the guest leaves a positive review.

**6 stars:** the host meets the guest at the door with a small welcome (coffee, snack, local recommendations). The place is meaningfully better than expected.

**7 stars:** the host has anticipated the guest's specific needs. If the guest is a runner, there are running routes printed. If a coffee drinker, fresh beans and a pour-over.

**8 stars:** the host arranges something specific to the guest's trip purpose. Dinner reservations. A tour. A surprise.

**9 stars:** the host meets the guest at the airport with a personalized sign.

**10 stars:** there is a marching band at the airport. A red carpet. A personal welcome from the mayor.

**11 stars:** the guest is greeted at the airport by Elon Musk, who offers them a trip to space.

The 11-star version is absurd on purpose. Quoted from the Masters of Scale episode:

> If you don't go all the way to 11, you'll never know what 7 looks like.

## Why The Absurd Endpoint Is Load-Bearing

The point of 11 stars is not that you build the 11-star version. The point is that the 11-star version is so far outside what the team currently imagines that it shakes loose the constraints they have been operating under. Once they can see what 11 looks like, 7 and 8 become specific, actionable, and ambitious.

Without the 11-star prompt, teams optimize for 5.5 stars. They tweak the existing experience. The 11-star exercise produces qualitatively different ideas because it requires qualitatively different thinking.

## The Strategic Use

Chesky's framing in the podcast and subsequent retellings:

> Most of what we built at Airbnb that mattered, we built somewhere between 7 and 9 stars. 6 is incrementalism. 10 is too expensive. 7 and 8 are where the actual moats live.

The ladder is a strategy tool for finding the **operationally feasible** version of an extraordinary experience. The CEO does not need to fund 11. The CEO needs the team to be able to imagine 11 so they can build 8.

## The Method Travels

The 11-star method has been adopted across product organizations far outside Airbnb's domain. Used in:

- B2B SaaS onboarding design (what would 11-star onboarding look like? Then back off to 8.)
- Developer experience design (Stripe and Vercel both use variants).
- Customer support escalation design.
- Internal employee experience design (the 11-star new-hire week).

Portability comes from the fact that the tool requires no specific data, no research budget, and no infrastructure. The cost is one good 45-minute workshop. The output is a redirected sense of what is possible.

## Connection to Founder Mode

11 Stars is Founder Mode in the strategy domain. The CEO is the person who can credibly authorize the team to imagine the absurd version, because the CEO is the person who decides what gets resourced. A middle manager running the same exercise produces a list that gets shelved. The CEO running the exercise produces a roadmap.

Chesky has been explicit that the 11-star ladder was his personal tool, used in product reviews he ran personally, not a process owned by a design ops team.

## Why It Beats "Think Big"

Standard product advice ("think 10x not 10%", "moonshots", "big hairy audacious goals") fails because it does not produce specifics. "Think 10x" is a slogan. The 11-star ladder is a procedure with five rungs you have to populate with actual ideas.

The forcing function is the ladder structure. You cannot skip from 5 to 11. You have to articulate 6, 7, 8, 9, 10 along the way. Each rung produces specific product ideas.
