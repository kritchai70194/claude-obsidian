# Blank Startup Owners Manual

The operational follow-up to [[Blank Four Steps to Epiphany]], co-written with Bob Dorf and published in March 2012 through K&S Ranch. Where the *Four Steps* book argued the methodology, the *Owner's Manual* is the working document: checklists, hypothesis grids, decision criteria, and explicit variations for different startup types. Written in the seven years between the two books and informed by Blank's experience teaching the material to thousands of founders at Stanford and Berkeley.

## What This Book Adds to the First One

The *Four Steps* is a conceptual argument. The *Owner's Manual* is a 600-page checklist for actually running Customer Development. Three substantial additions justify the rewrite:

1. **The explicit hypothesis grid.** Every startup hypothesis named, mapped to a test, with pass/fail criteria.
2. **The pivot decision framework.** Named pivot types (zoom-in, zoom-out, customer-segment, customer-need, platform, business-architecture, value-capture, growth-engine, channel, technology) with criteria for when each applies.
3. **Variations.** Web/mobile startups vs physical-product startups, B2B vs B2C, with different exit criteria, different sales motions, different timelines.

## The Customer Development Checklist

The book is organized as a series of checklists, one per stage of Customer Development. Each checklist enumerates the activities, artifacts, and exit criteria for that stage. The intended use is operational: print the checklist, work it down, do not exit the stage until everything is checked.

Discovery stage examples:

- Draft initial Business Model Canvas (Customer, Problem, Solution, Channel, Revenue, Cost, Resources, Activities, Partners).
- Identify customer archetype with enough specificity to find one in the wild.
- Schedule and conduct at least 50 customer interviews; some sources from Blank cite 100.
- Build the Minimum Viable Product (MVP) only after the problem is validated, not before.
- Validate that customers will commit (time, reference, money, or pre-order) before declaring Discovery complete.

The pattern repeats for Validation, Creation, and Building. The discipline the book imposes is not the existence of checklists but the refusal to skip stages.

## The Hypothesis Grid

The single most useful artifact in the book. A grid that names every hypothesis a startup is making and pairs each with the experiment that would falsify it:

| Hypothesis | Test | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|---|
| Customer is X | Interview 20 X candidates | 15+ acknowledge the problem unprompted | Pivot customer hypothesis |
| Problem is Y | Same interviews | 15+ rank Y as top-3 problem | Pivot problem hypothesis |
| Solution is Z | Demo or MVP test | 5+ commit | Pivot solution hypothesis |
| Channel is C | Test sales motion | Repeatable acquisition | Pivot channel hypothesis |
| Market size is M | Top-down + bottom-up estimate | Plausible $100M+ TAM | Pivot or kill |

The discipline is in the explicit pre-commitment to what would constitute pass and fail. Without the pre-commitment, every result is rationalized.

## The Pivot Framework

> A pivot is a substantive change to one or more components of the business model.

Blank and Dorf name ten pivot types. The two most cited:

- **Zoom-in pivot:** what was one feature becomes the whole product. Discovery revealed the product was overbuilt.
- **Customer-segment pivot:** the product solves a real problem, but for a different customer than the founders thought.

The framing is important. A pivot is a planned change to one specific hypothesis based on evidence from the previous stage. It is not a panic response to a missed quarter. The most common pathology is the failure to pivot when the evidence is in: sunk-cost commitment to a falsified hypothesis.

## Web/Mobile vs Physical-Product Variations

The book separates Customer Development for software businesses (faster iteration, MVPs in days, A/B test as a validation tool) from physical-product businesses (longer iteration, MVP is often a paper prototype or concierge service, validation happens through pre-orders).

The variations matter operationally. A SaaS founder who tries to run Discovery on a physical-product timeline ships too late. A hardware founder who tries to run Discovery on a SaaS timeline ships an unmanufacturable product.

## B2B vs B2C Variations

Different sales motions, different decision-maker structures, different stage exit criteria. B2B Customer Validation typically requires a documented sales process that closes deals without founder presence. B2C Validation typically requires a measurable acquisition channel that produces unit economics consistent with scale.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Customer Development is a checklist, not a metaphor.** The operational discipline is the value.
- **Every hypothesis must have a pre-committed pass/fail criterion.** Without it, every result is rationalized.
- **A pivot is a planned change to one hypothesis, not a panic response.** Most startups pivot too late.
- **Web/mobile and physical-product startups have different Customer Development timelines.** Methodology, same; cadence, different.
- **Do not exit a stage until every checklist item is checked.** Skipping is the modal failure.
