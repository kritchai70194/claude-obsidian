# Bezos 2004 Letter ("Free cash flow per share")

[[Jeff Bezos]]'s 2004 shareholder letter, published April 2005. The letter that pins down **free cash flow per share** as Amazon's ultimate financial measure with a fully-worked numerical proof. The earnings-can-destroy-value parable here is one of the most-cited primary texts on capital allocation.

## Headline Takeaways

1. **Free cash flow per share is the ultimate financial measure.** Not earnings. Not earnings-per-share. Not earnings growth. Not EBITDA. The letter walks through why.
2. **The transportation-machine parable.** A worked hypothetical: 100% compound earnings growth over 4 years and $150M of cumulative earnings, but cumulative free cash flow of negative $530M. Earnings growth can actively destroy value.
3. **EBITDA is misleading.** "EBITDA isn't cash flow." Same parable applied to EBITDA produces 100% three-year EBITDA growth and the same negative-cash-flow disaster.
4. **Cash-generative operating cycle.** Inventory turns of ~13x; pay-suppliers-after-collecting-from-customers. Working capital is negative. This is the structural advantage.
5. **4% of sales in fixed assets.** $246M of fixed assets on $6.9B of sales: extreme capital efficiency for a retailer.

## Key Quotes (verbatim)

### The thesis statement (opening line)

> "Our ultimate financial measure, and the one we most want to drive over the long-term, is free cash flow per share."

### Why not earnings

> "Earnings don't directly translate into cash flows, and shares are worth only the present value of their future cash flows, not the present value of their future earnings."

> "Though some may find it counterintuitive, a company can actually impair shareholder value in certain circumstances by growing earnings. This happens when the capital investments required for growth exceed the present value of the cash flow derived from those investments."

### On EBITDA

> "Notice, too, that a focus on EBITDA, Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortization, would lead to the same faulty conclusion about the health of the business. Sequential annual EBITDA would have been $50, $100, $200 and $400 million, 100% growth for three straight years. But without taking into account the $1.28 billion in capital expenditures necessary to generate this 'cash flow,' we're getting only part of the story. EBITDA isn't cash flow."

### On the paradox of growth

> "Paradoxically, from a cash flow perspective, the slower this business grows the better off it is."

### On the cash-generative operating cycle

> "We have a cash generative operating cycle because we turn our inventory quickly, collecting payments from our customers before payments are due to suppliers. Our high inventory turnover means we maintain relatively low levels of investment in inventory: $480 million at year end on a sales base of nearly $7 billion."

### On capital efficiency

> "The capital efficiency of our business model is illustrated by our modest investments in fixed assets, which were $246 million at year end or 4% of 2004 sales."

### On dilution

> "Total shares outstanding plus stock-based awards are essentially unchanged at the end of 2004 compared with 2003, and are down 1% over the last three years. ... Efficiently managing share count means more cash flow per share and more long-term value for owners."

### On the through-line to 1997

> "This focus on free cash flow isn't new for Amazon.com. We made it clear in our 1997 letter to shareholders, our first as a public company, that when 'forced to choose between optimizing GAAP accounting and maximizing the present value of future cash flows, we'll take the cash flows.'"

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Earnings can destroy value.** The numerical proof is the single most useful pedagogical tool for any capital-allocation conversation. Most operators believe "more earnings = more value." The parable demonstrates with arithmetic that this is false.
- **EBITDA is not a substitute for cash flow.** This is a load-bearing claim for anyone evaluating private companies, M&A, or operating businesses with heavy capex.
- **Capital efficiency is the operator's edge.** Negative working capital + low fixed assets + high inventory turn = a structural cash machine. The retail business model that Amazon ran in 2004 is the same model that funded AWS, Prime, Kindle, and Echo.
- **Share count as a managed variable.** Pair with the [[Bezos 2001 Letter]] 3% dilution target. Bezos treats dilution as a controllable input metric, not a downstream consequence.
- **The 1997 letter is the through-line.** Every Bezos letter appends 1997. The 2004 letter explicitly says: the cash-flows-over-GAAP line in 1997 is the seed of everything in this letter.
