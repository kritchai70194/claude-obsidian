# High Output Management (Andy Grove, 1983)

The most-cited management book in the technology industry. Written by [[Andy Grove (Intel)|Andy Grove]] (Intel co-founder, CEO 1979 to 1997) originally as an internal Intel management course textbook. Endorsed by [[Patrick Collison]] and central to the Stripe Press canon.

## Provenance

- Author: Andrew S. Grove
- Year: 1983 (original); 1995 and 2015 (revisions)

## Headline Takeaway

The single biggest reframe: a manager's output is not their personal output. It's the output of the teams they influence. This sounds obvious; it's not. Most managers default to high-individual-contributor habits and resist the harder discipline of being a multiplier. Grove gives the mechanism (training, one-on-ones, mission alignment) that operationalizes the reframe.

## The Core Formula

> A manager's output = the output of his organization + the output of the neighboring organizations under his influence

This formula is the spine of the entire book. Everything else (one-on-ones, OKRs, TRM) is downstream of "how do I maximize my multiplier effect."

## High-Leverage Activities

> "The art of management is selecting and concentrating on one, two, or three high-leverage activities and ignoring the rest."

Grove's explicit examples:

- **Training** is "the highest leverage activity a manager can do to increase the output of an organization."
- **Motivating and teaching** are high-leverage because they have long-term compounding effects on team output.
- **One-on-one meetings** are how you transmit those effects to individual reports.

## One-on-One Meetings (the specific mechanism)

Grove's recipe:

- The meeting is **the subordinate's meeting**, not the manager's.
- **Agenda set by the subordinate**, provided in advance.
- Manager asks questions; surfaces issues; mentors.
- **Frequency calibrated to TRM** (more frequent for low-TRM, less for high-TRM).

This is more specific than "have weekly 1:1s." The structure is what makes 1:1s leverage, not the frequency.

## Task-Relevant Maturity

> The salesman got promoted because of his high TRM in sales. But in his new position as a manager, he had low TRM.

TRM is task-specific competence. Promoting someone resets their TRM in the new role to zero. Implication: management style must vary by report's TRM in their current task, not by their seniority.

## Manufacturing as Management

The book's pedagogical move: start with a "breakfast factory" analogy. Establish manufacturing principles (output rate, bottleneck, throughput, work-in-progress). Then transfer those principles to management work: meetings, decisions, performance reviews.

The implication: **management is operations, not strategy.** Identify the bottleneck. Measure the output. Optimize the workflow.

## OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

Grove's framework. Later carried to Google by John Doerr; now industry standard. Concept:

- **Objectives:** what you're trying to achieve, qualitative.
- **Key Results:** how you'll measure achievement, quantitative.

Originated at Intel as a way to align distributed teams on direction without micromanagement.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Managers are multipliers.** New principle to add to Operating Principles. The manager's individual output is the wrong metric; the team's output is the right one.
- **Training as highest-leverage activity.** Counterintuitive operator move: when overwhelmed, spend MORE time on training, not less. Compounds.
- **One-on-ones as subordinate's meeting.** Mechanism specificity. The structure makes the meeting work, not the frequency.
- **TRM as a calibration variable.** Don't treat all reports the same way. Match style to current-task competence.
- **OKRs originate here.** When citing OKRs in operator contexts, the source is Grove, not Doerr or Google.
- **Hybrid organizations are inevitable at scale.** "Grove's Law": all large organizations with a common business purpose end up in a hybrid form. Companion to Bezos's two-pizza teams.
