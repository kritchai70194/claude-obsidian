# Gurley All Markets Not Equal

Bill Gurley's 2012 essay at abovethecrowd.com: *All Markets Are Not Created Equal: 10 Factors to Consider When Evaluating Digital Marketplaces*. The canonical marketplace evaluation framework, written from the board seat at OpenTable and during the early Uber years. Cited as foundational by [[Andrew Chen]] and most marketplace operators since.

## The Thesis

Two marketplaces with identical headline GMV can be radically different businesses. The differences come from 10 structural factors that govern whether the marketplace can sustain its take rate, expand its market, and produce durable economics. Marketplaces that fail on multiple factors do not become venture-scale outcomes regardless of how good early traction looks.

> Not all online marketplaces are created equal. There are huge differences between the most attractive marketplaces and the rest.

## The 10 Factors

### 1. New Experience vs Old

The marketplace must offer a meaningfully better experience than the offline or incumbent online alternative. eBay vs newspaper classifieds. Uber vs hailing a cab. OpenTable vs calling 12 restaurants. If the new experience is only marginally better, the marketplace will not pull users at venture speed.

### 2. Economic Advantage to Suppliers and Buyers

Both sides must come out ahead versus the alternative. If only one side wins, the other side defects.

### 3. Highly Fragmented on Both Sides

A marketplace works when neither buyers nor sellers can route around it. If supply is concentrated in three vendors, those three vendors will eventually negotiate their way around your rake. Fragmentation on both sides is what gives the marketplace its leverage.

### 4. Ability to Use Network to Police Quality

Reviews, ratings, reputation systems. Without them, lemons drive out quality and the marketplace collapses to the bottom. With them, quality compounds.

### 5. Expansion Aspect (Total Market Size)

The marketplace must address a market that is (a) large in dollars and (b) can be expanded by the marketplace itself. Uber did not just take taxi rides; it expanded the total ride market by orders of magnitude. Airbnb did not just take hotel nights; it created a category of inventory that did not exist before.

### 6. Frequency

> High frequency = high engagement = high lifetime value.

Restaurant reservations (OpenTable), rides (Uber), and meals (DoorDash) are higher-frequency than mattresses or weddings. High-frequency marketplaces compound; low-frequency marketplaces stall.

### 7. Payment Flow

Marketplaces that intermediate payment (vs just listings) capture more value, have stronger data on both sides, and have stronger lock-in. The classified-ad model (Craigslist) leaves money on the table; the transactional model (eBay, Uber) captures it.

### 8. Value of Bringing Sellers Online

If suppliers had no good way to reach buyers before, the marketplace creates massive value and can command a real rake. If suppliers already had distribution (e.g., they had their own websites), the marketplace adds less and can command less.

### 9. Friction Reduction (UX)

The marketplace must reduce friction at every step. Fewer clicks, faster matching, automated payment, automated trust. Friction is what kills marketplace conversion.

### 10. Take Rate (Rake) Sustainability

> The take rate is the most important number in any marketplace.

Gurley's load-bearing claim: sustainable take rate sits in the low double digits, roughly 10-15% for most consumer marketplaces. Higher rakes invite disintermediation. Suppliers route around you; buyers route around you; or a thinner-rake competitor enters and wins.

> If your take rate is in the 30-40% range, you are vulnerable. Someone will undercut you, or your suppliers will go direct.

The rake math is what made Gurley skeptical of many late-stage marketplace valuations: the headline GMV could only be reconciled with the valuation by assuming take rates that history said were not sustainable.

## Reproduced Passages

> The take rate is the most important number in any marketplace. It is also the most overlooked. Many investors and entrepreneurs get caught up in GMV and forget that the company only ever sees a fraction of that.

> A 30% rake is not a moat. It is an invitation for someone to come in at 10% and take the market.

> Frequency matters more than founders typically think. A low-frequency marketplace, even a large one, struggles to build the data, trust, and habit that compound into a durable position.

> Network effects are claimed more often than they exist. The test is whether the marketplace gets harder to compete with as it grows, not whether it gets bigger.

## Why This Source Matters

This is the 2012 marketplace canon. Every subsequent marketplace essay (Chen, a16z, Lenny, NFX) builds on or argues with these 10 factors. Gurley wrote it from the board seat at OpenTable (high frequency, sustainable take, expanded market) and just before Uber became the textbook case (every factor checked).

The framework is also the diagnostic Gurley applies to the 2014-2016 unicorn party: many of the cited "marketplaces" failed multiple factors (low frequency, unsustainable take, no real network effect) and were funded as if they had all 10. The structural critique in [[Gurley J-curve and Recap]] depends on this evaluation framework being available first.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **All markets are not created equal.** Two marketplaces with identical GMV can have radically different economics.
- **Sustainable take rate sits in the low double digits.** Above that, disintermediation is the long-run outcome.
- **Frequency compounds; low frequency stalls.** A marketplace below ~1 transaction per user per quarter struggles to build trust and habit.
- **Fragmentation on both sides is the source of marketplace leverage.** Concentrated supply or demand will route around the rake.
- **Payment flow capture is structurally more valuable than listings.** The Craigslist model leaves money on the table.
- **Network effects are claimed more often than they exist.** The test is whether competition gets harder as you grow.
