# Raskin Zuora Pitch

Andy Raskin's 2016 Medium essay titled "The Greatest Sales Pitch I've Seen All Year", analyzing Zuora's enterprise pitch deck and extracting the structure that became the canonical 5-part Strategic Narrative arc. The breakthrough piece. Reproduced and cited across enterprise GTM literature for the next decade.

## Context

Zuora sold subscription billing software. In 2016, ahead of its eventual IPO, the company faced a category-creation problem: most enterprise buyers did not yet think "subscription billing" was a distinct category. The default frame was "we already have billing systems". Raskin observed the pitch deck Zuora's sales team was using and identified the structural move that made it work.

The essay is not a marketing piece for Zuora. It is a reverse-engineering exercise: Raskin walks through the deck slide by slide and extracts the underlying pattern.

## The 5-Part Structure (as Raskin Reads Zuora)

### 1. Name a Big, Relevant Change in the World

Zuora's deck did not open with the product. It opened with a claim about the world: the economy is shifting from one-time product transactions to ongoing subscription relationships. This is not a Zuora claim; it is a claim about the buyer's reality. The deck cited Netflix, Salesforce, Uber, and other companies whose business models were already subscription-based.

> If you can establish your change as a credible, inexorable trend, you will frame everything that follows in its terms.

### 2. Show There Will Be Winners and Losers

Zuora named the stakes. In the new subscription economy, companies that adapt thrive; companies that do not adapt lose. The deck cited specific incumbents whose stock had cratered as digital subscription competitors emerged. The stakes were not abstract. They were named companies, named outcomes.

The move forces the buyer to ask: am I a winner or a loser in this world? The cognitive default is to want to be a winner.

### 3. Tease the Promised Land

What does the future look like for the winners? Zuora painted it: companies that thrive in the subscription economy have direct, ongoing relationships with customers; they capture rich behavioral data; they monetize through recurring revenue with predictable LTV; they grow through expansion, not just acquisition.

Critically, the Promised Land is not "use Zuora". It is the outcome the buyer wants in their own terms. Zuora is not yet in the deck.

### 4. Introduce Features as "Magic Gifts" for Overcoming Obstacles

Now the obstacles. Why is it hard to reach the Promised Land? Because the existing financial systems were built for one-time transactions. ERPs cannot model recurring revenue properly. GAAP revenue recognition is broken under subscription accounting. Pricing models, billing cycles, dunning workflows, all of it was designed for a different world.

Raskin's key insight: at this point, and not before, the product appears. Each Zuora capability is framed as a tool for overcoming a named obstacle. The features are not features. They are "magic gifts" the guide hands the hero so the hero can reach the Promised Land.

### 5. Present Evidence That You Can Make the Story Come True

The final move: proof. Customer logos, specific outcomes, deployment data, the names of subscription-economy winners who run on Zuora. The evidence is not generic; it is tied to the Promised Land. "Companies that operate the new way run on Zuora" is the implicit claim.

## Why This Beats Product-Led Pitches

The default enterprise pitch opens with the product. "Hi, we are Zuora, we make subscription billing software." The buyer's mental response: "we have billing software". The pitch is now a feature war the seller is unlikely to win.

The narrative pitch opens with the world. "The economy is shifting to subscriptions, and your financial systems were built for the old world." The buyer's mental response: "yes, that is true, and I had not put it that way". Now the buyer is leaning in, not defending.

Once the world is established and the buyer agrees on the world change, the product framing is dramatically easier. The product is the obvious response to a problem the buyer has already accepted.

## Reproduction of the Core Claim

> The single most important thing you have to do is name what's changing in the world.

> If you start with your product, your prospect is going to start asking, "Why do I need this?" And that's a hard sell. If you start with the change in the world, your prospect starts asking, "How am I going to survive this?" That's a very different sale.

## What Made This Essay Travel

The essay went viral in enterprise GTM circles because it gave a name and a structure to something experienced sales leaders intuited but could not teach. Before Raskin, the move was tribal knowledge. After Raskin, it was a workshop, a checklist, and a hiring rubric.

Zuora itself went on to IPO in 2018, and the strategic narrative is widely credited as a contributing factor in how the company built its category. The deck became one of the most-studied artifacts in modern enterprise sales.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Open with a named world change, not with the product.** Frames the buyer's decision as "which side of the change am I on" instead of "do I want this feature".
- **The Promised Land is the buyer's outcome, not your product.** The product enters only after the world is established.
- **Features are magic gifts the guide hands the hero for overcoming named obstacles.** Reframes feature-listing as obstacle-removal.
- **Evidence is the proof that you can make the story come true.** Customer logos must connect to the Promised Land, not to the product.
