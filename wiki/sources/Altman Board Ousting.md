# Altman Board Ousting

Structural summary of the November 17-22 2023 OpenAI board crisis and the 2024 retrospectives that followed. Sources: New York Times reporting (Cade Metz, Mike Isaac), The Information (Stephanie Palazzolo, Amir Efrati), Platformer (Casey Newton), Stratechery (Ben Thompson), Hard Fork (Kevin Roose, Casey Newton), Coda's "Inside OpenAI" coverage, and the various employee statements and board statements published during the five-day window.

No privileged sources. This is the public record stitched together, with the gaps named honestly.

## Why This Source

The most public CEO governance crisis in tech in 2023, and the most important governance event in AI to date. The OpenAI structure, non-profit board governing a capped-profit subsidiary, had been described as novel and untested. The November events were the stress test. The structure did not break, but the gaps it exposed are now the template for every conversation about AI lab governance.

For founders, the lessons are structural. For policymakers, the lessons are about which institutions actually hold authority in an AI lab.

## Timeline

### Friday Nov 17 2023

At 12:19pm Pacific, Altman is fired by the OpenAI board via video call. Board statement released shortly after: he was "not consistently candid in his communications with the board, hindering its ability to exercise its responsibilities." Greg Brockman is removed from the board chair but invited to remain at OpenAI; he resigns within hours. Mira Murati is named interim CEO.

The board at the time of the firing: Ilya Sutskever (OpenAI co-founder and Chief Scientist), Adam D'Angelo (Quora CEO), Tasha McCauley (RAND scientist), Helen Toner (Georgetown's CSET). Three independent directors plus Sutskever.

### Saturday Nov 18 - Sunday Nov 19

Microsoft, OpenAI's largest investor at roughly $13B committed, is informed of the firing minutes before the public announcement. Satya Nadella's public statement is measured but the private reaction is described as furious. The board declines to provide specific examples of the "not candid" charge.

Investor pressure mounts. Thrive Capital, Tiger, Khosla, and other major OpenAI shareholders push for Altman's reinstatement. The board considers an alternative CEO candidate (Emmett Shear, former Twitch CEO, named interim CEO Sunday night) but cannot reach a workable arrangement with Altman.

### Sunday Nov 19 evening - Monday Nov 20

Microsoft announces it will hire Altman, Brockman, and any OpenAI employees who wish to join, to lead a new AI research division at Microsoft. Within hours, more than 700 of OpenAI's 770 employees sign an open letter threatening to resign and follow Altman to Microsoft unless the board resigns and reinstates him.

Sutskever publicly reverses position, signing the employee letter and stating "I deeply regret my participation in the board's actions." This effectively ends the board's position.

### Tuesday Nov 21 - Wednesday Nov 22

Multi-day negotiations. New board structure agreed: Bret Taylor (former Salesforce co-CEO) as chair, Larry Summers (former Treasury Secretary), Adam D'Angelo retained. McCauley and Toner depart. Altman returns as CEO on Nov 22. Brockman returns. Sutskever's status is left ambiguous (he eventually departs OpenAI in May 2024).

Total elapsed time: 5 days from firing to reinstatement.

## What the Board Said and Did Not Say

The board's stated rationale ("not consistently candid") was vague at the time and never substantively expanded. Subsequent reporting suggested several contributing concerns: Altman's outside investments and the question of whether they were disclosed, internal disputes about safety release timing for GPT-4 Turbo, the dynamics around Helen Toner's published paper (which was somewhat critical of OpenAI), and a longer-running concern about whether the board could effectively oversee Altman.

The board never published a specific case. The absence of a specific case is itself a key data point. A board that cannot or will not articulate the case loses procedural legitimacy quickly, even if the underlying concerns were substantive.

## The Employee Revolt

The single most-discussed event of the five days. Over 700 of 770 employees signed an open letter within roughly 24 hours of the firing. The letter committed them to resign and follow Altman to Microsoft unless the board resigned.

Two readings.

The **loyalty reading**: Altman had built a culture so committed to the mission and the leadership that the employees voted with their feet. This is the reading Altman supporters emphasize.

The **capital reading**: a tender offer at an $86B valuation was in progress when the firing happened, and employees stood to lose substantial paper wealth if the company collapsed. The loyalty was real and the financial alignment was also real. Both can be true.

Either way, the employee revolt functioned as the de facto board override. The structural authority of an institution is what its people actually do when tested, and OpenAI's people aligned with Altman.

## The Microsoft Role

Microsoft was the structural lever. Two moves mattered.

First, the immediate offer to hire Altman and any OpenAI employees. This converted an internal governance dispute into an external capital-and-talent flight risk. The board was no longer choosing whether to keep Altman; it was choosing whether OpenAI continued to exist as an institution.

Second, the quieter pressure throughout the week to find a workable arrangement that returned Altman. Satya Nadella was reportedly central to the multi-party negotiations that produced the new board.

The lesson: a strategic investor with capital, distribution, and a credible alternative venue holds more board-equivalent power than the board itself.

## What the Structure Exposed

The OpenAI structure: a non-profit board with ultimate fiduciary authority over a capped-profit subsidiary that holds the operating company and the commercial relationships. The board's stated obligation is to the mission ("AGI that benefits all of humanity"), not to shareholders.

The structure was designed to prevent commercial pressure from overriding safety commitments. The November events exposed three gaps.

First, **no specific failure mode for governance failure**. The board could remove the CEO under its charter, but the structure had no specified mechanism for what happens if the removal itself is contested by employees and investors. There was no escalation path, no constitutional appeals process, no defined succession plan.

Second, **the board lacked the operating context to act on a serious case**. Independent directors meeting periodically cannot accumulate the operating knowledge required to make a credible "not candid" case stick. The structure assumed the case would be self-evident if it had to be made. It was not.

Third, **the capital base became the de facto board**. Microsoft, the employees, and the tender-offer investors together held the structural authority the formal board could not exercise. The capped-profit subsidiary's commercial reality outweighed the non-profit board's nominal authority.

## Lessons for Founder-CEO + Board Governance

Five take-aways that 2024+ AI labs and founders are operating on.

1. **Board composition matters more in fast-moving AI companies than in normal startups.** Independent directors with limited operating context cannot effectively oversee a CEO running a 700-person company at a $86B+ valuation moving at the pace OpenAI moves.

2. **A board firing without a specific, articulated case loses legitimacy quickly.** Procedural transparency is the precondition for board authority.

3. **Employees and strategic investors are the de facto board.** If they leave, the institution ceases to function. Any board action that does not have the prior consent of employees and key investors is structurally weak.

4. **Founder-CEO concentration of authority is the operating reality at AI labs**, partly because the technical complexity makes it hard to substitute another CEO at speed, and partly because the talent network is loyal to the founder more than the institution.

5. **The novel structure was novel for a reason and also fragile for the same reason.** OpenAI returned to a more conventional board after November, with Bret Taylor and Larry Summers anchoring it. The capped-profit experiment continues; the governance experiment quietly retreated.

## Open Questions Honest Fidelity Requires Naming

The board may have had substantive concerns that were correct on the merits and incorrect on execution. The public record cannot resolve this. The lessons above hold regardless of whether the original concerns were valid.

Altman's outside investments, the structure of the Atomic / WorldCoin / various AI infrastructure positions, and the question of whether these created material conflicts of interest with OpenAI remain partially public. The 2024 Senate testimony and the various journalistic accounts disagree on how much was disclosed and to whom.

Sutskever's departure in May 2024, and the broader departures of safety-focused researchers (Jan Leike, others) through 2024-2025, suggest the underlying tensions did not resolve with the November reinstatement. They were displaced.
