# Roberge Math of B2B Sales

Mark Roberge's essays at Stage 2 Capital and his Stripe Press conversation on the unit economics that determine whether a B2B sales motion is durable or terminal. The thesis is that founders routinely flatter their unit-economics math to raise the next round, and the flattering breaks the company between Series B and Series C when the numbers have to clear at scale. Roberge specifies the metrics, the honest formulas, and the failure modes.

## The Four Numbers That Matter

Roberge's claim: four metrics, calculated honestly at scale, determine whether the sales motion is real or a temporary hallucination.

1. **CAC payback period**. How many months of gross profit does it take to recover the fully-loaded cost of acquiring the customer? Healthy SaaS at scale: under 12 months. Excellent: under 6.
2. **LTV / CAC ratio**. Lifetime value of the customer divided by acquisition cost. Healthy: 3:1 or better. Anything under 1:1 means each customer destroys value.
3. **Gross Revenue Retention (GRR)**. The percentage of last year's ARR that you keep this year, ignoring upsells and expansion. Healthy: 90%+. Below 80% indicates a product or fit problem that no sales hire fixes.
4. **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**. GRR plus expansion revenue from the existing customer base. Healthy: 110%+. Best-in-class: 130%+. NRR above 100% means the installed base grows even with zero new customers.

> If these four numbers do not converge at scale, no sales playbook saves the company. The problem is upstream in product, pricing, or ICP.

## Calculating CAC Honestly

Roberge spends paragraphs on the calculation, because this is where founders cheat themselves.

**Fully-loaded CAC includes:**

- All sales team compensation (base, commission, benefits)
- All sales team overhead (tools, travel, training)
- All marketing spend (paid, content, events, brand)
- All marketing team compensation and overhead
- Allocated portion of sales leadership and operations

**Fully-loaded CAC excludes:**

- Customer success and onboarding (those are CoGS, not CAC)
- Product and engineering (those are R&D)

**Per-customer CAC**: total fully-loaded sales and marketing spend in the period divided by net new customers acquired in the period.

The cheats Roberge calls out:

- **Excluding sales leadership and ops.** Those people exist to make sales work; their cost is CAC.
- **Counting only the marketing channels you like.** All marketing spend that contributed to any acquired customer is in the denominator.
- **Using bookings instead of cash.** CAC payback is a cash metric; ARR commitments do not pay the payroll.
- **Smoothing across years.** Calculate CAC per cohort and per quarter; smoothing hides the deterioration.

## Calculating LTV Honestly

The naive formula: average revenue per customer divided by annual churn rate. This is wrong in two directions.

The honest formula uses **gross profit** (not revenue) and **net dollar retention curves** (not point-in-time churn).

> A customer paying you $100K of revenue at 70% gross margin is worth $70K per year, not $100K. Multiply by the years they stay, accounting for the fact that retained customers expand and lost customers disappear.

The cheats Roberge calls out:

- **Using revenue instead of gross profit.** SaaS gross margins vary widely (70 to 90%); using revenue inflates LTV by 10-40%.
- **Ignoring discount rates.** $100K of revenue ten years from now is not worth $100K today.
- **Using initial ACV instead of cohort revenue curves.** Cohorts expand and contract; the LTV is the area under the cohort curve, not the first contract value times average tenure.

## Why CAC Payback Under 12 Months Matters

The capital-efficiency claim: a company with CAC payback under 12 months can fund its own growth from operations after the first round or two. A company with CAC payback at 24+ months requires continuous outside capital to keep growing. The first company controls its destiny; the second does not.

> CAC payback is not a vanity metric. It is the test of whether your sales motion is a business or a venture-funded charity.

The math compounds: every dollar recovered within 12 months can be reinvested in the next acquisition cohort within the same fiscal year. At 24-month payback, you are two years behind on every reinvestment cycle.

## Why NRR Above 110% Matters

If NRR is below 100%, you are running up the down escalator. New customers must replace lost revenue before they contribute to growth. The faster you grow, the bigger the absolute leak.

If NRR is above 110%, the installed base grows on its own. The new-customer engine compounds an already-growing baseline. The math gets easier every year.

> The best public SaaS companies operate at 120-130% NRR. The mediocre ones operate at 95-105%. The difference is not the sales motion; it is the product's expansion mechanics (seat-based pricing, usage-based pricing, multi-product land-and-expand).

## Where Founders Fudge The Numbers

Roberge's pattern catalog of the lies founders tell themselves and their boards:

- **The "blended" CAC** that combines paid and organic to mask the paid CAC deterioration.
- **The "logo retention" that hides the dollar shrinkage** when accounts stay but downgrade.
- **The "ACV" claim that inflates by counting multi-year prepay** as if it were annual revenue.
- **The "pipeline coverage" that includes deals from quarters ago** to make this quarter look healthier.
- **The "cohort LTV" that extrapolates from six months of data** to claim a five-year customer life.

Each cheat survives one round of investor diligence. None of them survives the operating reality at $50M ARR.

## The "Unit Economics At Scale" Test

Roberge's diagnostic question, used in his Stage 2 Capital investment process and his HBS teaching:

> What will your CAC look like at 3x today's spend, in your second-best channel, against more competitors?

The answer is rarely what current spreadsheets show. Channel saturation, competitor entry, and market exhaustion bend the curve. The companies that survive Series B model the bent curve and price accordingly. The companies that die straight-line the current numbers and bet that nothing changes.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **CAC payback under 12 months, LTV/CAC over 3:1, NRR over 110%. These are the unit-economics floors.**
- **Calculate CAC fully loaded. Sales leadership and ops are in. Customer success is out.**
- **Calculate LTV in gross profit, not revenue. Use cohort curves, not extrapolated point-in-time churn.**
- **NRR above 100% is the threshold of compounding growth. Below it, you run up the down escalator.**
- **Unit economics at scale, not today. Model the saturated channel, the bent curve, the competitive entry.**
- **If the four numbers do not converge at scale, no sales playbook fixes it. The problem is upstream in product, pricing, or ICP.**
