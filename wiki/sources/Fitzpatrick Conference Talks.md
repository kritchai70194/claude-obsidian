# Fitzpatrick Conference Talks

Distillation across Fitzpatrick's conference talks and podcast appearances: YC Startup School, Stanford eCorner, various Lean Startup and product-management conferences, and podcast interviews on Lenny's Podcast and the Indie Hackers podcast. The book gives the framework; the talks give the in-the-room Q&A where the framework gets stress-tested by founder questions about edge cases.

## Theme 1: Applying the Mom Test in Earliest-Stage Startups

The most-asked question at early-stage talks: "I haven't built anything yet. What do I ask?"

Fitzpatrick's answer is consistent across talks. When you have no product, you have the highest-leverage moment for Mom Test discipline. You cannot pitch because there is nothing to pitch. The entire conversation is by necessity about them. Use the moment.

Specific questions for pre-product interviews:

- "What does your day look like?" (build context)
- "Where in that day does this kind of problem show up?" (find the workflow slot)
- "How are you handling it now?" (existing workaround)
- "What's the most painful version of this problem you've had recently?" (recency + intensity)

The pre-product interview produces the highest signal-to-noise ratio because there is no product to project onto, no pitch to lean on, no idea-defense reflex to trigger. Founders who build before interviewing throw away this window permanently.

## Theme 2: Interviewing Reluctant or Non-Talkative Customers

Common Q&A: "My customers are technical / introverted / busy / not talkative. They give one-word answers. What do I do?"

The answer, across multiple talks: the customer is not the problem. The questions are still the problem. Specifically:

- Open-ended questions die on non-talkers. Switch to specific anchored questions: "Last time you did X, walk me through what happened next."
- Use silence aggressively. After they give a one-word answer, count to five. They will often expand.
- Switch from talking to observing. "Could you show me your screen while you do that?" extracts more signal from a non-talker than 30 minutes of questions.
- Bring artifacts. A screenshot, a workflow diagram, a printed mockup. Non-talkers respond to concrete things to point at.

## Theme 3: B2B Customer Interviews vs B2C

A recurring distinction in the enterprise-focused talks.

**B2C interviews** are about the individual's habits, preferences, and workarounds. The Mom Test pattern applies directly: ask about past behavior, observe current workflow, secure behavioral commitments.

**B2B interviews** add a layer: the buyer's behavior is constrained by organizational politics. The right questions extend:

- "Who else would have to approve this?"
- "What's the budget cycle for this kind of purchase?"
- "What happened the last time you tried to introduce a new tool?"
- "What would your boss say if you switched away from X?"

The B2B commitment hierarchy also adjusts. Time and reputation commitments matter more because money commitments often require a procurement process that takes months. An intro to the buyer's boss is often a stronger early signal than a verbal "we'd buy this."

## Theme 4: Cadence Between Interview and Product Iteration

A recurring tactical question: how fast should the team move from interview insight to product change?

Fitzpatrick's frame across talks: interview faster than you build, but synthesize before you build. Specifically:

- 5 to 10 interviews per learning loop, then pause.
- Synthesize for one to two days. Update hypotheses. Decide what to test next.
- Then either run another interview cohort with refined questions, or build the smallest possible thing that tests the hypothesis.
- Do not build for weeks based on one interview. Do not run 50 interviews without synthesizing.

The right tempo is interview-synthesize-interview-synthesize-build, not interview-build or interview-interview-interview-build.

## Theme 5: Why Most Startups Still Fail to Use This

The most candid theme across the later talks. The Mom Test has sold 200K+ copies. It is required reading in YC, Lean Startup, and most accelerator programs. And yet, in Fitzpatrick's consulting work, the majority of product teams he encounters still run customer interviews that violate every rule in the book.

His diagnosis, across multiple talks:

- **Reading is not practicing.** The patterns feel obvious in the book; they are surprisingly hard to execute live.
- **The pitch reflex is biological.** Founders have been trained to sell. The interview asks them to do the opposite. The reflex wins under conversational pressure.
- **Confirmation feels better than learning.** A bad interview that confirms the idea feels good. A good interview that disconfirms it feels bad. Founders unconsciously gravitate toward the first.
- **Synthesis is the unglamorous step.** Running interviews is energizing. Sitting in a room for two days re-listening to recordings and updating hypotheses is not. The latter is where the value comes from, and it is the first thing to get skipped.

> The book is widely read. The discipline is rarely practiced. The gap between those two is where most startups die without knowing why.

## Principles Cited From This Source

(Added to [[Operating Principles]] Customer Discovery section.)

- **Pre-product interviews are the highest-leverage interviews you will ever run.** No pitch reflex; no product projection.
- **For non-talkers, switch from asking to observing.** "Show me" extracts more signal than 30 minutes of questions.
- **B2B adds an organizational-politics layer to every interview.** Procurement cycle, approval chain, budget timing.
- **Interview-synthesize-interview-synthesize-build is the right tempo.** Not interview-build; not interview-interview-build.
