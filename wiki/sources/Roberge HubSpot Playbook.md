# Roberge HubSpot Playbook

Structural summary of Mark Roberge's HubSpot operating playbook as taught in his MIT Sloan and Harvard Business School courses since 2014, supplemented by the case study material he has published on the HubSpot scale-up. This is the operational substrate behind the abstractions in [[Roberge Sales Acceleration Formula]]: how the formula actually ran at the company that grew from $0 to $100M ARR in six years.

## The Zero-to-One Hundred Million Arc

Roberge joined HubSpot in 2007 as employee #4, the first sales hire. By 2013 the sales team was 450 people across multiple geographies, revenue had crossed $100M ARR, and the company was a year out from IPO. The arc breaks into phases, each with a different operating mode.

**Phase 1, $0 to $1M ARR (2007 to 2008).** Founder-led sales. Roberge and the founders (Brian Halligan, Dharmesh Shah) closed every deal personally. The product was unfinished; the buyer was undefined; the goal was to find the first 20 customers who would stay. No formula yet, just learning.

**Phase 2, $1M to $10M ARR (2008 to 2010).** Hire the first cohort of full-cycle reps (prospect and close, no SDR layer yet). Roberge codified the first version of the hiring rubric and the training curriculum. The product solidified around inbound marketing software for SMBs. Lead volume was still hand-cranked.

**Phase 3, $10M to $50M ARR (2010 to 2012).** Specialize the org. Split SDRs (prospecting and qualification) from AEs (closing). Tier the AEs by deal size: small-business AEs for the long tail, mid-market AEs for the larger contracts. Build the demand-gen engine: blog, free tools, marketing automation. Lead routing becomes a systems problem, not a tribal one.

**Phase 4, $50M to $100M+ ARR (2012 to 2013).** Geographic expansion (Dublin office for EMEA). Vertical specialization (agencies, ecommerce, SaaS). Manager training becomes the constraint, not rep training. The org's middle layer (front-line managers) determines whether the playbook survives scale.

## The Inbound Marketing Engine

HubSpot's structural advantage was that the company sold inbound marketing software using inbound marketing. The marketing org generated leads at a cost that outbound shops could not match. The sales org consumed those leads with a process built around the buyer's self-education stage at lead arrival.

The lead flow:

1. **Top of funnel**: blog posts (1,000+ over the period), free tools (Marketing Grader, Website Grader), social presence. Search-engine traffic at scale.
2. **Mid-funnel**: gated assets (ebooks, templates, webinars) capture the email and identify the buyer's role and intent.
3. **Lead scoring**: behavioral scoring (which assets did they consume, which pages did they visit) plus demographic scoring (company size, role, vertical) produces an MQL.
4. **SDR layer**: outbound on the highest-scored MQLs within minutes of the trigger. The "speed-to-lead" metric (time from MQL trigger to first SDR contact) was an obsessive operating metric.
5. **AE handoff**: SDR books the meeting, qualifies fit, hands to the AE for discovery and close.

The compound effect: by 2012, HubSpot was generating roughly 60,000 leads per month from its inbound machine. Outbound shops at similar revenue scale were paying 5-10x more per lead.

## Early SDR Hires

Roberge's early SDR hiring pattern broke convention. He hired recent graduates, often from non-sales backgrounds (liberal arts, engineering), and trained them ground-up. The conventional wisdom was to hire experienced inside-sales reps from competitors. Roberge's bet was that coachability mattered more than pattern-matched experience, especially for an unfamiliar product in a new category.

The result: SDR ramp time was four months, but retention to AE promotion at month 18 was significantly higher than the industry average. The SDR seat became a feeder for the AE bench. The internal pipeline reduced AE hiring cost.

## The Freemium-to-Paid Funnel

HubSpot launched freemium tools (Marketing Grader, Website Grader, eventually free CRM) as both lead-generation assets and product-led growth channels. The mechanics:

- Free tool produces value (the user gets a website audit, a marketing grade, a CRM)
- Free tool captures behavioral signal (the user's website, the user's CRM data, the user's marketing maturity)
- Behavioral signal feeds scoring
- High-scoring freemium users become MQLs into the sales motion
- Low-scoring freemium users stay free, generate referrals, and produce content (case studies, reviews) that feeds back into the marketing engine

Roberge teaches this as a structural decision: the freemium tier is a sales asset, not a product gimmick. The CAC reduction is the entire point.

## Manager Layer as the Scale Constraint

By 2012, Roberge identified the front-line manager layer as the binding constraint on quality at scale. Rep performance distributions narrowed when managers ran the playbook consistently and widened when managers improvised. The fix was to apply the four-step formula to managers themselves: hire managers from a rubric (typically promoted from AE bench, never lateral hires for the first management cohort), train them on a standardized curriculum, manage them with a consistent cadence, measure them on team-level metrics (team quota attainment, rep retention, ramp time).

> Reps who hit quota with a bad manager are an accident. Reps who miss quota with a good manager are a hiring or training problem upstream. The manager layer is the unit of analysis.

## Themes in the HBS Teaching Material

Roberge's HBS course material (the "Entrepreneurial Sales" case curriculum) emphasizes:

- **Diagnose before prescribe.** Most sales problems are misdiagnosed. Low close rate could be a qualification problem (wrong leads), a pricing problem (wrong package), a positioning problem (wrong narrative), or a hiring problem (wrong reps). Each diagnosis prescribes a different intervention.
- **Stage-appropriate motion.** The right sales motion at $1M ARR is wrong at $10M ARR. Founder-led works under $1M; specialization is needed past $10M; geographic and vertical layering past $50M.
- **Operator-first VC.** Stage 2 Capital (Roberge's fund) explicitly funds companies at the moment they need to move from founder-led to repeatable. The fund's thesis: GTM is the most underinvested operational discipline in venture-backed software.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Stage-appropriate sales motion: founder-led under $1M, generalist reps to $10M, specialized SDR/AE to $50M, geographic and vertical layering beyond.**
- **Inbound marketing is a structural CAC advantage where the market supports it.**
- **Freemium tools are sales assets, not product gimmicks.**
- **Hire SDRs for coachability, promote from within to AE.**
- **The front-line manager layer is the binding scale constraint past $50M ARR.**
- **Diagnose before prescribing. Low close rate has at least four possible upstream causes.**
