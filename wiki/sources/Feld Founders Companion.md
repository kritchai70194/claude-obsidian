# Feld Founders Companion

June 2018 post on feld.com announcing *Depression - A Founder's Companion*, a book project by Mahendra Ramsinghani (Feld's co-author on *Startup Boards*). Feld wrote the foreword and helped recruit contributors. The post matters less for its prose than for what it represents: the moment founder depression stopped being one VC's blog confession and became a category with a book, a survey instrument, and a peer-research base.

## What the Project Is

- **Author:** Mahendra Ramsinghani (co-author of *Startup Boards*; see [[Feld Startup Boards]]).
- **Foreword:** Brad Feld.
- **Method:** open call for stories, anecdotes, triggers, advice, poetry, and support from people who had struggled with depression. Anonymous Google Form survey, with optional contact for follow-up.
- **Audience:** primarily entrepreneurs, but designed to be accessible to a broader audience.

The book's structural choice is the load-bearing one: by mixing first-person stories with practical content, it treats depression the way [[Brad Feld]] and Jason Mendelson treated term sheets in *Venture Deals*. Make the previously-private knowledge legible. Give founders a reference manual.

## Frames

- **Timing.** Feld notes that "more people are talking openly about depression, especially among highly creative and successful people." Published in the wake of several high-profile deaths (Anthony Bourdain, Kate Spade, and earlier founder suicides). The cultural ceiling on the conversation had cracked.
- **Stance.** The founder community should act proactively rather than waiting for further tragedies. The proactive move is operational: write the book, run the survey, distribute the tools.
- **Reframe.** Depression is a "manageable challenge affecting accomplished people across industries, not a moral issue." The "moral issue" reading is the obstacle.
- **Continuity with the 2013 work.** Where [[Feld On Depression]] named the condition publicly, and [[Feld Bringing Depression Out of the Shadows]] operationalized episode-recognition, the *Founder's Companion* project formalized the field. It moved from "one person's blog" to "a peer-research base and a permanent reference."

## Why This Matters

This is the institutionalization point. Three things changed by 2018:

1. **Peer-research base.** The Ramsinghani survey produced anonymized data on triggers, recovery practices, and support structures. Founders dealing with their own first episode had something to reference besides a blog post.
2. **A permanent artifact.** A book sits on shelves, gets gifted, gets quoted in accelerator curricula, and gets returned to. A blog post fades down the feed.
3. **A second author.** Ramsinghani, not Feld, carrying the work forward. The signal: this is no longer one VC's idiosyncratic confession. It is a field with multiple practitioners.

## Frames Cited

- Depression is common-and-treatable infrastructure for the founder community, not an individual moral failing.
- Move from confession to institution: name the condition, then operationalize it, then formalize it as a reference field.
- Cultural ceiling on the conversation cracked post-Bourdain/Spade. Act in the window.
- Co-author and pass the work forward: a category needs multiple practitioners.
