# Duke Thinking in Bets

Annie Duke's canonical decision-quality book. Portfolio, February 2018. Subtitle: *Making Smarter Decisions When You Don't Have All the Facts*. The poker frame applied to life and business decisions: every choice under uncertainty is a bet, and the quality of the bet is separate from the quality of the result.

## Core Thesis

Most people judge decisions by outcomes. That is wrong. The quality of a decision is the quality of the reasoning available at the time it was made. The quality of an outcome is partly that, plus a large component of luck. Conflating them is the central error Duke names as "resulting", and it is the bias that does the most damage to operator learning.

> What makes a decision great is not that it has a great outcome. A great decision is the result of a good process.

## Key Extracts

### Resulting

The book opens with the Super Bowl XLIX call: Pete Carroll throws on the one-yard line, gets intercepted, loses. The press calls it the worst call in Super Bowl history. Duke shows that on the math (defensive formation, time on the clock, available downs, interception base rate) the call was defensible, perhaps correct. The result was bad. The decision was not the result.

> Resulting, our tendency to equate the quality of a decision with the quality of its outcome, is one of the most insidious habits of mind.

### Decisions Are Bets

Duke's reframe: every decision under uncertainty is a bet. You are choosing among options whose outcomes are probabilistic, and the alternative you did not pick had its own probabilistic distribution. To take the decision seriously, make the bet explicit.

> When we think probabilistically, we are less likely to use adverse results alone as proof that we made a decision error, because we recognize the possibility that the decision might have been good but luck and/or incomplete information (and a sample size of one) intervened.

The mechanical move: ask yourself "Wanna bet?" when stating a belief. The question forces calibration. If you would not actually bet at the odds your belief implies, your belief is weaker than your assertion.

### Confidence as a Percentage

Duke teaches operators to attach explicit probabilities to beliefs. "I am 70% confident this hire works out" is operative. "I think this hire works out" is not. The 70% can be reviewed later. The vague statement cannot.

The act of writing the number is most of the value. It surfaces the difference between "I believe this" and "I have evidence for this", a gap most people never notice.

### The Decision Journal

The applied protocol. Before the outcome is known, record:

1. The decision being made
2. The reasoning
3. The alternatives considered and rejected
4. The expected outcomes
5. The confidence level (as a percentage)
6. What would change your mind

Then, when the outcome arrives, review the entry. Did the reasoning hold? Was the confidence calibrated? Most operators discover they were over-confident on the wrong things and under-confident on the right things, which is the calibration data they did not have before.

### Truthseeking Pods

The social protocol. Find two or three people who will give you honest feedback about your decisions. Default-praise is useless. Default-critique is useful. The pod's job is to challenge the reasoning before you commit, and to evaluate the reasoning (not the result) after.

> The only way to develop expertise is to get high-quality feedback. The path of least resistance is the path of low-quality feedback, where everyone tells you what you want to hear.

### Hindsight Bias and Narrative

Duke devotes a chapter to the cognitive habits that make the past feel predictable. We reconstruct our prior beliefs to match the outcome. We tell stories that erase the alternative paths. The discipline is to evaluate the decision as it looked in the moment, with the information available then, not as it looks now with the result in hand.

### Reconnaissance on the Future

Run pre-mortems and back-casts. Imagine the project failed; write the post-mortem. Imagine it succeeded; write the success story. Both reveal assumptions that the forward planning hid. The mental time-travel is cheap and the surfacing is large.
