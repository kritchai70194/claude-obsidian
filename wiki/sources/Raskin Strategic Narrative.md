# Raskin Strategic Narrative

Andy Raskin's multi-essay series on Medium (2017-2020) systematizing the 5-part Strategic Narrative arc he first identified in the [[Raskin Zuora Pitch]] piece. Where the Zuora essay was a worked example, this series is the framework: each beat broken out with structural detail, the role of obstacle-naming, the argument for why product-led pitches fail, and the multi-use property of strategic narrative across investor, sales, recruiting, and internal alignment.

## The Framework Systematized

Raskin's 5-part arc is the same across the series. The contribution of this body of work is the operational detail.

### Beat 1: Name a Big Change in the World

Not a trend. Not a vibe. A specific, named shift the prospect can see when it is pointed at. Examples Raskin walks through across the series:

- Zuora: "the world is moving from one-time transactions to subscription relationships"
- Drift: "messaging is the new home page"
- Snowflake: "data is moving from on-premise warehouses to the cloud"
- Mulesoft: "every company is becoming a software company, and software companies require integration"

The test: can the buyer hear the named change and say "yes, that is true, and I had not put it that way before"? If the reaction is "duh" or "I do not buy that", the naming is off.

Raskin's structural rule: the world change must be both **provocative** AND **undeniable**. Too provocative and nobody believes it. Too undeniable and nobody cares. The right naming sits in a narrow zone.

### Beat 2: Show Winners and Losers

The stakes. In the new world, some companies thrive and others do not. Name the dynamic, ideally with specific examples. The Zuora deck named Netflix as a winner and Blockbuster as a loser. The Drift deck named companies that respond in real time vs companies stuck in form-and-funnel marketing.

The cognitive move: the buyer now asks which side they are on. The default is to want to be a winner.

### Beat 3: Paint the Promised Land

What does the future look like for the winners? Specific, vivid, attainable. Critically, the Promised Land is the buyer's outcome in their own terms, not a description of the product.

Raskin's emphasis: the Promised Land must be aspirational enough to be worth working toward, but concrete enough to be believable. Vague aspiration ("transform your business") fails. Specific outcome ("predict revenue 18 months out with 90% accuracy") succeeds.

### Beat 4: Identify Obstacles

Why is it hard to reach the Promised Land? What stops most people from getting there?

This is the beat most companies skip, and Raskin's most-cited claim across the series is that **obstacle-naming is the trust move**. When the seller names the obstacles the buyer is actually facing, the buyer's internal reaction is "this seller understands my world". When the seller skips obstacles and jumps straight from Promised Land to product, the buyer's reaction is "you do not get it".

Obstacles are almost always structural, not motivational. Not "buyers are afraid of change", but "the legacy ERP cannot model recurring revenue without rebuild" or "the existing marketing stack assumes asynchronous communication and breaks under real-time conversation".

### Beat 5: Position as the Guide with Evidence

Now, finally, the company appears. The buyer is the hero. The company is the guide with a plan and proof. Evidence is customer logos, specific outcomes, deployment data, named winners who already operate the new way.

Raskin draws the structural parallel to Joseph Campbell's monomyth and to Donald Miller's StoryBrand: the hero is the customer, the guide is the company, the magic gifts are the features. The product never becomes the hero of the story; it remains the tool the hero uses.

## Why Product-Led Pitches Fail

Raskin's argument, repeated across the series: the default enterprise pitch opens with the product. The buyer's mental response is to run a cost-benefit calculation against whatever they currently use. The pitch becomes a feature war.

The narrative pitch opens with the world. The buyer's mental response is to ask whether they agree with the world description. Once they agree, they have implicitly accepted the frame that something must change. The product is now the answer to a question they already asked.

The structural error of product-led pitches: they ask the buyer to evaluate a product before establishing why the product matters. Narrative pitches establish stakes first, product second.

## The Multi-Use Property

A single strategic narrative powers multiple surfaces:

- **Investor pitch**: same 5-part arc, emphasis on market size and stakes
- **Sales pitch**: same arc, emphasis on Promised Land and obstacles
- **Recruiting pitch**: same arc, emphasis on the world change and mission
- **Analyst briefing**: same arc, emphasis on category creation
- **Internal alignment**: same arc, emphasis on shared mission

Companies that maintain one narrative across all five surfaces compound trust. Companies that have five different stories fragment in the buyer's mind. Raskin recommends a single canonical narrative document maintained by the CEO and reused everywhere.

## The Most Common Failure Modes

Raskin names the recurring failures across the series:

- **Tagline confusion**: treating narrative as a clever phrase instead of a structured arc
- **Product-first opening**: jumping to the product before establishing the world
- **Soft world change**: naming something obvious that the buyer already accepts (no provocation, no movement)
- **Skipping obstacles**: jumping from Promised Land directly to product (loses trust)
- **Hero confusion**: making the company the hero instead of the guide (alienates the buyer)
- **Top-down narrative**: drafted by the CEO without customer-interview grounding; the world described is not the world the buyer recognizes

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **The 5-part arc is the structural backbone of every great pitch.** Change, Stakes, Promised Land, Obstacles, Evidence.
- **The world change must be provocative AND undeniable.** The narrow zone where the buyer says "yes, that is true, and I had not put it that way".
- **Obstacle-naming is the trust move.** Skipping it loses the buyer; doing it well signals you understand their world.
- **The product is the guide, not the hero.** The buyer is the hero of their own story.
- **One narrative, five surfaces.** Investor, sales, recruiting, analyst, internal. Same story, different emphasis.
