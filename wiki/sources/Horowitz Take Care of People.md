# Horowitz Take Care of People

Ben Horowitz's expanded thesis behind one of his most-quoted aphorisms: take care of the people, the products, and the profits, in that order. The full treatment appears in [[Horowitz Hard Things Book]] and in companion posts on the a16z blog. The order is load-bearing; reversing it is the most common executive mistake Horowitz has seen.

## The Full Thesis

> Take care of the people, the products, and the profits, in that order.

The ordering is not a slogan. Each element creates the conditions for the next. Take care of the people, they take care of the products. Take good products to market, the profits follow. Skip the first step and the second never happens; skip the first two and the third becomes extractive rather than productive.

The opposite ordering, the one Wall Street trains executives into, is profits, products, people. Maximize for the quarter. Cut costs. Treat people as a line item. Horowitz argues that this is precisely how good companies become bad companies. Once people stop trusting that the executive will take care of them, the culture dies on the inside before the company dies on the outside.

## Why People First

Three reasons:

**Culture is what survives the founder.** A founder cannot personally ship every product or close every deal. The culture is the persistent operating system that runs when the founder is not in the room. Culture is built by how people are treated, not by what the founder says about culture.

**Engaged people build better products.** The argument is mechanical, not sentimental. A person who believes the company will take care of them will tell the truth in meetings, push back on bad decisions, raise problems early, and invest extra effort in the work. A person who believes the company will discard them if convenient will do the minimum and hide the problems.

**Treating people well in good times is cheap. Treating them well in hard times is expensive but compounds.** The defining test is not how a company treats people during the boom. It is how it treats people during the layoff, the demotion, the pivot, the firing.

## The Layoff Case Study

Horowitz's signature operational example is the Loudcloud / Opsware pivot. Loudcloud IPO'd into the dot-com collapse, the managed-hosting market evaporated, and the company had to pivot into enterprise software (Opsware). The pivot required laying off 80 of 140 people.

Horowitz's account in [[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]: how the layoff was handled mattered more than the fact of the layoff. He insisted on doing the announcement personally, being honest about the reasons, treating the people leaving with respect, and being transparent with the survivors about what was changing. The remaining 60 stayed and built Opsware because the way the layoff was handled demonstrated that the people-first ordering was not a slogan.

Most executives in his position outsource the layoff communication, hide behind HR, and treat the survivors as the only audience that matters. Horowitz's argument: the audience is both groups, and the way you treat the people leaving is what the people staying actually see.

> If your employees believe you, they will tell you what you need to know to fix your problems. If your employees do not trust you, they will tell you what you want to hear.

## The Wall Street Failure Mode

Public companies face quarterly profit pressure. Boards, analysts, and the press push the CEO toward the profits-first ordering: cut costs, narrow scope, optimize margins.

Horowitz's counter: profits-first companies eat their own seed corn. Cost cuts come from the people line. People leave (the good ones first, because they have options). The remaining team works harder on a worse product. Quality declines. Customers notice. Revenue declines. The next quarter's profit pressure is worse.

The companies that maintain the people-first ordering during public-company pressure are the ones that compound. The companies that cave are the ones that flame out one or two cycles later.

> Take care of the people, the products, and the profits, in that order.

It is repeated because it is easy to forget. The pressure to reverse it never goes away.

## What "Take Care of People" Actually Means

Horowitz is precise that this is not "be nice to everyone." Taking care of people includes:

- Telling them the truth, even when the truth is bad.
- Firing low-performers fast, because low-performers degrade the experience of high-performers.
- Setting clear expectations and holding people accountable.
- Investing in training and development.
- Handling layoffs, demotions, and firings with dignity and clarity.
- Paying market or above for the people whose work matters.
- Defending the team from external pressure that is not their job to absorb.

> The first thing you have to understand is that taking care of people is not the same as making people happy.

The discipline is to do what is actually good for the people, not what makes them feel good in the short term.

## Why This Matters For This Brain

The principle is the operational expression of the founder-resilience pole. A wartime CEO under existential threat is most tempted to reverse the ordering: cut people to save profits, ship a worse product to keep customers, sacrifice culture for survival. Horowitz's argument: the wartime CEO who maintains the ordering is the one who comes out the other side with a company worth running. The one who reverses it survives the immediate threat and dies of cultural rot afterward.
