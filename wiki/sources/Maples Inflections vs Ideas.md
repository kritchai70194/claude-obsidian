# Maples Inflections vs Ideas

The Floodgate essay that draws the canonical Maples distinction, restated as Chapter 3 of [[Maples Pattern Breakers]]. Available on floodgate.com and widely cited as the cleanest short-form statement of the "why now" question asked with rigor. Operates as the inflection-side anchor of [[Secrets and Inflections]].

## The Distinction

> An idea is a proposed solution. An inflection is an empirical shift in the world that enables previously impossible solutions.

The essay opens with the distinction that most founder pitches collapse. The founder describes their idea (their proposed solution). The investor asks what makes it possible now. The founder either has an answer that is a real inflection (a specific, recent, verifiable shift in the world) or an answer that is not (a generic claim about industry trends, an aspiration about behavior change, a vibe).

The Maples claim: most founders pitch ideas without inflections, and ideas without inflections fail not because they are bad ideas but because the world's physics do not yet support them.

## What Counts as an Inflection

The essay catalogues the inflections that produced the largest venture outcomes of the past three decades:

- **Mobile broadband (2007-2010)** enabled Uber, Lyft, Instagram, Snapchat, WhatsApp.
- **The smartphone with front-facing camera (2010-2013)** enabled Snapchat, Instagram, TikTok, FaceTime-based services.
- **Cloud compute (2006-2012)** enabled the SaaS wave: Salesforce ecosystem, Workday, ServiceNow, Stripe.
- **Two-sided marketplace UX maturation (2010-2014)** enabled Airbnb, Uber, DoorDash, Instacart.
- **Open-source ML toolchains (2015-2018)** enabled the first ML application wave.
- **Transformer architecture and large language models (2018-2023)** enable the current AI application wave.
- **Regulatory shifts** (GLBA, HIPAA carve-outs, cannabis, fintech sandboxes) enable category creation.
- **Generational behavior shifts** (digital-native trust in strangers, comfort with publicness) enable categories that previous generations rejected.

The pattern: inflections are concrete, recent, and verifiable. "Everything is moving online" is not an inflection. "Mobile broadband penetration crossed 50% in 2010" is an inflection.

## Ideas Without Inflections Are Tar Pits

> Webvan and Pets.com were not bad ideas. They were ahead of their inflection.

The essay's most-cited section. The 1999 grocery delivery and pet supplies categories failed not because the ideas were bad, but because the supporting inflections (smartphone, last-mile logistics, payment infrastructure, generational comfort with online ordering) did not yet exist. Instacart, Chewy, and DoorDash later built the same categories successfully because the inflections had arrived.

The lesson for the seed investor: an idea that has been tried before and failed is not automatically disqualified. The relevant question is whether the inflection has changed since the previous attempt. If yes, the idea is investable. If no, it is a tar pit.

## The 80/20

> Most successful startups ride one to three inflections.

The empirical claim from the Floodgate portfolio plus broader venture data: breakout outcomes correlate strongly with the number of supporting inflections. Single-inflection startups can work but are fragile; the inflection can stall or reverse. Two-to-three-inflection startups are more durable because multiple supporting trends reinforce.

The diagnostic test: the founder should be able to name the inflections by hand, with specific dates and specific empirical evidence. A founder who cannot list the inflections is unlikely to be riding them deliberately.

## The "Why Now" Question, Asked With Rigor

The essay's practical contribution: it converts "why now" from a soft conversational prompt into a structured diagnostic.

The soft version: "Why now?" Answer: "Because AI is exploding." (Vague, unfalsifiable.)

The rigorous version, applied by Maples in pitch meetings:
1. What specific shift in the world makes your solution possible today that was not possible three years ago?
2. What is the empirical evidence that the shift has actually occurred? (Penetration numbers, behavior data, cost curves, regulatory changes.)
3. What is the timing window? When did the inflection start, and when will the market consensus catch up?
4. Are you the team best positioned to ride this specific inflection, and why?

A founder who can answer all four with specifics is riding a real inflection. A founder who answers with abstractions is not.

## Inflection Timing

> The inflection has to be recent enough that the market does not yet believe it, and old enough that it is real.

Too early: the inflection has not yet produced enough enabling conditions; the startup burns through capital waiting for the market to arrive. Too late: the inflection is consensus, the category is crowded, the returns compress.

The seed investor's sweet spot, per Maples: inflections that are 12-36 months old, with verifiable enabling conditions, that the consensus market has not yet processed as a category.
