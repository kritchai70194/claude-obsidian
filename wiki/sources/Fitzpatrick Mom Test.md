# Fitzpatrick Mom Test

The canonical book on customer interviews. Self-published in 2013, 200K+ copies sold by 2024, required reading inside YC, Lean Startup, and most product-management curricula. The full title is the thesis: *How to Talk to Customers and Learn If Your Business Is a Good Idea When Everyone Is Lying to You*.

## The Central Thesis

Most customer interviews produce false positives. The fix is not finding more honest customers. The fix is redesigning the questions so that even your mother (who loves you and wants to encourage you) cannot give you a false positive. It is called the Mom Test because the test target is your mom, the most biased possible interviewer. If a question survives the Mom Test, it works on anyone.

> If you ask your mom whether your business is a good idea, she'll say yes. That's not because she's a liar. It's because she loves you and wants to be supportive. The questions we ask are the problem, not the people we ask them of.

## The Three Rules

The book is organized around three rules that every interview question must satisfy.

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The moment you start describing the product, the conversation becomes about you, the interviewee becomes polite, and the data ends.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.** Past behavior is data. Future intentions are fiction.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** The founder talking is the founder learning nothing. Silence is uncomfortable; silence is also where the signal lives.

## The Bad-Question / Good-Question Pattern

The book's most-cited content is the table contrasting bad and good versions of common founder questions.

| Bad question | Why it fails | Good question |
|---|---|---|
| "Do you think it's a good idea?" | Asks for an opinion from a polite person | "What did you do the last time you had this problem?" |
| "Would you buy a product that does X?" | Hypothetical future commitment is free | "Can you show me how you currently solve this?" |
| "How much would you pay for it?" | No skin in the game; the number is invented | "How much budget do you have allocated to this problem today?" |
| "Would you use it?" | Hypothetical; politeness skews yes | "Why do you bother solving this at all?" |

The pattern: bad questions ask for opinions and predictions; good questions probe for specific past behavior and observed workflow.

## Commitment and Progress

A central chapter. Verbal interest is not signal. Behavioral commitment is. Three commitments scale, each one stronger than the previous:

1. **Time.** Will they meet with you again, do a follow-up call, send you their workflow documentation? Free verbally; costly in their calendar.
2. **Reputation.** Will they introduce you to other potential customers, give you the name of their boss, vouch for you internally? Costly because they're putting their relationships at risk.
3. **Money.** Will they pre-pay, sign a letter of intent, put down a deposit? The strongest possible signal short of the full purchase.

> Any meeting that doesn't end with a clear next step you've both committed to is a waste of time disguised as progress.

## Cadence: 5 to 10 Interviews per Learning Loop

The recommended interview cadence: do five to ten interviews, then pause and synthesize. Then update your hypotheses, then do the next batch. Doing 50 interviews before synthesizing is wasted; the early patterns inform later questions.

## Handling Compliments Mid-Interview

When a customer says "this is a great idea," the founder feels validated. This is the failure mode. The right response is to deflect:

- "Thanks. Can you tell me about the last time you actually had this problem?"
- Redirect every compliment back to specific past behavior.

Compliments are pleasantries. The pleasantry response of the trained interviewer is to say thank you and immediately ask about something concrete.

## Anchor First, Question Second

Before asking a question, share a specific anchor that grounds the conversation in their context. Without an anchor, the customer drifts into hypotheticals. The anchor can be: "Last week you mentioned you spend two hours a day on X. I want to understand what that looks like."

## The Signal Hierarchy

Implicit throughout the book, made explicit in later interviews: observed behavior > stated past behavior > stated hypothetical behavior > stated opinion. Most untrained interviews collect the bottom of the hierarchy and confuse it for the top.

## Principles Cited From This Source

(Added to [[Operating Principles]] Customer Discovery section.)

- **The fix is not finding honest customers; it's redesigning the questions.** The interview pattern, not the relationship.
- **Past behavior is data; future intentions are fiction.** Specific historical questions; never hypothetical ones.
- **Behavioral commitments are signal; verbal interest is noise.** Time, reputation, money, in that order of strength.
- **Compliments are pleasantries. Deflect to past behavior.** When the customer praises the idea, redirect to what they actually did last time.
- **5 to 10 interviews per learning loop, then synthesize.** Batch and update hypotheses before the next batch.
