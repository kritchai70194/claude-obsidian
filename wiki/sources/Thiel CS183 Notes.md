# Thiel CS183 Notes

Stanford CS183 "Startup", taught by Peter Thiel in Spring 2012. Blake Masters, then a Stanford Law student, posted session-by-session notes at blakemasters.com. The notes were widely read at the time and became the seed material for *Zero to One* (2014). The book is the polished distillation; the CS183 notes are the longer, looser, more conversational original.

20 sessions. The structure of *Zero to One* roughly mirrors the lecture sequence, but several sessions contain material that did not survive into the book.

## Why the Notes Still Matter

The book is tighter. The notes are richer. Several frames in the notes were softened or omitted in editing:

- **Founders as function**: Class 18 ("Founder as Victim, Founder as God") goes much deeper into the psychology of the founder archetype than the book's closing chapter.
- **Mafias as design**: Class 5 ("The Mechanics of Mafia") treats the PayPal Mafia as an engineered talent network, not an accident.
- **Last mover argument**: Class 6 ("Thiel's Law") has the full economic argument with the diagrams Thiel drew on the board; the book chapter is the executive summary.
- **Power law as portfolio construction**: Class 7 ("Follow the Money") is the most operational treatment of how Founders Fund actually thinks about concentration.

## Representative Sessions

### Class 1: The Challenge of the Future

Opens with the contrarian question and the 0-to-1 vs 1-to-n distinction. The framing of "globalization" (1-to-n) vs "technology" (0-to-1) is laid out as the central organizing axis for the entire course.

> Globalization without new technology is unsustainable.

The class argues that the modern era has been mostly 1-to-n (China industrialized using known patterns) and that 0-to-1 work has slowed in most domains except software. The premise of the course: students should aim for 0-to-1.

### Class 5: The Mechanics of Mafia

> The PayPal Mafia is the most successful, most networked group of people in the technology industry today.

The session breaks down how Thiel built the PayPal team: hire people who already know each other, prefer high-trust over high-credential, optimize for talent density over skill match. The "mafia" framing is taken seriously: a network of high-trust collaborators that persists beyond any single company. The argument is that the strongest founding teams are formed before the company starts.

The class also covers compensation theory: equity over salary, lock-in via vesting, the danger of cash-rich early employees.

### Class 6: Thiel's Law (Last Mover Advantage)

The original presentation of the last mover argument that became chapter 5 of *Zero to One*.

> A startup messed up at its foundation cannot be fixed.

Thiel's Law: a poorly conceived startup cannot be fixed by execution. The corollary: the early decisions about what market to enter, what monopoly to aim for, and what secret to act on determine the company's ceiling. Late-stage execution cannot rescue early-stage thesis errors.

The session also walks through the four characteristics of monopoly with examples that the book version compresses: Google as proprietary tech + scale + brand; Facebook as network effects; Apple as brand + scale; Amazon as scale + nascent network effects (Marketplace).

### Class 7: Follow the Money

The power law session. Thiel walks through Founders Fund's portfolio data and argues that the median venture investment loses money, the mean is positive, and the mode of returns is "near zero". Concentration in conviction bets is the only strategy that survives the math.

> The biggest secret in venture capital is that the best investment in a successful fund equals or outperforms the entire rest of the fund combined.

Implication for founders: do not diversify your career or your equity. The thing that works will dwarf everything else; act accordingly.

### Class 13: You Are Not a Lottery Ticket

The "definite vs indefinite optimism" session. The 2x2 of attitudes toward the future is presented at full length, with historical examples spanning a century. The argument: indefinite optimism is the dominant modern American attitude, and it is the wrong one for building things.

> A startup is the largest endeavor over which you can have definite mastery.

The session makes the founder argument the book softens: that founders should reject the lottery-ticket framing of their lives entirely. The future is not random; it is built. Building it requires planning, not optionality.

### Class 18: Founder as Victim, Founder as God

The most controversial session. The argument: founders are pulled into archetypal roles by their companies. The successful ones are mythologized (gods); the failed ones are scapegoated (victims). The line between greatness and madness is structural, not psychological.

The session is more uncomfortable than the book's closing chapter. It treats the founder's distortions of personality as functional consequences of the role, not as character traits. Steve Jobs and Howard Hughes are read at length.

## Concepts That Did Not Survive Editing

A few CS183 frames did not make the book and remain useful:

- **The Indefinite Long Tail**: a critique of "the long tail" as an indefinite-optimism artifact. Real businesses are won at the head, not the tail.
- **Engineering vs Sales as cultural opposition**: stronger in the CS183 version than the book; the argument that great companies need both but most cultures favor one and lose.
- **Cleantech as 0-to-1 failure**: Class 9 ("If You Build It, Will They Come?") uses cleantech (circa 2012) as a worked example of how 0-to-1 ambition fails when the 7 questions are not answered, especially distribution and durability.
