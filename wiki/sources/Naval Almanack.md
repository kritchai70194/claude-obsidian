# Naval Almanack

*The Almanack of Naval Ravikant: A Guide to Wealth and Happiness*, compiled and edited by Eric Jorgenson (2020). Freely available at navalmanack.com as PDF, EPUB, and web. A curated synthesis of a decade of Naval's tweets, essays, podcasts, and interviews, organized into two coherent books in one volume.

## Structure

Two parts:

**Part I, Wealth.** How to create durable, compounding economic freedom. Built around the 2018 "How to Get Rich" tweetstorm, expanded with supporting interviews and essays.

**Part II, Happiness.** How to be content, present, and free of the trap of perpetual wanting. Built around Naval's writing on desire, meditation, and the skill of contentment.

Both halves share the same underlying claim: most of life's durable outcomes come from compound interest on rare inputs, and most people compound the wrong things.

## Key Chapters

### Seek Wealth, Not Money or Status

The opening frame. Three different games, regularly confused:
- **Wealth** is the means of producing while you sleep. Positive-sum.
- **Money** is the transfer mechanism for wealth.
- **Status** is your rank in the social hierarchy. Zero-sum and addictive.

> Status games are zero-sum. Wealth-creation is positive-sum.

### Find and Build Specific Knowledge

Knowledge that cannot be trained because if it could, it would already be commoditized. Found by pursuing genuine curiosity. Recognizable because it feels like play to you and looks like work to others.

> Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity and passion rather than whatever is hot right now.

### Play Long-Term Games With Long-Term People

Compound interest applies to relationships, knowledge, and reputation just as much as to capital. The same people in repeated games produce disproportionate returns.

> All the returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest.

### Take On Accountability

Risk taken under your own name. Society rewards visible, named accountability with equity, responsibility, and leverage. Most people choose the salary because they cannot bear the named downside.

### Build or Buy Equity in a Business

The only path to durable wealth. Hourly wages cap at biological limits. Equity does not.

> You're not going to get rich renting out your time.

### Find a Position of Leverage

The multiplier. Four kinds: labor, capital, code, media. Code and media are permissionless.

> Fortunes require leverage. Business leverage comes from capital, people, and products with no marginal cost of replication (code and media).

### Prioritize and Focus

Naval's claim: most people work hard at the wrong thing. The leverage point is choosing what to work on, not how hard.

> If you can't decide, the answer is no.

### Find Work That Feels Like Play

The diagnostic for specific knowledge: it is the work that feels like play to you and looks like work to others. If you are forcing yourself to do it, it is not your specific knowledge.

### Build Mental Models

Charlie Munger's frame, adopted by Naval. Carry a latticework of cross-disciplinary models for decision-making under uncertainty. The point is not memorization; it is having multiple lenses available for any new situation.

### Learn To Love Reading

> Read what you love until you love to read.

The single most-recommended habit in the book. Not specific titles. Not curricula. Just: read voraciously across disciplines, follow curiosity, and trust that the compounding will produce judgment over time.

### Happiness Is a Skill

The Happiness half of the book opens here. Happiness is not a circumstance; it is the absence of desire. Built through meditation, reduction of wants, and presence.

> Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want.

## Direct Quotes

> The most important skill for getting rich is becoming a perpetual learner.

> Earn with your mind, not your time.

> Escape competition through authenticity. Basically, when you're competing with people, it's because you're copying them. It's because you're trying to do the same thing. But every human is different. Don't copy.

> Read what you love until you love to read.

> Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want.

> A rational person can find peace by cultivating indifference to things outside of their control.

## Five Cited Principles

(Added to [[Operating Principles]] Founder Resilience section.)

- **Wealth, money, and status are three different games.** Confusing them is the most common mistake. Most ambition is misdirected status-seeking.
- **Become a perpetual learner.** Reading across disciplines, followed for decades, produces judgment that cannot be shortcutted.
- **Escape competition through authenticity.** Doing what only you can do removes you from comparison entirely.
- **Earn with your mind, not your time.** Equity, ownership, and leverage scale; hourly work does not.
- **Happiness is a skill, not a circumstance.** Built through reducing desire, not increasing acquisition. Independent of the wealth game.

## Operating Implications for a Technical CEO

The book is the slow-form synthesis of the tweetstorm. Where the tweetstorm gives you the compressed sequence, the book gives you the supporting habits: read voraciously, practice meditation, cultivate indifference to outcomes outside your control, escape competition by being authentically yourself, and never confuse status with wealth.

The Happiness half is the part most operators skip and then regret. Naval's claim is that the wealth game without the happiness game produces ambitious unhappy people. Both games run in parallel for the rest of your life. Neither ends.
