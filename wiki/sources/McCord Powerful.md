# McCord Powerful

Patty McCord's 2018 book *Powerful, Building a Culture of Freedom and Responsibility* (Silicon Guild). The book-length unpacking of the Netflix culture model, written six years after McCord left the company. Eight chapters, each on a culture principle, structured around specific decisions made at Netflix between 1998 and 2012.

The book is the canonical long-form text on the Netflix model. The [[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]] is the slideware version; the [[McCord HBR Essays]] are the short-form versions. *Powerful* is where McCord shows her work.

## The Central Frame

> The greatest motivation is contributing to success. The most powerful people are those who don't need to be managed.

The book's thesis. People are at their best when they are doing meaningful work alongside other excellent people, trusted to make decisions, and held accountable to honest performance conversations. Everything else, policies, perks, performance review rubrics, is downstream noise.

## The Eight Principles

McCord structures the book around eight principles, each given a chapter with Netflix-specific stories.

1. **Treat People Like Adults.** ([[McCord HBR Essays]] restates this as the first lesson.) The policy stack at most companies is built for the worst employee anyone has ever met. The cost falls on the median. Replace policy with hiring rigor and contextual judgment.

2. **Communicate Constantly About the Challenge.** High context, low control. The executive team's job is to make sure every employee can articulate the company's current strategic problem in their own words. If they cannot, the executives have not done the work.

3. **Humans Hate Being Lied To and Spun.** Tell people the truth about performance, strategy, finances, customer reality. The cost of honest communication is short-term discomfort; the cost of avoiding it is the slow rot of trust.

4. **Debate Vigorously.** Disagreement is the input to good decisions. Surface it, make it explicit, decide, commit. Connects to [[Andy Grove]]'s pre-decision dissent / post-decision commitment frame.

5. **Build the Company Now That You Want to Be Then.** Do not let process pile up because you are too busy to remove it. Every quarter, ask which policy, meeting, or workflow can go away. The default direction of an organization is more process; counteracting that is a leadership job.

6. **The Person in the Job Has to Be the Person the Job Needs.** The Keeper Test framing. The team you needed at 20 people is not the team you need at 200. Recognize the mismatch, have the honest conversation, run a generous off-ramp.

7. **Pay People What They Are Worth to You.** Top of market for top performers. Do not benchmark salaries against the median; benchmark against what a competitor would pay to hire your best person away. Pay that, or accept that you will lose them.

8. **The Art of Good Goodbyes.** The exit is part of the job. Severance, references, framing. Alumni done right become referrers; alumni done wrong become the loudest critics in the market.

## The Keeper Test

The book's most-cited operational practice. The annual performance review is replaced by a continuous question:

> If one of my people came to me and told me they were leaving for a similar job at another company, would I fight to keep them?

If yes, they are a Keeper. If no, they are a candidate for an honest conversation, generous severance, and a clean exit. The test is fast (a manager can run it in their head about every report), honest (it forces the calibration), and humane (the alternative is keeping someone in a role they are failing).

McCord is explicit that the Keeper Test is not a layoff list or a stack rank. It is a forcing function for the conversation managers are otherwise tempted to avoid.

## Why "Freedom and Responsibility" Cannot Be Copied Casually

McCord is direct that the Netflix model has been widely cherry-picked and widely misapplied. Companies adopt "no vacation policy" without adopting "fully formed adults" or "pay top of market." The result is predictable: a few employees abuse the absence of policy, leadership panics, and the policy returns. The model is a system; the components are non-decomposable.

The three preconditions:

1. **High hiring bar.** Without it, the absence of policy is a vulnerability.
2. **High context from leadership.** Without it, freedom becomes confusion.
3. **Willingness to part with people whose skills no longer match.** Without it, the bar drifts down over time and the model collapses from the inside.

## On Performance Reviews

> Annual performance reviews are like trying to run a major league sports team with one game per year.

The chapter on reviews is the book's bluntest section. Annual reviews calibrate the manager's memory of the last six weeks against a rubric designed by HR for the entire company. They produce ratings, not learning. They consume weeks of organizational time and change almost nothing about behavior.

The replacement is the continuous honest conversation, with the Keeper Test as the calibration anchor. McCord acknowledges this is harder for managers than the review; that is the point.

## On HR

McCord's case against HR-as-commonly-practiced is structural. Most HR departments exist to enforce policy compliance, minimize legal exposure, and run rituals like reviews and engagement surveys. They are organized to protect the company from employees. The Netflix model needs HR organized to amplify managers' ability to run high-performance teams.

The reframe: talent leaders should think like business people, not like benefits administrators. The right question is "what does this team need to win?" not "what does the policy say?"

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Hire fully formed adults; treat them as professionals.** The policy stack is a tax on the median for the misbehavior of the few.
- **The Keeper Test is the continuous calibration anchor; annual reviews are a substitute for the work, not the work.**
- **Freedom and responsibility is a system with three preconditions; cherry-picking the famous lines breaks it.**
- **Pay top of market for top performers; benchmark against what a competitor would pay to poach.**
- **The team you need at the next stage is not the team you have; recognize, converse, off-ramp generously.**
- **Build the company now that you want to be then; remove process continuously or it accumulates.**
