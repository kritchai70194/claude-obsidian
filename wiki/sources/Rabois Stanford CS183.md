# Rabois Stanford CS183

Stanford CS183B, "How to Start a Startup", Fall 2014, organized by Sam Altman as the broader successor to Peter Thiel's 2012 CS183 (which became *Zero to One*). Rabois gave two lectures: lecture 14 on hiring and executive operations, and lecture 19 on later-stage operating discipline. Notes published on the startupclass.samaltman.com site and widely reposted.

## Why This Source Matters

The two Rabois lectures are the densest single public exposition of his hiring rubric and operating philosophy. They predate the popular "founder mode" framing by a decade and contain the load-bearing version of the Barrel and Ammunition framework before it circulated as a tweet thread. The lecture format forces structure that interview clips lack.

## Lecture 14: Hiring

The thesis: hiring is the CEO's primary job until at least Series B, and probably longer. Most CEOs underestimate this because the work feels indirect; you spend a day on recruiting and the company does not visibly change. The compounding is invisible until it is the only thing that matters.

**The Barrel framework.** Every employee is either a Barrel or Ammunition. A Barrel takes a problem from idea to shipped: defines the work, recruits the team, manages the budget, owns the outcome. Ammunition executes within someone else's plan. Both are necessary at different ratios. The CEO must be deliberate about which they are hiring.

> Most companies fail because they hire too much ammunition and not enough barrels. The barrels are the rare ones. Once you find a barrel, give them more.

**The interview loop as measurement instrument.** Each interviewer picks one or two dimensions. The same questions go to every candidate. Scores roll up to a rubric, not a hallway conversation. The most common failure mode: every interviewer asks "do I like this person", and the loop becomes a five-vote popularity contest.

**The 90-day test.** Before extending an offer, write down the first 90 days: objectives, context, success criteria. Hand it to the candidate on day one. If they cannot deliver, the rubric was wrong. The new hire is not the unit of accountability; the rubric is.

**Bias against MBAs for operating roles.** Rabois is explicit that an MBA is trained to optimize within a plan someone else wrote. Barrels write their own plan. This is not a credential bias so much as a skill-mix bias.

## Lecture 19: Later-Stage Operations

The thesis: as the company grows, the CEO's job changes from doing the work to setting the standards that decide how the work gets done. Most founders do not stage-transition and the company stagnates.

**Founder mode (predates Chesky's term).** The CEO stays in the details on the dimensions that matter, even at scale. Hiring, product taste, key customer relationships. Delegate everything else.

**Executive recruiting.** Senior hires are different. Reference calls do most of the work. Rabois's heuristic: the people who refuse to give a candid reference are telling you the answer. Ask explicit ranking questions: "is this person in the top 10% you have worked with?"

**Speed as signal.** Take 24 hours to decide on a candidate when possible. Six-week interview loops tell candidates the company is slow. Tempo at hiring sets tempo at execution.

**Pay top of market.** Compensation is a small number relative to the output differential between great and average. Underpaying great hires is the most expensive false economy in startup operating.

## Notable Passages

On the categorization:

> When someone walks into your office, can they take this problem and own it end to end? If yes, you have a barrel. If no, you have ammunition. Both are valuable. Confusing them is fatal.

On hiring as the CEO's job:

> The CEO's job is hiring, fundraising, and setting strategy. In that order. If you are spending less than 25% of your time recruiting, you are doing it wrong.

On the cost of a wrong hire:

> One bad senior hire does not cost you their salary. It costs you the team that has to work around them, the calendar of every person who has to manage them, and the bar reset for everyone you hire after them.

## How the Two Lectures Connect

Lecture 14 is the selection function. Lecture 19 is the operating discipline that the selection enables. Without the right people, the discipline cannot be applied. Without the discipline, the right people leave. Rabois's view: most operating failures trace back to the selection function, not the discipline.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Each interviewer evaluates one or two dimensions deeply.**
- **Score on rubric, not feelings.**
- **Write 90-day success criteria before extending the offer.**
- **Recruiting is at least 25% of the CEO's time.**
- **Decide in 24 hours; the candidate is reading your tempo.**
- **Pay top of market; the cost gap is small, the output gap is huge.**
- **Bias against MBAs for operating roles; Barrels write their own plans.**
