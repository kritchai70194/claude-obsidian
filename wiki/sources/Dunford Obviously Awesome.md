# Dunford Obviously Awesome

*Obviously Awesome: How to Nail Product Positioning so Customers Get It, Buy It, Love It* by April Dunford, Ambient Press, May 2019. The canonical text on B2B positioning as strategic discipline, derived from Dunford's two decades of operator work at IBM Cognos and multiple acquired B2B SaaS companies, and her independent consulting practice at Ambient Strategy.

## Core Argument

> Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about.

Positioning is not a tagline, an elevator pitch, a product description, or a slogan. Those are downstream artefacts. Positioning is the strategic decision about which market category the product competes in, which competitive alternatives the product is best against, what unique attributes drive that advantage, what value those attributes deliver, and which customers care most.

The book's central claim: most B2B products are weakly positioned not because the team lacks creativity, but because the team never made the strategic decision. Default positioning emerges by accident from product roadmaps, founder bios, and competitor copy. Deliberate positioning emerges from a structured exercise.

## The 5 Components of Positioning

The structural backbone. Every Dunford engagement runs through these five in order.

1. **Competitive alternatives.** What customers would do if your product did not exist. Often a spreadsheet, a manual process, a hire, or doing nothing, not another vendor.
2. **Unique attributes.** Specific, demonstrable features or capabilities your product has that the alternatives lack.
3. **Value.** The benefit customers derive from those attributes. The bridge from "what it has" to "what it gives".
4. **Target customers who care most.** The narrowest defensible segment for whom that value is most acute.
5. **Market category.** The frame of reference the product asks to be compared in.

The ordering is load-bearing. Alternatives determine attributes; attributes determine value; value determines target customer; target customer determines category. Skipping the order produces positioning that does not connect.

## The 10-Step Repositioning Process

The applied workflow the book teaches. The skeleton:

1. Understand the customers who love your product
2. Form a positioning team
3. Align your positioning vocabulary and let go of your positioning baggage
4. List your true competitive alternatives
5. Isolate your unique attributes or features
6. Map the attributes to value themes
7. Determine who cares a lot
8. Find a market frame of reference that puts your strengths at the center
9. Layer on a trend (optional, used carefully)
10. Capture your positioning in a written document

The deliverable is a written internal positioning document, not a tagline. The tagline, deck, and pitch are downstream of the document.

## The 4 Styles of Positioning

The strategic options once the 5 components are clear.

- **Head-to-Head.** Compete directly in an established category by being demonstrably better on the buyer's existing criteria. Hardest mode; usually requires capital, time, and a clear feature edge.
- **Big Fish, Small Pond.** Win a defensible sub-segment of a large category. The category exists; you carve the corner where you are unambiguously best.
- **Reframe the Game.** Change the criteria the buyer uses to evaluate the category. Tableau against legacy BI. Slack against email.
- **Pioneer a Category.** Create a new market frame that did not previously exist. The most ambitious mode; requires sustained category-building work alongside product work.

Most B2B startups default to Head-to-Head and lose. Most successful repositionings move into one of the other three.

## On Trends

> A trend on its own does not position you. A trend layered onto strong positioning amplifies it. A trend used to replace positioning kills you.

Dunford is sharp on this. Layering AI, blockchain, or any current trend onto a product without first establishing positioning produces marketing that ages badly and resonates with nobody. The trend is a multiplier on existing positioning, not a substitute.

## On "Who Is This NOT For?"

The diagnostic question. If the team cannot answer "who is this not for?" in a single sentence, the team does not have positioning yet.

Negative positioning is more powerful than positive positioning. It prevents wasted sales time, qualifies prospects faster, and sharpens the value claim for the prospects who remain.
