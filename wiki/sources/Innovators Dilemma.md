# The Innovator's Dilemma (Christensen, 1997)

Clayton Christensen's 1997 book introducing the disruptive innovation framework. The concept is now industry vocabulary, often used loosely; Christensen's strict definition is narrower than the casual usage. Central to the strategy canon Patrick Collison endorses.

## Headline Takeaway

Why do well-managed incumbents lose to disruptors? Because the practices that made them successful (listening to customers, maximizing profit on existing lines, focusing on biggest markets) become counterproductive when disruptive technology appears. The dilemma is structural, not a failure of competence. Doing everything "right" by the playbook of the existing business actively prevents you from responding to disruption.

This complicates [[Bezos 2015 Letter|Bezos's customer obsession]]: customer-first does not mean current-customer-first to the exclusion of future customers.

## Sustaining vs Disruptive (the core distinction)

### Sustaining innovation

- Improves a product's performance on metrics mainstream customers care about
- Driven by listening to existing customers
- This is what incumbents excel at
- Most innovation effort in mature companies

### Disruptive innovation

- *Lower* performance on the metrics mainstream customers care about
- *Higher* performance on different metrics that don't initially matter to the mainstream
- Initially serves new or emerging markets that incumbents can ignore
- Eventually improves enough on the mainstream metrics to take the market

The technical structure: "Disruptive technology is usually built around proven technologies put together into a novel product architecture that offers the customer a new set of attributes never before available."

## Why Incumbents Lose (Christensen's four mechanisms)

- **Resource dependence:** current customers determine where resources flow, including innovation budget. Disruptive ideas can't compete for resources against sustaining ones in the same allocation process.
- **Small markets don't move the needle:** a niche market that could become huge doesn't look material when it's still small. Incumbents assign it low priority.
- **Disruptive futures are unknowable:** by definition, the market and use case for a disruptive technology are not yet legible. Traditional market analysis can't predict the trajectory.
- **Capabilities are constraints:** an organization's processes and values are tuned for the current business. Asking it to pursue a different business with different economics asks the impossible.

## The Strategic Response

Christensen's recommended mechanism for incumbents: create a separate organization with its own resources, metrics, and decision-making authority to pursue the disruptive technology. The standard organization will starve the disruption; a separate organization can grow it.

This is structurally similar to Amazon's [[Working Backwards Book|single-threaded leadership]] discipline applied to disruption: one team, one objective, separated from competing claims on resources.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Customer obsession has limits.** Important nuance on Bezos's frame. Current-customer obsession can blind you to future-customer needs.
- **Listen to non-customers.** What do the people NOT using your product use instead? That's where disruption usually lives.
- **Resource allocation is the failure mode.** Most disruptive ideas die because they can't get resources, not because they're wrong.
- **Separate the disruption from the core.** New principle worth considering. If the core business will starve the new effort, structurally separate them.
- **Sustaining/disruptive is a binary, not a spectrum.** A 10% improvement is sustaining. A 10x improvement on a different metric is disruptive. Don't conflate.
