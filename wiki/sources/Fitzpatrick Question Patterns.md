# Fitzpatrick Question Patterns

The question-pattern reference. Distilled from the first three chapters of [[Fitzpatrick Mom Test]] and the companion essays on the Mom Test blog. Where the book gives the philosophy, this distillation gives the operational question templates and the cognitive bias each one neutralizes.

## The Canonical Bad / Good Pattern Table

| Bad question | Bias it invites | Good question | Bias it neutralizes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Do you think it's a good idea?" | Social desirability bias; the polite yes | "What did you do the last time this problem came up?" | Anchors in specific past behavior |
| "Would you buy a product that does X?" | Hypothetical bias; future selves are generous | "How are you solving this today?" | Forces observation of current workflow |
| "How much would you pay for it?" | Numbers without skin are fiction | "What budget do you have allocated to this problem?" | Reveals real economic constraint |
| "Would you use it daily?" | Forecasting bias; people overestimate future engagement | "Walk me through the last time you tried to solve this." | Stays in the actual past |
| "Is this a problem for you?" | Politeness yes; problem inflation | "When was the last time it happened?" | Forces recency, exposes frequency |
| "Could you see yourself switching from X?" | Costs of switching are imagined; not felt | "What would have to be true for you to switch?" | Surfaces real switching costs |

The structural insight: bad questions invite the interviewee to predict a future, judge an idea, or volunteer an opinion. Good questions force the interviewee to describe what already happened.

## The Core Question Templates

Each template is paired with what bias it neutralizes and what it surfaces.

### "When was the last time you had this problem?"

Neutralizes: problem inflation. Polite interviewees will say "yes, that's a problem" even if it never actually happens. Forcing recency exposes frequency. If they can't remember the last time, the problem is theoretical.

Surfaces: real frequency, real recency, real emotional weight.

### "How are you solving it now?"

Neutralizes: the assumption that absence of your product means absence of a solution. Most problems already have a workaround. The workaround is your real competitor, and understanding it is the first step.

Surfaces: existing workflow, existing tools, existing budget allocations, willingness to switch.

### "What's frustrating about how you're solving it?"

Neutralizes: founder projection. The founder assumes the workaround is bad because the founder has built a better thing. The customer may not agree. Asking for frustration in their own words exposes the real pain.

Surfaces: actual pain points, in the customer's own language, with the customer's prioritization.

### "Why have you not solved it differently?"

Neutralizes: assumption that the customer has unlimited optimization energy. If the workaround is bad, why have they not already moved? The answer reveals constraints (budget, risk tolerance, integration cost, organizational politics) that determine whether your product can land.

Surfaces: switching cost, organizational constraint, hidden inertia.

### "What would make you switch?"

Neutralizes: hypothetical generosity. Slightly hypothetical, but anchored in their current situation, so usable. Pair with: "Have you ever switched away from something similar before? What triggered that?"

Surfaces: real trigger conditions, real decision criteria.

### "Why do you bother?"

Neutralizes: founder assumption that the problem is important to the customer. The customer may have learned to live with it. Asking why they bother forces them to articulate the motivation, which exposes whether the motivation is real or invented for the conversation.

Surfaces: underlying motivation, organizational mandate, personal incentive.

### "Can you show me?"

Neutralizes: stated behavior vs observed behavior gap. Observation is the highest signal in the hierarchy. Asking the customer to walk through their actual workflow on their actual screen exposes the gap between what they say they do and what they do.

Surfaces: actual workflow, friction points, tool stack, workaround complexity.

## The Anchor-First Discipline

Every question is more useful when prefixed with a specific anchor that grounds the conversation in their context.

Bad: "How do you handle customer support tickets?"
Good: "You mentioned last call that your team handles around 200 tickets a week. Walk me through what happens when the next one comes in."

The anchor prevents drift into generic answers. Without an anchor, the customer gives the textbook description; with an anchor, the customer describes what actually happens.

## The Talk-Less Discipline

A specific tactical rule from the companion essays: after asking a question, count to five silently before saying anything. Most untrained interviewers fill the silence and lose the deepest answer. The interviewee's first response is often surface; the real signal arrives in the second or third sentence, after a pause.

> The most valuable sentence in a customer interview is the one the customer says ten seconds after you thought they were done.

## Principles Cited From This Source

(Added to [[Operating Principles]] Customer Discovery section.)

- **Every question maps to a bias it neutralizes; if you can't name the bias, the question is probably bad.**
- **Anchor first, question second.** Ground the conversation in their specific context before asking.
- **Count to five after asking; the second sentence is where the signal lives.**
- **"Show me" beats "tell me" every time.** Observation beats self-report.
- **"Why do you bother?" is the highest-leverage diagnostic question.** It exposes whether motivation is real.
