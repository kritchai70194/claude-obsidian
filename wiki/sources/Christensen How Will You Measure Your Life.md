# Christensen How Will You Measure Your Life

The Harvard MBA graduation address Christensen gave to the Class of 2010, turned into an HBR essay, then a book. The unusual move: the operator who built the strategy canon turned the frames on his own life and asked his students to do the same. Written after Christensen's diagnosis with follicular lymphoma, which he believed would kill him (it did not; cancer killed him a decade later, in 2020).

## Provenance

- Author: Clayton M. Christensen (with James Allworth, Karen Dillon for the 2012 book)
- Year: 2010 (HBR essay), 2012 (book)
- Publisher: HBR (essay), HarperBusiness (book)

## The Central Move

The same frameworks that predict business success predict personal life success. Strategy emerges from resource allocation, not from the strategy document. Culture is what an organization does without thinking about it. Marginal-cost thinking is a useful tool that becomes a trap when applied to ethics. These claims hold at the firm level (in *The Innovator's Dilemma* and *The Innovator's Solution*) and at the personal level.

The address was prompted by Christensen's observation that his Harvard MBA classmates, twenty-five years out, were often professionally distinguished and personally unhappy. Many had divorced or were estranged from children. Several had served prison sentences. He set out to ask what explained the pattern.

## Strategy Emerges From Resource Allocation

> Your decisions about allocating your personal time, energy, and talent ultimately shape your life's strategy.

The frame from [[Christensen Innovators Solution]]: a company's stated strategy is whatever the strategy document says. A company's actual strategy is wherever its capital and management attention actually flow. The two diverge constantly, and where they diverge, the actual allocation wins.

Applied personally: where you spend time and attention is your real life strategy, regardless of what you say it is. Saying "family is most important to me" while spending eighty hours a week at work has already chosen the company over the family. The choice has been made through allocation, even if it has not been made consciously.

The implication: the only way to actually invest in a relationship, a craft, or a long-term goal is to allocate the time. Stated intent without allocated time does not produce the outcome.

## The Trap of Marginal-Cost Thinking

> It is easier to hold to your principles 100% of the time than it is 98% of the time.

Marginal-cost analysis is the right tool for many business decisions. When you have a piece of capacity you have already paid for, the marginal cost of using it for an additional unit is low; the marginal-cost decision is almost always to use it.

Applied to ethics, marginal-cost thinking produces the "just this once" failure mode. The argument is always: this one violation costs little; the long-term cost of the principle is much higher; therefore violate this once. The trap is that the principle is the thing being protected, and "just this once" destroys it. The full cost is not the one violation; it is the loss of the principle that prevented all future violations.

Christensen's example: Nick Leeson, the trader who destroyed Barings Bank. The first unauthorized trade was a "just this once" to cover a small loss. Each subsequent trade was another "just this once." The 100% rule was easier than the 98% rule; once Leeson allowed one exception, the rule was effectively gone.

The personal application: hold the line on the principle at 100%, because 98% is an unstable equilibrium that collapses into 0%.

## Culture Eats Strategy

The same observation from [[Christensen Innovators Solution]], applied personally. Culture is what an organization does without having to think about it. It answers questions before they are asked. A team with strong culture does not need to decide each new situation; the culture has already decided.

The family corollary: a family's "culture" is what the family does by default. It is built by repeated small choices that compound until they become invisible. If a family does not deliberately build the culture it wants, it gets the culture that emerged from its accumulated defaults, which usually optimize for nobody's actual values.

The prescriptive: deliberately design the culture you want for your family or organization, then enforce it through repetition until it becomes invisible. Once it is invisible, it scales without your continuous attention.

## Why Successful MBAs End Up Unhappy

Christensen's diagnosis: the most successful Harvard MBA classmates often over-applied corporate frames to family. The frames are not wrong for the corporate domain; they are wrong for the family domain.

Specific failure modes:

- **Optimizing for visible short-term returns.** A career produces visible feedback (promotions, raises, titles). A family produces invisible long-term returns. The MBA's instinct to optimize for the visible metric directs effort to the career.
- **Treating family as a fixed asset.** Spouses and children do not require active investment in the way a P&L does, so they get under-invested. By the time the under-investment is visible, the asset has decayed.
- **Marginal-cost thinking applied to "just one more late night at work."** The first violation costs little. The accumulation destroys the principle.
- **Outsourcing the wrong things.** Outsourcing parenting to nannies and schools transfers the highest-leverage work to people whose values are not your own.

The implication: the family requires the same allocation discipline as the career, with the recognition that the feedback loop is decades long instead of quarters long.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Strategy is what you actually allocate, not what you say.** Personally as well as at the firm.
- **Hold principles at 100%, not 98%.** Marginal-cost thinking applied to ethics is a trap.
- **Culture is what happens without thinking.** Deliberately design it or get the emergent default.
- **The visible short-term metric is the wrong target for long-term outcomes.** Especially in family and craft.
- **Active investment is required even when the asset looks stable.** Relationships decay without it.
