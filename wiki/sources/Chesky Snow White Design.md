# Chesky Snow White Design

The origin story of the design-as-strategy frame at Airbnb. In 2012, Brian Chesky watched a documentary about the making of Walt Disney's *Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs* (1937). Disney's team had storyboarded the entire film frame by frame before drawing a single piece of final animation. The storyboards forced the team to confront every emotional beat of the customer (audience) experience before committing resources to production.

Chesky hired Nick Sung, a former Pixar storyboard artist, to do the same for Airbnb. The project was named "Snow White" internally. Sung drew 45 frames covering the end-to-end experience of an Airbnb host and an Airbnb guest, from the first moment of intent through the post-stay reflection.

In the corpus as **C3**, the source-of-record for the design-as-strategy frame. Companion to [[Chesky 11-Star Experience]] (same operating logic, different tool).

## What The Storyboarding Revealed

The exercise produced three claims that reshaped Airbnb product priorities for years:

**Mobile was load-bearing in ways the team had underweighted.** Many of the 45 frames happened on mobile (the airport, the taxi, the front door, the kitchen). Pre-Snow White, Airbnb was a desktop-first company. Post-Snow White, mobile was the strategic priority for the next two product cycles.

**The moments of trust between strangers were the actual product.** Frames depicting the first meeting between host and guest, the handing-over of keys, the awkward arrival at a stranger's home revealed that Airbnb's job was not "rent a room" but "manage a trust transaction between strangers". This reframed everything from review design to host onboarding to the brand voice.

**The team had been optimizing the wrong fifteen frames.** Most engineering and product effort had been concentrated on a narrow slice of the journey (search, booking, payment). The storyboards revealed entire sequences (pre-arrival communication, the in-stay experience, the post-stay review) that had been under-invested. Resource reallocation followed.

## The Principle: Design Is How the Company Thinks

Chesky's framing in subsequent interviews (paraphrased across multiple sources):

> Design is not a department. Design is how the company thinks about the customer end-to-end. If you can't draw the experience, you don't understand it.

This is the operational meaning of "design the company like a product". The storyboard is not a deliverable for the design team. It is the artifact that forces every function (product, engineering, marketing, ops) to look at the same picture of the customer and confront where they are failing it.

## Why Storyboarding Beats the Alternatives

Most companies attempt to align around the customer experience using one of three tools: customer journey maps (PowerPoint), personas (lengthy documents), or user research summaries (slide decks). Chesky's claim from the Snow White project: all three fail because they are abstract. The storyboard is concrete. A specific person, in a specific moment, with a specific feeling. The team cannot hide behind generalizations.

Quoted from interviews:

> When you draw something, you have to make decisions. Is this person happy or anxious? Is it day or night? Are they alone or with someone? You can't draw "the user". You have to draw a person.

The forcing function is the specificity, not the artistry.

## The Method Travels

Post-Snow White, Airbnb institutionalized storyboarding as a design-review tool. Major product initiatives begin with a storyboard. The Olympics, Trips, Plus, Experiences (when launched) were all storyboarded before being built. The 2022 product re-architecture ("Airbnb Categories") used the same approach.

Other companies have adopted variations. The Pixar method (the "story trust" review structure where storyboards are critiqued before production) maps directly onto product review structure: review the artifact before you commit resources to building it.

## Connection to Founder Mode

Snow White is what Founder Mode looks like in the design domain. Chesky personally directed the project, sat with the storyboard artist, made specific frame-by-frame decisions, and used the result to redirect company resources. A manager-mode CEO would have approved a budget for "improve customer experience" and read the executive summary. The artifact would not exist.

The pattern is portable: founders find or design the artifact that forces specificity, then sit inside the artifact long enough that taste spreads.

- **Design is not a department; it is how the company thinks about the customer.** Storyboarding is the forcing function for end-to-end thinking.
- **Storyboard before you build.** The artifact forces specificity that PowerPoints and personas hide.
- **If you cannot draw the experience, you do not understand it.** Drawing forces decisions about emotion, context, and detail.
- **Resource allocation should follow the storyboard, not the org chart.** Most companies under-invest in entire sequences of the customer journey because no team owns them.
