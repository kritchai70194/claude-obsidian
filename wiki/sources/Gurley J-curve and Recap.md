# Gurley J-curve and Recap

Two Bill Gurley essays at abovethecrowd.com, treated as a single source because they are the same argument across two years.

1. *Investors Beware: Today's $100M+ Late-Stage Private Rounds Are Very Different from an IPO* (Feb 25, 2015): the burn-rate warning and the "dangerous seduction" of the late-stage J-curve.
2. *On the Road to Recap* (Apr 21, 2016): the unicorn-party diagnosis. The essay that named the structural problem with dirty terms at headline valuations.

Both predicted the 2022-2023 markdowns with uncomfortable specificity.

## The 2015 Essay: Investors Beware

The setup: 2014-2015 saw a wave of $100M+ private rounds at billion-dollar+ valuations, often led by hedge funds and mutual funds with no prior private-market experience. Founders treated these rounds as IPO substitutes. Gurley's claim: they were not. They were structurally worse.

> Late-stage private capital looks like an IPO. It is not an IPO. It is a private round with terms that protect the latest money at the expense of everyone earlier.

The mechanics Gurley flagged:

- **Ratchets** (the price adjusts down if the next round is lower; the last investor is made whole at the expense of founders and employees).
- **Participating preferred** (the late investor gets their money back AND their pro-rata share of the upside).
- **Multi-x liquidation preferences** (2x, 3x preferences mean the late investor takes 2-3x their money before common holders see a dollar).
- **IPO ratchets** (if the IPO prices below the last round, the late investor gets additional shares to bring them whole).

The asymmetry: the founder accepts these terms to preserve the headline valuation. The terms are invisible in the press release. At exit, the founder discovers the headline was never theirs.

> The dirty terms are the price you pay for the headline number. Most founders accepting them have not done the math.

## The 2016 Essay: On the Road to Recap

The diagnostic: many 2014-2016 unicorns were already underwater on a clean-share-class basis, and were heading for one of three outcomes.

1. **Down round** at an honest price.
2. **Recapitalization** that washes out earlier holders to reset the cap table.
3. **Acquisition** at a price below the last private round.

> A unicorn at $1B with dirty terms may actually be worth $300-700M to its common holders.

Gurley's three predictions for the asset class:

1. Most of the "unicorns" would not IPO at or above their last private round.
2. Many would recapitalize, washing out employees and early investors.
3. The whole private late-stage market would correct, with the largest losses borne by the funds with the least experience underwriting them.

All three came true. The 2022-2023 markdowns at Klarna, Instacart, Stripe (Series I down round), and dozens of others vindicated the call with specificity.

## The J-curve

The shared frame across both essays: late-stage growth at any cost produces a J-curve in unit economics. The company looks great because growth is great. Unit economics are negative because every transaction is subsidized. The next round becomes mandatory because burn outruns runway. Each successive round is at terms more punishing than the last. The trajectory ends at a recap or a shutdown, not at an IPO.

> The J-curve is seductive because the top of the curve looks like growth. It is actually burn.

The structural problem: at the level of individual companies, blitzscaling can be the right answer ([[Reid Hoffman]] in *Blitzscaling*). At the level of the late-stage capital market, an entire cohort blitzscaling simultaneously with no proven unit economics creates the unicorn party. The party ends.

## Reproduced Passages

> The very best companies are not raising at any cost. They are raising what they need and being disciplined about burn. The companies in trouble are the ones whose burn rate has run away from them and whose valuations cannot be justified at any sensible multiple.

> Burn rate is the company killer. Not competition. Not market. Burn rate. Founders who do not understand this end up in a position where the only money they can raise is the money that destroys them.

> Headline valuation is a press release. Real valuation is what comes out of the cap table at exit. They are not the same number and have never been.

> Customer concentration that looks like a moat is fragility waiting to surface. Whales eventually use their leverage.

## Why This Source Matters

The capital-strategy pole's central warning. Hoffman names when blitzscale is correct. Gurley names when blitzscale (or its imitation) becomes self-immolation. Both essays are the canonical reference for founders being offered late-stage rounds: the question is not "is the valuation high enough?" but "do I understand what I am giving up to get that headline number?"

The essays also matter as a vindicated forecast. Most predictions in venture do not age well. These did, with specificity, across multiple companies and a full market cycle.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Burn rate is the company killer.** Founders confuse high growth with sustainable growth and run out of credible next-round capacity.
- **Late-stage private capital removes optionality.** Each dirty round forecloses outcomes available at a lower honest price.
- **Headline valuation is not the real valuation when terms are dirty.** Many unicorns were 30-70% below their last round on a clean-share basis.
- **The J-curve is seductive because growth and burn look the same from outside.** Negative unit economics dressed up as scale.
- **Ratchets, participating preferred, multi-x preferences are the price of the headline.** Most founders accepting them have not done the math.
- **High growth is not the same as sustainable growth.** The most-load-bearing distinction in capital strategy.
