# Munger Poor Charlies Almanack

The compilation of Charlie Munger's major talks and letters, edited by Peter Kaufman. First edition Donning Co. 2005. The fourth edition (2023) was released for free at poorcharliesalmanack.com, making it the most accessible version of the canon. The book is the single best entry point to Munger's thinking because it bundles the eleven major talks (including the 1995 Harvard "Psychology of Human Misjudgment" speech) with the lattice essays and Munger's annotated reading list.

## Structure

The book is organized in four parts:

1. **A portrait of Charlie**, biographical and personal context.
2. **Mungerisms**, aphorisms and quotations indexed by topic.
3. **Eleven Talks**, the canonical speeches given 1986-2007.
4. **The Psychology of Human Misjudgment**, the 1995 Harvard speech in its 2005 revised form.

Plus Munger's personal reading list, which is itself a curriculum for the latticework.

## The Eleven Talks (most cited)

- **Talk One (1986)**: Harvard School Commencement (the precursor to the 2007 USC misery speech)
- **Talk Two (1994)**: "A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom as it Relates to Investment Management and Business" (USC Business School) — the foundational latticework lecture
- **Talk Four (1995)**: "Practical Thought About Practical Thought?" — the case study lecture (building a trillion-dollar business from $2 trillion)
- **Talk Eight (2003)**: "Academic Economics: Strengths and Faults After Considering Interdisciplinary Needs" — the critique of single-discipline thinking
- **Talk Eleven (2007)**: USC Law School commencement — the inversion masterclass, separately cited as [[Munger USC Misery]]

Plus the Psychology of Human Misjudgment speech, separately cited as [[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]].

## Key Concepts (in this book)

### The Latticework

The core thesis. Worldly wisdom requires holding **the big ideas from the big disciplines** in working memory and applying them in combination. Most academic specialists know one model and force every problem through it. The fix is the lattice.

> You must know the big ideas in the big disciplines and use them routinely, all of them, not just a few. Most people are trained in one model, economics, for example, and try to solve all problems in one way. You know the old saying: to the man with a hammer, the world looks like a nail. This is a dumb way of handling problems.

Disciplines Munger names as load-bearing: psychology (especially the 25 causes), economics, biology (especially evolution), physics (especially critical mass), mathematics (especially compound interest, permutations, combinations), accounting, history, statistics.

### Practical Thought About Practical Thought

The case-study lecture. Build a $2 trillion business from $2 trillion in 1884 dollars (Coca-Cola). The exercise: how would you reason through it using only the latticework? The answer applies psychology (mere-association, social proof), economics (scale, distribution), biology (taste-physiology), and mathematics (compound growth) simultaneously.

> Properly executed, this trade-marked beverage product, when it becomes old, would be associated in millions of human minds with a happy childhood and youth.

The Lollapalooza is implicit: multiple psychological and economic forces compounding in the same direction.

### Avoid Stupidity Over Seek Brilliance

The Berkshire investment philosophy in one sentence. The compounding only happens if you survive. The catastrophic loss ends the compounding. So the central discipline is avoiding the stupid move, not making the brilliant one.

> It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent.

### Academic Economics: The Critique

Munger's critique of economic departments that teach in isolation from psychology, history, and biology. The efficient-market hypothesis is the canonical example: it is true under certain conditions and disastrously wrong in others, and a one-model economist cannot tell the difference.

### The Reading Discipline

The input the lattice runs on.

> In my whole life, I have known no wise people (over a broad subject matter area) who didn't read all the time, none, zero. You'd be amazed at how much Warren reads, and at how much I read. My children laugh at me. They think I'm a book with a couple of legs sticking out.

The empirical claim: 500 pages a day across multiple disciplines is what produces the lattice.
