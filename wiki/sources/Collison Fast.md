# Collison, "Fast" Page

[[Patrick Collison]]'s ongoing list of historical projects completed in surprisingly short timeframes. The thesis is one sentence: ambitious work can move much faster than people assume, and the historical record proves it. The page consists almost entirely of dated examples; the argument is the list.

## Headline Takeaway

This isn't an argument made through prose. It's an argument made through 24 concrete data points. JavaScript took 10 days. Git took 17 days. Amazon Prime took 6 weeks. The Spirit of St. Louis: 60 days. Apollo 8: 134 days. Disneyland: 366 days. The Empire State Building: 410 days.

If "things take this long because they have to" were true, the list could not exist.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Why-now framing inverted.** Patrick implicitly asks: what's the analogous project today that *could* be done in 60 days if anyone tried? The list is a calibration tool for what "normal speed" actually allows.
- **Boring fundamentals.** Many of these projects succeeded because of relentless attention to schedule, coordination, and decision velocity, not technological breakthrough. The Pentagon was built in 491 days because that was the requirement, not because anyone invented new concrete.
- **Long-horizon over local maxima** (inverted). The list refutes the assumption that quality work always takes long. Long timelines can be a *symptom* of slow decision-making, not a requirement.
- **Demos over decks.** Patrick's argument format itself: data, not exhortation.

## Caveat Patrick Notes

The page closes by observing that most infrastructure entries are pre-1970, and surveys academic explanations for why modern project completion has slowed. The list isn't a triumphalist claim that anything can be done fast. It's a question: why can't more things be done fast now?
