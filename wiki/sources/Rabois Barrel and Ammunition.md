# Rabois Barrel and Ammunition

The single most-cited Rabois framework, distilled across interviews, Quora answers, podcast appearances, and Twitter threads. No single canonical essay exists; this entry synthesizes the recurring structure. First publicly articulated in the [[Rabois Stanford CS183]] lecture 14 in 2014, then refined across a decade of repetition.

## The Framework

Every person in a company is either a Barrel or Ammunition. The categorization is binary and load-bearing.

**A Barrel** takes a problem from idea to shipped. They define the work, recruit the team, manage the budget, own the outcome. They do not wait for a plan; they write the plan. When they encounter a constraint, they route around it. When they need a teammate, they go recruit one. Barrels run functions.

**Ammunition** executes tasks well within someone else's plan. They are competent, often excellent at the work they are given. They do not generate the work. They do not own outcomes. They do not recruit. When the plan changes, they wait for the new plan. Ammunition runs tasks.

Both categories are necessary. The mistake is not in hiring Ammunition; the mistake is failing to distinguish.

> The barrel of a gun is what's important. Ammunition without a barrel is worthless. A barrel without ammunition is still useful.

## Why Most Companies Misallocate

Three reasons.

**First, Ammunition is easier to recognize.** They interview well on technical questions. They have clean resumes. They answer questions cleanly. Barrels often interview unevenly because they are more comfortable owning problems than answering questions.

**Second, Ammunition is cheaper per unit of visible output.** A Barrel demands top-of-market comp, demands authority, demands a real problem. Ammunition takes what is offered. Companies under cost pressure default to Ammunition.

**Third, Ammunition does not threaten the existing org chart.** Barrels reshape the org around the problems they own. Existing managers feel that. The political path of least resistance is to hire Ammunition.

The compounding cost: a company that over-hires Ammunition cannot grow because there is no one to write the next plan. Every problem routes back to the founder. The founder becomes the bottleneck.

## How Barrels and Ammunition Recruit Differently

A Barrel hired into a function will go recruit other Barrels (and the Ammunition that Barrels need). They raise the bar of the function around them.

Ammunition hired into a function will hire more Ammunition. They are uncomfortable hiring people who might out-perform them. The bar of the function drifts down over time.

This is why the first hire in a new function is so load-bearing. A Barrel as the first hire seeds a strong function. Ammunition as the first hire seeds a weak one that is hard to repair later.

## Diagnostic Questions

Rabois's recurring diagnostic, articulated across interviews:

- Would this person take initiative without being asked?
- Do they ask for context, or wait for instruction?
- When they describe past work, do they describe what they owned, or what they were assigned?
- Could you give them a function and walk away for six months?
- Have they recruited people to follow them before?

A Barrel scores cleanly on all five. Ammunition typically scores cleanly on none.

## The Right Ratio at Different Stages

The ratio shifts with company stage.

**Seed and Series A.** Almost all Barrels. The company is too small to absorb Ammunition; every hire must be able to define their own scope.

**Series B and C.** Roughly half and half. Barrels run the functions; Ammunition fills them out.

**Scale.** Barrels at every leadership layer plus a leadership layer that hires Ammunition for execution. The Barrel-to-Ammunition ratio at scale is closer to one to five or one to ten, but the Barrels must still exist at the leaf-node leadership level. Lose them and the company calcifies.

The error is hiring the late-stage ratio early.

## Why the Framework Holds Up

The categorization compresses a noisy domain (hiring) into a single binary that maps onto org design, comp, recruiting strategy, and stage transitions. It survives because it predicts: companies that get the ratio right grow; companies that get it wrong stall. Rabois has watched the framework hold across PayPal, LinkedIn, Slide, Square, and a portfolio of dozens of companies at Founders Fund and Khosla.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Every employee is either a Barrel or Ammunition.**
- **The Barrel test: would this person take a function and run it end to end?**
- **The first hire in a new function determines the function's ceiling.**
- **Barrels recruit Barrels; Ammunition recruits Ammunition.**
- **The seed-stage ratio is almost all Barrels; the scale ratio is one Barrel to five-plus Ammunition; the error is using the scale ratio early.**
- **Pay top of market for Barrels; the cost gap is small, the output gap is huge.**
