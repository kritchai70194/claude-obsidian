# Lenny North Star Metric

Lenny's canonical essay on choosing a North Star Metric. Based on a survey of current and past employees at 40+ growth-stage companies. The most-cited NSM framework in modern product practice.

## Definition

> "The top-line metrics that all company priorities are aligned around."

A single metric. Not a dashboard. The strategy compressed into one number.

## The Central Thesis

> "Your North Star Metric is your strategy, and your strategy is your North Star Metric."

Reciprocal claim. The NSM is not a measurement tool; it IS the strategy. If you cannot articulate the strategy by pointing to one metric, you do not yet have a strategy.

## Six Types (from 40+ company survey)

1. **Revenue**, money generated. ~50% of companies use this. Lenny cautions against (see Common Mistakes).
2. **Customer Growth**, paying users. ~35%.
3. **Consumption Growth**, intensity of usage beyond visits.
4. **Engagement Growth**, active users. ~30%.
5. **Growth Efficiency**, spend vs make money. ~10%.
6. **User Experience**, enjoyability and ease of use.

## Examples by Business Model

| Model | Companies | Typical NSM Category |
|---|---|---|
| Marketplaces/Platforms | Airbnb (nights booked), Uber, Lyft, Twilio, Plaid | Consumption Growth |
| Paid-Growth | Blue Apron, Casper, Hims, Calm | Customer Growth |
| Freemium B2B | Coda, Slack, Airtable, Asana, Dropbox | Engagement Growth |
| UGC Subscription | Twitch, Loom | Engagement Growth |
| Ad-Driven | Facebook, Pinterest, Snap, Spotify | Engagement Growth |
| Consumer Subscription | Duolingo, Tinder, Strava, Patreon | Customer Growth |

## Jobs-to-Be-Done Framing

The NSM should answer: "What jobs are our users hiring our product to do?"

- Plaid: "link my bank account" → "bank accounts linked"
- Lyft: "get a quick ride" → "number of rides"

JTBD keeps the NSM customer-centered. "Monthly active users" is company-centered; "rides completed" is customer-centered.

## Common Mistakes

**Revenue as NSM (the most common error):**
- "It's spiky, and thus hard to make operational"
- "Can lead to suboptimal decisions, such as spending too much time optimizing pricing"
- "Can be uninspiring to the team"

Revenue is the OUTPUT; NSM should be the leading INPUT.

**Vanity metrics:** Total registered users, total downloads, total pageviews. These can rise even as the product fails.

**Composite metrics:** Made-up scores ("Engagement Index" = .3*DAU + .2*sessions). Not intuitive, not actionable, not honest.

## The Selection Question

> "Which metric, if it were to increase today, would most accelerate my business' flywheel?"

The answer is your NSM candidate. Then identify the input metrics that drive it, focus team activities on those inputs, tie OKRs to the NSM.
