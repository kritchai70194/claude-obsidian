# PG How to Start a Startup

Paul Graham's foundational 2005 essay on starting a startup. Pre-Y Combinator at publication; later became the conceptual seed for what YC would teach for the next two decades. The compressed answer to "what should I focus on when starting?"

## The Three Things

> "You need three things to create a successful startup: to start with good people, to make something customers actually want, and to spend as little money as possible."

People, product, money. The whole essay is elaboration on these three.

## Why Startups Fail

> "Most startups that fail do it because they fail at one of these."

The three failures mirror the three requirements:
1. Wrong people (bad cofounders, wrong hires)
2. Wrong product (build something nobody wants)
3. Spending too fast (burn through funding before learning what works)

## Cofounders

> "Ideally you want between two and four founders. It would be hard to start with just one... you don't want so many founders that the company starts to look like a group photo."

The animal test for technical cofounders:
> "Could you describe the person as an animal?"

Someone obsessively excellent at their craft. Not literal; a metaphor for the kind of relentless competence that survives the early years.

## Make Something People Want

> "There is no magically difficult step that requires brilliance to solve."

> "Look at something people are trying to do, and figure out how to do it in a way that doesn't suck."

The reframing of "innovation" as "remove the suck from things people are already trying to do". Cuts against the "find a breakthrough idea" myth.

## Spend Slowly

> "The slower you burn through your funding, the more time you have to learn."

The deep claim: money buys learning time. The point of being frugal is not asceticism; it is preserving the ability to iterate before running out.

## Early Operations

> "We were compelled by circumstances to grow slowly, and in retrospect it was a good thing. The founders all learned to do every job in the company."

The early constraints forced cross-functional learning. Each founder learned support, sales, ops, billing. This becomes durable advantage when the company scales: the leaders know what each function feels like from inside.

## What This Source Teaches

- Three things matter: good people, product people want, low spend.
- Failure modes mirror requirements: wrong people, wrong product, wrong burn rate.
- 2-4 cofounders; the "animal" test for technical cofounders.
- "Make something people want" = remove the suck from things they already try to do.
- Frugality buys learning time, the most important resource in the early stage.
- Founders learning every job is durable advantage, not a temporary inconvenience.
