# Feld VC Rights

Short 2012 blog post on feld.com. Title in full: "VC Rights: Up, Down, And Know What The Fuck Is Going On." Among the most-cited pieces in the Feld term-sheet canon. Companion frame to *Venture Deals* (the book builds the legal scaffolding around the same three rights). Reduces a term sheet to its load-bearing substance.

## The Frame

[[Brad Feld]] recounts a story from an HBS VC Alumni event where a veteran VC distilled what a venture investor actually needs out of a deal into three rights. Feld attributes the lineage through Fred Wilson to Wilson's mentor Milt Pappas, signaling that this is consensus wisdom among experienced investors, not idiosyncratic Feld.

> VCs only need three rights: Up, Down, and Know What The Fuck Is Going On.

The three rights, unpacked:

**Up (pro-rata rights).** When the company performs well, the VC wants the right to keep investing proportionally to maintain ownership. This protects the upside case.

**Down (liquidation preference).** When the company performs poorly, the VC wants priority on the exit waterfall: their money out first. This protects the downside case.

**Know What The Fuck Is Going On (board seat or equivalent).** Beyond the colorful language, this is situational awareness: a seat on the board, or strong information rights, so the investor can see operations and decisions as they happen rather than reconstructing them after the fact.

## Why This Matters

Term sheets are long and dense with jargon. The substantive negotiation, in Feld's framing, reduces to these three rights. Everything else is either:

- Standard machinery (vesting, anti-dilution math, registration rights) that has good and bad defaults.
- Lawyer-driven complexity that signals nothing about the investor.
- Pathology: terms that exceed Up/Down/KWTFIGO and are forecasting an adversarial relationship.

This is the load-bearing intuition pump behind the "clean term sheet vs. dirty term sheet" distinction that pervades [[Feld Startup Boards]] and *Venture Deals*. A clean term sheet gives the investor those three rights and not much more. A dirty term sheet piles on participating preferred, ratchets, multiple liquidation, control thresholds, and other devices designed for the failure scenario.

## Operational Implications

- **Read deal terms as a behavioral forecast.** If an investor is asking for substantially more than Up/Down/KWTFIGO, they are planning for an adversarial post-close relationship and will likely behave that way. The terms are a signal, not a tax.
- **Don't lose the substance in the jargon.** Founders should be able to map every clause back to one of the three rights or recognize it as deviation. If they cannot, they are negotiating noise.
- **Investors should remember the substance too.** Feld's note is also aimed at VCs: avoid the temptation to load up a term sheet just because lawyers will draft it. Trust matters more than control on a multi-year company-building horizon.

## Frames Cited

- The three load-bearing VC rights: Up, Down, KWTFIGO. (Pro-rata, liquidation preference, board/info rights.)
- Clean term sheet vs. dirty term sheet — pathological terms predict pathological investor relationships ([[Feld Startup Boards]], *Venture Deals*).
- Trust over control on long horizons.
- Lineage: Milt Pappas → Fred Wilson → Feld. The consensus position among experienced investors.
