# Dunford Sales Pitch Book

*Sales Pitch: How to Craft a Story to Stand Out and Win* by April Dunford, Ambient Press, October 2023. The follow-up to [[Dunford Obviously Awesome]]. Where the first book defines positioning, this book defines the downstream artefact: the actual narrative a salesperson uses in a discovery or demo call, and how that narrative is built from positioning rather than from product features.

## Core Argument

> A great sales pitch is not a polished demo. It is a story that helps the buyer understand the world, see why their current path is broken, and choose your category and approach as the obvious answer.

The book's premise: most B2B sales pitches are product walkthroughs. They lead with features, then claim differentiation, then ask for the close. Buyers tune out. The replacement structure leads with a market insight, then names competitive alternatives explicitly, then introduces the product as the obvious response to the insight.

The pitch is downstream of positioning. A clear positioning produces a clear pitch. A muddled positioning produces a pitch that is either feature soup or generic value claims. Fix positioning first.

## The 7-Part Sales Pitch Structure

The structural backbone of the book.

1. **Setup.** The market context. The change happening in the world that makes this category relevant now.
2. **Insight.** The non-obvious observation about the market or the buyer's problem that reframes how they should think about it. The highest-leverage move in the pitch.
3. **Alternatives.** Explicitly name what buyers usually do (vendors, spreadsheets, in-house builds, doing nothing) and the limitations of each. Naming alternatives is counterintuitive but earns trust.
4. **Perfect World.** Describe what the ideal solution would look like if it existed. Frame the criteria for "good" before introducing your product. This is where positioning does the work; the perfect-world criteria are the criteria on which your product wins.
5. **Introducing the Solution.** Now show the product. By this point in the pitch the buyer is primed to evaluate it on the right criteria.
6. **Special Sauce.** The unique attributes that drive the differentiation. Not the full feature list; the 2-3 attributes that make the value claim defensible.
7. **Proof.** Customer evidence. Case studies, metrics, named accounts. The minimum the buyer needs to believe the story.

## The Insight as Highest-Leverage Move

> If you can teach the buyer something they did not already know about their own market, you have already won most of the deal.

The "insight" step is the move most B2B pitches skip and the move with the highest return. The insight is a reframe: the way you have been thinking about this problem is incomplete, here is what is actually going on, here is why your existing approach falls short.

The insight is what differentiates a pitch that closes from a pitch that ends in "send me a follow-up". A buyer who learns something will return your calls. A buyer who watched a demo will not.

The insight is produced by positioning. It is the synthesis of "what change is happening in the market" + "what your competitive alternatives miss" + "what unique attributes your product has". You cannot bolt an insight onto a weak positioning; the insight is the surfacing of the positioning.

## On Naming Alternatives Explicitly

The counterintuitive move. Most sales teams refuse to name competitors in the pitch, fearing it gives the competitor airtime. Dunford's argument: the buyer already knows the competitors and is going to evaluate them whether you bring them up or not. Naming them yourself, fairly, with their real strengths, earns trust. Then naming their limitations against the perfect-world criteria carries weight.

> If you do not name the alternatives, the buyer assumes you either do not know them or are afraid of them. Both kill the deal.

## On "Perfect World" Before Product

The order is load-bearing. If you show the product first, the buyer evaluates it against whatever criteria they walked in with, which are usually the criteria of your strongest competitor.

If you establish the perfect-world criteria first, derived from your positioning, the buyer evaluates the product against your criteria, which are the criteria on which your product wins. This is not manipulation; it is teaching the buyer to evaluate the category correctly.

## Principles Cited From This Source

(For [[Operating Principles]] GTM/Sales section.)

- **The sales pitch is downstream of positioning.** Fix positioning first.
- **The insight is the highest-leverage move in the pitch.** Teach the buyer something they did not know about their market.
- **Name competitive alternatives explicitly and fairly.** Refusing to name them costs trust; naming them honestly earns it.
- **Establish perfect-world criteria before showing the product.** The buyer evaluates against whatever criteria you teach them.
- **Special sauce is 2-3 attributes, not the full feature list.** The pitch is not a product walkthrough.
- **A pitch that teaches closes; a pitch that demos follows up.** The buyer remembers the reframe, not the screenshots.
