# McCord Netflix Culture Deck

The 125-slide deck posted to SlideShare in August 2009 by Reed Hastings, co-authored with Patty McCord. Titled *Netflix Culture, Freedom & Responsibility*. Viewed more than 20 million times. Named by Sheryl Sandberg as "the most important document ever to come out of the Valley." The most-influential single artifact in the Silicon Valley culture-doc tradition; widely imitated, rarely understood.

## Provenance

- Authors: Reed Hastings + Patty McCord
- Year: 2009, August (first public version)
- Publisher: SlideShare (public document, free)
- Fidelity: extracts (deck is widely reproduced; key slides preserved verbatim)

## The Seven Aspects of Culture

The deck is structured around seven sections.

1. **Values are what we value.** Real values, not aspirational ones.
2. **High performance.** What being on a high-performance team looks like and what it does not.
3. **Freedom and responsibility.** The operating philosophy.
4. **Context, not control.** The management model.
5. **Highly aligned, loosely coupled.** The cross-team coordination model.
6. **Pay top of market.** The compensation philosophy.
7. **Promotions and development.** How careers work at Netflix.

Each section is a few slides, mostly text, often a direct sentence per slide. The deck format is misleading; the content is dense and operationalized.

## Real Values, Not Aspirational

The deck's first move is to reject corporate-poster values. The slide that landed hardest:

> Actual company values, as opposed to the nice-sounding values, are shown by who gets rewarded, promoted, or let go.

The Netflix values list, presented as nine items, was meant to describe actual employee behavior that Netflix rewarded:

1. Judgment
2. Communication
3. Impact
4. Curiosity
5. Innovation
6. Courage
7. Passion
8. Honesty
9. Selflessness

The deck is explicit that listing values is cheap; aligning rewards, promotions, and exits with them is the actual work.

## The Sports Team Metaphor

The deck's most-quoted reframe:

> We're a team, not a family.

Netflix is a pro sports team, not a family. Families are unconditional. Pro teams put the best players on the field for each game. The reframe is meant to dissolve the loyalty calculus that keeps mediocre performers in place. It is also the line most often quoted out of context to justify casual firing; in the deck, it sits alongside the Keeper Test and generous severance and only works as a system.

## The Keeper Test

The deck's operational anchor:

> Which of my people, if they told me they were leaving for a similar job at a peer company, would I fight hard to keep?

A Keeper is one the manager would fight to keep. Everyone else gets the honest conversation and a generous severance. The Keeper Test replaces the annual review as the primary calibration mechanism.

## Freedom and Responsibility

The deck's title and central claim. As companies grow they typically add more process to compensate for increasing complexity. The cost is the loss of the best people, who do not want to work inside process. Netflix's bet, articulated in the deck:

> Avoid chaos as you grow with high-performance people, not with rules.

The model is summarized in a single slide pair:

- Responsible people thrive on freedom, and are worthy of freedom.
- Our model is to increase employee freedom as we grow, rather than limit it, to continue to attract and nourish innovative people, so we have a better chance of long-term continued success.

The cost the deck does not hide: this only works with disciplined hiring and willingness to part with people who do not fit. Without both, freedom degenerates into chaos.

## Context, Not Control

The management model:

> The best managers figure out how to get great outcomes by setting the appropriate context, rather than by trying to control their people.

The deck is explicit that control is the wrong move except in specific cases (emergencies, junior employees still learning, work where errors are catastrophic). Default mode is context. Managers who default to control are not doing the harder, higher-leverage work of building shared understanding.

Connects directly to [[Andy Grove]]'s Task-Relevant Maturity frame: control vs context calibrates to where the report is in the learning curve, not to the manager's preference.

## Highly Aligned, Loosely Coupled

The cross-team model:

> Strategy and goals are clear, specific, and broadly understood.
> Team interactions are about strategy and goals, rather than tactics.
> Trust between groups on delivery and quality without checking on each other.

Highly aligned: leadership invests in making sure every team understands the strategic context in the same way. Loosely coupled: teams do not coordinate tactics with each other; they trust each other to deliver on commitments. The combination scales when alignment is real; it collapses when alignment is faked.

## Pay Top of Market

Three slides, all blunt:

- Pay top of market for each employee.
- Pay more than anyone else likely would.
- Pay more than the value of similar work elsewhere.

The deck rejects salary bands, internal-equity adjustments, and "tenure-based" comp. The benchmark is the offer a competitor would make to hire the person away today. Pay that, or accept the loss.

## The Rejection of Generic Perks

The deck rejects the perks-arms-race version of culture. No ping-pong tables, no free lunches, no "fun" budget. The investment goes into salary and the work. McCord and Hastings argue that the people Netflix wants do not need a slide in the office to do their best work; perks are a substitute for compensation and meaningful work, not a complement.

## Why Other Companies Misread the Deck

The deck's reception, addressed in the [[McCord Powerful]] book and [[McCord Conference Talks]], is that operators cherry-pick the famous lines ("we're a team, not a family", "no vacation policy") and ignore the system around them. The deck is a system: the values list, the Keeper Test, freedom and responsibility, pay top of market, willingness to part with people, sports team metaphor, and context-not-control are all mutually reinforcing. Removing any one undermines the others.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Values are what gets rewarded, promoted, and let go; lists are cheap, alignment is the work.**
- **Sports team, not family; the reframe dissolves loyalty calculus but only works inside the full system.**
- **Freedom and responsibility scales when hiring rigor and context are real.**
- **Context, not control; control is the exception not the default.**
- **Highly aligned, loosely coupled is the cross-team scaling pattern.**
- **Pay top of market; benchmark against a poach offer, not against internal bands.**
- **Reject the perks arms race; invest in pay and meaningful work instead.**
