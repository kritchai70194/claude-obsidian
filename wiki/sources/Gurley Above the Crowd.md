# Gurley Above the Crowd

Structural summary of the broader *Above the Crowd* archive at abovethecrowd.com, treated as a compendium source covering essays from 1996 through present that are not pulled out as individual sources elsewhere. Gurley has been writing publicly on venture, marketplaces, and capital cycles for nearly 30 years; this is the long tail.

## The Archive

*Above the Crowd* started in 1996 as Gurley's research note at CS First Boston and migrated to a personal blog when he moved into venture. The archive has roughly 100 essays. Most are between 1,500 and 5,000 words. The voice is consistent: data-driven, contrarian, willing to call specific companies and specific terms by name.

Below: the recurring topics across the archive that do not have their own dedicated source page.

## Recurring Topics

### The Rake Calculation

Gurley returns to take-rate math in dozens of essays. The headline number is rarely the operating number. Gross merchandise volume minus refunds minus chargebacks minus payment-processing fees minus subsidies = net rake. The net rake is what funds the business; the gross GMV is what funds the press release.

> A 10% take rate on $10B GMV is $1B. A 10% take rate on $10B GMV minus 4% in payment fees minus 2% in subsidies is $400M. These are different businesses.

### The Cost of Customer Concentration

A recurring warning across the archive: revenue concentration that looks like strength (a marquee logo, a whale account) is actually fragility. Whales eventually leverage their concentration to renegotiate. The "moat" of an anchor customer is the anchor customer's moat against you.

> Customer concentration above 20% in any single account is a structural vulnerability. The customer knows this. They will use it.

### The Moat Illusion

Gurley's running critique of "moats" cited at fundraising: most of them are growth-rate artifacts, not structural defensibility. A company growing at 200% a year looks defensible because no one is catching up. When growth slows, the absence of real defensibility shows up immediately.

> Network effects are claimed more often than they exist. Switching costs are claimed more often than they exist. Brand is claimed more often than it is real.

The framing here is the precursor to [[Hamilton Helmer]]'s 7 Powers, with which it shares the underlying skepticism: most "moats" do not survive structural scrutiny. Gurley names the disease; Helmer names the cure.

### Network Effects in Marketplaces

A recurring set of essays distinguishing the marketplaces that actually have network effects from the marketplaces that have growth-rate artifacts mistaken for network effects. The diagnostic: does competition get *harder* as you grow, or does it just get *bigger*? Real network effects make competition harder. Scale alone does not.

> The test for a network effect is whether the next competitor has a harder time entering than the previous one did. If not, you do not have a network effect. You have scale.

### The Dangerous Quality of Multiples

A recurring critique of valuation-by-multiple in early and growth-stage rounds. Multiples imply that the comparable companies are actually comparable. Marketplaces differ on 10 factors ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]]). Two marketplaces with the same GMV multiple can have radically different long-run trajectories.

> When you pay a 20x revenue multiple, you are not betting on the revenue. You are betting on the next round paying 25x. That is a different game.

### Analyst Expectations as Operating Constraint

Drawing on his sell-side analyst career, Gurley argues that analyst expectations become an operating constraint for public companies. The same companies that fundraised on "we will dominate the category" then run their quarters on "we will beat the consensus by a penny." The two postures are incompatible. Many post-IPO underperformers are companies that never reconciled them.

### Take Rate Sustainability (Reinforced)

Across the archive, the load-bearing claim from [[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]] gets restated and reinforced with new data: sustainable take rate sits in the low double digits. Anything materially higher invites disintermediation. The pattern holds across two decades of marketplaces.

## Why This Source Matters

The compendium source is the long memory of the capital-strategy pole. The individual essays ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]], [[Gurley J-curve and Recap]]) are the load-bearing arguments. This source is the broader pattern: 30 years of consistent reasoning, applied to dozens of cases, with the structural arguments holding across multiple market cycles.

The archive also matters as a methodological model. Gurley shows what a 30-year operator-investor blog looks like when the writer treats their public reasoning as part of the work. Most VCs do not write because they do not want to be wrong in public. Gurley wrote because he wanted to think clearly, and the public record is the discipline.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Net rake, not gross GMV, funds the business.** The headline number is rarely the operating number.
- **Customer concentration above ~20% is a structural vulnerability.** The whale's leverage compounds.
- **Most "moats" cited at fundraising are growth-rate artifacts.** When growth slows, the absence of defensibility shows up immediately.
- **The test for a network effect is whether competition gets harder, not just bigger.** Scale is not a network effect.
- **Multiples assume comparability that often does not exist.** A 20x revenue multiple is a bet on the next round, not on the revenue.
- **Take rate above the low double digits is the long-run vulnerability.** The pattern holds across two decades of marketplaces.
