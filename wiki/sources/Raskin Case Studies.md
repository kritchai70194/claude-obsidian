# Raskin Case Studies

Andy Raskin's collected case studies of strategic narrative redesigns at Drift, Salesforce, Mulesoft, Yammer, and others. Drawn from his Medium essays, consulting writeups, and talks. Each case follows the before-and-after pattern: what the pitch looked like as a product story, what it became as a strategic narrative, what changed in the commercial result.

## Drift: "Messaging Is the New Home Page"

**Before**: Drift's pitch was "live chat for your website, but better". A feature-pitch against Intercom and Olark. The buyer's mental response: "we already have chat", or "chat is a nice-to-have, not a budget priority".

**The narrative redesign**: Raskin worked with Drift co-founders David Cancel and Elias Torres to name the world change. The resulting frame was "messaging is the new home page". Buyers are no longer willing to fill out forms and wait for emails. They expect real-time conversation, the same way they communicate with friends. Companies that respond instantly win; companies stuck in form-and-funnel marketing lose.

**The 5-part arc**:
1. **Change**: messaging is replacing forms and email as the primary buyer-seller interface
2. **Stakes**: sellers who adapt capture buyers in the moment of intent; sellers who do not lose to faster competitors
3. **Promised Land**: real-time conversations replace forms; pipeline accelerates; the marketing-to-sales handoff disappears
4. **Obstacles**: legacy marketing automation systems were built for asynchronous communication and cannot handle real-time; sales teams are organized around lead routing, not conversation
5. **Evidence**: Drift's conversational marketing platform, customer outcomes (e.g., pipeline velocity improvements at named accounts)

**The commercial result**: Drift became the category-defining brand for conversational marketing. The frame was so successful that "conversational marketing" became the analyst category. Drift was acquired by Vista Equity Partners in 2021 at a reported $1B+ valuation.

## Salesforce: "The End of Software"

**Before**: Salesforce in its early years was pitched as "CRM in the cloud", a feature-pitch against Siebel and Oracle. Buyers compared on feature parity and price.

**The narrative redesign**: the canonical Salesforce strategic narrative was "the end of software". Marc Benioff branded this aggressively, including the No Software logo (a circle with "Software" struck through). Raskin retrospectively analyzes this as a textbook 5-part arc.

**The 5-part arc**:
1. **Change**: enterprise software is moving from on-premise installation to cloud delivery; the entire model of buying, deploying, and maintaining software is shifting
2. **Stakes**: companies that move to cloud are faster, cheaper, more agile; companies stuck on-premise pay for IT overhead and lose to faster competitors
3. **Promised Land**: software you log in to, no servers to maintain, instant updates, true SaaS economics
4. **Obstacles**: incumbent on-premise vendors have lock-in via deployment and customization; IT departments are organized around on-premise operations
5. **Evidence**: Salesforce as the proof of cloud-delivered enterprise software; the customer logos that already operate the new way

**The commercial result**: Salesforce became the category-defining cloud-software company. The narrative was so successful that "SaaS" became the dominant enterprise software model and Salesforce captured a generation of category-leadership.

## Mulesoft: "Every Company Is Becoming a Software Company"

**Before**: Mulesoft was pitched as "integration middleware", a feature-pitch against Informatica and IBM. The buyer's mental response: "we have an integration layer, why would we replace it".

**The narrative redesign**: the world change was that every company, regardless of industry, was being forced to become a software company. Banks ship apps; retailers ship apps; airlines ship apps. Software companies require integration because no single vendor provides every capability. Integration is now strategic, not a cost center.

**The 5-part arc**:
1. **Change**: every company is becoming a software company, by competitive necessity
2. **Stakes**: companies that build strong integration capability ship faster and capture digital revenue; companies that do not get out-shipped by digital natives
3. **Promised Land**: an API-led enterprise architecture where new digital products are composed in weeks, not years
4. **Obstacles**: legacy point-to-point integrations create brittle dependencies; existing middleware is too heavyweight for the velocity required
5. **Evidence**: Mulesoft's Anypoint Platform; customer outcomes across financial services, retail, healthcare

**The commercial result**: Mulesoft IPO'd in 2017 and was acquired by Salesforce in 2018 for $6.5B, one of the largest enterprise software acquisitions of the period.

## Yammer: "Enterprise Social"

**Before**: Yammer was pitched as "Facebook for the enterprise", a feature comparison that triggered immediate skepticism from IT buyers (security, governance, productivity concerns).

**The narrative redesign**: the world change was that work communication was moving from email (asynchronous, hierarchical, slow) to social (real-time, networked, transparent). Knowledge workers were already living in social tools personally; the workplace was the lagging context.

**The 5-part arc**:
1. **Change**: work communication is moving from email to social; the consumer pattern is invading the enterprise
2. **Stakes**: companies that adopt enterprise social move information faster and decisions get made closer to the customer; email-bound companies move slower
3. **Promised Land**: a transparent, networked workplace where knowledge flows, not where it gets trapped in CC chains
4. **Obstacles**: IT governance built for email; cultural resistance from hierarchical management; lack of a tool designed for the enterprise context
5. **Evidence**: Yammer's enterprise-grade social platform; customer outcomes at scale

**The commercial result**: Yammer was acquired by Microsoft in 2012 for $1.2B and became the foundation of what later evolved into Microsoft Teams.

## The Common Pattern Across All Four

Raskin's recurring observation: every successful narrative redesign follows the same structural moves.

1. **The product framing is symptom-first, not strategy-first.** The breakthrough pitch always names a world change that makes the product category necessary, not just useful.
2. **The buyer's frame shifts from "do I want this feature" to "which side of the world change am I on".** This is the cognitive shift that drives enterprise budget allocation.
3. **The product appears late in the deck, after the world is established.** In every case study, the company itself shows up in beat 4 or 5, never in beat 1.
4. **Category creation is a side effect of strategic narrative, not a separate effort.** Drift created "conversational marketing"; Salesforce created "SaaS"; Mulesoft created "API-led integration"; Yammer created "enterprise social". The narrative was the category.
5. **Obstacle-naming is the move that builds trust.** Each redesign names structural obstacles the buyer recognizes from their own world.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Every breakthrough enterprise pitch named a world change first.** The product framing is downstream of the world framing.
- **Category creation is a side effect of strategic narrative.** Naming the world change well names the category.
- **The buyer's decision frame shifts from feature comparison to side-of-history.** This is the cognitive lever.
- **The product appears late, not early.** Beat 4 or 5, never beat 1.
- **Obstacle-naming makes the seller credible.** Skipping it loses the buyer.
