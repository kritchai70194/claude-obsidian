# Blank Customer Development Blog

steveblank.com, running since 2009 with more than 1,500 published posts. Blank has used the blog as his primary surface for refining and extending the Customer Development methodology in the years since the *Owner's Manual*. The blog is where new pivot types are named, where the methodology is updated for new contexts (deep tech, defense, biotech, government), and where the most-quoted essays of the modern lean-startup canon were published.

This page covers a curated set of the most-cited essays.

## "The Most Important Slide in Your Pitch Deck"

The argument: every pitch deck has one slide that does the actual work. It is not the team slide, not the financials, not the market-size slide. It is the slide that shows the problem the customer has and the evidence that it is real.

> If you cannot show me, with evidence, that customers have the problem you are solving, every other slide is fiction.

The operational implication: the founder who has done Customer Discovery has this slide. The founder who has not, does not. Investors who know this trick read for that slide first.

## "Why the Lean Start-Up Changes Everything" (HBR, May 2013)

The Harvard Business Review essay that brought Customer Development to mainstream business audiences. Co-written with the HBR editorial team and structured to translate the methodology for managers and corporate-innovation leaders.

Three theses:

1. **Founders embrace failure as part of the search process.** A failed hypothesis is a successful experiment if it teaches something.
2. **The business plan is an artifact for execution, not for search.** Search-phase organizations use a business model canvas, not a plan.
3. **Get out of the building.** Restated for an HBR audience: customer learning happens in the customer's environment, not in management consulting.

The essay reframed corporate-innovation programs across F500 companies in the following decade. The methodology that had been a startup framework became an established management discipline.

## "Customer Development Manifesto"

Fourteen principles for Customer Development, posted as a single condensed list. The most-cited:

- There are no facts inside your building, so get outside.
- Pair Customer Development with Agile Development.
- Failure is an integral part of the search for the model.
- Make continuous iterations and pivots.
- No business plan survives first contact with customers.
- Not all startups are alike (B2B vs B2C, web/mobile vs physical).
- Startup metrics differ from those in large companies.
- Agreement among founders is required before pivoting.
- Customer Development requires a different leadership style.

The list is a working summary. Founders who internalize fourteen items can run Customer Development from memory.

## "The Pivot, Not Forced Family Planning"

The essay that pushed back against the misuse of the word "pivot" in startup culture circa 2010-2012. Blank's argument:

> A pivot is a substantive change to one component of the business model based on validated evidence. It is not a panic response to a bad quarter. It is not a marketing rebrand. It is not a strategic shift dressed up in startup vocabulary.

The corrective targeted founders who claimed to have pivoted three times in a quarter without having tested any hypothesis to falsification in between. The essay named this pattern as performative pivoting and identified it as a tell for founders who had not actually done Customer Development.

## "Why Founders Should Know How to Code"

A more recent essay arguing that the founder who can code is structurally faster at Customer Development. The argument is not that every founder must code, but that the founder who can build a prototype themselves can test more hypotheses per week than one who must ask an engineer.

The frame: Customer Development cadence is bounded by the slowest of (interview customers) and (modify the product). For technical founders, the bound is the first. For non-technical founders, the bound is the second. The first is harder to remove.

## Themes Across the Blog

Reading the blog longitudinally, three meta-themes emerge:

1. **Customer Development was designed for one context (tech startups) and has generalized to others** (defense, government, biotech, climate). The Hacking for Defense / Hacking for Diplomacy programs are the formalized version.
2. **The methodology has not changed structurally since 2005**, but the tools have. The Business Model Canvas, MVPs, the Lean LaunchPad teaching format, the I-Corps curriculum are all post-2005 implementations of the same underlying frame.
3. **Most startups still skip Discovery.** Blank's most repeated frustration across the blog is that founders read the books, attend the courses, and then build a product without interviewing customers. The frame is named, but the discipline is rare.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **The most important slide in your pitch deck is the problem evidence.** Without it, the deck is fiction.
- **A pivot is a substantive change to one hypothesis, not a panic response or a rebrand.** Performative pivoting is a tell.
- **Failure is an integral part of search.** A falsified hypothesis is a successful experiment.
- **Customer Development requires a different leadership style than execution.** The mode is search, not control.
- **Technical founders run Customer Development faster.** The bound is hypothesis-to-test cycle time.
