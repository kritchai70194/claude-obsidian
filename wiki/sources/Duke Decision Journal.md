# Duke Decision Journal

The applied decision-journal methodology, distilled from Annie Duke's blog at annieduke.com, the How to Decide curriculum, and various essays and workshops. The protocol *Thinking in Bets* and *Quit* both reference but never lay out in single-page form.

## Why a Journal At All

The case for the journal rests on a single observation: human memory is unreliable about its own prior beliefs. After an outcome is known, we reconstruct what we used to think to match what actually happened. This is hindsight bias plus narrative reconstruction working in concert, and it makes it nearly impossible to learn from decisions without an external record.

The journal fixes the record. The reasoning is written down before the outcome is known. The future you, who has the result, can read what the past you, who did not, actually thought. The gap between those two is the calibration data that turns experience into expertise.

## What to Record

Duke's protocol for a single journal entry contains six fields. Each field exists because a specific cognitive failure mode lives there.

### 1. The Decision

State what is being decided in one sentence. "I will hire X for the head-of-product role." "We will pivot from B2B to B2C by Q3." "I will turn down the funding offer from Firm Y."

The discipline of the one-sentence formulation forces clarity. If you cannot state the decision in a sentence, you do not yet know what the decision is.

### 2. The Reasoning

Why is this the chosen option? What evidence supports it? What model of the world is in play? Three to ten lines, no more. The point is not exhaustiveness; the point is to capture the actual reasoning, not the polished retrospective version.

### 3. The Alternatives Considered

What other options were on the table, and why were they rejected? Most operators discover they were really choosing between two options and the rest were window dressing. Naming the alternatives surfaces this.

### 4. The Expected Outcomes

What do you think will happen? Range, not point estimate. "Revenue between $400K and $1.2M in twelve months, most likely around $700K." The range matters because the point estimate gives an illusion of precision the reasoning does not support.

### 5. The Confidence Level

A percentage. "I am 70% confident this hire works out." "I am 40% confident the pivot grows revenue 2x in a year." The percentage is the most uncomfortable field for most operators, which is exactly why it is the most important. Writing the number forces calibration where vague feelings used to live.

### 6. What Would Change Your Mind

In advance, name the signals that would cause you to change the decision. "If churn rises above 8% in the first quarter, I would reconsider." "If three of the next five customer interviews say X, I would pivot." This field is the kill-criteria field from *Quit*, ported into the journal protocol.

## When to Review

Three review cadences.

**Short-cycle review (weekly).** Scan the journal for decisions where the outcome has arrived. For each, ask: was the reasoning sound? Was the confidence calibrated? Did the change-your-mind signal trigger? Note any pattern.

**Medium-cycle review (quarterly).** Aggregate by decision type. Are you well-calibrated on hires? On pricing? On product bets? Most operators find they are good at some categories and systematically off in others. The pattern is the value.

**Long-cycle review (annual).** Look at the highest-stakes decisions of the year. Were they decided well? What would you change about the process, not the result? The honest answers compound.

## How to Score Yourself

Duke is emphatic: score the decision, not the outcome. A good decision with a bad outcome is still a good decision. A bad decision with a good outcome is still a bad decision. Resulting is the bias the journal is designed to defeat.

Practical heuristics:

- Did I consider the alternatives honestly?
- Did I assign explicit probability?
- Was my reasoning consistent with my prior reasoning on similar decisions?
- Did I update on new information when it arrived, or did I anchor on the original commitment?
- Was the change-your-mind condition specified before the decision was made?

A "yes" on these scores is a good decision regardless of the outcome.

## Team Versions

Duke's organizational adaptation. The team journal records decisions made by the team, with the reasoning, the dissenters, the alternatives, the confidence levels. The discipline forces the team to surface disagreement before commitment rather than after.

The most-cited benefit: when the outcome arrives, the team can identify whether the decision was good or bad without needing to relitigate. Most team disputes after bad outcomes are arguments about "you should have known", which the journal resolves by recording what was knowable in the moment.

## Friction and Adoption

Duke acknowledges the protocol is unpopular. Operators are time-poor. The journal feels like overhead. The first three entries are clumsy. The fifth feels useful. The fiftieth produces calibration data nothing else can.

The recommended ramp: start with one decision a week, the biggest. Add more as the habit forms. Do not try to journal every decision; the marginal value drops fast for small choices, and the protocol exhausts.
