# Christensen JTBD

The Jobs to Be Done framework. Christensen's other foundational contribution, alongside Disruption Theory. Distilled here from the 2016 HBR essay "Know Your Customers' Jobs to Be Done" (Christensen, Hall, Dillon, Duncan) and the Christensen Institute writeups that reproduce the Milkshake Marketing case. The companion book is *Competing Against Luck* (HarperBusiness, 2016).

## Provenance

- Authors: Clayton Christensen, Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, David Duncan
- Year: 2016 (HBR essay) + ongoing Christensen Institute material
- Companion book: *Competing Against Luck* (2016)
- Fidelity: extracts (direct passages preserved where load-bearing)

## The Central Reframe

> Customers don't buy products; they hire them to do jobs.

The unit of analysis is the job, not the product category. A customer has a job (a progress they are trying to make in a particular circumstance), hires a product for that job, and fires it when something else does the job better. The competitive set is whatever else competes for that same job, regardless of product category.

This is a causal frame, not a correlational one. Demographics correlate with purchase behavior; jobs cause it. Twenty-year-old men and fifty-year-old men can hire the same product for the same job; a single twenty-year-old man hires different products for different jobs at different moments.

## The Milkshake Marketing Story

The case study that made JTBD legible. McDonald's wanted to grow milkshake sales. Standard demographic segmentation produced no actionable insights: target customers were profiled, focus groups were run, product attributes were optimized along the dimensions customers said they wanted. Sales did not move.

The reframe was procedural. A researcher (Bob Moesta, working with Christensen) spent eighteen hours watching who actually bought milkshakes and when. Forty percent of milkshakes were sold before 8:00 AM. The buyer was alone, took the milkshake to go, and got back in a car.

The job: a long, boring morning commute that needed something to occupy a free hand and prevent stomach-grumbling until lunch. The competitive set was not other milkshakes. It was bagels (crumb-prone, gone too fast), bananas (gone too fast, not satisfying), donuts (crumb-prone, sticky), and coffee alone (does not handle the hunger).

The milkshake won the job because it was thick enough to last twenty minutes through a straw, occupied one hand, and held off hunger until noon.

Product implications followed directly. Make the milkshake thicker (extends consumption time). Add chunks (variety reduces boredom). Move the dispenser to the drive-through window (eliminates the wait that costs the busy commuter). None of these would have surfaced from demographic segmentation.

There was also an after-school job. Parents bought milkshakes for children as a treat. That milkshake needed to be small (because dinner is coming) and consumed quickly (because the parent is in a hurry). The same product was being hired for two different jobs by two different buyers, requiring two different product designs.

## The Framework

A job has three dimensions:

1. **Functional:** the practical task being done (occupy commute time and stomach).
2. **Social:** how the customer wants to be perceived (a treating parent, a productive professional).
3. **Emotional:** how the customer wants to feel (capable, in control, indulgent).

A complete job statement: *When [circumstance], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].*

The diagnostic question for any product:

- What job is this product being hired for?
- What is it competing against for that job?
- Under what circumstance is the job being done?
- What are the customers firing when they hire this product?

The last question is the most actionable. Firing reveals the unmet need that opened the door for the new hire.

## Why JTBD Beats Demographic Segmentation

Demographic segmentation answers "who is the customer." JTBD answers "what is the customer trying to do." The first describes correlation; the second describes causation.

A founder using demographic segmentation optimizes against the wrong target. Two customers in the same demographic hire the product for different jobs and need different things. One customer in different circumstances hires different products for the same job.

The implication for product strategy: build for the job, not for the demographic. The market is defined by the job, not by the category code.

## Implications for Competitive Analysis

The competitive set expands beyond the product category. Netflix's competitive set is not Hulu and HBO; it includes sleep, Instagram, YouTube, board games. Whatever is hired for the evening-relaxation job.

This reframes "what business are we in." The traditional answer is the product category. The JTBD answer is the job. The job is more stable than the product; the product evolves to do the job better.

## Connection to Disruption Theory

JTBD and Disruption Theory connect through the non-consumption frame. A disruption often serves a customer who was firing the entire category (could not afford it, could not access it, did not have the skill to use it) and hiring nothing for the job. The new-market disruption competes against non-consumption.

PCs vs mainframes: home users were firing the entire mainframe category and hiring nothing for the home-computing job. The PC won by being good enough for that job at a price the new market could pay.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Customers hire products for jobs.** The unit of analysis is the job.
- **The competitive set is whatever else does the job.** Not the product category.
- **Circumstance defines the job more than demographics do.** Same customer, different circumstance, different job.
- **Firing reveals the unmet need.** Ask what the customer is firing when they hire your product.
- **Build for the job, not the demographic.** Demographics correlate; jobs cause.
- **Non-consumption is the largest competitor.** Especially in new-market disruption.
