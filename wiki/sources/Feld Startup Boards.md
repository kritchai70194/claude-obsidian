# Feld Startup Boards

*Startup Boards: A Field Guide to Building and Leading an Effective Board of Directors*, by Brad Feld and Mahendra Ramsinghani (Wiley, first edition 2013, revised second edition 2022). The operator manual for the founder-board relationship, written from the rare position of someone who has sat on the board side of the table for thousands of meetings across decades. Most founder material treats the board as either decorative or adversarial; this book treats it as a tool to be wielded.

## Core Thesis

> The board exists to help the CEO succeed. If it does not, change the board.

The framing is operational, not deferential. The CEO curates the board, sets the agenda, runs the meeting, and removes members who do not contribute. The board provides three things: oversight, network, and capital. The board does not provide strategy. Strategy is the CEO's job.

Most first-time founders invert this. They treat the board as a body to be informed, appeased, or managed around. The book argues that posture produces worse boards AND worse companies. The healthy posture is: the board works for the company, and the CEO runs the meeting.

## How to Choose Board Members

Three roles on a venture-backed board: investor directors, founder/management directors, and **independent directors**. The book argues independents are systematically undervalued by first-time founders.

> Independent directors are the most underrated asset on most startup boards.

Investor directors carry investor incentives (fund returns, portfolio dynamics, follow-on logic). Founder/management directors carry inside knowledge. Independents carry the company's interests as their primary loyalty. A board with no independents is a board where every voice has a secondary agenda.

The criteria for an independent director: operational pattern-matching in the company's stage and category, a skill the existing board does not have, willingness to do real work between meetings, and no overlap with investor relationships that could compromise judgment.

## How to Run a Board Meeting

The operational mechanics get a chapter of their own.

**Pre-reads go out 72 hours in advance.** Financials, KPIs, status updates, anything the board needs to know. The pre-read is the report. Reading the report in the meeting is a failure mode.

**The meeting is for strategy and discussion, not status.** If a board member is hearing the metrics for the first time in the meeting, the pre-read failed. The actual meeting time is for the two or three strategic questions where the board's perspective matters.

**No surprises.** The CEO calls or emails board members between meetings on any material development. The board should never learn something material for the first time inside the boardroom.

**The CEO runs the meeting.** Agenda set by the CEO. Pace set by the CEO. Topics gated by the CEO. The investor director chairing the meeting is a common failure pattern.

> A board meeting where the board learns the metrics for the first time has already failed.

## The Founder-Board Contract

The implicit contract that most first-time founders never get told explicitly:

**The board's job:** hire/fire the CEO, approve financings, approve material transactions, provide oversight, network, and capital. That is it.

**The CEO's job:** everything else. Strategy, hiring, product, culture, execution, the day-to-day operation. The board does not run the company.

> The board does not run the company. The CEO runs the company.

Boards that drift toward running the company are doing so because the CEO let them, the board has the wrong members, or the company is in a wartime mode where the contract has been temporarily renegotiated. None of those are stable states.

## Pathological Term Sheets

A motif Feld carries from *Venture Deals* into *Startup Boards*: deal terms predict relationship dynamics.

> Investors should write deal terms with the assumption that the company will succeed, not fail.

Aggressive control terms, punitive ratchets, multi-x liquidation preferences, and elaborate protective provisions signal an investor who is planning for failure. That investor will then BEHAVE in line with their planning. A pathological term sheet is the leading indicator of a pathological board relationship.

The downstream principle: read the term sheet for the relationship signal, not just the economics.

## Removing a Bad Board Member

The chapter most first-time founders skip and most experienced founders re-read.

An underperforming board member damages the board more than an empty seat. The mechanics of removal depend on the seat type: independent directors can usually be replaced with board consent, investor directors require negotiation with the firm (sometimes a different partner takes the seat), and founder/management seats are governed by the charter. None of these are easy. All of them are sometimes necessary.

## Advisor vs Board Member, and CEO Coach vs Board Member

Three distinct relationships that first-time founders often conflate.

**Advisor:** informal, no fiduciary duty, can be ended at any time, low time commitment. Use advisors for specific gaps.

**Board member:** fiduciary duty to the company, formal removal mechanics, real time commitment. Use board members for ongoing strategic oversight.

**CEO coach:** confidential, fiduciary duty to the CEO not the company, focused on the CEO's development. Use a coach when the CEO needs a thinking partner without board-room dynamics.

The error is using a board seat for what should be an advisor relationship, or using an advisor for what should be a CEO coach.
