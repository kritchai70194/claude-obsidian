# PG Startup = Growth

Paul Graham's 2012 essay redefining what a "startup" is. Cuts through industry confusion ("is my barbershop a startup?") with a clean operational definition. Influences how YC evaluates applicants and how founders self-evaluate progress.

## The Definition

> "A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup."

Newness does not define a startup. Industry does not define a startup. Fast growth, intentionally pursued, defines a startup.

## The Numbers

> "A good growth rate during YC is 5-7% a week. If you can hit 10% a week you're doing exceptionally well. If you can only manage 1%, it's a sign you haven't yet figured out what you're doing."

The compressed metric:
- 1%/week: not yet PMF
- 5-7%/week: good, on track
- 10%+/week: exceptional

A 5%/week growth rate compounds to 12.6x annually. 10%/week compounds to 142x. The asymmetry is enormous.

## Growth as Compass

> "You can use that target growth rate to make all your decisions for you; anything that gets you the growth you need is ipso facto right."

A single metric collapses the decision-making complexity of running a startup. If a decision aids growth, it is right. If it does not, it is wrong (or premature).

This is a discipline, not a strategy. The discipline forces the team to evaluate every decision against one number.

## Why Startups Differ From Small Businesses

> "Most are service businesses, restaurants, barbershops, plumbers, and so on. These are not startups, except in a few unusual cases. A barbershop isn't designed to grow fast."

Two requirements for startup-class growth:
1. Make something many people want
2. Reach those people

A barbershop fails the second (geographic limit on reach). A restaurant fails both at scale (you cannot serve millions). A SaaS product can pass both with the right design.

The implication: when evaluating a business idea, ask both questions. If either fails, the business is not a startup (which is fine; just be honest about what you are building).

## Growth as Discipline

> "Focusing on hitting a growth rate reduces the otherwise bewilderingly multifarious problem of starting a startup to a single problem."

The startup problem-space is overwhelming: hiring, product, sales, marketing, fundraising, ops, support. Growth as the focus reduces all of it to "does this hit the number?".

This is the same shape as Lenny's North Star Metric thesis: collapse strategy into a single number, optimize relentlessly.

## What You Focus On

> "The most important thing that the constraints on a normal business protect it from is not competition, however, but the difficulty of coming up with new ideas."

Normal businesses operate in mature markets with known patterns. Startups operate in unexplored spaces. The constraint is not competitors; it is the difficulty of finding paths that work.

Startups must discover underexplored solutions to large-scale problems. The obvious ones are already taken.

## What This Source Teaches

- A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Newness does not define it.
- 5-7% weekly is good, 10%+ is exceptional, 1% means you have not figured it out.
- Growth as compass: any decision that aids growth is right.
- Two requirements: many people want it, you can reach them.
- Growth as discipline reduces complexity to one number.
- The constraint is finding underexplored paths, not competition.
