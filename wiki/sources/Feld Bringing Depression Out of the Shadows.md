# Feld Bringing Depression Out of the Shadows

April 2015 Q&A on feld.com (reposted from a Denver Business Journal interview). Sits two years downstream of the 2013 breakthrough posts cataloged in [[Feld On Depression]]. Where the 2013 writing named the condition and broke the taboo, this 2015 piece does the next move: operationalizes the experience by naming three discrete episodes, naming the triggers, and naming the early-stage obstacles to seeking help. Short, dense, and widely cited in founder-mental-health curricula.

## Three Episodes Named

Feld is unusual in that he writes about depression as a recurrent condition with specific episodes rather than a vague always-on mood. He names three:

**Mid-20s.** First clinical episode, while he was running a successful company. He did not talk about it. The reason given is plain: he was afraid people would stop taking him seriously or stop respecting him. The fear is presented as the obstacle, not the disease.

**2001, post-9/11.** Second episode. Feld was in New York City the morning of the attacks. The episode that followed was an exhaustion-and-trauma episode layered on a pre-existing vulnerability. He describes himself as terrified, exhausted, and emotionally distressed.

**Late 2012 into 2013.** Third episode and the one he wrote about publicly. Triggered by an accumulation: physical exhaustion, a bike accident, intensive book-writing projects, pet loss, and medical emergencies in his circle. This is the episode that produced the breakthrough 2013 post.

The framing matters. Depression is not "one bad year." It is recurrent. Founders should expect episodes across a career, not a single bad patch.

## Frames

- **Prevalence:** depression in startup circles is "very common, although it is rarely discussed." The silence, not the rate, is what's anomalous.
- **Blur:** stress, anxiety, and depression often blur together in founders. Most founders experience some mental-health difficulty at some point. Don't wait for a textbook diagnosis to take it seriously.
- **The cultural obstacle:** "leaders and entrepreneurs are programmed to 'never show weakness.'" This programming, not the underlying condition, is what keeps founders unmedicated and isolated.
- **Triggers Feld names:** physiological exhaustion, lack of learning opportunities, and the accumulation of small physical and emotional injuries. Not "stress" in the abstract. Specific compounding loads.

## Recommendations

The Q&A's prescriptive layer is short and unfussy.

- Talk to mentors and peers. The single highest-leverage move.
- Practice vulnerability as a discipline, not as a personality trait.
- Use organizations built for this: he names Jerry Colonna's Reboot.io as the structured peer-support resource for founders.
- The implicit prescription, consistent with the rest of [[Feld On Depression]]: treat depression medically. Therapy, medication, and time off all work.

## Frames Cited

- Depression is recurrent in founders, not a single episode. Plan for it.
- The cultural "never show weakness" programming is the obstacle, not the disease.
- Triggers are specific (exhaustion, accumulated injuries, lack of learning), not vague stress.
- Peer support is the highest-leverage intervention; structured programs (Reboot) outperform ad-hoc.
- Vulnerability is a discipline.
