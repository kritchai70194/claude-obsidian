# PG How to Get Startup Ideas

Paul Graham's 2012 essay on where startup ideas come from. Cuts the most common founder mistake (brainstorming ideas) with a sharp inversion: ideas are noticed, not invented. The compressed answer to "I want to start a startup, what should I build?"

## The Opening Frame

> "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."

The inversion. Most founders try to invent ideas; successful founders notice problems they (or someone they observe) actually has.

## Notice, Don't Think Up

> "The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'"

The distinction is precise. Thinking-up produces ideas that sound plausible but lack the underlying pain that makes someone pay. Noticing finds pain that already exists.

## Origin Stories

PG illustrates with how major companies actually started:

- **Mark Zuckerberg** "already lived online" when he built Facebook. Not a product idea; a continuation of how he was already operating.
- **Drew Houston** "realized he'd forgotten his USB stick" before building Dropbox. A specific moment of personal pain.
- **Stripe founders** saw payment processing as unnecessarily painful, a problem thousands of programmers had noticed but avoided. Stripe is the cleanest example of the pattern: noticed pain + willingness to do the unpleasant work.

The common shape: the founders were not searching for an idea. They were living the problem.

## Live in the Future

> "Live in the future, then build what's missing."

How to position yourself to notice startup ideas. Operate at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field; the gaps that are about to matter become visible from there.

This is why startup ideas cluster in domains undergoing change: the future-livers in those domains see the next gap.

## Schlep Blindness

> "Schlep blindness": the psychological barrier where founders unconsciously avoid ideas involving tedious, messy work.

PG's deep insight. Most "obvious" startup ideas have been avoided not because they are bad but because they are unappealing to work on (regulatory complexity, customer hand-holding, boring infrastructure).

> Stripe succeeded partly because most programmers shrank from handling payment complexities, leaving the opportunity uncontested.

The strategic implication: if an idea seems obvious but no one is doing it well, the moat is "willingness to do the schlep". This is a real competitive advantage.

## The Tar Pit Warning

> Cautions against "made-up" startup ideas, plausible-sounding concepts that fool founders into months of wasted effort.

PG's canonical tar pit example: a social network for pet owners. Friends say "maybe"; actual demand never materializes; founders spend years before admitting it.

The diagnostic for tar pit ideas:
- Friends say "that's interesting" but do not preorder
- The market consists of people who "might" use it, not people who do
- The use case is plausible but not painful
- You have to convince yourself it is a real problem

If you have to argue for the existence of the problem, it is a tar pit.

## What This Source Teaches

- Ideas are noticed, not invented. The verb is "notice", not "think up".
- The best ideas come from problems the founder personally lives.
- Live at the leading edge of a changing field; the gaps become visible from there.
- Schlep blindness: avoid the obvious idea because no one wants to do the work. This is competitive advantage in disguise.
- Tar pit ideas seem plausible but never produce real demand. If you have to argue for the problem, the problem is not there.
