# Horowitz The Struggle

Ben Horowitz's 2011 a16z post (later Chapter 4 of [[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) that named the default psychological state of running a startup. One of the most-reproduced passages in startup operator literature, because most founders mistake the state for personal failure and Horowitz's contribution is to name it as the baseline.

## The Defining Passage

> The Struggle is when you wonder why you started the company in the first place.
> The Struggle is when people ask you why you don't quit and you don't know the answer.
> The Struggle is when your employees think you are lying and you think they may be right.
> The Struggle is when food loses its taste.
> The Struggle is when you don't believe you should be CEO of your company. The Struggle is when you know that you are in over your head and you know that you cannot be replaced. The Struggle is when everybody thinks you are an idiot, but nobody will fire you. The Struggle is where self-doubt becomes self-hatred.
> The Struggle is when you are having a conversation with someone and you cannot hear a word that they are saying because all you can hear is The Struggle.
> The Struggle is not failure, but it causes failure. Especially if you are weak. Always if you are weak.
> Most people are not strong enough.

The passage is widely quoted because it puts language on a condition most founders experience but cannot name and cannot share with their teams.

## The Central Thesis

The Struggle is the default state of running a startup, not the exception. Most founders feel like they are failing most of the time, even when, by external metrics, they are succeeding. The internal experience and the external metrics decouple.

Horowitz's argument: knowing that this is normal is half the battle. The other half is operating anyway.

The piece is not a self-help essay. It does not offer five tips to overcome The Struggle. It explicitly says:

> Embrace the Struggle.

The Struggle does not get resolved by clever thinking or by changing the company. It is intrinsic to the work. Every great founder has been here. Most have considered quitting. The job is to keep operating while The Struggle continues.

## Why This Matters Operationally

The Struggle's central danger is not the emotional content; it is the cognitive distortion that comes with it. Founders in The Struggle make worse decisions: they over-index on the negative, they delay hard calls, they avoid the team, they isolate.

The discipline is to recognize the state and operate anyway. Horowitz's framing: feeling like you are failing does not mean you are failing. Feeling like you should quit does not mean you should quit. The data and the feelings are separate inputs.

> By far the most difficult skill I learned as CEO was the ability to manage my own psychology.

This is the named contribution of the essay. CEO psychology is a separate discipline from operational skill. A founder can be a great operator and still be destroyed by the internal game. Most founders are not prepared for the internal game because nobody warned them it was coming.

## What The Struggle Is Not

Horowitz is careful to distinguish The Struggle from clinical depression or from a signal that the founder is wrong about the company. The Struggle is the cost of admission. It does not mean the strategy is wrong. It does not mean the team has lost confidence. It does not mean the market has turned. It is the structural psychological condition of carrying responsibility for an organism (the company) that could die.

The corollary: if a founder is not in The Struggle, they may not be in deep enough. Comfortable founders are usually not building something that matters.

## Connection to Loudcloud / Opsware

The essay reads as written from direct experience. Loudcloud IPO'd into the dot-com collapse, watched the public stock lose 95% of its value, laid off 80 of 140 people in the pivot to Opsware, and operated for years before the eventual $1.6B sale to HP. The arc from peak crisis to eventual success was years long, and most of it was lived inside The Struggle.

Horowitz's framing is what made him able to operate through it. He did not believe success was just around the corner. He believed The Struggle was the baseline and that operating well through it was the job.

## What To Do About It

The essay offers a short list, paraphrased:

- Do not put it all on your shoulders. Many of the greatest CEOs share the burden with someone (cofounder, mentor, partner).
- Play long enough to get lucky. The Struggle is not permanent if you keep operating; quitting is permanent.
- Do not take it personally. Other people are not the cause; the structural condition is the cause.
- Remember that this is what separates the women from the girls and the men from the boys. The fact that it is hard is the point.

> Embrace the Struggle.
