# Helmer Acquired Podcast

The canonical long-form Helmer interview. ~2 hours with Ben Gilbert and David Rosenthal (Acquired podcast hosts), recorded March 2020, remastered December 2021. The format leverages Acquired's deep familiarity with specific company histories to apply the 7 Powers framework to real cases.

Notable for being the source where most modern strategy practitioners first encountered the 7 Powers framework, before the book reached its current mainstream status.

## Helmer's Background (Episode Open)

- PhD in Economics, Yale
- Started career at Bain & Company (under Bill Bain)
- Founded Strategy Capital, 1992
- Led 200+ strategy projects: Adobe, Hewlett-Packard, Netflix, Raychem, Spotify
- Wrote 7 Powers in 2016 to compress 40 years of practice into a teachable framework

The biographical setup matters: the framework is practitioner-derived, not academic. Each Power was first observed in client work, then formalized.

## The Framework as Discussed in the Podcast

The episode walks through all 7 Powers with real-company examples. The treatment matches the book but adds Acquired's specific lens (they know the company histories deeply).

### Recurring theme: most "moats" are not Powers

The hosts repeatedly try to apply standard "moat" terminology and Helmer corrects them:
- "First mover advantage" → not a Power. First mover only matters if it produces one of the 7 Powers.
- "Brand recognition" → not Branding Power. Recognition is necessary but not sufficient. Branding Power requires *durable willingness to pay premium*.
- "Network effects" → not always Network Economies. Helmer is precise: must have Benefit (value increases with users) AND Barrier (competitors cannot pay users to switch to sub-scale alternative).

The precision is the value. Hand-waved "competitive moats" become testable: does it satisfy Benefit + Barrier per Helmer's definition?

## Specific Company Applications

### Netflix

Multiple Powers stacked over time:
- **Counter-Positioning** (early): DVD-by-mail, no late fees, beat Blockbuster
- **Scale Economies** (streaming era): content costs spread across larger subscriber base
- **Branding** (current): "Netflix" as shorthand for streaming, premium pricing power

### Spotify

- **Scale Economies**: music licensing costs spread across larger user base
- **Switching Costs**: playlists, recommendation algorithm, ecosystem (devices, smart speakers)
- Lacks Network Economies in pure form (does not get more valuable as more friends join, in the way Facebook does)

### Adobe (Helmer client)

- **Switching Costs**: enterprise design teams trained on Photoshop/Illustrator workflow
- **Cornered Resource**: Adobe owns the de facto file formats (PSD, PDF, AI)
- **Process Power**: decades of subtle UI refinements competitors cannot easily replicate

### Pixar

- **Cornered Resource (Human)**: the specific creative directors (John Lasseter, Brad Bird, Pete Docter, Andrew Stanton) — Helmer's textbook example of human cornered resource
- The directors create the films; the films create the brand; both reinforce each other

## Counter-Positioning Discussion

Helmer's favorite Power gets the deepest treatment. Hosts ask "why is this the most underrated?"

Helmer's answer (paraphrased from the episode): because the incumbent *consciously decides not to compete* even when they see you coming. The Power is psychological/organizational, not technical. Most strategists analyze technical moats and miss the structural incentive problem incumbents face.

### Examples from the episode:
- **Netflix vs Blockbuster**: Blockbuster could have launched DVD-by-mail in 2000. They explicitly chose not to because it would cannibalize $800M in late fees (16% of revenue).
- **Vanguard vs Fidelity**: Fidelity could have launched index funds in the 1980s. The active-management fee revenue was too valuable to cannibalize.
- **Dell vs Compaq**: Compaq could have sold direct-to-consumer. The retail channel relationships were too valuable to sacrifice.
- **Amazon vs Borders**: Borders could have built an online store. The store-traffic-based economics made online a poor fit.

The pattern: incumbent makes the rational short-term decision. The rational short-term decision is also the strategically wrong long-term decision.

## Strategy = Route to Power

The closing theme of the episode. Helmer's strongest statement:

> A strategy that does not lead to one of the 7 Powers is not a strategy. It is an operating plan in fancy dress.

For founders, this means the discipline: name the Power you are building toward. If you cannot, you do not have a strategy. You have a plan to do work.

## What This Source Teaches

- The 7 Powers framework was practitioner-derived, then formalized; not academic.
- Most "moats" terminology is loose; the 7 Powers are precise.
- Each Power requires Benefit AND Barrier; one without the other is not a Power.
- Counter-Positioning is the most underrated because it is structural/psychological, not technical.
