# Grove OKR Origin

The origin story and original specification of OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) as [[Andy Grove]] designed them at Intel in the early 1970s. Carried out of Intel by John Doerr (who joined Intel in 1974 and learned OKRs directly from Grove), brought to Kleiner Perkins in 1980, evangelized to portfolio companies through the 1990s and 2000s, adopted at Google in 1999 through Doerr's portfolio relationship, and documented in Doerr's *Measure What Matters* (Portfolio, 2017).

This page reconstructs Grove's original OKR specification before the modern industry water-down. Fidelity is structural-summary because the primary sources are Intel internal documents, Grove memos, and Doerr's later reconstruction; no single canonical Grove text on OKRs exists, though the planning chapter of [[Grove High Output Management]] (1983) contains the published version.

## The Drucker-to-Grove Lineage

OKRs descend from Peter Drucker's Management by Objectives (MBO), introduced in *The Practice of Management* (1954). Drucker's MBO had three core ideas:

1. Managers and employees jointly set objectives.
2. Progress against objectives is measured.
3. Reward and accountability follow the measurement.

Grove encountered MBO at Fairchild Semiconductor in the 1960s. When he joined Intel as employee #3 in 1968 and became responsible for operations, he reformulated MBO into something tighter, more quantitative, and more suited to a fast-moving technology company. The reformulation became OKRs.

Grove's core complaints about Drucker MBO: objectives drifted toward the qualitative and unmeasurable; the cadence was annual, which was too slow for semiconductor competition; rewards were too closely coupled, which made people sandbag the objectives to ensure bonuses.

## The Original Grove Specification

The Grove OKR, as documented in HOM (1983) and Doerr's reconstruction:

**Objectives** are qualitative, ambitious, time-bounded, and direction-setting. They answer "what." Each Objective should be inspirational enough to mobilize effort and concrete enough that everyone knows what success looks like. Example Intel Objective (1980): "Establish the 8086 as the highest-performance 16-bit microprocessor family."

**Key Results** are quantitative, measurable, and verifiable. They answer "how will we know." Each Objective gets 3-5 Key Results. Each Key Result should have a number, a deadline, and a binary judgment of met-or-not-met. Example KRs for the 8086 Objective:

- Develop and publish five benchmarks demonstrating superior performance (by June 1).
- Re-release the entire 8086 family (by June 1).
- Get the 8MHz part into production (by July 1).
- Sample the arithmetic coprocessor by June 15.

The arithmetic is the rigor. Either you shipped five benchmarks by June 1 or you did not.

## The Four Constraints That Made OKRs Work at Intel

Grove's original specification included four constraints that are often dropped in the modern watered-down version:

**Quarterly cadence.** Annual OKRs were too slow for semiconductor competition. Quarterly was the natural matching of the planning cycle to the rate of change. Grove set OKRs each quarter, scored them at the end of the quarter, and reset for the next quarter.

**3-5 Objectives, 3-5 Key Results each.** More than five was unfocused; fewer than three was insufficient ambition. The discipline was forcing the choice.

**Scoring 0.0 to 1.0, with 0.7 average as healthy.** Grove insisted that OKRs should be ambitious enough that you missed some. A 1.0 average meant the OKRs were not stretch; a 0.4 average meant they were unrealistic. 0.7 average across an org was the signal of correctly-calibrated stretch.

**Public OKRs across the organization.** Every Intel employee's OKRs were visible to every other Intel employee. The CEO's OKRs were posted publicly. The transparency was the alignment mechanism: horizontal alignment without vertical micromanagement. If your OKRs and a peer's OKRs were misaligned, you saw it before the misalignment became a fight.

## The Decoupling From Compensation

Grove's most-counterintuitive design choice: OKRs are explicitly decoupled from compensation. If OKR achievement directly drives bonus, employees will sandbag the OKRs to ensure achievement. Grove insisted that compensation be a separate, manager-driven evaluation that takes OKRs as one input among many, not as the formula.

This is the constraint most modern OKR implementations get wrong. Companies that wire OKRs directly into bonus formulas turn OKRs into sandbagging exercises within two quarters.

## The Doerr Carry-Out and Google Adoption

John Doerr joined Intel in 1974 at age 23, worked directly under Grove, learned OKRs in his first quarter, used them for the rest of his Intel tenure. Left Intel for Kleiner Perkins in 1980 as a partner. Through the 1980s and 1990s, Doerr evangelized OKRs to Kleiner portfolio companies. None adopted them at scale until Google.

In 1999, Doerr made a $12.5M Series A investment in Google. As part of the investment, he taught OKRs to Larry Page and Sergey Brin and the early Google team. Google adopted them, ran them through every employee at quarterly cadence, kept them public, and used them through scaling from 30 people to 100,000+.

Google's adoption made OKRs the industry-standard framework. Most companies that "do OKRs" today are running a version learned from Google blog posts, not from Grove's original specification. The Grove specification (quarterly, 3-5/3-5, 0.7 healthy, public, decoupled from bonus) is tighter than the modern version.

## What This Brain Takes

- **Grove is the source, not Doerr or Google.** When citing OKRs in operator contexts, cite Grove first.
- **Quarterly cadence, not annual.** Annual OKRs are the failure mode.
- **3-5 Objectives, 3-5 Key Results each.** The constraint forces focus.
- **0.7 average across the org is healthy.** Higher means insufficient stretch.
- **Public OKRs are the alignment mechanism.** Drop the public-ness and you lose horizontal alignment.
- **Decouple OKRs from compensation.** Wiring them into bonus formulas creates sandbagging.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **OKRs are quarterly, public, scored 0-1, with 0.7 as healthy stretch.**
- **OKRs are decoupled from compensation; coupling produces sandbagging.**
- **Public OKRs across the org create horizontal alignment without vertical micromanagement.**
- **3-5 Objectives and 3-5 Key Results each is the focusing constraint.**
