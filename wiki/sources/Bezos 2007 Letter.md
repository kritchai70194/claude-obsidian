# Bezos 2007 Letter ("Kindle, and missionaries build better products")

[[Jeff Bezos]]'s 2007 shareholder letter, published April 2008. The Kindle-launch letter. Kindle shipped November 19, 2007 and sold out in 5.5 hours. The letter is short and almost entirely about Kindle as a worked example of the Bezos invention method: pick a 500-year-old object, identify what makes it work ("it disappears"), then build something that gets out of the way while adding capabilities only the new medium can offer.

## Headline Takeaways

1. **Three years of work, sold out in 5.5 hours.** The longest-runway product Amazon had built to that point.
2. **The book's most important feature is that it disappears.** The author's world remains; the paper-ink-glue-stitching dissolves. Kindle's design constraint: do the same.
3. **Don't out-book the book.** Instead, add what only the new medium can do: wireless delivery, dictionary lookup, search, cloud-stored notes, automatic place-keeping, adjustable font size.
4. **Information snacking vs long-form reading.** Bezos explicitly worries that BlackBerry/laptop/PDA tools shift us toward shorter attention spans. Kindle is purpose-built as a counterbalance.
5. **Missionaries build better products.** The cleanest primary-source statement of the missionaries-vs-mercenaries frame.

## Key Quotes (verbatim)

### The vision

> "Our vision for Kindle is every book ever printed in any language, all available in less than 60 seconds."

### On the design constraint

> "We started by setting ourselves the admittedly audacious goal of improving upon the physical book. We did not choose that goal lightly. Anything that has persisted in roughly the same form and resisted change for 500 years is unlikely to be improved easily."

> "At the beginning of our design process, we identified what we believe is the book's most important feature. It *disappears*. When you read a book, you don't notice the paper and the ink and the glue and the stitching. All of that dissolves, and what remains is the author's world."

> "We knew Kindle would have to *get out of the way*, just like a physical book, so readers could become engrossed in the words and forget they're reading on a device. We also knew we shouldn't try to copy every last feature of a book, we could never out-book the book. We'd have to add *new* capabilities, ones that could never be possible with a traditional book."

### The analog to Amazon's early days

> "Instead of trying to duplicate physical bookstores, we've been inspired by them and worked to find things we could do in the new medium that could never be done in the old one."

### On low friction begets more usage

> "Anytime you make something simpler and lower friction, you get more of it."

### On co-evolution with tools

> "We humans co-evolve with our tools. We change our tools, and then our tools change us. Writing, invented thousands of years ago, is a grand whopper of a tool, and I have no doubt that it changed us dramatically."

> "Lately, networked tools such as desktop computers, laptops, cell phones and PDAs have changed us too. They've shifted us more toward *information snacking*, and I would argue toward shorter attention spans."

### Missionaries vs mercenaries (the load-bearing line)

> "I'm convinced books are on the verge of being improved upon, Amazon has no sinecure as that agent. It will happen, but if we don't execute well, it will be done by others."

> "I'll also point out that ... missionaries build better products."

### Closing

> "Your team of missionaries here is fervent about driving free cash flow per share and returns on capital. We know we can do that by putting customers first."

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Find the load-bearing feature, then make it invisible.** This is the canonical Bezos product-design move. For books: it disappears. For 1-Click shopping: friction goes to zero. For AWS: capacity provisioning becomes invisible. Same move at every layer.
- **Don't copy the old medium; add what only the new medium can do.** This is the cleanest single-sentence statement of why Kindle won and why Sony Reader, Rocket eBook, etc. did not.
- **"Missionaries build better products" is the load-bearing hiring claim.** Pair with [[Bezos 1998 Letter]]'s three hiring questions and [[Working Backwards Book]]'s bar-raiser tradition. The institutional preference is for missionary disposition, not pedigree.
- **Friction is a control variable.** "Anytime you make something simpler and lower friction, you get more of it." This becomes the operating logic of Prime, FBA, AWS pricing, and Echo.
- **The 500-year filter.** Bezos explicitly notes the book has resisted change for 500 years and that this should make us humble about the difficulty. Then he goes anyway. The hubris and the humility together are the Bezos move.
