# Bezos 2018 Letter ("Intuition, curiosity, wandering, failure at scale")

[[Jeff Bezos]]'s 2018 shareholder letter, published April 2019. The **wandering** letter. The opening data table (third-party seller percentage 1999-2018) is the cleanest visual argument for the long-term compounding of an [[Bezos 2005 Letter]] judgment-based decision. The middle is the load-bearing primary text on **intuition, curiosity, and wandering** as deliberate counter-balances to efficiency. The closing on **failure needing to scale** is the most explicit invitation to multibillion-dollar Amazon failures ever printed.

## Headline Takeaways

1. **Third-party sellers went from 3% to 58% of GMV (1999-2018).** Compound 52% CAGR over 19 years; Amazon's own first-party also grew 25% CAGR from $1.6B to $117B. "Third-party sellers are kicking our first party butt. Badly."
2. **Wandering as a deliberate counterbalance to efficiency.** Not random. Guided by hunch, gut, intuition, curiosity.
3. **AWS as wandering's exemplar.** "No one asked for AWS. No one. Turns out the world was in fact ready and hungry for an offering like AWS but didn't know it."
4. **The biggest needle movers are things customers don't know to ask for.** Listening is necessary but insufficient; you must also invent on their behalf.
5. **Failure needs to scale.** "Amazon will be experimenting at the right scale for a company of our size if we occasionally have multibillion-dollar failures." Fire phone named.
6. **Echo origin story.** Inspired by Star Trek. Born from machine learning + AWS. "No customer was asking for Echo. This was definitely us wandering."
7. **The $15 minimum wage gauntlet.** Bezos invites competitors to match and exceed. "Better yet, go to $16 and throw the gauntlet back at us."

## Key Quotes (verbatim)

### Opening table reveal

> "Third-party sellers are kicking our first party butt. Badly."

> "Our first-party business has grown dramatically over that period, from $1.6 billion in 1999 to $117 billion this past year. The compound annual growth rate for our first-party business in that time period is 25%. But in that same time, third-party sales have grown from $0.1 billion to $160 billion, a compound annual growth rate of 52%."

### Why third-parties succeeded inside Amazon

> "We helped independent sellers compete against our first-party business by investing in and offering them the very best selling tools we could imagine and build. ... But of great importance are Fulfillment by Amazon and the Prime membership program. ... We invested in both of these programs at significant financial risk and after much internal debate. We had to continue investing significantly over time as we experimented with different ideas and iterations. We could not foresee with certainty what those programs would eventually look like, let alone whether they would succeed, but they were pushed forward with intuition and heart, and nourished with optimism."

### Intuition, curiosity, and the power of wandering (the load-bearing thesis)

> "From very early on in Amazon's life, we knew we wanted to create a culture of builders, people who are curious, explorers. They like to invent. Even when they're experts, they are 'fresh' with a beginner's mind. They see the way we do things as just the way we do things now. A builder's mentality helps us approach big, hard-to-solve opportunities with a humble conviction that success can come through iteration: invent, launch, reinvent, relaunch, start over, rinse, repeat, again and again."

> "Sometimes (often actually) in business, you do know where you're going, and when you do, you can be efficient. Put in place a plan and execute. In contrast, wandering in business is not efficient ... but it's also not random. It's guided, by hunch, gut, intuition, curiosity, and powered by a deep conviction that the prize for customers is big enough that it's worth being a little messy and tangential to find our way there."

> "Wandering is an essential counter-balance to efficiency. You need to employ both. The outsized discoveries, the 'non-linear' ones, are highly likely to require wandering."

### On listening and inventing

> "Much of what we build at AWS is based on listening to customers. It's critical to ask customers what they want, listen carefully to their answers, and figure out a plan to provide it thoughtfully and quickly (speed matters in business!). No business could thrive without that kind of customer obsession. But it's also not enough. The biggest needle movers will be things that customers don't know to ask for. We must invent on their behalf."

> "AWS itself, as a whole, is an example. No one asked for AWS. No one. Turns out the world was in fact ready and hungry for an offering like AWS but didn't know it. We had a hunch, followed our curiosity, took the necessary financial risks, and began building."

### Amazon Go (invisible technology)

> "We had to do it in a way where the technology worked so well that it simply receded into the background, invisible. The reward has been the response from customers, who've described the experience of shopping at Amazon Go as 'magical.'"

### Failure needs to scale (the load-bearing line)

> "As a company grows, everything needs to scale, including the size of your failed experiments. If the size of your failures isn't growing, you're not going to be inventing at a size that can actually move the needle. Amazon will be experimenting at the right scale for a company of our size if we occasionally have multibillion-dollar failures."

> "Of course, we won't undertake such experiments cavalierly. We will work hard to make them good bets, but not all good bets will ultimately pay out. This kind of large-scale risk taking is part of the service we as a large company can provide to our customers and to society. The good news for shareowners is that a single big winning bet can more than cover the cost of many losers."

> "Development of the Fire phone and Echo was started around the same time. While the Fire phone was a failure, we were able to take our learnings (as well as the developers) and accelerate our efforts building Echo and Alexa."

### Echo origin

> "No customer was asking for Echo. This was definitely us wandering. Market research doesn't help. If you had gone to a customer in 2013 and said 'Would you like a black, always-on cylinder in your kitchen about the size of a Pringles can that you can talk to and ask questions, that also turns on your lights and plays music?' I guarantee you they'd have looked at you strangely and said 'No, thank you.'"

### Minimum wage gauntlet

> "Last year, we raised our minimum wage to $15-an-hour for all full-time, part-time, temporary, and seasonal employees across the U.S. ... We strongly believe that this will benefit our business as we invest in our employees. But that is not what drove the decision. We had always offered competitive wages. But we decided it was time to lead, to offer wages that went beyond competitive. We did it because it seemed like the right thing to do."

> "Today I challenge our top retail competitors (you know who you are!) to match our employee benefits and our $15 minimum wage. Do it! Better yet, go to $16 and throw the gauntlet back at us. It's a kind of competition that will benefit everyone."

### Closing

> "Teams all across Amazon are listening to customers and wandering on their behalf! As always, I attach a copy of our original 1997 letter. It remains Day 1."

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **The opening table is the most-cited Bezos visual.** A single column of percentages (3% → 58% over 20 years) makes the case for the entire 2005 detail-page-third-party decision. Worth showing anyone who doubts compounding.
- **Wandering is the dual of efficiency, not its opposite.** This is the Bezos rejection of the Six Sigma / lean / OKR worldview. You need both; efficiency is for known territory; wandering is for the rest.
- **Listening is necessary but insufficient.** This is the single sharpest rebuttal to pure "voice-of-customer" methodologies. Customers cannot ask for what they cannot imagine. The 2018 letter is the canonical primary source for this argument.
- **Failure scaling is institutional.** Most companies hide failures. Bezos publishes a per-letter expectation that Amazon should occasionally have multibillion-dollar failures. The framing is: "we as a large company provide this service to society."
- **The Echo Pringles-can example is the cleanest market-research-fails example in business literature.** Worth memorizing.
- **$15 minimum wage as competitive challenge.** This is anti-pattern compared to traditional cost-minimization. Pair with [[Bezos 2012 Letter]]'s monthly author payments. The pattern: lead in employee terms; dare competitors to follow.
