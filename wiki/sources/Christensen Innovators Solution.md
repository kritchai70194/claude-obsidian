# Christensen Innovators Solution

The prescriptive sequel to [[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]]. Co-authored with Michael Raynor. Where the first book diagnosed why incumbents lose, this book is the playbook for what to do about it. The book also extends Disruption Theory with two important additions: the Resources/Processes/Values (RPV) framework for capability analysis, and the Law of Conservation of Modularity for predicting where value moves in a value chain.

## Provenance

- Authors: Clayton M. Christensen, Michael E. Raynor

## The Prescriptive Move

The Innovator's Dilemma left readers with a diagnosis but an open question: "what do I actually do?" The Innovator's Solution answers it. Build disruptive growth businesses inside existing companies, but do it through structural separation, not cultural exhortation. Identify the right jobs to be done. Pick the right competitors to worry about. Choose the right architecture for the right market position.

## Building Disruptive Growth Businesses Inside Incumbents

The recommended mechanism: an autonomous business unit with its own P&L, its own resource allocation process, its own customer base, and its own performance metrics. The unit must be small enough that small wins matter to it, located far enough from the core that the core's values do not override its decisions, and reporting high enough that it gets sustained executive cover.

The structural argument: a low-margin emerging business cannot compete for capital against the core's high-margin sustaining innovations in a unified P&L. The values of the parent organization will starve it every budget cycle. Separation is not a cultural preference; it is a structural requirement.

The risk: separation too far becomes abandonment. The unit needs the parent's capabilities (technology, brand, distribution where applicable) without inheriting the parent's values. Striking that balance is the operating challenge.

## The Resources / Processes / Values Framework (RPV)

A company's capabilities live in three things:

1. **Resources:** tangible assets (people, money, technology, brand, customer relationships).
2. **Processes:** the ways resources combine to produce output (development, manufacturing, sales).
3. **Values:** the criteria used to prioritize opportunities (which projects get funded, which customers get attention, which margins are "acceptable").

Capabilities are mobile across resources, fixed in processes, and most fixed in values. Hiring a great engineer transfers a resource. Importing a process is harder. Changing values is hardest of all because values are encoded in the compensation system, the budget rules, and the executive promotion criteria.

The diagnostic: when an incumbent fails at disruption, the failure is almost always at the values layer. The resources exist; the processes are present or buildable; the values reject the opportunity at the resource-allocation step.

The prescriptive implication: the separate organization must have its own values, which means its own resource allocation rules, its own performance metrics, and its own compensation structure. Without that, separation is theater.

## The Right Jobs to Be Done

This book imports the Jobs to Be Done frame from [[Christensen JTBD]] and applies it to identifying which disruptive opportunities are real.

The diagnostic question: is there a job customers are trying to get done that they currently cannot get done well? Disruption opportunities sit where non-consumers exist because the existing solutions are too expensive, too complex, too inaccessible, or too over-featured. The job exists; the customer is firing the entire category.

The trap: building a "better mousetrap" along the existing performance trajectory is sustaining innovation, not disruption. The opportunity is for the underserved or non-consuming customer, not the customer the incumbent is already overserving.

## The Law of Conservation of Modularity

Christensen and Raynor's structural argument about where margin lives in a value chain.

When a product is **not good enough** on the dominant performance metric customers care about, the architecture must be **interdependent** (custom-fit, integrated across the value chain). The choke point is the not-good-enough subsystem; whoever owns it captures margin. Mainframes were interdependent and IBM captured the margin. Early smartphones were interdependent and Apple captured the margin.

When a product is **more than good enough**, the architecture **modularizes**. Customers stop paying for further improvement on the overshot metric and start paying for customization, lower price, or speed. The choke point moves to whatever is still not good enough (often a new layer that has just emerged). PCs modularized; Intel and Microsoft captured the margin. Smartphones may eventually modularize again as commodity-grade hardware overshoots.

The "law" is conservation: modularity does not eliminate margin; it relocates margin. The not-good-enough layer is always the margin layer. Predicting where value will go in a value chain reduces to predicting which layer will be not good enough next.

The cross-reference to [[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]]: this is the architectural mechanism behind disruption. The disruptor often modularizes around an incumbent's integrated architecture, then captures margin from the new not-good-enough layer.

## Which Competitors to Worry About

The counterintuitive answer: not the ones serving customers most similar to yours. The ones serving customers you cannot profitably serve. The disruptor is the one growing in a segment your sales team would not call on.

The diagnostic: which competitor is improving fastest on the metrics your worst customers care about, in a segment your business model cannot reach without abandoning its margins? That is the future displacer.

The trap: incumbents benchmark against the head-on competitor most similar to them, win those comparisons, and lose to the unfamiliar competitor at the low end.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Disruption from inside an incumbent requires an autonomous unit with its own values.** Not just culture; structural separation including resource allocation rules.
- **Capability lives in Resources, Processes, and Values.** Values are the hardest to change and where disruption usually dies.
- **Identify the job, then identify the non-consumer.** That is where the disruption opportunity sits.
- **Interdependent architectures win in not-good-enough markets; modular architectures win in overshot markets.** Predicting where margin moves reduces to predicting which layer overshoots next.
- **Worry about the competitor serving customers you cannot profitably serve.** Not the head-on competitor.
