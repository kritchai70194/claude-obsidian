# Rabois 20VC Interview

Synthesis across Rabois's recurring appearances on 20VC with Harry Stebbings, Founder's Field Guide with Patrick O'Shaughnessy, and interviews with The Information. No single canonical episode; the framing repeats across venues with stable content and shifting examples drawn from the current portfolio.

## Why This Source Matters

The podcast and long-form interview format pulls Rabois into operating texture that the lecture format compresses. Specifically: the PayPal-era stories, the Square COO years, the rationale for leaving Founders Fund for Khosla in 2018, and the board-level observations on Affirm and Stripe scaling. The interviews fill in the empirical record behind the rubrics.

## Recurring Themes

**PayPal as the formative hiring laboratory.** Rabois returns to PayPal repeatedly as the template. Thiel, Levchin, and Hoffman were uncompromising on raw cognitive horsepower and willingness to work intensely. They tested candidates with real work, decided fast, and paid in equity rather than salary. The template propagated to YouTube, LinkedIn, Yelp, Slide, Square, Stripe, Palantir, Affirm, SpaceX, and Tesla through Mafia members.

> Everyone we hired at PayPal was someone we would have wanted as a co-founder. That was the bar. If you wouldn't co-found with them, you don't hire them.

**Square COO years (2010-2013).** Rabois ran operations under Jack Dorsey through the scale from roughly 30 to 600 people. He recounts the operating disciplines: standing meetings cut to the minimum, every senior hire personally interviewed by him and Jack, the bar held against pressure to fill seats. The Square experience is the empirical core of his hiring views; he has since called it the hardest and most-instructive operating role of his career.

**Why leave Founders Fund for Khosla.** Stated rationale: he wanted to lead a fund stage, build a brand, and operate with more autonomy on portfolio composition. The unstated rationale (audible across episodes): Khosla offered a partner economics structure better suited to his stage and reputation. He is candid that venture is a function of brand and access; both compound.

**Board observations on Affirm.** Affirm under Max Levchin is held up as the slow-and-correct version of fintech building: deep underwriting, hard regulatory work, capital structure that holds through cycles. Rabois contrasts it with the BNPL competitors who chased volume without the underwriting infrastructure and broke when rates rose. The lesson: in regulated markets, the slow-correct builder outlasts the fast-sloppy builder.

**Board observations on Stripe.** Stripe under the Collison brothers is held up as the unusual case of two founders who can stage-transition without external CEO swap. Rabois notes the Collisons' explicit hiring rubric (technical depth + product taste + low ego), the long-time horizon, and the willingness to refuse adjacent products until the core is dominant. The Stripe pattern is the closest external match to the Slootman "narrow focus" posture.

**Hiring lessons across the portfolio.** A recurring observation: the companies that fail to scale fail at hiring, not at product. Product mistakes are recoverable. Hiring mistakes compound through every subsequent decision. Rabois claims he has never seen a company die from a product mistake that did not also have a hiring mistake at the root.

**Post-2020 venture market commentary.** Rabois is bearish on the late-2020 / early-2021 valuation regime. The thesis: too much capital chasing too few real Barrels, valuations decoupled from underlying operating reality, and a generation of founders who never operated through scarcity. The correction is healthy.

## Notable Passages

On the hiring decision:

> The single highest-leverage thing a CEO does is the next hire. Not the strategy, not the fundraise, not the product spec. The next hire.

On compensation:

> Top of market is a small dollar gap on a big output gap. Cheap is the most expensive thing you can be on great talent.

On speed:

> If you take six weeks to make a hiring decision on a great candidate, they are going somewhere else. Even if they take your offer, they have already decided you are slow.

On the operating posture:

> You are either raising the bar or watching it drift. There is no steady state. Drift is the default. Raising it is the work.
