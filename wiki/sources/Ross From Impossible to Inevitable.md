# Ross From Impossible to Inevitable

Co-authored by Aaron Ross and Jason Lemkin, published by Wiley in 2016 (2nd edition 2019). Subtitled *How SaaS and Other Hyper-Growth Companies Create Predictable Revenue*. The follow-up to [[Ross Predictable Revenue]], focused on why most SaaS companies stall at $1-3M ARR and how a handful break through to $100M+. The vehicle for the **7 Ingredients of Predictable Revenue** framework.

## The Stall Problem

The book's premise: hypergrowth is not magic and it is not luck. Companies that break through from $1M to $100M ARR share a consistent set of operating disciplines. Companies that stall lack one or more.

> Most SaaS companies stall between $1M and $3M ARR. Not because the market is wrong. Because the company has stopped doing the things that got them to $1M and not yet started doing the things required for $10M.

The 7 Ingredients are the diagnostic. Stalled companies usually have 2-3 of the 7. Companies breaking through have all 7.

## The 7 Ingredients

### 1. Nail a Niche

Pick a specific ICP and dominate it before expanding. The same argument as in [[Ross Predictable Revenue]] but with hypergrowth-stage stakes. Companies that try to be broad at $3M ARR stay at $3M ARR.

The test: can you describe your customer in one sentence such that two-thirds of your AEs would identify the same accounts as in-niche or out-of-niche? If not, you have not nailed a niche.

### 2. Create Predictable Pipeline

The Seeds / Nets / Spears framework applied. By $5M ARR you should have a documented, repeatable pipeline-generation motion that does not depend on the founder. Pipeline coverage tracked weekly. Conversion rates by stage tracked monthly.

> If you cannot say with confidence how much pipeline you will have in 90 days, you do not have predictable pipeline. You have hopeful pipeline.

### 3. Make Sales Scalable

The SDR/AE split, the qualification discipline, the AE onboarding playbook. The motion has to work for the median hire, not just for the founder and the top AE. If your top AE does 3x the bottom AE, the playbook is missing. The fix is to document, train, and enforce, not to keep hiring stars.

The 90-day AE ramp test: can a new AE reach quota in 90 days following the documented playbook? If yes, sales is scalable. If no, every new AE is a multi-quarter risk.

### 4. Double Your Deal Size

The lever most founders ignore. Doubling ACV is usually easier than doubling deal count and produces the same revenue with half the operational load.

Mechanics:
- Move upmarket within the same ICP (sell to larger companies in the same niche).
- Add multi-year contracts at a discount.
- Bundle adjacent products.
- Raise prices (most early-stage SaaS is underpriced).

> Doubling deal size is not a pricing exercise. It is an ICP exercise. The same product sold to the right customer is worth twice as much.

### 5. Do the Time

The unsexy ingredient. Hypergrowth is a 7-10 year arc, not a 2-year sprint. Founders who expect to hit $100M in 3 years and bail when they do not are the largest source of stalled companies. The compounding requires patience that most founder timelines do not respect.

> Hypergrowth feels slow for 5 years and then very fast for 2. Most founders quit during the slow part.

### 6. Embrace Employee Ownership

Mature companies operate on accountability, not on heroics. The CEO cannot be in every deal at $20M ARR. Department heads own their numbers, hire their teams, and ship their plans. Founders who keep pulling decisions back to themselves cap the company at whatever the founder can personally execute.

The test: can the company hit its quarterly numbers if the CEO is on vacation for a month? At $1M, no. At $10M, ideally yes. At $50M, definitely yes or you do not have a real company yet.

### 7. Define Your Destiny

The strategic clarity ingredient. What is the company actually trying to become? A $100M acquisition? A multi-decade public company? A category-defining platform? The answer drives every hiring, fundraising, and product decision.

Founders who cannot articulate the destiny default to optimizing for the next round of funding, which is a worse target than the actual outcome they want.

## Why Most Companies Stall

The book's diagnostic argument: stalls are explained by missing ingredients. Common patterns:

- **Stalled at $1-3M**: usually missing ingredients 1 (niche) and 2 (pipeline). Founders are still selling to anyone and pipeline is whatever shows up.
- **Stalled at $5-10M**: usually missing ingredient 3 (scalable sales) and 4 (deal size). Founders have a niche and pipeline but every new hire is a leap of faith and ACV is flat.
- **Stalled at $20-50M**: usually missing ingredient 6 (employee ownership) and 7 (destiny). The company has a working motion but the CEO is still in every decision.

> No company is missing only one ingredient. Stalled companies are usually missing 3 or 4. The fix is sequential. Pick the most-broken ingredient and fix that.

## The Lemkin Contributions

Jason Lemkin (SaaStr) co-author contributions are most visible in:
- The deal-size chapter (Lemkin's repeated argument that early SaaS is underpriced by 2-3x).
- The "do the time" chapter (Lemkin's 7-10 year arc thesis).
- The employee-ownership chapter (Lemkin's repeated thesis that founders cap the company at their own attention).

## Why This Source Matters

[[Ross Predictable Revenue]] gives the playbook for one motion (outbound). *From Impossible to Inevitable* gives the operating system that surrounds the motion. Most companies have a motion that works at small scale; they stall because the surrounding operating system is missing.

> The motion gets you to $1M. The operating system gets you to $100M.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Nail a niche before expanding.** Broad targeting at $3M ARR stays at $3M ARR.
- **Predictable pipeline is the second ingredient.** If you cannot forecast 90-day pipeline, you do not have one.
- **The motion has to work for the median hire, not just the top AE.** Scalable sales is documented, trainable, enforceable.
- **Doubling deal size is an ICP exercise, not a pricing exercise.** The same product to the right customer is worth twice as much.
- **Hypergrowth is a 7-10 year arc.** Most founders quit during the slow 5 years.
- **Employee ownership is the lever above $10M ARR.** Founders who keep pulling decisions cap the company.
- **Define the destiny.** Without it, founders optimize for the next round, not the actual outcome.
