# Duke Quit

Annie Duke's second decision-quality book. Portfolio, October 2022. Subtitle: *The Power of Knowing When to Walk Away*. Where *Thinking in Bets* establishes decision quality, *Quit* extends the protocol to the most-avoided decision category: when to stop.

## Core Thesis

Quitting is a skill, not a failure. Most people quit too late, not too early, because the cognitive defaults push toward staying: sunk cost bias, identity attachment, optimism, fear of looking like a quitter. The result is that operators stay in losing positions for months or years past the point where the math says walk away. The book's job is to make quitting an explicit, pre-committed, normal-sounding decision rather than a moment of failure.

> The optimal time to quit is when you start to think about quitting. By the time you start to think about it, the odds are very high that you should have already quit.

## Key Extracts

### Quit and Grit Are Two Sides of the Same Skill

Duke pushes back on the cultural worship of grit. Grit without quit is just stubbornness applied indiscriminately. The skill is knowing which one to apply when. Persist on the things where the underlying math says persist. Walk away from the things where the math says walk away. Both judgments require the same machinery: explicit probability, explicit kill criteria, honest review.

### Sunk Cost as the Central Antagonist

Sunk cost bias is the cognitive habit of weighing prior investment in the decision about whether to continue. The money is spent. The time is spent. The relationship is built. Rational analysis says ignore all of it; only the forward expected value matters. Human cognition says the opposite, which is why people stay in failing startups, failing marriages, failing careers long past the point of viability.

> The expected value of an option is determined only by what is going to happen from this moment on. The past is a sunk cost.

### Kill Criteria, the Pre-Commitment

The operative protocol. Before starting, define the conditions under which you will quit. Write them down. Share them with the truthseeking pod. The pre-commitment removes in-the-moment rationalization, which is where sunk cost wins.

Example structure:

- "I will shut this down if revenue is below $X by date Y."
- "I will quit this job if I have not been promoted within Z months."
- "I will exit this market if churn does not drop below A% by quarter B."

The criteria do not have to be perfect. They have to exist. The act of pre-committing changes the in-the-moment decision from "should I keep going" (which sunk cost will always answer yes) to "did the pre-committed condition trigger" (which is a fact, not a feeling).

### Monkeys and Pedestals

Borrowed from Astro Teller, who runs X (formerly Google X). If the project is to teach a monkey to recite Shakespeare on a pedestal, do not start by building the pedestal. The pedestal is the easy, satisfying part. The monkey is the hard, blocking part. If the monkey is impossible, the pedestal was wasted.

The protocol: identify the hardest unknown in the project and attack it first. The fast failure is the cheap failure. Most projects fail because the team did the easy parts well and ran out of time before discovering the hard part was impossible.

### The In-the-Moment Trap

Duke documents the predictable failure mode: you set kill criteria in advance, then when the moment arrives you find reasons to ignore them. "It's almost working." "The market just needs another quarter." "If we just hire one more person..." The criteria existed to protect you from yourself in this moment, and you are now talking yourself out of the protection. The book's response: this is why the truthseeking pod exists. The pod can call the criterion. You may not be able to.

### Optionality and the Cost of Staying

Every day you stay in a losing position is a day you are not in a different position that might have worked. Duke calls this the "opportunity cost of grit". The cost of staying too long is not just the resources you continue to spend; it is the alternative paths you could not start because you were committed to this one. Quitting on time is what frees the capacity to do the next thing.

### Identity and the Quitting Stigma

Duke devotes a chapter to why quitting feels bad even when it is correct. Identity attachment: "I am the kind of person who finishes what I start." Social stigma: quitters are looked down on, persisters are admired, regardless of which decision was correct. The cultural script is upside down relative to the math. The corrective is to internalize that the right quit is a victory, not a defeat.
