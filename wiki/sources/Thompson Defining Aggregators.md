# Thompson Defining Aggregators

Thompson's 2017 essay refining Aggregation Theory. Provides the taxonomy: not all aggregators are equal. The hierarchy (Level 1 → Super-Aggregator) predicts which businesses will face the steepest competition and which have built unkillable structural advantages.

## Three Defining Characteristics

All three must be present for a business to be an aggregator:

### 1. Direct user relationship

> "Aggregators have a direct relationship with users. This may be a payment-based relationship, an account-based one, or simply one based on regular usage."

The user belongs to the aggregator. No intermediation.

### 2. Zero marginal costs

> "The goods 'sold' by an aggregator are digital and thus have zero marginal costs."

Plus zero distribution costs (internet) and near-zero transaction costs (automation). Cost of serving the next user is flat.

### 3. Demand-driven multi-sided networks

> "Customer acquisition costs decrease over time; marginal customers are attracted to the platform by virtue of the increasing number of suppliers."

The virtuous cycle. The longer the aggregator runs, the cheaper acquisition gets.

## The Four Levels

### Level 1: Supply Acquisition Cost Aggregators

**Example: Netflix**

Aggregator pays to acquire supply (content licensing, exclusive deals). Power from superior buying power; supply is finite and contested. Moat is operational scale; competitors can outbid.

### Level 2: Supply Transaction Cost Aggregators

**Example: Uber**

Supply is in principle infinite (any driver), but onboarding has friction (background checks, vehicle verification, regulatory compliance). Moat is the operational machinery for onboarding supply at scale.

### Level 3: Zero Supply Cost Aggregators

**Example: Google, social networks**

> "Suppliers...actively make themselves more easily searchable and discoverable."

Supply comes to the aggregator for free, because being discoverable is itself valuable. Aggregator pays nothing for supply; the supplier pays (attention, SEO labor, content production) for the chance to be aggregated.

Strongest structural position. Only cost is operating the platform.

### Super-Aggregators

> "Multi-sided markets with at least three sides, users, suppliers, and advertisers, and have zero marginal costs on all of them."

Only **Facebook** and **Google**. Users come for free (the product), suppliers come for free (to be discovered), advertisers come for free (to reach users). Three sides, all zero marginal cost.

Structurally unkillable. No cost lever a competitor can pull.

## Winner-Take-All Dynamic

> "Since the value of an aggregator to end users is continually increasing it is exceedingly difficult for competitors to take away users or win new ones."

The flywheel made explicit. Competitors face the inverse flywheel: fewer users → less value → harder to acquire → fewer users.

Two viable competitors at the Super-Aggregator level is unstable; one wins.

## Strategic Implications

**For existing businesses:**
- Identify which level you are. Level 1 (Netflix) faces ongoing supply cost pressure; Level 3 (Google) does not.
- Climb the levels when possible. Netflix originals are a Level 1 → Level 2 move.
- A Level 3 aggregator in your category is the existential competitor; everything else is noise.

**For new businesses:**
- Build for Level 3 from the start if the market allows it.
- Identify the multi-sided network early; design for it explicitly.
