# Helmer 7 Powers Book

Hamilton Helmer's 2016 book, the canonical text of his 7 Powers framework. 200 pages distilling ~40 years of strategy practice (Bain & Co, Strategy Capital, Adobe/Netflix/Spotify consulting). Adopted by Hollywood and Silicon Vally as a shared strategic vocabulary.

## Definition of Power

> Power is "a set of conditions creating the potential for persistent differential returns."

Three required elements:
1. **Persistent**, sustainable over time, not a temporary advantage
2. **Differential**, produces returns above the cost of capital
3. **Returns**, generates substantial free cash flow

Power manifests as: persistently high returns on capital, durable above-market growth, substantial free cash flow over extended periods.

## The 7 Powers (with Benefit + Barrier)

Every Power has TWO required components. If you have one without the other, you do not have a Power.

### 1. Scale Economies

**Definition:** A business where per-unit costs decline as production volume increases.

**Benefit:** Margin expansion, or lower consumer prices that drive volume growth (or a combination).

**Barrier:** Competitors must accept prolonged losses to gain enough market share to compete on cost. Requires substantial *relative* scale, not just absolute scale.

**Examples:** Netflix (content costs spread across larger subscriber base than competitors); Costco (passes savings to customers rather than expanding margins).

**Insight:** Scale only creates Power with meaningful relative advantage. Automotive OEMs and airlines are massive but lack differentiation. Size alone is not the Power.

### 2. Network Economies

**Definition:** A business where customer value increases as the installed base grows.

**Benefit:** Winner-take-all/most dynamics; insurmountable lead for first-scaled company; data advantage for monetization.

**Barrier:** Competitor must pay users to switch to a sub-scale network. Time and capital required becomes prohibitive.

**Examples:** Facebook (3 billion users create gravitational pull); LinkedIn (professional network switching is irrational).

**Insight:** Most durable Powers, but can be bounded by geography or segmentation. Network Power is undermined when poor execution breaks the user experience.

### 3. Counter-Positioning

**Definition:** A newcomer adopts a superior business model that incumbents won't match due to anticipated damage to existing business.

**Benefit:** Smaller players disrupt established competitors through wedge strategies.

**Barrier:** Incumbent unwillingness to inflict short-term pain on profitable core business; psychological bias toward status quo; agency problem (CEO won't sacrifice incentive structure).

**Examples:** Netflix DVD-by-mail vs Blockbuster (Blockbuster wouldn't cannibalize late-fee revenue); Vanguard index funds vs Fidelity active management (Fidelity wouldn't cannibalize high fees).

**Helmer's favorite Power.** Most underrated, particularly relevant for startups challenging incumbents.

### 4. Switching Costs

**Definition:** Value loss a customer would incur switching to an alternate supplier for future purchases.

**Benefit:** High customer retention; predictable, durable revenue; premium pricing; cross-sell opportunities.

**Barrier:** Prohibitively high financial, procedural, or relational costs of migration.

**Three Types:**
- **Financial:** ERP installation costs, custom integration sunk cost
- **Procedural:** Engineer training (AutoCAD, ERP, Salesforce CRM)
- **Relational:** Emotional connection to service providers

**Example:** Black Knight services ~50% of US first-lien mortgages; switching is "akin to ripping the lungs out of a bank's mortgage operation."

**Caveat:** Only applies to current customers. Acquiring new customers in mature markets remains difficult.

### 5. Branding

**Definition:** Durable attribution of higher value to objectively identical offerings due to historical seller information.

**Benefit:** Premium pricing power; outsized returns lasting decades.

**Barrier:** Time. Takes decades or longer to build. Cannot be recreated with capital alone. Economic hysteresis protects incumbents.

**Examples:** Coca-Cola and Tiffany's (built since the 1800s); Apple. Helmer estimates $100B couldn't recreate these brands.

**Risks:** Brand dilution (Halston going "downstream" to JC Penney destroyed the brand); changing consumer preferences; geographic limitations.

### 6. Cornered Resources

**Definition:** Preferential access at attractive terms to a coveted asset that can independently enhance value.

**Types:**
- **Physical:** Land, pipelines, mining claims
- **Intangible:** Patents, regulatory capture
- **Human:** Pixar's creative talent

**Benefit:** Irreplaceable competitive advantage; durable value creation.

**Barrier:** Asset uniqueness and scarcity; cannot be easily replicated.

**Requirements (5 conditions, all required):**
1. Idiosyncratic
2. Non-arbitraged
3. Transferable (creates value where it goes)
4. Ongoing
5. Sufficient to drive outsized economics

**Examples:** Texas Pacific Land (royalty streams); Pixar (top creative directors); tobacco companies (exclusive distribution).

### 7. Process Power

**Definition:** Embedded organizational and activity systems enabling lower costs or superior products, matchable only through extended commitment.

**Benefit:** Qualitative, lasting competitive advantage. The "secret sauce" deeply ingrained in company culture.

**Barrier:** Takes decades to develop; tacit rather than explicit knowledge; cannot be copied by studying competitors.

**Examples:** Toyota Production System (TPS); Constellation Software's VMS acquisition machine; GEICO and Progressive's operational systems.

**Distinction:** Process Power differs from mere "operational excellence" through emergence from prolonged bottom-up trial and error. It cannot be replicated by reading a book about it; only by living it.

## Strategy = Route to Power

Helmer's central thesis (paraphrased throughout the book):

> Strategy is the route to Power. Without Power, strategy is theater. Without strategy, Power is luck.

The implication for company-building: a strategy that does not lead to one of the 7 Powers is not actually a strategy. It is an operating plan dressed up. The discipline forces founders to name *which Power* they are building toward.

## The Industry-Level Layer

Beyond the 7 Powers (which operate at the firm level), Helmer adds:

> Industry economics + relative position = your firm's economic outcome.

A firm in a great industry with no Power still does poorly. A firm with strong Power in a structurally bad industry does poorly. Both must align.
