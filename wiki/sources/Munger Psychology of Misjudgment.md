# Munger Psychology of Misjudgment

The most-cited Munger work. Originally delivered in 1995 as a guest speech at Harvard Law School, then substantially revised and expanded by Munger himself for the 2005 publication of *Poor Charlie's Almanack*. The 2005 revision is the canonical text and is the version reproduced at fs.blog and elsewhere.

The speech is the foundational text on **applied behavioral psychology for operators**. Where [[Daniel Kahneman]] documents biases as a research program for academic publication, Munger compiles a checklist that a capital allocator or operating CEO can actually run before a decision. The 25 causes are named, examples-grounded, and explicitly cross-referenced with each other.

## Structure

Twenty-five standard causes of human misjudgment, each with:
- A name (most coined by Munger himself).
- A psychological mechanism.
- Real-world examples (often from law, business, advertising, cults, or military history).
- Cross-references to other causes that commonly stack with it.

The final section introduces the **Lollapalooza tendency**, which Munger considers the most important practical insight of the entire speech: extreme outcomes happen when multiple causes operate in the same direction at once.

## Selected Causes With Operator Examples

### 1. Reward-and-Punishment-Superresponse Tendency

The foundational cause. Munger considers incentives more powerful than almost any other force in human affairs, and consistently underestimated by people who think they understand them.

> Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives.

> Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome.

Operator application: every compensation system, every reporting structure, every metric you measure is shaping behavior whether you intended it or not. The FedEx night-shift story (paying by shift, not by package, fixed the throughput problem in one move) is the canonical illustration.

### 5. Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency

The brain conserves programming by resisting change, even when the original commitment is wrong. This is why cults work, why first-impressions stick, why people defend bad investments rather than sell them.

Operator application: every public commitment increases the cost of changing your mind. Make small commitments early; reserve the big public commitment for when you have actually decided.

### 10. Influence-from-Mere-Association Tendency

The brain links things that appear together, even when there is no causal connection. Coca-Cola with happy people. The CEO with the company performance. The new hire with the manager who recommended them.

> A man who is given a free ticket to attend a Hollywood movie premiere is more likely to enjoy the movie than the man who paid full price.

### 13. Overoptimism Tendency

People overestimate good outcomes and underestimate bad ones. The forecast is too rosy; the budget is too tight; the timeline is too aggressive.

> What a man wishes, that also will he believe.

(Munger quoting Demosthenes.)

Operator application: every forecast should be subjected to inversion. What does the failure mode look like? What is the base rate?

### 15. Social-Proof Tendency

People take cues from what others are doing, especially under uncertainty. This is the engine of bubbles, fads, and most fashion-driven hiring.

Operator application: the diligence shortcut "everyone else is doing it" is social proof masquerading as analysis.

### 25. Lollapalooza Tendency

The capstone. When multiple biases push in the same direction at once, the outcome is extreme rather than additive. This is Munger's explanation for cult dynamics, casino design, the Tupperware party, the dot-com bubble, and most market manias.

> When I describe the Lollapalooza effect, I am referring to the extreme consequences that come from the confluence of several psychological tendencies acting in favor of a particular outcome.

The diagnostic skill is not recognizing the single bias. It is recognizing the **stack**.

## The Checklist Use

Munger's central operating advice: use the 25 causes as a checklist you run before any major decision. Which of these is likely to be operating? Which combination might be producing a Lollapalooza? The checklist is a System-2 override of the System-1 quick judgment.

This is the most direct operational connection between Munger and [[Daniel Kahneman]]. Kahneman documented the biases as research. Munger packaged them as a checklist.

## Principles Cited From This Source

(Will be added to [[Operating Principles]] Mental Models section.)

- **Never underestimate the power of incentives.** The first and most-violated cause of misjudgment.
- **Multiple biases stacking in the same direction produce extreme outcomes.** The Lollapalooza is the diagnostic for cults, manias, and casino design.
- **Run the 25 causes as a checklist before major decisions.** System-2 override of System-1 reflex.
- **Inconsistency-avoidance defends bad commitments.** Reserve the public commitment for the actual decision.
- **Social proof is not analysis.** "Everyone else is doing it" is the diagnostic, not the answer.
