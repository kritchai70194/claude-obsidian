# Maples Live in the Future

The Floodgate essay that names Maples's most-cited heuristic for seed-stage founder evaluation. Available free on floodgate.com in multiple versions across 2018-2023, restated in [[Maples Pattern Breakers]] (2024). The essay is one of the canonical short-form pieces on how to identify breakout founders before the market has noticed them.

## Central Thesis

> Most founders try to imagine the future. The best founders already live in it.

The essay opens by separating two kinds of founders. The first kind reads industry reports, listens to podcasts, attends conferences, and tries to imagine what the future will be. The second kind is already living the future, by accident or by inclination, before anyone has labelled it. Their habit, their lifestyle, their work environment IS the future being broadly adopted.

The Maples claim: the second kind reliably produces the breakthrough companies, and the first kind reliably does not.

## Why Living Beats Imagining

The argument is informational. The future is not yet legible to the consensus market; if it were, it would already be the present. The investor who tries to forecast the future is competing with every other investor reading the same reports. The investor who finds founders already living the future is harvesting private information that the rest of the market cannot access without changing their daily life.

Maples generalises: if you cannot point to specific habits, tools, environments, or routines the founder uses that the rest of the world does not yet use, the founder is imagining the future, not living it. The diagnostic is empirical and physical, not rhetorical.

## The Brian Chesky / Airbnb Example

> Brian and Joe lived the host experience before they built the company.

Before Airbnb was a category, Chesky and Joe Gebbia hosted strangers on airbeds in their San Francisco apartment. The hosting was not market research. It was their actual living situation, dictated by their actual rent and their actual financial pressure. They lived the future of distributed peer-to-peer hospitality before anyone had named it. By the time they raised seed, they had operational experience the rest of the market lacked, including the painful texture of cleaning, of meeting strangers at the door, of trust failures, of pricing dynamics.

The seed investor's read: this team has private information about a future the market has not yet seen, and that private information is structural, not opinion-based. See [[Brian Chesky]] for the canonical Founder Mode case.

## The Anne Wojcicki / 23andMe Example

> Anne was inside the genomics conversation before consumer genomics existed.

Wojcicki was embedded in the genomics research community through marriage and through her own healthcare investing work before 23andMe was a category. Her daily conversations, her network, her reading were already structured around personal genomics. When she founded 23andMe, the company was not a strategic bet on a forecasted market. It was the externalisation of a life she was already living.

## The General Rule

The essay generalises from the case studies to a portable seed-stage heuristic:

> If the founder's daily life is indistinguishable from a typical professional's daily life, the founder is not living in the future. They are imagining it.

The empirical tests Maples applies:
- What does the founder use every day that most people do not?
- What environments does the founder spend time in that most people do not?
- What conversations is the founder having that most people are not?
- What problems does the founder personally feel that most people do not yet feel?

A founder who can answer all four with specific, observable detail is a candidate for backability. A founder who answers with abstractions is imagining, not living.

## The Trap: Imagining Founders Sound More Polished

The essay warns that the imagining founder typically pitches better than the living-the-future founder. The imagining founder has read the reports, knows the buzzwords, has rehearsed the deck. The living-the-future founder is often awkward, technical, focused on a problem that sounds small, unable to articulate the broader thesis cleanly.

The Maples claim: this is exactly backward from how it should be read. Polish in the pitch is weakly correlated with private information. The unrefined founder describing a specific lived problem is much more likely to be sitting on a real future than the polished founder describing a market trend.
