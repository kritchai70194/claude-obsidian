# Roberge Sales Acceleration Formula

Mark Roberge's *The Sales Acceleration Formula: Using Data, Technology, and Inbound Selling to Go from $0 to $100 Million* (Wiley, March 2015). The book is structured as the four-step formula Roberge built and ran at HubSpot from 2007 to 2013: hire the same way, train the same way, manage the same way, generate demand the same way. The thesis is that sales is an engineering problem with measurable inputs and outputs, not an art that depends on the heroics of individual reps.

## The Four-Step Formula

Each step is a function. Inputs you control. Outputs you measure. Iterate.

1. **The Sales Hiring Formula.** Identify the predictable traits of a top-performing salesperson at your company. Score every candidate against those traits. Validate the rubric by tracking which traits actually correlate with 12-month quota attainment.
2. **The Sales Training Formula.** Build a curriculum every new rep goes through. Test for mastery. Certify before they touch a real deal. The training, not the trainer, is the asset.
3. **The Sales Management Formula.** Run a consistent coaching cadence. Measure leading indicators (activity, pipeline coverage), not just lagging ones (closed revenue). Coach to the bottleneck stage in each rep's funnel.
4. **The Demand Generation Formula.** Build a lead-generation engine that produces a predictable monthly volume at a known cost. Match the demand machine to the sales capacity.

## Sales Hiring: The Rubric

Roberge's five traits that predicted HubSpot sales success:

> Coachability, curiosity, prior success, work ethic, intelligence.

Each scored on a defined scale during the interview process. Each interviewer scores independently. The aggregate score, not the gut feel, drives the hire/no-hire decision.

The validation discipline matters more than the traits themselves. Six months in, Roberge would correlate each trait's interview score against the rep's actual quota attainment. Traits that correlated stayed in the rubric. Traits that did not got dropped. The rubric was a living model, updated annually.

> The reps who scored highest on coachability outperformed the reps who scored highest on prior sales experience.

This finding was counter to the prevailing hiring wisdom of the time, which prioritized track-record SaaS reps. HubSpot's market (selling new-category software to SMBs via inbound) rewarded reps who could learn the unfamiliar product and motion fast, not reps with the strongest closing reflex from a different category.

## Sales Training: Curriculum as Asset

Every new HubSpot rep went through the same 30-day onboarding. Product training (you must build a marketing campaign in HubSpot before you sell it). Persona training (you role-play the buyer before you call one). Process training (you certify on each stage of the sales motion).

> Train every rep on the same material, in the same order, with the same certification gates. The trainer should be interchangeable. The curriculum is the asset.

The result was ramp-time consistency. New reps hit productivity at month four, not month nine. Variance across reps was a training defect, not a hiring defect.

## Sales Management: Coaching to the Bottleneck

Roberge taught managers to diagnose each rep's funnel separately. Where in lead → MQL → SQL → opportunity → closed-won is this specific rep losing the most? Coach there.

> The reps who attain quota and the reps who miss quota usually fail at different stages of the funnel. Generic coaching is malpractice.

Activity metrics (calls, emails, meetings booked) are leading indicators. Pipeline coverage (3x quota in qualified opportunities) is a leading indicator. Closed revenue is the lagging indicator. Managers who only measured closed revenue learned about problems three months too late.

## The SDR / AE Split

HubSpot ran the specialized model from early on. Sales Development Reps (often called BDRs at other companies) handled prospecting and qualification. Account Executives handled the close. The split is operationally cheaper (SDRs are paid less, run higher volume) and qualitatively better (each role builds deeper skill in its specialization).

> Asking one rep to prospect and close is asking them to context-switch between volume work and consultative work all day. Both suffer.

Roberge's lead-routing rule of thumb: an AE should not spend more than 20% of their time on lead generation activity. If they do, the SDR layer is understaffed.

## Demand Generation: The Inbound Engine

HubSpot built its sales engine on top of its own inbound marketing motion. Content (blog posts, ebooks, tools) drove organic search traffic. Forms captured leads. Marketing automation scored and routed them. By the time a lead hit an SDR, the SDR knew which content the lead consumed and at what intensity.

> The best sales motion is one where the lead arrives already half-educated about the problem. Inbound makes that the default state.

The economic claim: inbound leads cost less than outbound leads at scale (no per-call labor) and close at higher rates (the buyer is self-selected into the funnel). For HubSpot, the inbound engine was the moat.

## Daily and Weekly Metrics

Roberge's operating cadence at HubSpot:

- **Daily**: each rep tracks activity (calls, emails, meetings). Manager scans the dashboard. Outliers get a 1:1 ping.
- **Weekly**: pipeline review per rep. Each opportunity's stage, next step, and expected close date. Stuck deals get diagnosis.
- **Monthly**: quota attainment, win rate, average deal size, sales cycle length. Trends, not just snapshots.
- **Quarterly**: cohort hiring analysis. Which interview rubric scores correlated with which 12-month quota outcomes? Update the rubric.
