# Blank Lean Launchpad

The 10-week course Blank developed at Stanford in 2011 to translate Customer Development from a book methodology into a forced-practice curriculum. Taught at Stanford, Berkeley, Columbia, UCSF, and adopted by the National Science Foundation as the basis for the I-Corps program. As of the mid-2020s, the Lean LaunchPad and I-Corps formats have together trained more than 500,000 scientists, engineers, and founders worldwide. The curriculum is the operationalization that closed the loop between Blank's books and on-the-ground founder behavior.

## Why the Curriculum Matters

The books contain the methodology. The blog refines it. But Blank's most consequential observation about founders is that they read the books and then skip the discipline. The curriculum solves this by making the discipline non-optional.

The format is built around a single forcing function:

> Interview 100 customers in 10 weeks. Present your hypothesis updates every week. Iterate the business model canvas in public.

The number (100) and the cadence (weekly presentations) are deliberately calibrated. Less than 100 interviews and founders generalize from too small a sample. Less than weekly presentations and founders go heads-down on product instead of customers. The forcing function is the methodology.

## Curriculum Structure

A typical Lean LaunchPad 10-week course:

- **Week 0:** team formation; initial Business Model Canvas (BMC) with all hypotheses explicit.
- **Weeks 1-2:** Customer hypothesis interviews. Who exactly is the customer? Update BMC weekly.
- **Weeks 3-4:** Problem hypothesis interviews. What problem do they have and how is it currently solved?
- **Weeks 5-6:** Solution / Value Proposition hypothesis. MVP demos to candidate customers. Iterate.
- **Weeks 7-8:** Channel and Revenue hypothesis. Test sales motion and willingness to pay.
- **Week 9:** Market sizing and competitive analysis under the now-validated assumptions.
- **Week 10:** Final pitch. Hypothesis grid annotated with what was confirmed, what was falsified, what pivoted.

Each weekly presentation is a forced public update. Teams that skipped interviews cannot fake the presentation. Teams that did interviews have something specific to report. The format selects for actual Customer Development practice.

## The NSF I-Corps Implementation

NSF adopted the Lean LaunchPad in 2011 as the I-Corps (Innovation Corps) program. Scientists and engineers with NSF-funded research who wanted to commercialize their work were required to complete the curriculum. The program has produced:

- More than 2,500 teams trained through the national cohort.
- More than 1,400 startups launched from those teams.
- Adoption by the Department of Defense, NIH, NASA, and the Department of Energy, each with domain-specific variants.

The I-Corps data is the most cited evidence that Customer Development generalizes beyond consumer/SaaS startups. Scientists who had never interviewed a customer in their lives produced workable business models inside 10 weeks. The curriculum is the variable; the methodology is the constant.

## Why It Works for Technical Founders Specifically

Blank has argued repeatedly that the Lean LaunchPad format works disproportionately well for technical founders for three reasons:

1. **Hypothesis testing is the native mode of scientists and engineers.** Customer Development reframes business questions as experiments, which technical founders find natural.
2. **The weekly presentation cadence externalizes the search.** Technical founders default to building. The forced weekly update makes building without customer signal visibly unacceptable.
3. **The methodology is teachable.** It is a sequence of operations, not a personality trait. A technical founder who would never have started a sales conversation unprompted will do 100 interviews if the curriculum requires it.

The Hacking for Defense and Hacking for Diplomacy variants extend this further: defense / state-department problem owners pose real unsolved problems, and student teams run Customer Development against those problems. The curriculum becomes the bridge between technical research and operational application.

## What the Curriculum Is Not

It is not a startup accelerator. It is not a pitch competition. It does not fund teams. Its single output is methodology fluency: at the end of 10 weeks, the team either has a tested business model or has falsified the original hypothesis and learned why. Both outcomes count as success by the curriculum's metrics.

This is itself a Blank frame: in a search-phase organization, learning is the deliverable. Revenue and product come later, and only if the search succeeds.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Methodology requires a forcing function.** Reading the books is necessary but insufficient.
- **100 interviews in 10 weeks is the empirically-tuned cadence for Customer Discovery.** Fewer is too small a sample; longer dilutes learning.
- **Weekly public presentations externalize the search.** They prevent the default drift back into heads-down building.
- **In search-phase organizations, learning is the deliverable.** A falsified hypothesis is a successful experiment.
- **Customer Development generalizes beyond software startups.** The I-Corps data is the proof.
