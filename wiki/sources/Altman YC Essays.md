# Altman YC Essays

Two essays that together form Altman's pre-OpenAI YC-era playbook. "Startup Playbook" (2015) is the long-form summary of what Altman taught during his YC presidency, structured as the canonical reading for an incoming YC founder. "How to Succeed with a Startup" (2019) is the lecture-form distillation, delivered as a Y Combinator talk and later transcribed. Together they are the most-cited Altman text outside the OpenAI essays.

URLs:
- https://playbook.samaltman.com/
- https://blog.samaltman.com/how-to-succeed-with-a-startup

## Why This Source

Altman wrote these as YC president, not as OpenAI CEO. The lens is generic startup operation, not AI-specific. That is the value. It is the bridge between the [[Paul Graham]] essay tradition and the foundation-model era. Founders in 2026 still operate on this playbook even when their company is an AI-native one.

The two essays repeat each other deliberately. Where they overlap, the message is high-conviction. Where they diverge, the 2019 version is the more refined claim.

## Core Theses

### Idea matters more than people think

The 2010s consensus was "execution matters more than idea." Altman pushes back. The right idea is what makes execution possible. A mediocre idea executed well is a mediocre company. A great idea executed adequately can still be a great company. Most founders pick ideas too quickly and then defend them too long.

### Movement, not business

The best companies feel like movements to the people inside them and the people who care about them. Mission-orientation is not soft. It is the recruiting engine. It is the retention engine. It is the reason customers tolerate the rough edges of early product. A business without a mission is just an arbitrage and will be competed away.

### Make something a small number of users love

The 2015 framing. Better to have 100 users who love the product than 100,000 who like it. Love drives word of mouth. Like does not. Love is the leading indicator of product-market fit. Like is the lagging indicator of an okay product in a slow death.

### Hard problems attract good people

Counterintuitive recruiting argument. Smart people want to work on hard problems. A startup that frames its problem as ambitious and difficult will attract better people than one that frames it as easy and incremental. The implication: lower your ambition and you lower your talent pool.

### Have a strong worldview

The structural requirement of an outlier outcome. You cannot build a generationally important company by being right about things most people are right about. You have to be right about something most people are wrong about. This is the [[Peter Thiel]] secrets thesis restated in YC-friendly language.

### Have a very high level of ambition

Altman's most-repeated counsel. The founder who aims for a $100M outcome usually gets less. The founder who aims for a $10B outcome sometimes gets it and frequently gets more than the $100M founder. Ambition is the input variable that compounds.

## Direct Passages

On mission:

> The best companies are almost always mission-oriented.

On love vs like:

> You want to make something that a small number of users love, not something that a large number of users like.

On ambition and difficulty:

> It is often easier to start a hard startup than an easy startup. People want to be part of something exciting and feel that their work matters. If you are working on an incremental product, it is hard to get the best people to come work on it.

On the idea:

> The most important thing is to have a great idea. The way ideas turn into great companies is by being non-obvious, important, and big.

On the founder:

> A great founder is willing to look stupid. A founder who is unwilling to look stupid will not be able to do the things great founders do.

On growth as the proxy for everything:

> The really good companies grow. They might grow fast or slow, but they grow.

## Where Altman Diverges from Graham

[[Paul Graham]] anchors the YC essay tradition. Altman extends it in two places.

First, on ambition. Graham is more measured. Altman says aim higher than the room recommends. Graham says ramen-profitable. Altman says ramen-profitable is the floor, not the goal.

Second, on the idea. Graham was famous for "ideas are cheap, founders matter." Altman pushes back, saying the right idea is what unlocks the rest of the playbook. This is a meaningful update.

## Where the Playbook Holds for AI-Native Companies

The mission, love, ambition, worldview, and hard-problem-attracts-talent claims all hold cleanly for AI-native companies. The "make something a small number of users love" frame applies especially well, because AI products often have a small group of power users whose dependence is the leading indicator of broader adoption.

## Where the Playbook Needs Adjustment for AI-Native Companies

Capital structure. The YC-era playbook assumed seed rounds in the low millions. AI-native companies often need $50M+ before product-market fit because of compute. The "ramen-profitable" floor does not translate.

Defensibility. The YC playbook leaned on network effects, brand, and switching costs. For AI companies, the model-as-commodity dynamic and the wrapper-vs-platform-vs-workflow trichotomy are not addressed in the 2015 or 2019 essays. This is the gap [[Altman AGI Economics]] and [[Altman Interviews Synthesis]] start to fill.
