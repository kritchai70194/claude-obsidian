# Bezos Regret Minimization Framework

The canonical [[Jeff Bezos]] decision-making frame, formulated in 1994 when he was deciding whether to leave D.E. Shaw to start Amazon. Bezos has told this story consistently across many interviews; the framework is one of the few self-named tools he attributes to himself ("only a nerd would call it" a regret minimization framework).

## Headline Takeaway

When the analytical case is balanced (pros and cons roughly cancel), the regret asymmetry between *trying-and-failing* and *not-trying-at-all* becomes the deciding factor. Not-trying compounds with time; failure can be processed and moved past. The framework is most useful precisely when normal decision tools say "either choice is fine."

## The Framework (verbatim Bezos)

> "The framework I found, which made the decision incredibly easy, was what I called, which only a nerd would call, a 'regret minimization framework.'"

> "I want to project myself forward to age 80 and say, 'Okay, now I'm looking back on my life. I want to have minimized the number of regrets I have.'"

## How He Applied It (1994 Amazon decision)

> "I knew that when I was 80 I was not going to regret having tried this. I knew that if I failed I wouldn't regret that, but I knew the one thing I might regret is not ever having tried. I knew that that would haunt me every day."

## The Decision Criterion

The framework distinguishes:

1. **Regret of attempting** something that fails. Processable. The failure happened; you learned; you move on.
2. **Regret of not attempting** something at all. Permanent. The counterfactual lives in your head forever.

These are not symmetric. Most cost-benefit analyses treat them as equally weighted; Bezos's frame says they're not.

## When the Frame Bites

Useful when:

- The analytical case is balanced (pro/con lists cancel out)
- The decision is genuinely high-stakes (career change, major bet, irreversible choice)
- The opportunity has a closing window (the internet in 1994; the moment passes)

Not useful when:

- Reversible decisions (two-way doors, see [[Bezos 2015 Letter]])
- Decisions where the analytical case is clear in either direction
- Decisions that feel high-stakes but aren't actually irreversible

## Context: How This Sits With Two-Way Doors

The frame complements rather than contradicts the [[Bezos 2015 Letter|Type 1 / Type 2 decision framework]]. Regret minimization is for **Type 1** (one-way door) decisions where you've already noticed the asymmetry. For Type 2 decisions, the speed cost of using a heavy framework exceeds the value of the framework.

Bezos's life pattern is roughly: use Type 2 for the many small reversible choices; use regret minimization for the few big irreversible ones. Don't mix.
