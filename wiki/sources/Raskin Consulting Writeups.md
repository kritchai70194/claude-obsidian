# Raskin Consulting Writeups

Synthesis of Andy Raskin's process-oriented writing and interviews: the consulting blog, podcast appearances (First Round, SaaStr, Lenny's Podcast guest spots, B2B Growth Show), and various conference talks. Where the [[Raskin Strategic Narrative]] essays define the 5-part framework and the [[Raskin Case Studies]] show worked examples, this body of work covers the **process**: how a strategic narrative actually gets discovered, drafted, tested, and installed inside a company.

## The Discovery Workshop

Raskin's standard engagement opens with a discovery workshop spanning two to four weeks. The output is a draft strategic narrative the company can test against customers and refine.

Workshop structure:

1. **Customer interviews first.** Twenty to thirty conversations with current customers, lost prospects, and won prospects. The questions are deliberately open. "What was happening in your world that made you start looking for a solution?" "What were you using before?" "What changed?" The goal is to surface the world change in the customer's own words, not the company's.

2. **Executive interviews second.** Founder, CEO, head of sales, head of product. Same question structure, but with attention to where the executives' frame matches the customer's frame and where it diverges. Divergence is the most important signal; it tells you where the internal narrative has drifted from the buyer reality.

3. **Pattern extraction.** Across the customer interviews, what is the recurring phrase or frame that surfaces? What named change in the world do customers describe in their own words? This is the seed of the strategic narrative.

4. **Draft and test.** Raskin drafts the 5-part arc. The company tests it on three to five real prospects in sales conversations and observes reactions. Adjust based on what lands and what does not.

5. **Install.** The narrative is rolled into sales decks, investor decks, recruiting collateral, the website, and internal all-hands content. The CEO becomes the canonical carrier.

## Why Bottom-Up Beats Top-Down

Raskin's most repeated process claim: **the world change must come from customer interviews, not from the CEO's whiteboard**.

The failure mode: the CEO drafts a strategic narrative based on what they think the world change is. The narrative describes a world the CEO recognizes (often informed by industry-analyst reports, conferences, and peer conversations). The narrative is internally consistent and rhetorically polished. The sales team uses it. Prospects do not respond.

The diagnosis: the world the CEO described is not the world the buyer recognizes. The CEO is operating in a vendor-side echo chamber. The buyer's world is shaped by their own daily operating reality, which the CEO has been removed from for years.

The fix: build the narrative bottom-up from customer interviews. Use the customer's language. Name the change the customer would name, not the change the CEO believes is happening. The narrative is then a mirror the buyer recognizes, not a pitch the buyer must be convinced of.

## The Role of the CEO as Narrative Carrier

Once the narrative is installed, the CEO is its canonical carrier. Not the head of marketing, not the head of sales. The CEO.

Raskin's reasoning: the narrative is a strategic claim about the world. Only the CEO has the authority to make that claim credibly. When the CEO carries it personally (in keynotes, on podcasts, in all-hands meetings, in investor updates), the rest of the company picks it up. When the CEO delegates it to marketing, the narrative becomes "messaging" and loses force.

The operational implication: the strategic narrative is a CEO-level discipline, not a marketing-team task. It is one of the very few responsibilities Raskin argues the CEO cannot delegate.

## The Most Common Failure Modes

Raskin's recurring catalog of how narrative engagements fail:

**Tagline confusion.** The company asks Raskin for a "tagline" or "positioning statement". They receive a 5-part arc and try to compress it to a sentence. The result loses the structure and becomes a slogan.

**Product-first inertia.** The sales team has muscle memory of opening with the product. Even after narrative installation, sellers default to product-first pitching. Requires deliberate retraining and call coaching.

**Soft world change.** The CEO is uncomfortable with provocation and softens the world change to something undeniable but uninteresting. "The world is changing rapidly" is not a strategic narrative. "Messaging is replacing email" is.

**Skipping obstacles.** The natural sales instinct is to skip obstacles because they look like negatives. Without obstacles, the narrative loses the trust beat and becomes a sales pitch dressed as a story.

**Hero confusion.** The CEO wants the company to be the hero of the story. Raskin pushes back: the buyer is the hero; the company is the guide. CEOs sometimes resist this because it feels self-effacing. Raskin's counter: it is more effective, and the company captures more value precisely because it does not insist on being the hero.

**Drift over time.** Even successfully installed narratives drift as the company scales. New executives bring new vocabularies. New product launches re-introduce product-first framing. Raskin's recommendation: re-test the narrative against new customers every 12-18 months and refresh as needed.

## On Workshops vs Documents

Raskin distinguishes between **narrative documents** (artifacts that capture the 5-part arc) and **narrative discipline** (the operating habit of running every external communication through the arc). The document is necessary but not sufficient. Companies that have a polished narrative document but no operating discipline drift back to product-first pitching within months.

The discipline is: every external communication of meaningful length (keynote, sales call, investor deck, all-hands content, recruiting interview) walks the arc at least implicitly. Over time, the arc becomes the default cognitive structure for thinking about the company.

## On the Multi-Use Property in Practice

Across consulting writeups, Raskin gives operational guidance on how the same narrative is adapted across surfaces:

- **Investor pitch**: emphasis on market size in beat 1, stakes in beat 2, evidence at the close
- **Sales pitch**: emphasis on Promised Land (beat 3) and obstacles (beat 4); the parts of the arc closest to the buyer's immediate decision
- **Recruiting pitch**: emphasis on world change (beat 1) and mission (woven through Promised Land); the parts that attract talent
- **All-hands content**: emphasis on stakes and Promised Land; the parts that maintain internal alignment
- **Analyst briefing**: emphasis on category creation; how the world change names a new category the analyst should track

The structural backbone is identical across all surfaces. Only the emphasis shifts.

## Principles Cited From This Source

- **Discover the world change bottom-up from customer interviews, not top-down from the CEO.** The most-cited process claim.
- **The CEO is the canonical carrier of the strategic narrative.** Not delegable to marketing.
- **The narrative is a discipline, not a document.** A polished doc without operating discipline drifts back to product-first within months.
- **Re-test the narrative every 12-18 months.** Even successful narratives drift as the company scales.
- **Adapt emphasis across surfaces; never change the structural backbone.** Same arc, different beats foregrounded.
