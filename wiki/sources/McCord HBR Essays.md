# McCord HBR Essays

Patty McCord's *Harvard Business Review* essays, anchored by the January-February 2014 cover essay "How Netflix Reinvented HR" with follow-up pieces in the years after. The essays compress the [[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]] and what would become [[McCord Powerful]] into the HBR short-form length. Widely circulated as the executive-summary version of the Netflix model.

## Provenance

- Author: Patty McCord
- Year: 2014 (anchor essay), 2017-2020 (follow-ups)
- Publisher: Harvard Business Review
- Fidelity: extracts (HBR essays widely available)

## The Eight Lessons (from "How Netflix Reinvented HR")

The anchor essay is structured around eight lessons. Each is a short section with a Netflix-specific story.

### 1. Hire, Reward, and Tolerate Only Fully Formed Adults

The first move. Most companies write policy as if every employee were the worst employee they have ever had. The cost is borne by the median employee, who is treated as a potential problem rather than a professional. McCord:

> If you're careful to hire people who will put the company's interests first, who understand and support the desire for a high-performance workplace, 97 percent of your employees will do the right thing.

The remaining 3 percent are dealt with directly, not preempted by company-wide policy. Netflix removed the vacation policy, the expense policy, and most of the HR rulebook on this principle.

### 2. Tell the Truth About Performance

The case against the annual performance review:

> Annual reviews are too ritualistic and too infrequent. And 360-degree reviews, where employees solicit feedback from peers, often become popularity contests.

Replace the review with continuous, candid conversations. Managers tell people the truth about how they are doing as they go. The Keeper Test becomes the calibration anchor. The annual ritual is a substitute for the harder daily work.

### 3. Managers Own the Job of Creating Great Teams

HR does not own the team. The manager does. Managers should hire, develop, calibrate, and exit their reports as the central part of the management job, not as something delegated to HR or done once a year in a review cycle. HR's role is to amplify the manager, not to substitute for them.

### 4. Leaders Own the Job of Creating the Company Culture

Culture is not a poster on the wall or a values workshop. It is the set of behaviors leadership rewards and tolerates. If the CEO tolerates a rude executive, the company is one where rudeness is tolerated. The Culture Deck described actual Netflix behavior, not aspirational behavior; that is why it spread.

### 5. Good Talent Managers Think Like Businesspeople and Innovators First, and Like HR People Last

The reframe McCord pushes hardest in this essay. HR is usually organized as a compliance and benefits-administration function. The talent leader Netflix wanted thinks first about what the business needs to win and works backward to the people decisions that get it there. The HR craft (employment law, comp design, benefits) is necessary but not the primary frame.

### 6. Get Rid of HR Cliches

McCord lists the cliches she removed at Netflix:

- "Employee engagement surveys" as the proxy for whether the culture is working
- "High-potential lists" as the secret list of people who get fast-tracked
- "Performance improvement plans" as the standard pre-firing ritual
- "Talent reviews" as the annual ratings exercise

Each was replaced by something more direct. The honest conversation replaces the engagement survey. Continuous calibration replaces the high-potential list. The honest conversation about fit (and generous severance if needed) replaces the PIP. The Keeper Test replaces the talent review.

### 7. If We Want Only Performance As, We Have to Be Willing to Say Goodbye to Bs

The hardest lesson. The high-performance team is constantly being recomposed as the company's stage changes. The kindness is the honest, early conversation; the cruelty is keeping someone in a role they are failing.

The Keeper Test surfaces the mismatch. Generous severance closes it.

### 8. Challenge as Strongly as You Praise

Disagreement is part of the work. The norm is to challenge the leader's idea in the meeting, before the decision, with real arguments. The norm against "yes-culture" is enforced by the leadership team modeling the challenge themselves. Connects to [[Andy Grove]]'s pre-decision dissent / post-decision commitment frame.

## On Severance as Part of the Job

The essay's most-cited operational shift. Netflix's severance package was deliberately generous: months of pay, extended healthcare, clean references, time to find the next role. McCord's argument:

> Taking care of people in transition is part of treating them like adults.

The pattern: hire with rigor, calibrate continuously, run the Keeper Test, when the mismatch surfaces have the honest conversation, write the severance check generously. The alumni produced by this pattern become referrers and references; alumni produced by the alternative pattern (drag the conversation out, write a stingy check, send a cold reference) become the company's loudest critics.

## What the Essay Got Right in Hindsight

The 2014 essay made claims that have aged unevenly. The strongest claims, which have held up:

- The annual review is a poor substitute for continuous calibration.
- HR organized for compliance is organized against the business.
- High-performance teams require recomposition as stages change.
- Severance done well is a hiring tool.

The claims that have been complicated by hindsight, addressed in [[McCord Conference Talks]]:

- The "freedom and responsibility" model is harder to scale than the essay implies.
- The model assumes a hiring rigor that most companies cannot actually run.
- The "no vacation policy" lesson has been over-quoted relative to its importance.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Hire fully formed adults; 97 percent will do the right thing.**
- **Replace the annual review with continuous honest conversations.**
- **Managers own the team; HR amplifies, does not substitute.**
- **Leaders own the culture; tolerance is the actual signal.**
- **Talent leaders think as businesspeople first, HR people last.**
- **Get rid of HR cliches; the honest conversation is the work.**
- **The honest conversation about fit is more humane than keeping someone in a failing role.**
- **Challenge as strongly as you praise; the norm against yes-culture is enforced by leadership behavior.**
- **Severance is part of the job; alumni done right are referrers.**
