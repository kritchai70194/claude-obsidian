# Stripe Annual Letter 2024

Co-authored by [[Patrick Collison]] and [[John Collison]]. Published February 2025 covering fiscal 2024. The single best public source where John's voice is co-equal with Patrick's. The annual letter format is itself a Collison signature: deliberately written, long-form, addressed to "the Stripe community" rather than shareholders (Stripe is private).

## Headline Takeaway

The letter answers two operator questions explicitly: how did Stripe grow so fast in 2024, and why is the long-horizon profitability discipline non-negotiable? Both answers point back to investments made years earlier (ML/AI in the products) and to a structural reinvestment policy (durable profit, then plow back into R&D at a rate higher than comparable companies).

## Key Extracts

### Opening Frame (volume + thesis)

> "Businesses on Stripe generated $1.4 trillion in total payment volume in 2024, up 38% from the prior year, and reaching a scale equivalent to around 1.3% of global GDP. We attribute this year's rapid growth in part to our long-standing investments in building machine learning and artificial intelligence into our products."

### Profitability as Fuel for R&D

> "Stripe was profitable in 2024, and we expect to be so in 2025 and beyond. Durable profitability allows us to plow back much of our operating earnings into research and development. In each of the last six years, Stripe has reinvested a much higher proportion of our earnings in R&D than any comparable company."

Profit is not the goal. Profit is the *mechanism* that lets the company keep investing without external dilution or pressure.

### Customer Quality as Strategy

> "In aggregate, the revenue that businesses process on Stripe is growing seven times faster than that of all companies in the S&P 500."

> "Stripe customers share one important characteristic: outsized growth."

Customer selection IS the product strategy. Stripe doesn't optimize for customer count; it optimizes for customers whose own growth lifts Stripe's volume.

### Creative Destruction (the historical frame)

> "Of the 500 companies in the S&P 500 at its inception in 1957, only 53 remain in the index today. (More than half of that remaining 53 use Stripe.)"

> "Back in 1957, companies could expect to remain in the index for 61 years. In 1980, the average tenure was 36 years. Today, it's just under 20 years. Enduring businesses are increasingly rare."

The historical frame is characteristic of the Collisons (see also [[Collison Fast]] and [[Collison Questions]]). They reason from long time horizons, then point to where Stripe sits in that frame.

### Specific Product Investment Bearing Fruit

> "Stripe Billing is an example of such an investment bearing fruit. Over the last year, Billing was designated as a market leader in its own right. As we forecast in our 2023 letter, Stripe's Revenue and Finance Automation Suite, with Billing at its core, has now passed a $500 million revenue run rate. Billing is being used by more than 300,000 companies, manages nearly 200 million active subscriptions, and is emerging as the revenue engine of the AI era."

A forecast made in a prior letter is delivered on. The continuity across years is itself a discipline; readers can check.

### Customer Examples (the proof-by-list)

The letter inventories the breadth of Stripe's customer base across centuries:

- Oxford (founded 1096)
- Church of England (1534)
- Gaelic Athletic Association (1884)
- Hershey (1894)
- PepsiCo (1898)
- Comcast Universal Ads (1963)
- NVIDIA (1993)
- Perplexity (2022)

The argument by enumeration is a Collison move (see [[Collison Fast]]). The list IS the argument: institutions across nine centuries adopt the same infrastructure.

## What This Brain Takes From It

- **Durable profitability funds R&D reinvestment.** A specific operating principle worth adding: profit is the mechanism, not the goal. Stripe's R&D rate is the lever; profit makes the lever pullable.
- **Customer selection as strategy.** Stripe optimizes for customer growth-rate, not customer count. This is a different mental model than most startup-stage businesses use.
- **Long-horizon framing via historical comparison.** The S&P 500 tenure decay (61 → 36 → 20 years) is a quantitative version of why durability is hard. Companion to [[Collison Fast]] in the toolkit.
- **Make prior forecasts checkable.** The 2024 letter explicitly references the 2023 letter's Billing forecast and confirms it was met. Continuity across artifacts is itself a discipline.
- **Argument by enumeration.** The 9-century customer list is the same rhetorical move as the 24-project Fast list. Don't argue; show.
