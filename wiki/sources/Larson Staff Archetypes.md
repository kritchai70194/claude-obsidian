# Larson "Staff Archetypes" (StaffEng, 2021)

[[Will Larson]]'s canonical typology of staff-and-above individual-contributor (IC) roles, derived from interviews with practicing staff engineers and published in *Staff Engineer: Leadership beyond the management track* (2021) and on staffeng.com. The "four archetypes" frame is the single most cited piece of the entire staff-engineering literature.

## Headline Takeaway

There is no single "staff engineer" job. There are **four named archetypes** (Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand) with sharply different scopes, daily activities, and political situations. Conflating them is the cause of most failed staff-engineer hires, promotions, and self-conceptions. The first thing a senior IC has to do is identify which archetype they (or the role) actually are.

## The Four Archetypes

### Tech Lead

The most common archetype. Guides one team or a cluster of teams in approach and execution.

- Scopes complex tasks and coordinates teams toward solutions.
- Maintains team context and cross-team relationships.
- Partners closely with product managers.
- Defines technical vision and builds alignment on complex issues.
- Delegates implementation work to grow teammates.

Time allocation: reduced coding; increased coordination and vision-setting.

Defining quote: "They're comfortable scoping complex tasks, coordinating their team towards solving them, and unblocking them along the way."

### Architect

Owns the direction, quality, and approach within a critical technical domain that is both complex and central to company success.

- Maintains intimate understanding of business needs, user goals, and technical constraints.
- Identifies and advocates for effective approaches within the domain.
- Operates with earned organizational authority based on demonstrated judgment.
- May or may not write code depending on company philosophy.

Defining quote: "Architects are responsible for the success of a specific technical domain within their company."

### Solver

A trusted agent who goes deep on complex, high-risk problems that leadership has already identified as critical.

- Works on problems with unclear approaches or high execution risk.
- Continues until problems are resolved or contained.
- Operates with minimal organizational politics (problems already prioritized).
- Handles business-technology intersection issues.

Defining quote: "The Solver is a trusted agent of the organization who goes deep into knotty problems, continuing to work on them until they're resolved."

### Right Hand

The rarest archetype. Exists only in large organizations (hundreds of engineers). Operates as a senior leader without direct reports, borrowing authority from an executive.

- Scales the executive's impact by removing important problems from their plate.
- Addresses issues at the intersection of business, technology, people, culture, and process.
- Attends leadership staff meetings.
- Identifies fires, edits approach, delegates execution.

Defining quote: "The Right Hand extends an executive's attention, borrowing their scope and authority to operate particularly complex organizations."

## Why the Typology Matters

Each archetype has a different success criterion. A Tech Lead is succeeding if the team they support is shipping. An Architect is succeeding if the domain they own is healthy years out. A Solver is succeeding if knotty problems get resolved. A Right Hand is succeeding if the executive they support has more leverage.

Most staff-engineer dissatisfaction is **archetype mismatch**: a Solver hired into a Tech Lead role, a Tech Lead promoted into a Right Hand seat without the org size to support one, an Architect evaluated on weekly delivery rather than five-year domain health. The corrective is to name the archetype explicitly and align expectations to it.

## Connection to the Brain

This source is the **vocabulary** the brain uses to reason about senior IC work. When a hire, promotion, or self-conception question arises, the first move is to name the archetype. When the brain is itself acting as a staff-grade contributor inside a project, it should pick the archetype that fits the scope and stay in it; pretending to be all four simultaneously is the standard failure mode.
