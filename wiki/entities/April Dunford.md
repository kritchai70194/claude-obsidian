# April Dunford

Positioning consultant and founder of Ambient Strategy. Former VP Marketing at IBM Cognos and at multiple B2B SaaS companies before going independent. Author of *Obviously Awesome* (2019) and *Sales Pitch* (2023). The canonical voice on positioning as strategic discipline distinct from messaging, and the operator most often called when a B2B product has become a "category of one" or has muddled itself into a feature list nobody can rank.

Anchor mentor on the **gtm-sales pole**, complementary to [[Mark Roberge]] (data-driven sales process), [[Aaron Ross]] (outbound prospecting and the SDR/AE split), and [[Andy Raskin]] (strategic narrative). Where Roberge and Ross build the machine that converts pipeline into revenue, and Raskin frames the change-in-the-world story, Dunford fixes the upstream problem: deciding what the product actually is, who it is for, and what it is best against.

---

## Why This Mentor

The other three GTM mentors assume the product knows what it is. Dunford does not. Her recurring observation across hundreds of consulting engagements: most B2B sales problems are not sales-process problems. They are positioning problems pretending to be sales-process problems. The pipeline is leaky because the prospect does not understand what category the product belongs to, what alternatives they should compare it against, and why those alternatives are worse for their specific situation.

Her career arc is the credential. As VP Marketing at IBM Cognos and at multiple acquired B2B SaaS companies, she ran positioning resets that took stalled or muddled products and turned them into clear category contenders. She then made the discipline portable through Ambient Strategy and two books. *Obviously Awesome* gave the diagnostic and the 10-step process. *Sales Pitch* gave the downstream artefact: how positioning becomes the actual narrative a salesperson uses in a discovery call.

The contribution that matters most: positioning is treated as a deliberate strategic decision, not a creative writing exercise. The deliverable is not a tagline. It is a written internal document that says which competitive alternatives the product is best against, what unique attributes drive that advantage, what value those attributes deliver, which customers care most about that value, and which market category the product asks to be compared in. Everything downstream, messaging, pitch, pricing, sales motion, follows.

## The 5 Components of Positioning

The structural backbone of *Obviously Awesome* and the operating frame for every Dunford engagement.

1. **Competitive alternatives.** What the customer would do if your product did not exist. Often not another vendor; often a spreadsheet, a manual process, a hire, or doing nothing.
2. **Unique attributes.** The features or capabilities your product has that the alternatives lack. Specific, demonstrable, not "ease of use".
3. **Value.** The benefit the customer derives from those attributes. The bridge from "what it has" to "what it gives".
4. **Target customers who care most.** The segment of buyers for whom that value is most acute. Not everyone. The narrowest defensible group.
5. **Market category.** The frame of reference the product asks to be compared in. The choice of category is itself a strategic act; it sets the alternatives the customer reaches for.

The 5 components are an ordering, not a list. You start with competitive alternatives because alternatives determine attributes, attributes determine value, value determines target customer, target customer determines category.

## Core Frames

**Positioning is the deliberate context you set for the customer.** Without intentional positioning, the customer chooses the context, usually wrong, usually one where you lose. The act of positioning is the act of taking the choice of context away from the customer.

**Obvious is not creative; it is clear.** The best positioning reads as obvious in retrospect. A prospect should understand the value in seconds, not in a 90-minute deck. If your positioning needs explanation, it is not positioning yet; it is still marketing copy.

**Negative positioning is more powerful than positive positioning.** "Who is this NOT for?" prevents wasted sales time, qualifies prospects faster, and sharpens the value claim for the prospects who remain. Most teams cannot answer the negative question, which is the diagnostic that they do not have positioning yet.

**Narrowing the target sharpens the message.** Most repositioning failures are caused by trying to broaden the target market. The instinct is to expand TAM by including more buyers; the result is muddled positioning that resonates with nobody. The counterintuitive move is to narrow the target until the value claim becomes obvious.

**The sales pitch is downstream of positioning.** Fix positioning first, then build the pitch. Companies that try to fix the pitch without fixing positioning produce more polished versions of the wrong story.

**Categories evolve.** An obvious category creator (Salesforce defining CRM) wins big. A me-too entrant in a defined category competes on features and price. The strategic question is not "what category are we in?" but "what category do we want to be compared in, and can we credibly claim it?"

**Positioning is not messaging.** Positioning is a strategic decision about the market frame. Messaging is the tactical expression of that decision in copy, decks, and ads. Most marketing teams optimize messaging without revisiting positioning, which is why their messaging keeps not working.

**Founders are the wrong people to write the first positioning draft.** Too close to the product. Too in love with the features. The positioning exercise needs a facilitator who can ask the dumb questions and force the founder to defend choices.

## The 4 Styles of Positioning

A taxonomy from *Obviously Awesome* that names the strategic options once the 5 components are clear.

- **Head-to-Head.** Compete directly in an established category by being demonstrably better on attributes that matter to the buyer. The hardest mode; usually requires capital and time.
- **Big Fish, Small Pond.** Win a defensible sub-segment of a large category. The category exists; you carve out the corner where you are unambiguously best.
- **Reframe the Game.** Change the criteria the buyer uses to evaluate the category. Tableau did this against legacy BI; Slack did this against email.
- **Pioneer a Category.** Create a new market frame that did not previously exist. The most ambitious mode; requires sustained category-building work alongside product work.

The choice of style is itself a positioning decision. Most B2B startups default to Head-to-Head and lose. Most successful repositionings move from Head-to-Head into one of the other three.
