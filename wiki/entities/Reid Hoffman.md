# Reid Hoffman

Co-founder of LinkedIn (2003), sold to Microsoft in 2016 for $26.2B. Partner at Greylock Partners (2009-2023), now Partner Emeritus. Author of *Blitzscaling* (Currency, 2018, with Chris Yeh), *The Alliance* (HBR Press, 2014, with Ben Casnocha and Chris Yeh), and *The Start-Up of You* (Crown Business, 2012). Host of the Masters of Scale podcast since 2017. PayPal Mafia veteran (COO 2000-2002). Board member at Microsoft. Personal investor in Facebook, Airbnb, and dozens of category-defining networks.

Anchor mentor on the **capital-strategy pole**, complementary to [[Bill Gurley]] (marketplace economics, burn discipline), [[Peter Thiel]] (monopoly, contrarian thesis), and [[Mike Maples]] (inflections, backable founders). Where Gurley names the constraints, Thiel names the goal, and Maples names the trigger, Hoffman names the operating mode: when to spend recklessly because the cost of being second is permanent loss of the market.

---

## Why This Mentor

Hoffman fills the gap that capital-efficiency orthodoxy creates. The brain has [[Bezos Letters]] for long-term thinking, [[Helmer 7 Powers Book]] for naming what defensibility is, [[Andrew Chen]] for growth loop mechanics. None of them name the specific decision: when do you accept inefficiency in exchange for speed because the market structure rewards the first player to scale?

Two contributions matter most. First, **Blitzscaling**: a stage-aware operating doctrine that says capital efficiency is the wrong question once you have product/market fit in a winner-take-most market. Second, **The Alliance**: a re-framing of the employer/employee compact that fits both long careers and short product cycles, expressed as explicit "tours of duty" rather than the fiction of permanent employment.

Frames here: speed beats efficiency when the market is winner-take-most; the 5 stages of blitzscaling each have different operating norms; embrace bad management practices when the cost of slowness is higher than the cost of bad management; replace the employment fiction with explicit tours; the corporate alumni network is the durable long-term relationship.

## Key Frameworks

**Blitzscaling.** Prioritize speed over efficiency in conditions of uncertainty, in markets with winner-take-most dynamics, even when the move is capital-inefficient. The hidden cost of being second is permanent loss of the market. The classic counterexamples are companies that won the early lead and lost it to faster scaling (Friendster vs Facebook, MySpace vs Facebook, Path vs Instagram).

**The 5 Stages of Blitzscaling.** Family (1-9 people), Tribe (10-99), Village (100-999), City (1000-9999), Nation (10000+). Each stage has different operating norms, hiring patterns, span-of-control, and management techniques. The mistake most CEOs make is using one stage's playbook in another stage. What gets you from Family to Tribe will kill you between Village and City.

**Three Growth Factors.** Market size, distribution, gross margin. Sustainable competitive advantage requires winning on all three. A massive market with bad distribution and bad margins is a tar pit. A great margin in a tiny market is a lifestyle business.

**Three Growth Limiters.** Lack of product/market fit, scaling the wrong things, and premature scaling. Blitzscaling without PMF is the most expensive form of suicide.

**The Alliance.** The manager/employee compact is a series of explicit "tours of duty", not a permanent commitment in either direction. Three tour types: rotational (1-3 years, standard role), transformational (3-7 years, owns a meaningful outcome), foundational (lifetime, the person and company merge). The corporate alumni network is the durable relationship after the tour ends.

**The Counterintuitive Rule.** Embrace what would be considered bad management practices at a normal company, because the cost of slowness in a winner-take-most market is higher than the cost of bad management. Tolerate chaos, ignore some customer complaints, ship fast and embarrassed, hire ahead of need, fire fast.

## Primary-Source Citations

### From [[Hoffman Blitzscaling Book]] (2018)

The canonical text. Definition, 5 stages, growth factors and limiters, counterintuitive management rules at scale, network effects as the engine of winner-take-most dynamics, how to navigate stage transitions.

On the central decision:

> Blitzscaling is what you do when you need to grow really, really quickly. It's the science and art of rapidly building out a company to serve a large and usually global market, with the goal of becoming the first mover at scale.

On counterintuitive management:

> The counterintuitive rule of blitzscaling is that you should embrace what would otherwise be considered bad management practices.

On product launch tempo (the canonical Hoffman aphorism):

> If you're not embarrassed by the first version of your product, you've launched too late.

### From [[Hoffman Stanford CS183C]] (Fall 2015)

The course that became the book. Case-driven walkthrough of the management bottlenecks at each stage. Guest founder interviews with Brian Chesky (Airbnb), Patrick Collison (Stripe), Travis Kalanick (Uber), Jeff Weiner (LinkedIn). The course notes are the primary public record of how Hoffman and Yeh built the stage framework.

### From [[Hoffman The Alliance]] (2014)

The thesis: the employment relationship in tech cannot be permanent (long careers, short product cycles), but pretending it is purely transactional also fails (no trust, no investment in growth, no candor). Replace with explicit tours of duty. Make the contract explicit. Build the corporate alumni network as the long-term relationship.

> An alliance is a relationship between independent players who jointly recognize that they will be better off cooperating than competing.

### From [[Hoffman Masters of Scale]] (2017-present)

210+ episodes synthesizing the counterintuitive rules of scaling across industries. The "do things that don't scale" episode with Brian Chesky is the canonical cross-reference to [[PG Do Things That Dont Scale]]. The recurring theme: the founder profile, the role of networks, the staged transitions, the embrace of bureaucracy at the right stage (not the wrong one).

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Speed over efficiency in winner-take-most markets.** ([[Hoffman Blitzscaling Book]]) The hidden cost of being second is permanent loss of the market.
- **5 stages, 5 playbooks.** ([[Hoffman Blitzscaling Book]], [[Hoffman Stanford CS183C]]) Family, Tribe, Village, City, Nation. Each stage has different operating norms.
- **Growth factors: market size, distribution, gross margin.** ([[Hoffman Blitzscaling Book]]) Win on all three or do not blitzscale.
- **Growth limiters: lack of PMF, scaling the wrong things, premature scaling.** ([[Hoffman Blitzscaling Book]]) Blitzscaling without PMF is the most expensive form of suicide.
- **Embrace bad management at speed.** ([[Hoffman Blitzscaling Book]]) Cost of slowness exceeds cost of chaos when the market rewards the first mover at scale.
- **Tours of duty replace permanent employment.** ([[Hoffman The Alliance]]) Rotational, transformational, foundational. Make the compact explicit.
- **The corporate alumni network is the durable relationship.** ([[Hoffman The Alliance]]) The tour ends; the relationship does not.
- **Ship embarrassed.** ([[Hoffman Blitzscaling Book]], [[Hoffman Masters of Scale]]) If you are not embarrassed by v1, you launched too late.
- **Do things that do not scale, then scale fast.** ([[Hoffman Masters of Scale]]) The Chesky episode connects the manual recruitment phase to the blitzscaling phase.
- **Strong opinions, loosely held.** ([[Hoffman Masters of Scale]]) Applied to hiring, strategy, and pivots.
