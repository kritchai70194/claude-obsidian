# Clayton Christensen

Harvard Business School professor from 1992 until his death in January 2020. Author of *The Innovator's Dilemma* (Harvard Business School Press, 1997), *The Innovator's Solution* (HBS Press, 2003, with Michael Raynor), *The Innovator's DNA* (HBS Press, 2011), *Competing Against Luck* (HarperBusiness, 2016, with Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, David S. Duncan), and *How Will You Measure Your Life?* (HarperBusiness, 2012). Founder of the Christensen Institute. Originator of Disruption Theory and the Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) framework.

Anchor mentor on the **mental-models pole**, complementary to [[Charlie Munger]] (latticework, inversion, biases), [[Daniel Kahneman]] (System 1/2, prospect theory, noise), and [[Nassim Taleb]] (fat tails, antifragility, optionality). Where the other three give general-purpose reasoning tools, Christensen gives two specific causal frames that apply to strategy and customer behavior in any industry.

---

## Why This Mentor

Christensen fills the disruption/JTBD gap. The brain has [[Ben Thompson]] for Aggregation Theory (why platforms with demand-side scale win in zero-marginal-cost markets) and [[Hamilton Helmer]] for 7 Powers (the destination Powers a durable business can occupy). It did not have a clean causal model for why successful incumbents systematically lose to upstarts who look unimpressive on day one. Disruption Theory is that model. It also did not have a clean causal model for what customers are actually buying when they buy something. Jobs to Be Done is that model.

The cross-reference with Thompson matters. Aggregation Theory and Disruption Theory predict different competitive dynamics. Aggregators win by owning the demand side in markets with zero distribution and supply costs (Google, Facebook, Netflix). Disruptors win by entering at the low end or in a new market with a "good enough" offering that incumbents cannot match without abandoning their business model (steel mini-mills, personal computers, Honda motorcycles). Many cases are not disruption in Christensen's strict sense even though the word is used loosely. The companion concept page [[Disruption vs Aggregation]] separates the two frames.

Christensen's biographical arc anchors the credibility. He spent his career trying to falsify his own theory, refined it across five books, applied it to healthcare, education, and personal life, and died with the framework still in active use. *How Will You Measure Your Life?* is the unusual move where the same operator who built the strategy canon turned the frame on his own life and asked his Harvard MBA students to do the same.

Frames here: incumbents lose because they listen too well to current customers; disruption enters at the low end or in a new market that the incumbent cannot serve profitably; customers hire products for jobs, not buy products in categories; resource allocation determines strategy more than strategy documents do; the same frames that predict business success predict personal success.

## Key Frameworks (now backed by citations)

**Disruption Theory.** Incumbents lose to upstarts not because they fail to listen to customers but because they listen too well. Sustaining innovations serve the best (most profitable) current customers along established performance trajectories. Disruptive innovations enter at the low end or in a new market with an inferior offering on the dominant performance metric, then improve until they take the mainstream. The incumbent cannot respond without abandoning its own business model.

**Sustaining vs Disruptive Innovation.** Sustaining innovation improves performance along trajectories customers already value. Disruptive innovation creates a new trajectory the established business cannot reach. The binary matters because the strategic response is different. Sustaining innovations should be matched head-on; disruptive innovations require a separate organization with different metrics.

**Low-End Disruption.** Enter at the bottom of the market with a "good enough" product at a lower price, then move up. Steel mini-mills displacing integrated steel makers (started in rebar, the lowest-margin product, and worked up to structural steel). Honda displacing Detroit (started with small motorcycles American firms ignored). Walmart displacing department stores.

**New-Market Disruption.** Enable a previously non-consuming population to consume. Personal computers vs mainframes (new market: home and small office, not enterprise). Sony transistor radios vs vacuum-tube radios (teenagers listening on the go, not families gathered around a console). The mainstream incumbent does not feel attacked because the new market is not in its customer base.

**Jobs to Be Done.** Customers do not buy products; they hire them to do jobs. The unit of competitive analysis is not the product category but the job. A milkshake competes with bagels and bananas for the morning-commute job, not with other milkshakes.

**Milkshake Marketing.** The McDonald's case study. Demographic segmentation produced no actionable improvements. Reframing the question as "what job is the milkshake hired to do" revealed the morning-commute use case: long drive, one free hand, need to occupy the stomach until lunch. Product implications followed directly (thicker, with chunks to extend consumption time, dispensed faster at drive-through).

**Resources, Processes, Values (RPV).** Organizations have capabilities defined by three things: what resources they have (people, money, technology), what processes they use to convert resources into output, and what values prioritize which opportunities get funded. Disruption fails inside incumbents because the values reject low-margin emerging markets that look financially unattractive against the existing business.

**Modular vs Interdependent Architecture (Law of Conservation of Modularity).** When a product is not good enough on the dominant performance metric, integrated/interdependent architectures win and capture margin (the part of the value chain that is "not good enough" is the choke point). When a product overshoots customer needs, the architecture modularizes and margin moves to the next not-good-enough layer. Mainframes integrated; PCs modularized; smartphones integrated again; cloud services modularized again.

## Primary-Source Citations

### From [[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]] (1997)

The canonical text. The disk drive industry as the foundational case study (Christensen tracked every entrant 1976-1992 and found incumbents systematically lost generational transitions). The principles of disruption: small markets do not solve the growth needs of large firms; markets that do not exist cannot be analyzed; the technology supply trajectory often outruns the demand trajectory.

On why good management causes failure:

> The very decision-making and resource-allocation processes that are key to the success of established companies are the very processes that reject disruptive technologies.

### From [[Christensen JTBD]] (HBR 2016 + Christensen Institute)

The Milkshake Marketing case. McDonald's segmented milkshake buyers by demographics with no improvement. Reframing as "what job is the milkshake hired to do" exposed the morning-commute job and the after-school job, each with different product requirements.

The canonical reframe:

> Customers don't buy products; they hire them to do jobs.

The diagnostic question: what would the customer fire this product for? What is competing for the same job?

### From [[Christensen Innovators Solution]] (2003)

The prescriptive sequel with Michael Raynor. How to build disruptive growth businesses inside existing companies: separate organization with its own P&L, its own values, its own resource allocation. The Law of Conservation of Modularity: integrated architectures win in not-good-enough markets, modular architectures win in overshot markets, and the choke point in the value chain moves accordingly. Identifying which competitors to worry about: the ones serving customers you cannot profitably serve.

### From [[Christensen How Will You Measure Your Life]] (HBR 2010 + book 2012)

The Harvard MBA graduation address turned book. The thesis: the same frameworks that predict business success predict personal life success.

On strategy and resource allocation:

> Your decisions about allocating your personal time, energy, and talent ultimately shape your life's strategy.

On marginal-cost thinking applied to ethics:

> It is easier to hold to your principles 100% of the time than it is 98% of the time.

The "just this once" trap: marginal-cost analysis says the one-time deviation is cheap, but the full cost is the loss of the principle itself.

On culture: culture is what the organization does without having to think about it. Culture eats strategy because it answers questions before they are asked.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Incumbents lose because they listen to their best customers too well.** ([[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]]) Good management causes failure when disruption arrives.
- **Sustaining innovation improves the existing trajectory; disruptive innovation creates a new one.** ([[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]]) The binary matters for the strategic response.
- **Low-end disruption enters at the bottom with "good enough" and moves up.** ([[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]]) Steel mini-mills, Honda, Walmart.
- **New-market disruption enables non-consumers to consume.** ([[Christensen Innovators Dilemma]]) PCs vs mainframes, transistor radios vs vacuum-tube radios.
- **Customers do not buy products; they hire them to do jobs.** ([[Christensen JTBD]]) The unit of analysis is the job, not the category.
- **Disruption requires a separate organization with its own values.** ([[Christensen Innovators Solution]]) The core business will starve any low-margin emerging effort.
- **Resources, Processes, Values determine capability.** ([[Christensen Innovators Solution]]) The values block disruption more often than the resources or processes do.
- **Integrated architectures win in not-good-enough markets; modular architectures win in overshot markets.** ([[Christensen Innovators Solution]]) The Law of Conservation of Modularity.
- **Resource allocation is the real strategy.** ([[Christensen How Will You Measure Your Life]]) Where time and capital actually go, not what the strategy document says.
- **Marginal-cost thinking applied to ethics is a trap.** ([[Christensen How Will You Measure Your Life]]) "Just this once" costs the principle itself.
- **Culture eats strategy because it answers questions before they are asked.** ([[Christensen How Will You Measure Your Life]]) What the organization does without thinking.
