# Annie Duke

Former professional poker player from 1994 to 2012, World Series of Poker bracelet winner (2004 Tournament of Champions), $4M+ in career tournament earnings. Left poker to become the canonical decision-strategy author of the past decade. Author of *Thinking in Bets, Making Smarter Decisions When You Don't Have All the Facts* (Portfolio, 2018) and *Quit, The Power of Knowing When to Walk Away* (Portfolio, 2022). Co-founder of How to Decide, an applied decision-education organization. PhD ABD in cognitive psychology at the University of Pennsylvania, training that frames her entire output: poker as the laboratory, cognitive psychology as the theory, decision quality as the practice.

Anchor mentor on the **decision-customer-discovery pole**, the named voice on decision quality under uncertainty. Complementary to [[Steve Blank]] (Customer Development methodology, the pivot as planned action) and [[Rob Fitzpatrick]] (the Mom Test, interviews that produce signal). Cross-pole partners: [[Charlie Munger]] (inversion, the latticework of mental models) and [[Daniel Kahneman]] (System 1 vs System 2, the bias inventory). Duke is what happens when you take Munger and Kahneman seriously, then build a working protocol that an operator can actually run on Tuesday morning.

---

## Why This Mentor

The brain already has implicit decision discipline. Bezos two-way doors handle reversibility. Munger inversion handles avoiding stupidity. Kahneman gives the bias inventory. What was missing was the explicit protocol: a working method for the actual moment when a founder has to decide and does not yet know if it will work.

Duke fills that gap. She names the thing most operators do wrong (judging decisions by outcomes, which she calls "resulting") and gives the working alternative (decision journals, explicit probability assignment, truthseeking pods, pre-committed kill criteria). The output is portable. A founder can read her on Sunday and run the protocol on Monday.

The poker frame matters. Poker is the cleanest available laboratory for decisions under uncertainty: you make explicit probabilistic bets with real money under time pressure, then get fast feedback, then face the same structural decision again the next hand. Most operators never get that feedback density. Duke spent twenty years in that lab, then translated what she learned into a method that does not require sitting at a card table.

## Key Frames

**Resulting.** The bias of judging a decision by its outcome rather than by the quality of the decision process. A good decision can produce a bad outcome (you got unlucky). A bad decision can produce a good outcome (you got lucky). Most people learn from outcomes, not from decisions, which means they reinforce the wrong things. The discipline is to evaluate the decision before knowing the result, and to keep that evaluation when the result arrives.

**Decisions are bets.** Every choice under uncertainty is a bet: choosing among options with probabilistic outcomes. Make the bet explicit. What do I believe? Why? How confident am I, in percent terms? What would change my mind? The act of writing the percentage down is most of the value, because it forces calibration where vague feelings used to live.

**The Decision Journal.** Record the decision, the reasoning, the alternatives considered, the expected outcomes, and the confidence level BEFORE knowing the outcome. Review later to see whether your reasoning was sound regardless of result. Over time, the journal becomes calibration data: you find out which kinds of decisions you are good at and which you are not.

**Truthseeking pods.** A small group committed to giving each other honest feedback about decisions. Defaults to praise are useless. Defaults to critique are useful. The pod's job is to challenge the reasoning, not to validate the outcome.

**Quit is a skill.** The optimal moment to quit is "when you start to think about quitting", because most people quit too late, not too early. Sunk cost bias keeps people in losing positions long past the point where the math says walk away. Quitting on time is what frees the capacity to do the next thing.

**Kill criteria.** Define in advance the conditions under which you will quit. The pre-commitment removes in-the-moment rationalization, which is where sunk cost wins. "I will shut this down if X is not true by date Y" is operative; "I'll know when it's time" is not.

**Monkeys and pedestals.** Borrowed from Astro Teller at X. If the task is to teach a monkey to recite Shakespeare on a pedestal, do not start by building the pedestal. The pedestal is the easy part; the monkey is the hard part. If the monkey is impossible, the pedestal was wasted. Do the hard part first to find out fast whether the project is viable.

**Hindsight bias and the narrative fallacy.** Together these make us think the past was predictable. It was not. Most outcomes have a large luck component, and most "I knew it all along" stories are reconstructed after the fact. The honest move is to evaluate the decision as it looked in the moment, with the information available then.

**Decision quality is the controllable; outcome quality is not.** Optimize what you control. Over a long enough series of decisions, outcome quality and decision quality converge. Over any single decision, they may not. The operator's job is to play the long game and refuse to be ruled by single results.
