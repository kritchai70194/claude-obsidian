# Frank Slootman

Dutch-born enterprise software CEO. Three consecutive operating wins: Data Domain (CEO 2003-2009, sold to EMC for $2.4B), ServiceNow (CEO 2011-2017, IPO 2012, scaled from roughly $100M to $1.4B in ARR), Snowflake (CEO 2019-2024, IPO September 2020 at $33B, peak market cap above $100B in November 2021). Author of *Tape Sucks* (2011, on Data Domain) and *Amp It Up* (Wiley, 2022, the canonical operating manual). BA Erasmus University, Rotterdam.

Anchor mentor on the **operating-at-scale pole**, complementary to [[Andy Grove]] (managerial leverage, OKR cadence, the one-on-one), [[Patty McCord]] (Netflix culture, freedom and responsibility), and [[Keith Rabois]] (the barrel framework, hiring rubrics). Where Grove writes the management physics and McCord writes the culture operating system, Slootman writes the **posture**, the felt tempo and standards that a CEO sets and refuses to let drift.

---

## Why This Mentor

Most CEOs default to a mode that Slootman calls "the natural operating state": tolerable tempo, tolerable standards, tolerable hiring, tolerable focus. The company keeps moving. The numbers grow. Nobody is on fire. Slootman's claim: this is how good companies stay merely good. The CEO's job is to refuse the natural state. Amp the tempo. Raise the bar on every dimension at once. Cut the bureaucracy. Move people who do not fit. Concentrate on what is winning instead of hedging across what might.

He is the operator who took three different companies to outlier outcomes by running the same playbook each time. Not three different geniuses. Three executions of one posture. That repeatability is what makes him a mentor and not just a story.

He fills a gap that none of the other operating-at-scale mentors fill. Grove explains the management instruments. McCord explains the culture rules. Rabois explains the hiring rubric. Slootman explains the **CEO tone**, the voice and tempo at the top that decides whether any of those instruments actually get used.

## Key Frameworks

**Amp It Up.** The named operating posture. Raise standards and tempo on every dimension simultaneously. Not incremental improvement. A deliberate inflection in expectations: hiring bar, sales bar, engineering bar, deal quality, response time, follow-through. The four core principles in the 2022 book: raise standards, align as a team, sharpen focus, pick up the pace.

**Tempo is a choice.** Most companies operate at the tempo of the slowest function, often legal, finance, or compensation. The CEO's job is to set tempo at the level the opportunity demands, not the level the slowest function will tolerate.

> We're in a hurry around here.

The line is the message. The tone sets the tempo. Slack tone produces slack work.

**Narrow focus beats broad portfolio.** Snowflake refused to expand into adjacent products until the core data warehouse was dominant. Most CEOs hedge bets across multiple bets. Slootman concentrates them. Win one thing. Then earn the right to do the next.

**Raise the hiring bar continuously.** Every new hire must be better than the median of the function they join. Standards drift down by default; the CEO must actively push them up. The cost of one wrong senior hire is not the salary; it is the team that has to work around them and the bar they reset for everyone else.

**Get the right people on the bus and the wrong people off it.** Decisive on personnel changes. The cost of waiting on a known-wrong hire is paid by the team. Slow firing is not kindness; it is cowardice routed through the org.

**Mission alignment, not team-building.** Do not try to make the team happy. Make the mission compelling enough that the right people self-select to join and stay. Happiness follows mission; the reverse does not work.

**Anti-bureaucracy posture.** Layers of management slow tempo. Cut them. Push decision rights down. Refuse the org-chart instinct that solves every problem with a new VP.

**Pricing discipline.** Refuse to discount the way competitors do. Price for value, not for closing. Walk away from deals that do not fit the pricing model. The deals you refuse are as load-bearing as the deals you close.
