# Steve Blank

Serial entrepreneur with eight startups and four IPOs across 1978 through 2003, including Zilog, Convergent Technologies, MIPS Computer, Ardent Computer, SuperMac, Rocket Science Games, and E.piphany. Adjunct Professor at Stanford, Columbia, Berkeley, and UCSF. Author of *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* (K&S Ranch, 2005), the self-published foundational text of the lean-startup movement, and *The Startup Owner's Manual* (K&S Ranch, 2012, with Bob Dorf), the operational follow-up. Co-creator of the Lean LaunchPad curriculum and the related Hacking for Defense and Hacking for Diplomacy programs.

Anchor mentor on the **decision-customer-discovery pole**, complementary to [[Annie Duke]] (probabilistic decision quality, resulting, the decision journal) and [[Rob Fitzpatrick]] (interview technique discipline, the Mom Test question pattern). Where Duke covers how to decide under uncertainty and Fitzpatrick covers how to extract signal from a single conversation, Blank covers the full search-phase operating model: what a startup is doing before product-market fit, and how to know when it is done.

---

## Why This Mentor

The brain already has implicit customer discovery through [[Paul Graham]] (do things that don't scale, manual recruitment) and [[Lenny Rachitsky]] (PMF interviews, the 40% question, retention curves). What it does not have is the named methodology that contains both. Blank is that methodology.

His central claim is structural. A startup is not a small version of a large company. It is a temporary organization searching for a repeatable, scalable business model. The activity of search is qualitatively different from the activity of execution, and applying execute-phase tooling (waterfall plans, formal hiring, quarterly OKRs, detailed financial projections) to a search-phase company is the modal way startups die. The Customer Development model is the operating template for the search phase.

Two contributions matter most. First, **Customer Development as a parallel process** to Product Development, with four explicit stages (Discovery, Validation, Creation, Building) and explicit pivot criteria between them. Second, **"Get out of the building"** as the single behavioral mandate: founders learn customers by talking to them, not by analyzing them inside the office. Every other Blank frame is downstream of these two.

## Key Frameworks

**Customer Development.** A parallel process to Product Development, conducted explicitly to test customer assumptions before committing to product specifications. Four stages: Customer Discovery (find a problem worth solving and a customer who has it), Customer Validation (find a repeatable sales process), Customer Creation (drive end-user demand), Company Building (transition from search to execute). Each stage has explicit entry and exit criteria. The model is iterative; failure at any stage sends you back, not forward.

**Get out of the building.** Founders learn customers by talking to them in their environment, not by interpreting them from a deck. The single most cited Blank phrase. It is a behavioral instruction, not a metaphor.

**No business plan survives first contact with customers.** A startup is a search for a business model, not the execution of a plan. The plan is a list of hypotheses to be tested, not commitments to be honored. Treating it as commitment is the most expensive mistake a founder can make.

**The hypothesis grid.** Every startup begins with a set of unverified beliefs about Customer, Problem, Solution, Channel, and Market. Test them in roughly that order. Customer first because the wrong customer makes every other hypothesis irrelevant. Solution late because solution is cheapest to change.

**Pivot vs persevere.** A pivot is a planned, evidence-based change to one hypothesis. It is not a panic response to bad metrics. Most startups should pivot more times than they do; the pathology is sunk-cost commitment to a falsified hypothesis.

**Search vs execute.** Search-phase startups (pre-PMF) and execute-phase companies (post-PMF) require qualitatively different management models. Search rewards hypothesis testing, customer time, and agility. Execute rewards process, planning, and predictability. Mode confusion kills companies in both directions.

**Market-First, Product-Second.** Identify a market with a clear problem before building the product. Most failed startups built products in search of a market; the technology was real and the customer was theoretical.

**The Lean LaunchPad curriculum.** The 10-week format that translates Customer Development into a teachable practice: interview 100 customers in 10 weeks, present hypothesis updates weekly, iterate the business model canvas in public. Adopted by NSF I-Corps and taught to more than 500,000 scientists, engineers, and founders.

The relationship to Eric Ries: Ries's Build-Measure-Learn loop is a simplification of Blank's 4-stage Customer Development cycle. Blank's version is more rigorous about which hypotheses to test in which order; Ries's version is more accessible. Both belong in the brain, but Blank is the source.

## Core Frames

- **A startup is a search for a business model, not a small company.** ([[Blank Four Steps to Epiphany]], [[Blank Startup Owners Manual]]) Applying execute-phase discipline to a search-phase company is the modal failure mode.
- **Get out of the building.** ([[Blank Four Steps to Epiphany]], [[Blank Lean Launchpad]]) Customer learning happens in the customer's environment, not yours.
- **No business plan survives first contact with customers.** ([[Blank Four Steps to Epiphany]]) The plan is hypotheses to test.
- **Customer Development runs in parallel with Product Development.** ([[Blank Four Steps to Epiphany]], [[Blank Startup Owners Manual]]) Discovery, Validation, Creation, Building.
- **Test hypotheses in order: Customer, Problem, Solution, Channel, Market.** ([[Blank Startup Owners Manual]]) Customer first because the wrong customer makes everything else irrelevant.
- **Pivot is a planned change, not a panic response.** ([[Blank Customer Development Blog]]) Most startups pivot too late, not too early.
- **Search vs execute are different management models.** ([[Blank Four Steps to Epiphany]], [[Blank Lean Launchpad]]) Mode confusion kills companies.
- **Market-first, product-second.** ([[Blank Four Steps to Epiphany]]) Most failed startups built a product in search of a market.
