# Keith Rabois

General Partner at Khosla Ventures (2018-2025). Previously Partner / OIR at Founders Fund (2013-2018), COO at Square under Jack Dorsey (2010-2013), VP at Slide under Max Levchin (2007-2010), executive at LinkedIn under Reid Hoffman (2003-2007), and early team at PayPal under Peter Thiel (1999-2002). Stanford BA, Harvard JD. Board director on Stripe, Affirm, Faire, Front, and Rippling. A PayPal Mafia operator whose career has been a 25-year continuous experiment in hiring rubrics across consumer, fintech, and infrastructure.

Anchor mentor on the **operating-at-scale pole**, complementary to [[Andy Grove]] (managerial leverage, OKR cadence, the one-on-one), [[Frank Slootman]] (CEO tempo and standards), and [[Patty McCord]] (Netflix culture model). Where Grove writes the management physics, Slootman writes the posture, and McCord writes the culture rules, Rabois writes the **selection function**: who you let through the door in the first place.

---

## Why This Mentor

Most operators talk about hiring as a system problem (process, interview loops, scorecards). Rabois treats it as a categorization problem: every candidate is either a Barrel or Ammunition, and the company's future depends on getting the ratio right. The framework collapses a noisy domain into a single binary that maps cleanly onto org design, comp, and recruiting strategy.

He is the rare operator who has run the same hiring playbook across five companies (PayPal, LinkedIn, Slide, Square, plus the portfolio at Founders Fund and Khosla) and watched it propagate through the PayPal Mafia network to YouTube, Yelp, Stripe, Palantir, Affirm, SpaceX, and Tesla. The playbook is empirically validated at unusual scale.

He fills a gap that none of the other operating-at-scale mentors fill. Grove assumes the team is already hired. McCord assumes the culture sorts people. Slootman raises the bar but does not say what the bar tests for. Rabois says what the bar tests for: can this person take a problem from idea to shipped, recruit, manage budget, own outcome. That is the test.

## Key Frameworks

**Barrels and Ammunition.** Every company has Barrels (people who can take a problem from idea to shipped, recruit teammates, manage a budget, and own an outcome) and Ammunition (people who execute tasks well but cannot own outcomes). Both are necessary. The mistake is over-hiring Ammunition and starving the company of Barrels.

> The barrel of a gun is what's important. Ammunition without a barrel is worthless. A barrel without ammunition is still useful.

The Barrel test, applied to any candidate: would this person be capable of taking over an entire function and running it well? If no, they are Ammunition. Both categories deserve respect; the categorization deserves honesty.

**The 1-to-10 hiring multiplier.** One great hire is worth ten average hires. The math compounds: great hires recruit other great hires; average hires lower the bar for who follows them. Most companies treat hiring as a volume problem. Rabois treats it as a quality problem with a long compounding tail.

**Interview discipline.** Each interviewer picks one or two dimensions to evaluate deeply. The same questions go to every candidate for the role. Score on rubric, not feelings. The interview loop is a measurement instrument, not a vibes check. Most companies run vibes checks and call it a process.

**The first 90 days test.** Before hiring, write down: the objectives, the context, the success criteria for the first 90 days. Hand it to the new hire on day one. If they cannot crush the 90 days, the hiring decision was wrong. The new hire is not at fault; the rubric failed to predict the outcome.

**Recruiting is the CEO's job.** Outsourcing recruiting to a recruiter at the early stage is a signal that the CEO has not yet internalized the job. The CEO is the chief recruiter until at least Series B, often later. Anything that gets in the way of recruiting time should be cut first.

**Pay top of market.** Pay great hires above market. The cost differential between market and top-of-market is small in absolute dollars and tiny relative to output differential. Companies that underpay great hires lose them and pay the replacement cost.

**Speed is a signal.** Take 24 hours to make hiring decisions when possible. Six weeks signals weak leadership. The candidate is interviewing the company; slow decisions tell them what the operating tempo is. A fast yes or a fast no both beat a slow maybe.

**Top of market for everything.** Great compensation, great problem, great team. Each is necessary; none alone is sufficient. Candidates who only weigh compensation are not Barrels. Candidates who only weigh problem are also not. Barrels weigh all three and require all three.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Barrels run functions end to end; Ammunition executes tasks.** ([[Rabois Barrel and Ammunition]], [[Rabois Stanford CS183]]) The categorization is binary and load-bearing.
- **One great hire beats ten average hires.** ([[Rabois Stanford CS183]], [[Rabois 20VC Interview]]) The compounding effect through subsequent recruiting makes the math non-linear.
- **Recruiting is the CEO's job until at least Series B.** ([[Rabois Stanford CS183]], [[Rabois 20VC Interview]]) Outsourcing to recruiters early is a tell.
- **Each interviewer evaluates one or two dimensions deeply across all candidates.** ([[Rabois Stanford CS183]]) The interview loop is a measurement instrument.
- **Write 90-day success criteria before hiring; if the hire fails them, the decision was wrong.** ([[Rabois Stanford CS183]]) The rubric carries accountability, not the new hire.
- **Pay top of market for Barrels; the cost gap is small, the output gap is huge.** ([[Rabois Barrel and Ammunition]], [[Rabois PayPal Mafia Hiring]]) Underpaying great hires is the most expensive false economy in startup operating.
- **Decide in 24 hours, not 6 weeks; the candidate is reading your tempo.** ([[Rabois 20VC Interview]]) Slow hiring is weak leadership made visible.
- **Hire for raw cognitive horsepower, not credentials.** ([[Rabois PayPal Mafia Hiring]]) The PayPal template; credentials are a lagging proxy for what you actually want.
- **Test candidates with real work, not abstract puzzles.** ([[Rabois PayPal Mafia Hiring]]) The work sample is the strongest signal.
- **Bias against MBAs for operating roles.** ([[Rabois Stanford CS183]], [[Rabois Barrel and Ammunition]]) MBAs are trained to optimize within someone else's plan; Barrels write their own.
