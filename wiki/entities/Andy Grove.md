# Andy Grove

Hungarian-born American businessman, engineer, and author. Born András István Gróf in Budapest in 1936, escaped Hungary in 1956 during the revolution, arrived in New York with no English and no money, graduated CCNY in chemical engineering, earned a PhD from UC Berkeley, joined Fairchild Semiconductor, and was Intel's third employee (after Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore) in 1968. President 1979-1997, CEO 1987-1998. Under his tenure Intel revenue grew from $1.9B to $25.1B and the company became the dominant supplier of microprocessors for the PC era. Time Magazine Man of the Year 1997. Died March 21, 2016, after a long battle with Parkinson's disease.

Author of three foundational texts: *High Output Management* (1983, revised 1995), the canonical engineering-management manual; *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996), on strategic inflection points; and *Swimming Across* (2001), a memoir of escaping Hungary. The originator of OKRs (Objectives and Key Results), designed at Intel in the early 1970s, later carried to Kleiner Perkins by John Doerr and adopted at Google, then spread industry-wide.

Anchor mentor on the **operating-at-scale pole**, complementary to [[Frank Slootman]] (raising standards and tempo), [[Patty McCord]] (culture and adults-not-children HR), and [[Keith Rabois]] (high-bar hiring rubrics). Where Slootman runs the company hot, McCord builds the culture, and Rabois screens the talent, Grove provides the underlying production-engineering theory of management itself.

## Why This Mentor

Grove is the only operator who built a coherent theory of management work from production-engineering first principles and applied it through three decades of running a top-tier technology company through multiple platform shifts. Most management writing is folk wisdom from people who ran companies. Grove's writing is operations research applied to white-collar knowledge work by an engineer who happened to be a CEO. The structural rigor is the value.

Two contributions stand alone. First, the **Managerial Output Equation**, which reframes "management" away from individual contribution and toward team multiplication, and provides the specific high-leverage activities (training, one-on-ones, decision frameworks) that operationalize the reframe. Second, the **Strategic Inflection Point** framework, which names the moments when fundamental industry dynamics change by an order of magnitude and gives the CEO an early-warning system (middle management, closer to customers) for catching them before they kill the company.

Grove's third contribution, OKRs, has been so widely adopted that the original specification is often lost. The discipline Grove built was tighter and more demanding than the watered-down version most companies practice. Re-reading the original constraints (quarterly cadence, 70% scoring as healthy, public OKRs across the org) is the value.

[[Jeff Bezos]] cited *High Output Management* repeatedly. [[Brian Chesky]]'s Founder Mode framing is partly a counterweight to the professional-management orthodoxy that Grove's work codified; understanding what Chesky pushes back against requires understanding what Grove built.

## Key Frameworks (backed by citations)

**The Managerial Output Equation.** ([[Grove High Output Management]]) Manager's Output = Output of the manager's organization + Output of the neighboring organizations he influences. The formula reframes management as multiplication, not addition. The manager who codes one more feature themselves has added one feature; the manager who trains ten engineers to ship one more feature each has added ten. The reframe is structural and most managers resist it.

**Managerial Leverage.** ([[Grove High Output Management]]) The art of management is selecting and concentrating on one, two, or three high-leverage activities and ignoring the rest. High-leverage: training subordinates, building decision frameworks, removing organizational bottlenecks. Low-leverage: status reporting, meetings about meetings, individual contribution by the manager. The 10x-100x output differential between high and low leverage is the central operating insight.

**The One-on-One as Highest-Leverage Tool.** ([[Grove High Output Management]]) The 1:1 is the manager's single highest-leverage routine. Subordinate-led, weekly to bi-weekly, 90 minutes is the target length. The subordinate prepares the agenda and runs the meeting; the manager prepares to listen and ask questions. Training is delivered here; calibration happens here; the bottleneck gets surfaced here. The structure is load-bearing; without it, 1:1s degrade into status updates.

**Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM).** ([[Grove High Output Management]]) Management style must calibrate to the subordinate's TRM in the current task, not their seniority. A senior engineer promoted to manager has high TRM as engineer and low TRM as manager; style for that person should be directive, not hands-off. The same person 18 months later may need full autonomy.

**OKRs.** ([[Grove OKR Origin]]) Set 3-5 ambitious Objectives per quarter; define 3-5 measurable Key Results for each Objective. Score 0.0 to 1.0; a 0.7 average is healthy and signals appropriate stretch. Public OKRs across the org enable horizontal alignment without vertical micromanagement. The cadence is quarterly because that matches the natural rhythm of execution and the speed at which conditions change in technology.

**Strategic Inflection Points.** ([[Grove Only the Paranoid Survive]]) Moments when fundamental industry dynamics change by an order of magnitude. The "10x change." Examples Grove cites: Intel's pivot from memory to microprocessors in 1985, the shift from mainframe to PC, the disruption of vertical computing stacks by horizontal disaggregation. The CEO who fails to notice and pivot loses the company. Middle management is the early-warning system because they are closer to customers than the CEO is.

**Only the Paranoid Survive.** ([[Grove Only the Paranoid Survive]]) Not pessimism. Alertness to strategic inflection points before they become obvious. The CEO's job is to be in productive paranoia about the assumptions the company is built on, and to surface dissent that contradicts those assumptions before the contradiction is fatal.

**Pre-Decision Dissent, Post-Decision Commitment.** ([[Grove Stanford Talks]]) Before a decision is made, all dissent is welcomed and rewarded. After the decision is made, complete commitment is expected from everyone, regardless of which side they argued. The frame separates inquiry from execution and prevents post-decision sabotage by losers of the debate.

**Production Thinking for White-Collar Work.** ([[Grove High Output Management]]) Knowledge work has inputs, processes, outputs. Identifying bottlenecks and optimizing throughput is the manager's daily job. The "breakfast factory" analogy in HOM is the pedagogical bridge: if you can run a breakfast factory, the same principles apply to a sales team or an engineering org.

**Training is the Boss's Job.** ([[Grove High Output Management]]) Outsourcing training to HR loses the leverage point. The manager who trains their reports compounds team output. The manager who delegates training to a department loses the multiplier.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **The manager's output is the team's output, not the manager's individual output.** ([[Grove High Output Management]])
- **Pick the one to three highest-leverage activities; ignore everything else.** ([[Grove High Output Management]])
- **The 1:1 is subordinate-led; the manager prepares to listen.** ([[Grove High Output Management]])
- **Task-Relevant Maturity calibrates management style, not seniority.** ([[Grove High Output Management]])
- **OKRs are quarterly, public, scored 0-1, with 0.7 as healthy stretch.** ([[Grove OKR Origin]])
- **Strategic Inflection Points are 10x changes; middle management catches them first.** ([[Grove Only the Paranoid Survive]])
- **Paranoia is alertness, not pessimism.** ([[Grove Only the Paranoid Survive]])
- **Pre-decision dissent is welcomed; post-decision commitment is required.** ([[Grove Stanford Talks]])
- **Training is the boss's job; do not outsource it.** ([[Grove High Output Management]])
- **Knowledge work is production work; find the bottleneck.** ([[Grove High Output Management]])
