# Rob Fitzpatrick

Founder of Foundercentric, the consulting practice that trains product teams to run customer interviews that actually produce signal. YC alum (2007 cohort). Author of *The Mom Test: How to Talk to Customers and Learn If Your Business Is a Good Idea When Everyone Is Lying to You* (2013, self-published, 200K+ copies sold) and *The Workshop Survival Guide* (2019). The single most-cited voice on the mechanics of customer interviews, required reading inside Lean Startup, YC, and product-management curricula globally.

Anchor mentor on the **decision-customer-discovery pole**, paired with [[Steve Blank]] (the Customer Development methodology, "get out of the building," the pivot as planned action) and [[Annie Duke]] (decision quality, resulting, when to quit). Where Blank gives the strategy and Duke gives the decision discipline, Fitzpatrick gives the question-by-question tactics for the conversation itself.

---

## Why This Mentor

The brain already had implicit customer-discovery discipline from [[Paul Graham]] ("do things that don't scale," manual recruitment) and Lenny ("PMF is when users pull the product"). What was missing was the granular mechanics of the interview itself. Most founders run customer interviews and come back with confirmation. Fitzpatrick explains why that confirmation is almost always false, and how to redesign the questions so it cannot be.

His thesis is narrow and load-bearing. Most customer interviews produce false positives because customers lie to be polite. The fix is not to find more honest customers. The fix is to redesign the questions so that even your mother (who loves you and wants to encourage you) cannot give a false positive. The relationship is not the failure. The question pattern is the failure.

This frame, when applied, collapses the gap between "we did customer interviews" and "we have actual signal." It is the discipline that separates founders who genuinely learned from founders who merely confirmed.

---

## Key Frames

**The three rules of the Mom Test.** (1) Talk about their life, not your idea. (2) Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future. (3) Talk less, listen more. Every other rule in the book is a corollary of these three.

**Bad questions produce false positives.** "Do you think it's a good idea?" gets a polite yes. "Would you buy a product that does X?" is hypothetical and people overcommit to imagined futures. "How much would you pay for it?" has no skin in the game; the number is fiction. These are the questions every untrained founder asks.

**Good questions produce signal.** "What did you do the last time this problem came up?" (specific past behavior). "Where in your day does this fit?" (workflow context). "Why do you bother?" (motivation, reveals whether the problem is even real). "Can you show me how you currently solve this?" (observed behavior). Each one neutralizes a specific cognitive bias the bad version invites.

**The signal hierarchy.** Observed behavior beats stated past behavior beats stated hypothetical behavior beats stated opinion. Most founders only collect the bottom of the hierarchy and call it research.

**Commitments and progress.** A real customer signal is a behavioral commitment, not a verbal one. Three commitments scale, each stronger than the previous: time (will they meet again), reputation (will they introduce you to other potential customers), money (will they pay, pre-pay, or sign a letter of intent). Verbal interest without one of these is not signal.

**Anchor first, question second.** Before asking, share a specific contextual anchor so the customer doesn't drift into hypotheticals. Without an anchor, every answer is imagination.

**The conversation is about THEM, not about you.** If you're explaining your product, you're not learning. The interview is a research instrument, not a sales call. The moment you start pitching, the data ends.

**Deflect compliments.** When the customer says "great idea," the right response is to redirect to specifics about their own behavior. Compliments are pleasantries, not data.
