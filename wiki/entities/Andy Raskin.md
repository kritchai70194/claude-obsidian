# Andy Raskin

Strategic Narrative consultant. Former product manager at multiple startups and former senior editor at TIME and Inc Magazine. Has built strategic narratives for Salesforce, Yammer (pre-Microsoft acquisition), Zuora (pre-IPO), Drift, ServiceMax, Mulesoft, and others. The canonical voice on the 5-part narrative arc that turns a product pitch into a movement.

Anchor mentor on the **gtm-sales pole**, complementary to [[Mark Roberge]] (data-driven sales hiring and process), [[Aaron Ross]] (outbound prospecting and the SDR/AE split), and [[April Dunford]] (positioning as strategic discipline). Where Roberge and Ross describe the mechanics of the sales motion, and Dunford defines the category position, Raskin defines the story the company tells about the world it operates in. The narrative comes first; everything else hangs off it.

---

## Why This Mentor

Most pitches lead with the product. They open with "we built X that does Y", and the prospect immediately runs an implicit cost-benefit calculation against whatever they currently use. The pitch becomes a feature war. Raskin's contribution is to invert this entirely: the pitch should open with a named change in the world that the prospect cannot deny, and only then introduce the product as the response to that change.

Raskin discovered this pattern by reverse-engineering pitches from companies that broke through, most famously Zuora in 2016. He observed that the same 5-part structure showed up across every great enterprise pitch deck: (1) Name the World Change, (2) Stakes, (3) Promised Land, (4) Obstacles, (5) Evidence. The product appears late, after the world has been established. He named the structure, taught it as a workshop, and built a consulting practice around installing it inside companies.

The structure has three properties that matter. First, it is multi-use: the same narrative powers the investor pitch, the sales pitch, the recruiting pitch, the analyst briefing, and internal alignment. Second, it builds trust through obstacle-naming, the move that most companies skip. Third, it shifts the buyer's decision frame from "do I want this feature" to "which side of the world change am I on".

Frames here: the world change must be both provocative AND undeniable; the product is the guide, not the hero; obstacle-naming is the trust move; narrative is upstream of positioning, messaging, and copy; if your customers cannot name the world change in their own words, you do not yet have a narrative.

## Key Frameworks

**The 5-Part Strategic Narrative Arc.** The structure Raskin extracts from every great enterprise pitch.

1. **Name a named, undeniable change in the world.** Not a trend, not a vibe, a specific shift the prospect can see. "The world is moving from one-time transactions to subscription relationships." "Messaging is the new home page." This is the single hardest move.
2. **Show the stakes.** Winners and losers in the new world. Who thrives, who is left behind. The stakes make the change matter.
3. **Paint the Promised Land.** What the future looks like for the winners. Specific, vivid, attainable. Not your product; the outcome.
4. **Identify Obstacles.** What stops most people from getting there. Naming these honestly is the trust move. It signals that you understand the buyer's reality.
5. **Position as the Guide with Evidence.** Now, finally, the company appears. Specific capabilities, proof points, customer outcomes. The company is the guide that helps the buyer cross to the Promised Land.

**Strategic narrative is not a tagline, not a product story, not a pitch deck.** It is the story of the world the company operates in. The product appears only after the world has been established. Confusing narrative with tagline is the most common failure.

**The single hardest move: naming the world change.** Too provocative and nobody believes it; too undeniable and nobody cares. The right naming sits in the small zone where the prospect's reaction is "yes, that is true, and I had not put it that way before".

**Narrative is multi-use.** Investor pitch, sales pitch, recruiting pitch, internal alignment, analyst briefing. Same 5-part arc, different emphasis on each beat. Companies that maintain one narrative across all five surfaces compound trust; companies that have five different stories fragment.

**The product is the guide, not the hero.** The buyer is the hero of their own story. The company shows up as the guide with a plan that helps the hero reach the Promised Land. This is structurally borrowed from Joseph Campbell and Donald Miller's StoryBrand, but Raskin operationalizes it for enterprise sales.

**Obstacle-naming builds trust.** Most pitches skip obstacles because they look like negatives. Done right, naming the obstacles the buyer is actually facing signals that the seller understands the buyer's world. The buyer's internal reaction: "this seller gets it".
