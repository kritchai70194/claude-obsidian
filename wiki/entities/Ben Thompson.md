# Ben Thompson

Author of Stratechery (2013-present), daily strategy analysis of the technology industry. Originated Aggregation Theory (2015), one of the most-cited frameworks for understanding modern tech competition. Operates a ~$15M/year subscription analyst business as a solo practitioner: the canonical proof that one writer with a frame can outperform whole research firms.

Anchor mentor on the **strategy and moats pole** of this brain's [[Persona]]. Where the existing founder-operators describe what they built, Thompson describes *why* certain builds become unkillable and others stay vulnerable.

---

## Why This Mentor

Aggregation Theory is the single most-cited modern strategy framework. It answers a question that pre-internet strategy (Porter's Five Forces, Christensen disruption) could not: why do digital companies converge on winner-take-all dynamics that don't exist in physical markets? Thompson's answer: because the marginal cost of distribution online is zero, and whoever owns the user relationship at zero marginal cost wins.

The implications cascade: build a moat with distribution, not with product features; modularize your suppliers and integrate your demand; never let a third party own your user. Modern unicorns (Stripe, Airbnb, Shopify, OpenAI) can be analyzed cleanly through this frame.

Thompson's business model is itself a proof point: Stratechery is the canonical premium-tier subscription analyst business. He chose to own his audience directly (the aggregator's logic applied to himself) rather than write for a publication.

Frames here: distribution is the moat, not the product; aggregators win by owning the user relationship at zero marginal cost; modularize suppliers, integrate demand; the platform/aggregator distinction matters more than the network-effect framing.

## Core Frames (Pending Citation)

*Stub list, each will graduate to a primary-source citation as ingestion completes.*

- **Distribution is the moat, not the product.** The product is necessary but not sufficient. The durable advantage is owning the user relationship.
- **Aggregators win at zero marginal cost.** Software has zero marginal cost of distribution. Whoever owns the user at scale wins; suppliers become commoditized.
- **Modularize your suppliers, integrate your demand.** The aggregator's playbook. Many suppliers, one user relationship.
- **The platform vs aggregator distinction.** Platforms (Microsoft, AWS) enable third parties to build products that they sell to users directly. Aggregators (Google, Facebook, Amazon) intermediate between users and suppliers. Critical for positioning.
- **Build for the audience you own.** Stratechery itself is the proof: the writer who owns the relationship outperforms the writer who borrows it from a publication.
