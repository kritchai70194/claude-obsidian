# Mike Maples Jr

Co-founder and Partner at Floodgate (founded 2008 with Ann Miura-Ko), one of the earliest dedicated seed funds. Stanford engineering background. Operated two startups (Motive Communications, Tivoli Systems) before turning full-time to seed investing. Author of *Pattern Breakers* (Public Affairs, July 2024, with Peter Ziebelski). Host of the Starting Greatness podcast. Seed investor in Twitter (2007), Twitch, Lyft, Okta, Outreach, Bazaarvoice, Demandforce, ngmoco, Chegg, Sumo Logic.

Anchor mentor on the **capital-strategy pole** alongside [[Reid Hoffman]] (blitzscale at scale), [[Bill Gurley]] (marketplace economics and burn discipline), and [[Peter Thiel]] (monopoly and secrets). Where Thiel asks what secret you know, Maples asks what inflection you are riding. Where Hoffman names how to deploy capital at scale, Maples names what makes a founder worth funding at seed.

---

## Why This Mentor

The other three capital-strategy mentors operate downstream of seed. Hoffman's blitzscaling assumes you already have a product worth pouring fuel onto. Gurley's marketplace math assumes the marketplace exists. Thiel's monopoly thesis assumes you already have a contrarian idea worth executing on. Maples names what comes before all of that: the conditions under which a breakthrough startup is even possible, and the founder traits that make one investable.

His Twitter seed story is the canonical case. In 2007 he invested when the product was a fail-whale-prone SMS curiosity that most professional investors considered a toy. His thesis was not about the product as it then existed. It was about the founders' insight into what public, asynchronous, low-friction status broadcasting would become once mobile broadband was ubiquitous. He bet the inflection, not the idea.

Two contributions matter most. First, **inflections precede ideas**: most startup ideas are actually responses to an empirical shift in the world (mobile, social, cloud, AI, deregulation), and ideas without inflections are tar pits. Second, **live in the future**: the founders who reliably see opportunities the rest of the market misses are not the ones who imagine the future, but the ones whose daily life already runs on the technology of the future.

Frames here: pattern matching kills returns and pattern breaking produces them; the secret manifesto is the founder's articulated belief about the future that the world does not yet accept; movements pull customers in, products push messaging out; backable founders combine insight, authenticity, creativity, persuasiveness, and stamina.

## Key Frameworks (now backed by citations)

**Inflections vs Ideas.** An idea is a proposed solution. An inflection is an empirical shift in the world that makes previously impossible solutions newly possible. Mobile broadband, the smartphone, cloud compute, the transformer, GLP-1s, regulatory change, generational behavior shifts. Most successful breakout startups ride one to three inflections. Ideas without inflections behave like tar pits: they look plausible, they attract effort, and they cannot escape because the underlying physics of the market do not yet support them.

**Live in the Future.** Founders who reliably see what is coming next are not forecasting it; they are already living it. The Airbnb founders hosted guests on airbeds before they built the company. The 23andMe founders were inside the genomics conversation before consumer genomics was a category. The habit, lifestyle, and work environment of the founder IS the future being broadly adopted later. The lesson for seed investors: look for founders whose daily life already runs on the inflection.

**Pattern Breakers vs Pattern Matchers.** Most VCs reward pattern matching: this team looks like that successful team, this product looks like that successful product. The biggest seed returns historically came from pattern breakers: Twitter looked like SMS spam, Airbnb looked like a couch surfing toy, Lyft looked like illegal taxis. The pattern-matching filter actively rejects the largest outcomes.

**The Secret Manifesto.** A backable founder can articulate, specifically, what they believe about the future that the world does not yet accept. Not "social is big" but "public asynchronous status updates will become a primary information channel because mobile broadband makes them frictionless." The specificity of the manifesto is the diagnostic. Vague founders fail the test.

**Insights vs Assumptions.** Most pitches are assumptions framed as insights. A real insight is a specific, falsifiable claim about how the world works that the pitcher can defend with evidence the rest of the market has not yet processed. The seed investor's job is to separate the two.

**Backable Founder Traits.** Insight, authenticity, creativity, persuasiveness, stamina. Insight sees what others miss. Authenticity lives the problem. Creativity designs solutions others would not consider. Persuasiveness recruits and raises. Stamina survives the multi-decade game. Missing any of the five and the founder is fundable but not backable.

**Movements, Not Products.** Breakthrough startups build movements that pull customers in. Products push messaging out. The movement is a felt belief about the future that customers want to be part of, and the product is its tangible expression. Lyft was not "an Uber competitor"; it was a movement about reclaiming the commute.
