# Patty McCord

Chief Talent Officer at Netflix from 1998 to 2012, working alongside Reed Hastings through the company's transition from a DVD-by-mail subscription business into a global streaming platform. Co-architect of the **Netflix Culture Deck** (2009), the 125-slide SlideShare document on "freedom and responsibility" that has been viewed more than 20 million times and named by Sheryl Sandberg as "the most important document ever to come out of the Valley." Author of *Powerful, Building a Culture of Freedom and Responsibility* (Silicon Guild, 2018). Now a speaker and consultant on culture for high-performance companies.

Anchor mentor on the **operating-at-scale pole**, complementary to [[Andy Grove]] (management cadence), [[Frank Slootman]] (raising standards and tempo), and [[Keith Rabois]] (high-bar hiring rubrics). Where Grove builds the management substrate, Slootman runs the company hot, and Rabois screens at the input, McCord defines the cultural operating system inside which the other three actually function.

---

## Why This Mentor

Most "culture" writing is aspirational. It describes the company the executive team wishes they had, not the one they actually run. McCord's work is the inverse. The Netflix Culture Deck described actual Netflix behavior, including the hard parts: paying top-of-market, willingness to part with people whose skills no longer matched the stage, the rejection of process-as-control, the absence of vacation and expense policies. The deck spread because operators recognized it as a description rather than a wish.

Her contribution to the brain is the **adults-not-children frame** for HR. Most companies write policies for the worst employee they have ever had. The result is a policy stack that insults the median employee and constrains the best. McCord's reframe: hire fully formed adults, give them context and authority, expect professional judgment, and run a generous off-ramp for people whose skills no longer match the work. The model only functions if hiring rigor is real, which is why it pairs structurally with [[Keith Rabois]].

Her second contribution is the **Keeper Test** as severance discipline. The annual performance review is replaced by a continuous question: would I fight to keep this person if they tried to leave? The framing turns the awkward conversation about fit into a tractable management question and pairs it with generous severance for the people the test surfaces. Honest, expensive, more humane than keeping someone in a role they are failing.

Cross-pole, McCord pairs with [[Ben Horowitz]] on "take care of the people first" but disagrees on tenure. Horowitz argues for fighting to keep talented people through hard transitions. McCord argues that the team you need at 200 people is often not the team you needed at 20, and the kindest move is to recognize the mismatch early. Both are right in different conditions.

## Key Frameworks (backed by citations)

**Hire Fully Formed Adults.** ([[McCord Powerful]], [[McCord HBR Essays]]) Treat employees as professionals capable of judgment. Do not write policies for the worst case. The policy stack at most companies exists because someone, once, did something dumb; the cost of the resulting policy falls on everyone forever. Replace the policy stack with hiring rigor and contextual judgment.

**Freedom and Responsibility, High Context Low Control.** ([[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]], [[McCord Powerful]]) The executive layer communicates strategy, priorities, and outcomes in high resolution. The team chooses how to deliver. Trust replaces approval workflows. The model breaks if the context is thin or the hiring bar is low; both are non-negotiable preconditions.

**The Keeper Test.** ([[McCord Powerful]], [[McCord HBR Essays]]) Replace annual performance reviews with a single recurring question, would I fight to keep this person if they tried to leave for a comparable role elsewhere? If yes, they are a Keeper. If no, they are a candidate for an honest conversation and a generous severance. The test is fast, honest, and forces the manager to do the calibration work continuously rather than annually in a rubric.

**Pay Top of Market for Top Performers.** ([[McCord Powerful]], [[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]]) The rockstar principle. One excellent engineer is worth 5-10 average ones. Pay the rockstar 3x and the company is net ahead on output, coordination cost, and culture. The corollary, do not retain mediocre performers at any price, because the cost is paid in the morale of the strong performers.

**No Vacation Policy, No Expense Policy.** ([[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]], [[McCord HBR Essays]]) "Use good judgment in the interest of Netflix." The absence of policy worked because the company hired fully formed adults and fired the few who abused the absence. Most companies that copy the policy do not copy the hiring rigor and get burned. The policy is downstream of the bar.

**Culture is What You Do, Not What You Say.** ([[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]], [[McCord Conference Talks]]) Mission statements and values posters describe aspirational behavior. Real culture is described by what gets rewarded, what gets tolerated, and who gets promoted. The Culture Deck documented actual behavior; that is why it spread.

**The Team You Need Now is Not the Team You Have.** ([[McCord Powerful]]) Hire for the skills the company needs at its current stage, not for the skills the company will need in 18 months and not out of loyalty to the skills the company needed two years ago. The Keeper Test surfaces the mismatch. Generous severance closes it.

**Honest Conversation is the Job.** ([[McCord Powerful]], [[McCord HBR Essays]]) Most managers avoid the hard conversation about fit until it becomes a firing. The honest conversation, held early and often, is more humane and more effective. Tell people the truth about their performance continuously. Annual reviews are a substitute for the conversation, not the conversation itself.

**Take Care of People in Transition.** ([[McCord HBR Essays]]) The exit is part of the job. Generous severance, clean references, honest framing. Done well, the people you part with become alumni who refer talent and customers back to you. Done badly, they become the loudest critics in the market.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Hire fully formed adults; do not write policies for the worst case.** ([[McCord Powerful]], [[McCord HBR Essays]])
- **Freedom and responsibility require high context and a high hiring bar as preconditions.** ([[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]])
- **The Keeper Test replaces the annual review with a continuous calibration question.** ([[McCord Powerful]])
- **Pay top of market for top performers; do not retain mediocre at any price.** ([[McCord Powerful]])
- **Use good judgment in the interest of the company; no vacation or expense policy needed when the bar is real.** ([[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]])
- **Culture is what you do; the Culture Deck documents actual behavior.** ([[McCord Netflix Culture Deck]])
- **The team you need now is not the team you have; recognize the mismatch and resolve it generously.** ([[McCord Powerful]])
- **Tell people the truth continuously; do not save it for the annual review.** ([[McCord HBR Essays]])
- **Severance is part of the job; alumni done right are referrers, alumni done wrong are critics.** ([[McCord HBR Essays]])
