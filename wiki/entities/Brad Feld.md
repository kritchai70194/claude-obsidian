# Brad Feld

Co-founder and Managing Director of Foundry Group (Boulder, founded 2007), co-founder of Techstars (founded 2006, now a global accelerator network), and one of the longest-running operator-bloggers in venture (feld.com runs back to 2004). Author or co-author of *Venture Deals* (with Jason Mendelson, Wiley, first edition 2011 and now in its fifth), *Startup Boards* (Wiley 2013, revised 2022 with Mahendra Ramsinghani), *Startup Life* (with his wife Amy Batchelor, Wiley 2013), *Startup Communities* (2012), and *Do More Faster* (2010).

Anchor mentor on the **founder-resilience pole**, complementary to [[Ben Horowitz]] (wartime CEO, the texture of the fight), [[Naval Ravikant]] (leverage, specific knowledge, the philosophical frame), and [[Brian Chesky]] (Founder Mode at scale). Where Horowitz covers the single-company near-death arc, Feld covers the multi-decade rhythm: how to stay operational as a founder across many companies, many cycles, and many years.

---

## Why This Mentor

Feld fills three gaps the rest of the corpus does not touch. The first is **founder mental health**. Almost no public operator has written as openly, as early, or as repeatedly about depression in founders as Feld did, starting in 2013. Before his essays, founder depression was a private conversation. After them, it became a category VC firms, accelerators, and CEO peer groups had to address.

The second is **board dynamics**. Most founder material treats the board as either decorative or adversarial. Feld treats it as a tool to be wielded. *Startup Boards* is the operator manual for how to choose directors, structure meetings, and fire bad ones, written from the rare position of having sat on the board side of the table for thousands of meetings.

The third is **the multi-decade rhythm**. Feld has been doing this work for ~35 years. He has explicit frames for what compounds over 5, 10, and 20 years that founders planning quarter-to-quarter rarely see. The "Boulder thesis" (build a startup community where you are, not where you wish you were) is one downstream artifact of that long horizon.

Frames here: integrate work and life rather than balancing them; depression is medical, not weakness; the board exists to help the CEO succeed; strategy is the CEO's job, not the board's; pathological term sheets predict pathological investor relationships; most things compound on horizons longer than founders plan for.

## Key Frameworks (now backed by citations)

**Work-Life Harmony, not Work-Life Balance.** The "balance" frame implies two buckets that trade off. Feld and Batchelor argue the buckets are not separable for founders and that pretending they are produces worse outcomes in both. Better frame: design the integration intentionally. Qualitative time (presence, attention) matters more than quantitative time (hours).

**Depression is common in founders and is medical, not characterological.** Isolation, identity fusion with the company, financial precarity, and all-in commitment combine to produce elevated rates of clinical depression. Treatment (therapy, medication, peer support, time off) works. Silence makes it worse.

**The board is a tool, not a body to be appeased.** A bad board is worse than no board. The founder/CEO actively curates the board, sets the agenda, runs the meeting, and removes members who do not contribute. The board provides oversight, network, and capital; the board does not provide strategy.

**Pathological term sheets predict pathological investor relationships.** Investors should write deal terms with the assumption that the company will succeed. Aggressive control terms, ratchets, and punitive liquidation preferences signal an investor who is planning for failure and will behave accordingly.

**The 5-year / 10-year / 20-year horizon.** Most things that matter (community, reputation, compounding networks, durable companies) operate on horizons longer than founders plan for. Boulder vs Valley is not a one-decade question.

## Primary-Source Citations

### From [[Feld Startup Life]] (2013)

Co-authored with Amy Batchelor. The thesis of work-life harmony, with practical chapters on travel, sleep, relationships, vacation, and stress. The qualitative-vs-quantitative time distinction is the load-bearing reframe.

> We don't believe in work-life balance. We believe in work-life harmony.

Off-the-grid weeks (no phone, no email, fully present) are operationalized as a discipline, not a vacation. The book treats relationships with partners and friends as infrastructure that needs maintenance, not background that survives on its own.

### From [[Feld On Depression]] (2013-present)

The blog series that broke the silence on founder depression. Feld wrote openly about his own clinical depression starting in 2013, then kept writing about it for over a decade.

> I have been a depressive my entire adult life. I have been on antidepressants for over 20 years.

The contribution is the public modeling: a successful VC and operator naming the condition, naming the treatment, and treating it as ordinary medical management rather than a private shame. The cultural shift across the founder community traces back in part to these posts.

### From [[Feld Startup Boards]] (2013, revised 2022)

The operator manual. Chapters on choosing directors, running meetings, the founder-board contract, removing a bad board member, and the advisor-vs-director distinction.

> The board exists to help the CEO succeed. If it does not, change the board.

Pre-reads go out 72 hours in advance. Meetings discuss strategy, not status. Surprises are a failure mode. Independent directors matter more than founders think.

### From [[Feld VC Rights]] (2012)

The most-cited Feld term-sheet frame. Distills a venture term sheet to the three rights an investor actually needs: Up (pro-rata), Down (liquidation preference), KWTFIGO (board seat or info rights). Everything beyond is either standard machinery, lawyer-driven complexity, or pathology. Companion to the *Venture Deals* book and load-bearing for the clean-vs-dirty term-sheet distinction.

### From [[Feld Bringing Depression Out of the Shadows]] (2015)

Two years downstream of the 2013 breakthrough. Names three discrete depressive episodes (mid-20s, post-9/11, late 2012) and specific triggers (physiological exhaustion, lack of learning, accumulated injuries). Operationalizes the "never show weakness" programming as the cultural obstacle. Points at Reboot.io as the structured peer-support resource.

### From [[Feld Founders Companion]] (2018)

The institutionalization moment. Feld writes the foreword for Mahendra Ramsinghani's *Depression - A Founder's Companion*: anonymized peer-research survey, contributed stories and recovery practices, a permanent reference shelf. Marks the transition from one VC's blog confession to a category with multiple practitioners and a peer-research base.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Work-Life Harmony, not Balance.** ([[Feld Startup Life]]) The buckets are not separable for founders; design the integration.
- **Qualitative time beats quantitative time.** ([[Feld Startup Life]]) Presence over hours.
- **Depression in founders is common and medical.** ([[Feld On Depression]]) Treatment works; silence does not.
- **The board is a tool the CEO wields.** ([[Feld Startup Boards]]) Curate it actively; remove members who do not contribute.
- **Strategy is the CEO's job; oversight is the board's.** ([[Feld Startup Boards]]) Confusing the two breaks both roles.
- **Pre-reads, no surprises, in-meeting strategy discussion.** ([[Feld Startup Boards]]) The operating discipline of a good board meeting.
- **Independent directors matter more than founders think.** ([[Feld Startup Boards]]) Investor directors carry investor incentives; independents carry the company's.
- **Pathological term sheets predict pathological investor relationships.** ([[Feld Startup Boards]], [[Feld VC Rights]]) Term sheets written for failure produce relationships that behave for failure.
- **The three load-bearing VC rights: Up, Down, KWTFIGO.** ([[Feld VC Rights]]) Pro-rata, liquidation preference, board/info rights. Everything beyond is noise or pathology.
- **Depression is recurrent in founders; plan for episodes, not one bad year.** ([[Feld Bringing Depression Out of the Shadows]]) Specific triggers, not vague stress.
- **From confession to institution.** ([[Feld Founders Companion]]) Name the condition, then operationalize it, then formalize it as a field with multiple practitioners.
- **Build the community where you are.** ([[Feld Startup Life]], adjacent to *Startup Communities*) Boulder thesis: long-horizon community-building beats geographic migration.
- **Most things compound on horizons longer than founders plan for.** ([[Feld Startup Life]], [[Feld Startup Boards]]) The 5/10/20-year frame.
