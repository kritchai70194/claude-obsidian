# Charlie Munger

Vice Chairman of Berkshire Hathaway from 1978 until his death in 2023 at age 99. Co-architect with Warren Buffett of the Berkshire value-investing philosophy that turned a failing textile company into the world's most valuable holding conglomerate. Founded the law firm Munger Tolles & Olson in 1962. Chairman of Wesco Financial (1984-2011) and the Daily Journal Corporation. Trained as a meteorologist in the Army Air Corps, then as a lawyer at Harvard Law (1948), then converted to full-time investing in the late 1960s.

Anchor mentor on the **mental-models pole**, complementary to [[Daniel Kahneman]] (cognitive biases as a research program), [[Nassim Taleb]] (uncertainty, optionality, fat tails), and [[Clayton Christensen]] (disruption and Jobs to Be Done). Where Kahneman catalogues biases as a psychologist, Munger weaponized them as an operator. Where Taleb writes about uncertainty, Munger lived inside it for sixty years of capital allocation.

## Why This Mentor

Munger fills the gap that the other four poles assume away. [[seed-corpus-technical-ceo]], [[seed-corpus-engineer-planners]], [[seed-corpus-unicorn-builders]], and [[seed-corpus-founder-resilience]] are all domain-specific. They assume the founder already has a way to reason across domains they have never operated in. Munger names that toolkit explicitly and is the canonical voice behind it.

His central claim is that **most people think with one model and force every problem through it**. The economist sees incentives. The psychologist sees biases. The engineer sees systems. Each is right inside their model and wrong outside it. The fix is to build a **latticework of mental models** drawn from the major disciplines (psychology, economics, biology, physics, mathematics, history, statistics, accounting) and apply many of them to every problem.

The second contribution is **inversion**. Forward reasoning is biased toward action and confirmation. Backward reasoning ("what would guarantee failure?") exposes constraints the forward view hides. Munger applied inversion to investing (avoid stupidity beats seek brilliance), to life (the 2007 USC commencement is the inversion masterclass), and to capital allocation (avoid the catastrophic loss, the compounding takes care of itself).

The third contribution is the **25 Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment**, the 1995 Harvard speech that became the foundational checklist for applied behavioral economics. Where Kahneman documented biases for academic publication, Munger codified them as a checklist an operator can run before a decision.

Frames here: build the latticework, invert always, know your circle of competence, watch for Lollapalooza effects (multiple biases compounding in the same direction), prefer not-stupid over brilliant, read deeply across disciplines, never underestimate the power of incentives.

## Key Frameworks (now backed by citations)

**Latticework of Mental Models.** Build a working knowledge of the big ideas from the major disciplines and use them in combination. ([[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]]) Most academic specialists are intellectually crippled by knowing only their own model. The cure is the multidisciplinary lattice.

**Invert, Always Invert.** ([[Munger USC Misery]], [[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]]) Adopted from Carl Jacobi. To solve a problem, restate it backwards. To live a happy life, ask what would guarantee misery, then avoid that. To invest well, ask what would guarantee ruin, then avoid that.

**25 Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment.** ([[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]]) The canonical bias inventory built for operators: reward-and-punishment superresponse, liking/loving, disliking/hating, doubt-avoidance, inconsistency-avoidance, curiosity, Kantian fairness, envy/jealousy, reciprocation, influence-from-mere-association, simple pain-avoiding psychological denial, excessive self-regard, overoptimism, deprival-superreaction, social-proof, contrast-misreaction, stress-influence, availability-misweighing, use-it-or-lose-it, drug-misinfluence, senescence-misinfluence, authority-misinfluence, twaddle-tendency, reason-respecting, and the Lollapalooza tendency.

**Lollapalooza Effect.** ([[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]]) When multiple biases push in the same direction simultaneously, the outcome is extreme rather than additive. Cult dynamics, casino design, Tupperware parties, the dot-com bubble, and most market manias are Lollapalooza events. The diagnostic skill is recognizing the stack, not the single bias.

**Circle of Competence.** ([[Munger Berkshire Q&A]], [[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]]) Know the perimeter of what you actually understand. The size of the circle matters less than knowing where the edge is. Most disasters happen one inch outside.

**Avoid Stupidity Over Seek Brilliance.** ([[Munger Berkshire Q&A]], [[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]]) The asymmetry that underwrote Berkshire. Compounding rewards the survivor. The catastrophic loss is what ends the compounding, so the central discipline is not making the brilliant move, it is avoiding the stupid one.

## Primary-Source Citations

### From [[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]] (2005, 4th ed. 2023)

The compilation of Munger's talks and letters, edited by Peter Kaufman. The fourth edition (2023) was made freely available at poorcharliesalmanack.com. The signature talks ("Practical Thought About Practical Thought", "Academic Economics", "Psychology of Human Misjudgment") plus the latticework essays and Munger's reading list form the core canon.

On the latticework:

> You must know the big ideas in the big disciplines and use them routinely, all of them, not just a few.

On the operator's stance:

> It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent.

### From [[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]] (1995 Harvard speech, revised 2005)

The most-cited Munger work. Twenty-five standard causes of human misjudgment, each with a name, a mechanism, and operator examples. The 2005 revision (in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*) is the canonical text.

On incentives, the first cause:

> Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives.

The Lollapalooza concept is introduced here as the explanation for outcomes that look irrational from a single-bias view. The checklist use of the 25 causes is the operator-facing application.

### From [[Munger USC Misery]] (2007 USC Law School commencement)

The inversion masterclass. Munger refuses to give a normal commencement speech and instead lists the things that would guarantee a life of misery, so the graduates can avoid them. Be unreliable. Learn everything from your own experience only. Go down and stay down when life delivers reversals. Ignore the lessons of the dead.

### From [[Munger Berkshire Q&A]] (1996-2023 annual meetings)

Decades of Q&A transcripts from Berkshire Hathaway, Wesco, and Daily Journal annual meetings. The applied layer of everything in the books: incentives drive behavior, the value of saying "I don't know", the asymmetry of avoiding stupidity, why most active investors lose, the right cost of capital.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Build a latticework of mental models from the major disciplines.** ([[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]], [[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]]) One-model thinkers are intellectually crippled inside their model.
- **Invert, always invert.** ([[Munger USC Misery]], [[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]]) Forward reasoning is biased; backward reasoning exposes the real constraints.
- **Multiple biases compounding in the same direction produce extreme outcomes.** ([[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]]) The Lollapalooza effect explains cults, manias, and casino design.
- **Operate inside your circle of competence; know the perimeter.** ([[Munger Berkshire Q&A]]) Size of circle matters less than knowing where the edge is.
- **Avoid stupidity beats seek brilliance.** ([[Munger Berkshire Q&A]]) The catastrophic loss ends the compounding; the central discipline is not the brilliant move but the avoided stupid one.
- **Never underestimate the power of incentives.** ([[Munger Psychology of Misjudgment]], [[Munger Berkshire Q&A]]) "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome."
- **Read 500 pages a day.** ([[Munger Poor Charlies Almanack]]) Compound knowledge across disciplines is the input the latticework runs on.
- **Saying "I don't know" is a competitive advantage.** ([[Munger Berkshire Q&A]]) Most investors fail because they pretend to know.
