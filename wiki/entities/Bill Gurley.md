# Bill Gurley

General Partner at Benchmark Capital (1999-2020). Board director at Uber (2011-2017), OpenTable, GrubHub, Zillow, Stitch Fix, NextDoor. Author of *Above the Crowd* at abovethecrowd.com, an essay archive running from 1996 through the present. Before venture, Gurley was a sell-side semiconductor analyst at CS First Boston (covering Dell, Compaq, and Intel) and later a partner at Hummer Winblad. Six-foot-nine, Texan, the contrarian voice on late-stage private valuations during the 2014-2016 unicorn boom.

Anchor mentor on the **capital-strategy pole** alongside [[Reid Hoffman]] (blitzscale at scale), [[Peter Thiel]] (monopoly thesis), and [[Mike Maples]] (inflections and backable founders). Where Hoffman names when speed beats efficiency, Gurley names when speed becomes self-immolation. Where Thiel argues for the unique monopoly, Gurley audits whether that "monopoly" actually has the marketplace economics to sustain it.

---

## Why This Mentor

Gurley fills the gap between thesis and survival. The rest of this brain assumes capital can be deployed productively. Gurley audits the deployment. Marketplaces, late-stage rounds, burn rates, and the gap between paper valuations and real outcomes are his beat.

Two contributions matter most. First, the **marketplace evaluation framework** ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]]), which says that two marketplaces with identical GMV can have radically different long-term economics depending on take-rate sustainability, frequency, friction, and network-effect quality. Second, the **late-stage capital warnings** of 2015-2016 ([[Gurley J-curve and Recap]]), which named the structural traps of dirty terms at inflated valuations years before the 2022-2023 markdowns vindicated the call.

Frames here: liquidity is the early marketplace metric; take rate above the low double digits invites disintermediation; the J-curve of late-stage burn destroys companies even when growth looks healthy; dirty terms at sky-high valuations are a worse outcome than a flat round at a real price; the board's job is to push back, not cheerlead.

## Key Frameworks

**The 10 Marketplace Factors.** Not all marketplaces are equal. New experience vs old, large dollar market, ability to expand the market, transaction frequency, value delivered before any take, network effects, friction reduction (UX), expansion opportunity, founder fit, take-rate sustainability. A marketplace that fails on multiple factors will not produce venture-scale outcomes regardless of early traction. ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]])

**Take Rate and Rake.** Sustainable take rate sits in the low double digits. Anything materially higher invites disintermediation: suppliers route around you, buyers route around you, or a competitor enters with a thinner cut. The rake math is the single most-load-bearing variable in marketplace economics. ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]], [[Gurley Above the Crowd]])

**Burn Rate as Company Killer.** Spending more than you can credibly raise destroys the company even when growth looks great. The relevant ratio is not GMV growth or revenue multiple; it is months of runway against credible next-round demand. ([[Gurley J-curve and Recap]])

**The Unicorn Party and Dirty Terms.** Late-stage rounds at headline valuations come with terms (ratchets, participating preferred, multi-x liquidation preferences, IPO ratchets) that protect the last money in at the expense of every prior holder. Founders accepting these terms to preserve the paper valuation often discover at exit that the headline was never theirs to own. ([[Gurley J-curve and Recap]])

**Customer Concentration is a Moat-Killer.** Whales eventually leverage their concentration to extract terms. A revenue base that looks defensible because it includes a marquee logo is actually fragile: the logo has all the leverage. ([[Gurley Above the Crowd]])

**The Board's Job is to Push Back.** Board members who cheerlead instead of challenging the CEO are not earning their seat. Excessive enthusiasm at the board level is how good companies set fire to their own runway. ([[Gurley All-In Podcast]])

## Core Frames

- **Liquidity is the only thing that matters in early marketplaces.** ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]]) Take rate, brand, UX all follow. Without liquidity in at least one local segment, none of the rest exists.
- **Take rate above the low double digits is a strategic vulnerability.** ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]], [[Gurley Above the Crowd]]) High rakes invite disintermediation and competitor entry.
- **Burn rate is the company killer.** ([[Gurley J-curve and Recap]]) Not competition, not product, not market. The thing that actually ends most well-funded startups is spending past credible raise capacity.
- **High growth is not the same as sustainable growth.** ([[Gurley J-curve and Recap]]) Founders consistently confuse "we are growing" with "we will continue to grow at this unit economics."
- **Headline valuation is not the real valuation when terms are dirty.** ([[Gurley J-curve and Recap]]) Many 2014-2016 unicorns were 30-70% below their last round at any honest accounting.
- **Customer concentration is fragility, not strength.** ([[Gurley Above the Crowd]]) Whales extract terms over time.
- **The board is a check on enthusiasm, not a cheerleader.** ([[Gurley All-In Podcast]]) Directors who do not push back are passengers.
- **Late-stage private capital removes optionality.** ([[Gurley J-curve and Recap]]) Each dirty round forecloses outcomes that would have been available at a lower honest price.
- **"All markets are not created equal."** ([[Gurley All Markets Not Equal]]) Two marketplaces with identical GMV can have radically different long-term outcomes.
- **Network effects are claimed more often than they exist.** ([[Gurley Above the Crowd]]) Most "moats" cited at fundraising are growth-rate artifacts, not structural defensibility.
