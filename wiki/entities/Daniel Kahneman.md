# Daniel Kahneman

Israeli-American psychologist (1934-2024). Nobel laureate in Economics (2002) for prospect theory, developed with Amos Tversky in the 1970s. Author of *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011), the canonical popular text on dual-process cognition, and *Noise: A Flaw in Human Judgment* (Little, Brown Spark, 2021, with Olivier Sibony and Cass Sunstein). Senior Scholar at Princeton. Died March 27, 2024 at 90.

Anchor mentor on the **mental-models pole** for cognitive bias and dual-process cognition, complementary to [[Charlie Munger]] (latticework architect and inversion), [[Nassim Taleb]] (uncertainty and antifragility), and [[Clayton Christensen]] (disruption and Jobs to Be Done). Where Munger names a list of standard biases from observation, Kahneman supplies the experimental and theoretical foundation underneath them. Munger and Kahneman do not contradict; they triangulate the same target from different angles.

## Why This Mentor

Kahneman fills the systematic-bias and dual-process gap that the other four poles assume away. [[seed-corpus-technical-ceo]], [[seed-corpus-engineer-planners]], [[seed-corpus-unicorn-builders]], and [[seed-corpus-founder-resilience]] all presume the operator's judgment is approximately rational once data is on the table. Kahneman's life work is the evidence that this is false in specific, predictable, repeatable ways. The brain needs an explicit inventory of those failure modes.

Two contributions matter most. First, the **System 1 / System 2** model, which says that most cognition is fast, automatic, and pattern-based, and that the slow, deliberate, logical override is expensive and often does not engage when it should. Second, **Prospect Theory**, the experimentally grounded replacement for expected utility theory, which explains why people make systematically inconsistent choices under risk. The third pillar, added late in his career, is **Noise**, the claim that unrecognized variability in judgment (the same case judged differently by different experts on different days) is as costly as bias and far less discussed.

Operational frame here: judgment is a process to be designed, not a faculty to be trusted. Decision hygiene beats decision genius. The number that goes on the table first will anchor everything that follows. Losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good, and that asymmetry shapes negotiation, pricing, and exit dynamics. The vivid recent case is overweighted; the base rate is underweighted; you can correct for both if you know to.

## Key Frameworks (now backed by citations)

**System 1 vs System 2.** Two modes of thought. System 1 is fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional, and runs nearly all everyday cognition. System 2 is slow, deliberate, logical, effortful, and is the override that has to be consciously engaged. Most decisions are System 1 dressed up as System 2 after the fact. The job is to know when the problem actually requires System 2 and force the switch.

**Prospect Theory.** People evaluate outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point, not as absolute states of wealth. Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains (loss aversion). The value function is concave in gains (diminishing positive sensitivity), convex in losses (diminishing negative sensitivity), and steeper in losses than in gains. Probability is weighted nonlinearly; small probabilities are overweighted, moderate-to-large probabilities underweighted. Expected utility theory cannot reproduce this; prospect theory can.

**Anchoring.** The number presented first biases all subsequent estimates, even when the number is obviously irrelevant. Documented in dozens of experiments including the wheel-of-fortune-and-UN-membership study. The strongest and most robust bias in the catalog.

**Availability and Representativeness.** Two heuristics that substitute easy questions for hard ones. Availability: probability is estimated by how easily examples come to mind, so vivid and recent events dominate. Representativeness: probability is judged by stereotype-fit instead of base rate, producing the base-rate fallacy (the Linda problem, the engineer/lawyer problem).

**WYSIATI: What You See Is All There Is.** System 1 builds the most coherent story available from the information at hand and does not flag the absence of what is missing. Confidence is a function of story coherence, not evidence quality. This is the engine behind overconfidence.

**Endowment Effect and Hindsight Bias.** People demand more to give up an object than they would pay to acquire it (the coffee mug experiments). Once an outcome is known, people believe they predicted it (the I-knew-it-all-along effect), which corrupts learning from past decisions.

**Noise.** Variation in judgment is distinct from bias and is roughly as large. Two judges scoring the same case on different days will disagree. Bias is the average; noise is the scatter. Almost all organizational discussion is about bias; noise goes unmeasured. The cure is decision hygiene: structured protocols, independent judgments aggregated, algorithms where defensible, noise audits.

## Primary-Source Citations

### From [[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]] (2011)

The canonical popular synthesis. Five parts: Two Systems, Heuristics and Biases, Overconfidence, Choices (Prospect Theory), Two Selves.

> System 1 operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control. System 2 allocates attention to the effortful mental activities that demand it, including complex computations.

The Linda problem: subjects rate "Linda is a bank teller and active in the feminist movement" as more likely than "Linda is a bank teller," violating elementary probability. Representativeness beats logic in System 1.

### From [[Kahneman Prospect Theory]] (1979)

The foundational Econometrica paper with Tversky. Documents the certainty effect, reflection effect, and isolation effect. Introduces the S-shaped value function.

> Losses loom larger than gains.

The coefficient of loss aversion in subsequent work converges on roughly 2.0 to 2.5. The reference point is the operative anchor, not absolute wealth.

### From [[Kahneman Noise Book]] (2021)

The late-career thesis, written with Sibony and Sunstein. Variation in judgment within or across people is as costly as bias and almost never measured.

> Wherever there is judgment, there is noise, and more of it than you think.

Decision hygiene as the remedy: structured protocols, independent assessments aggregated only at the end, algorithms where defensible, mandatory noise audits.

### From [[Kahneman Lex Fridman]] (2020-2024 series)

Late-career retrospective. The experiencing self vs the remembering self distinction. Pessimism about humans overcoming biases through awareness alone. *Noise* as the missing companion to *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Most cognition is System 1; System 2 is the expensive override that must be triggered deliberately.** ([[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]]) Design the moments where the override has to fire.
- **Losses loom roughly 2x larger than equivalent gains.** ([[Kahneman Prospect Theory]], [[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]]) Loss aversion shapes pricing, negotiation, hiring decisions, exit dynamics.
- **Anchoring is the strongest documented bias.** ([[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]]) The first number on the table biases every later number, including obviously irrelevant ones.
- **Availability and representativeness substitute easy questions for hard ones.** ([[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]]) Vivid recent cases dominate; base rates get ignored.
- **WYSIATI: confidence tracks story coherence, not evidence quality.** ([[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]]) The absence of information is invisible to System 1.
- **The endowment effect makes you overvalue what you already hold.** ([[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]]) Reference-point dependence applied to ownership.
- **Hindsight bias corrupts the learning loop.** ([[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]]) Without a written prior prediction, you will believe you predicted the outcome.
- **Noise is as costly as bias and almost never measured.** ([[Kahneman Noise Book]]) Decision hygiene, independent judgments, and noise audits beat exhortations to be unbiased.
- **The experiencing self and the remembering self disagree.** ([[Kahneman Thinking Fast and Slow]], [[Kahneman Lex Fridman]]) The peak-end rule means memory is not an honest record of experience.
- **Awareness alone is insufficient to overcome bias.** ([[Kahneman Lex Fridman]]) Process design beats personal vigilance.
