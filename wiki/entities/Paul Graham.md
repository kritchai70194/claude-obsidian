# Paul Graham

Co-founder of Y Combinator (2005), the seed accelerator that funded Stripe, Airbnb, Dropbox, Reddit, DoorDash, Coinbase, and ~50 other unicorns. Author of 180+ essays since 2001, the canonical body of early-stage startup wisdom in the modern era. Prior: co-founded Viaweb (sold to Yahoo, became Yahoo Store), Lisp programmer, author of *On Lisp* and *Hackers and Painters*.

Anchor mentor on the **early-stage epistemics pole** of this brain's [[Persona]]. Where the existing founder-operators describe what they built post-PMF, Graham defines the discipline of evaluating ideas, validating them with users, and surviving the years before PMF.

---

## Why This Mentor

PG essays are the single largest corpus of practitioner-derived startup wisdom in the modern era. The Y Combinator partner perspective gives him a unique data set: ~3,000 startups funded, ~250 reaching billion-dollar valuations, direct observation of why some succeeded and most failed. Unlike most startup writing, the essays are not theoretical; they encode patterns from observed cohorts.

The signature PG move: take a startup belief that "everyone knows" and either invert it or sharpen it past the conventional version. "Do things that don't scale" inverts "build for scale from day one". "Startup = Growth" sharpens "startups are different from small businesses". "How to get startup ideas" inverts "brainstorm ideas" with "notice problems you have".

His prose style is itself a teaching: short sentences, plain words, concrete examples, ruthless removal of qualifiers. Every essay reads like he removed half of what he wrote.

Frames here: startup = growth rate (definitional, not a stage); do things that don't scale in early stage; ideas should be noticed, not brainstormed; talk to users; pick cofounders carefully; tar pit ideas eat years.

## Core Frames (Pending Citation)

*Stub list, each will graduate to a primary-source citation as ingestion completes.*

- **Startup = growth.** The definition of startup is "company designed to grow fast". Not stage, not industry. If you are not growing, you are not a startup.
- **Do things that don't scale.** Early stage is when manual work that does not scale is the work. Recruit users by hand. Do their setup for them. Read every support email.
- **Ideas are noticed, not brainstormed.** Brainstormed ideas are bad. Good ideas come from noticing problems you (or someone you observe) actually has.
- **Talk to users.** Not "do customer research". Talk to them. Often. In person. Watch them use the thing.
- **Pick cofounders carefully.** The cofounders matter more than the idea. Bad cofounders are the most common cause of startup death.
- **Tar pit ideas.** Some ideas are seductive but kill you slowly. The classic example: "social network for X". Easy to start, hard to find PMF, eats years.
