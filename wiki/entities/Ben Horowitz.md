# Ben Horowitz

Co-founder and General Partner at Andreessen Horowitz (a16z, founded 2009 with Marc Andreessen). Former Co-founder and CEO of Loudcloud (later Opsware), which he ran from 1999 through the dot-com collapse and pivoted into an enterprise software company before selling to HP for $1.6B in 2007. Author of *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* (Harper Business, 2014), the canonical operator text on running a company through existential threat, and *What You Do Is Who You Are* (Harper Business, 2019), on culture as a CEO discipline.

Anchor mentor on the **founder-resilience pole**, complementary to [[Naval Ravikant]] (leverage, specific knowledge, philosophical operator frame), [[Brad Feld]] (mental health, board dynamics, multi-decade rhythm), and [[Brian Chesky]] (Founder Mode as named operating posture). Where the other three describe what a durable founder looks like, Horowitz describes the texture of the actual fight.

---

## Why This Mentor

Horowitz fills the gap that the other three poles assume away. [[seed-corpus-technical-ceo]], [[seed-corpus-engineer-planners]], and [[seed-corpus-unicorn-builders]] all assume the founder can keep operating. Horowitz explains what happens when you cannot, and what the job actually is when the company is twelve months from death.

His Loudcloud run is the foundational case study: IPO'd into the dot-com collapse, watched the public market wipe out 95% of the stock, laid off 80 of 140 people in the pivot, eventually sold the residual business to HP for $1.6B. He did not write a memoir of success. He wrote a manual for the periods where success is not yet visible.

Two contributions matter most. First, the **Peacetime CEO / Wartime CEO** distinction, which says that the operating mode of a company has to switch depending on whether you are expanding the market or fighting for survival, and that most CEOs are trained only for peacetime. Second, **The Struggle**, the named psychological default state of running a startup, which most founders mistake for personal failure.

Frames here: wartime is the unnatural mode but often the necessary one; The Struggle is the baseline, not the exception; take care of the people first or the company dies on the inside before it dies on the outside; hire for strength, not lack of weakness; firing executives is part of the job, not a failure of judgment.

## Key Frameworks (now backed by citations)

**Peacetime CEO vs Wartime CEO.** Different operating modes, different decision rights, different team norms. Peacetime: expand the market, optimize culture, big-picture leadership. Wartime: survive, focus, mode-switch the org, tight control, almost any means necessary to keep the company alive. Most CEOs are trained for peacetime. Wartime is the unnatural mode that most founders eventually must learn.

**The Struggle.** The default psychological state of running a startup, not the failure mode. When you wonder why you started the company. When people ask why you do not quit and you do not have an answer. Most founders feel like they are failing most of the time. Knowing this is the normal condition is half the battle.

**Take Care of the People, the Products, and the Profits, in That Order.** The order is load-bearing. Reverse it and the culture dies. Wall Street trains executives to put profits first; that is how good companies turn into bad ones.

**Lead Bullets, Not Silver Bullets.** Hard problems require sustained hard work, not clever solutions. When a competitor is beating you on product, the answer is to build a better product, not to outflank them with marketing.

## Primary-Source Citations

### From [[Horowitz Hard Things Book]] (2014)

The canonical operator text. Structured around CEO psychology and the operational mechanics of running a company through wartime.

On the central skill:

> The hard thing isn't setting a big, hairy, audacious goal. The hard thing is laying people off when you miss the big goal.

On hiring discipline:

> I've never seen a CEO try hard and fail at hiring. I've seen many fail by not trying.

On the right ordering:

> Take care of the people, the products, and the profits, in that order.

On the texture of the job: the chapters on layoffs, demoting a loyal friend, firing an executive, and "the CEO psychology" are the most-quoted operator pages in startup literature.

### From [[Horowitz Peacetime Wartime CEO]] (2011)

The named distinction. Peacetime CEO maximizes a strong market position. Wartime CEO operates under existential threat where any single mistake can kill the company. Peacetime tolerates dissent and process; wartime cannot afford either. Most CEOs are peacetime-trained and must learn wartime under fire.

> Peacetime CEO knows that proper protocol leads to winning. Wartime CEO violates protocol in order to win.

### From [[Horowitz The Struggle]] (2011)

The definitive passage on founder psychology, reproduced widely.

> The Struggle is when you wonder why you started the company in the first place. The Struggle is when people ask you why you don't quit and you don't know the answer.

The thesis: The Struggle is the default, not the failure. Every great founder has lived in it. Most have considered quitting. The job is to keep operating anyway.

### From [[Horowitz Take Care of People]]

The expanded thesis behind one of the most-cited Horowitz aphorisms. People first because culture is what survives founders. Products second because product is what people build. Profits third because profits follow the first two. Reverse the order and you optimize the wrong thing for the wrong reason.

The Loudcloud / Opsware case study is the proof: Horowitz laid off 80 of 140 people in the pivot from Loudcloud to Opsware. The remaining 60 stayed because the way he handled the layoff demonstrated that the order was real.

## Core Frames (now backed by citations)

- **Wartime mode and peacetime mode require different CEOs (or the same CEO operating differently).** ([[Horowitz Peacetime Wartime CEO]]) Wrong mode at wrong time kills companies. Most founders default to peacetime and must learn wartime under fire.
- **The Struggle is the default state, not the failure mode.** ([[Horowitz The Struggle]], [[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) Knowing this is normal is half the battle.
- **Take care of the people, then the products, then the profits, in that order.** ([[Horowitz Take Care of People]], [[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) Reverse it and the culture dies.
- **Hire for strength, not lack of weakness.** ([[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) Senior executives are too strong-attribute-skewed for balanced rubrics.
- **Firing executives is part of the job.** ([[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) Not doing it is choosing the wrong person over the company.
- **There are no silver bullets, only lead bullets.** ([[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) Hard problems require sustained hard work, not clever solutions.
- **The hard thing isn't setting the goal. The hard thing is laying people off when you miss it.** ([[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) The CEO's actual job is execution under conditions where the easy answers do not work.
- **CEO psychology is a separate discipline.** ([[Horowitz Hard Things Book]]) The internal game of staying functional when everything is on fire is itself a skill that has to be developed.
